Alcohols

Alcohols are organic compounds containing the hydroxyl group (–OH) bonded to a saturated carbon atom. The general formula is R–OH. They are named by replacing the "-e" of the parent alkane with "-ol" (methanol, ethanol, propanol, etc.).

Classification

  • Primary (1°) alcohol: The –OH is on a carbon bonded to only one other carbon. Example: ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH).
  • Secondary (2°) alcohol: The –OH is on a carbon bonded to two other carbons. Example: 2-propanol (CH₃CHOHCH₃).
  • Tertiary (3°) alcohol: The –OH is on a carbon bonded to three other carbons. Example: 2-methyl-2-propanol ((CH₃)₃COH).

Monofunctional alcohols have one OH group (methanol, ethanol). Polyfunctional alcohols have multiple OH groups: diols have two (e.g., ethylene glycol, HOCH₂CH₂OH) and triols have three (e.g., glycerol, HOCH₂CHOHCH₂OH).

Physical Properties

  • Hydrogen bonding: The OH group forms hydrogen bonds, giving alcohols higher boiling points than alkanes of similar molecular weight.
  • Water solubility: Small alcohols (C1–C3) are fully miscible with water. Solubility decreases as the hydrocarbon chain grows.
  • Methanol is extremely toxic: as little as 5–10 mL can cause permanent blindness (from formic acid damage to the optic nerve) and 30 mL can be lethal, producing severe metabolic acidosis. Ethanol is the drinkable alcohol, metabolized at approximately 0.15 g per kg body weight per hour. Chronic consumption above 50–60 g/day leads to fatty liver, progressing to hepatitis and cirrhosis at higher intake levels.
  • Ethylene glycol (the main component of antifreeze) is similarly dangerous: a lethal dose is approximately 100 mL. It is metabolized to oxalic acid, which precipitates as calcium oxalate crystals in the kidneys, causing renal failure.

Acidity Comparison of O–H Compounds

The relative acidity of compounds with O–H bonds follows: carboxylic acids (Kₐ ≈ 10⁻⁵) > phenols (Kₐ ≈ 10⁻¹⁰) > water > alcohols (Kₐ ≈ 10⁻¹⁷). Phenols are stronger acids than alcohols because the phenoxide anion is stabilized by resonance delocalization of the negative charge into the aromatic ring, which is not possible for alkoxide ions.

Reactions of Alcohols

Dehydration (elimination): Heating an alcohol with a strong acid catalyst (H₂SO₄) removes water, producing an alkene. The ease of dehydration follows: tertiary > secondary > primary.

Oxidation: This depends on the class of alcohol:

Alcohol ClassOxidation ProductFurther Oxidation
Primary (1°)Aldehyde (R–CHO)Carboxylic acid (R–COOH)
Secondary (2°)Ketone (R₂C=O)No further oxidation
Tertiary (3°)No reaction (resistant)-

Reaction with alkali metals: Alcohols react with alkali metals (Na, K) to form alkoxides, which are strong bases, plus hydrogen gas. For example, sodium reacts with ethanol to produce sodium ethoxide (CH₃CH₂O⁻ Na⁺) and H₂. This reaction is slower than water's reaction with sodium, reflecting the lower acidity of alcohols compared to water.

Nucleophilic substitution with HX: Alcohols undergo substitution when treated with hydrohalic acids (HCl, HBr), replacing the –OH with a halogen. For example, butan-1-ol reacts with HBr to form 1-bromobutane and water. The ease of this substitution follows the order: tertiary > secondary > primary.

Esterification: An alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid (in the presence of an acid catalyst) to form an ester and water. This is a reversible (equilibrium) reaction.

R–OH + R'–COOH ⇌ R'–COOR + H₂O

Phenols

Phenols have an –OH group bonded directly to an aromatic ring. The simplest is phenol (C₆H₅OH). Unlike alcohols, phenols are weakly acidic (pKa ~ 10) because the negative charge of the phenoxide ion (formed after losing H⁺) is stabilized by delocalization into the aromatic ring.

Important Names

  • Glycerol (propane-1,2,3-triol): Three OH groups; used in fats, moisturizers.
  • Phenol (hydroxybenzene): Antiseptic; weakly acidic.
  • Cresols: Methylphenols (ortho-, meta-, para- isomers). Disinfectants.
  • Hydroquinone (benzene-1,4-diol): Used in photography and skin-lightening products.
  • Pyrocatechol (benzene-1,2-diol): Component of catecholamine pathway.

Phenol in Medical History

Phenol (carbolic acid) was the first antiseptic used in surgery, introduced by Joseph Lister in 1867 as a dilute aqueous solution for wound treatment. Lysol, a commercial disinfectant still in use today, contains a mixture of the three cresol isomers (ortho-, meta-, and para-methylphenol). While phenols are effective germicides, they are also toxic to the central nervous system, liver, and kidneys, and are caustic to skin at higher concentrations.

Quinone Formation from Diphenols

Diphenols (dihydroxybenzenes) can be oxidized to quinones, which are cyclic diketones. Hydroquinone (benzene-1,4-diol) is oxidized to p-benzoquinone, and this reaction is reversible. This quinone/hydroquinone interconversion is biologically significant: ubiquinone (coenzyme Q) uses the same redox chemistry in the mitochondrial electron transport chain to shuttle electrons between complexes.

Key Points - Chapter 22

  • Alcohols (R–OH): classified as 1°, 2°, 3° based on the carbon bearing OH.
  • Hydrogen bonding gives alcohols high boiling points and water solubility (for small ones).
  • Oxidation: 1° → aldehyde → carboxylic acid; 2° → ketone; 3° → no oxidation.
  • Phenols are acidic (stabilized by ring delocalization); alcohols are not appreciably acidic.
  • Esterification: alcohol + carboxylic acid → ester + water.

Chapter 2

Nomenclature of Inorganic Compounds

2.1 Why Naming Matters

Chemistry has millions of known compounds. Without a systematic naming scheme, communication would be impossible. The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) provides rules so that every compound has one unambiguous name, and every name corresponds to exactly one compound.

2.2 Binary Compounds (Two Elements)

Metal + Nonmetal (Ionic Compounds)

Name the metal first, then the nonmetal with its ending changed to -ide.

  • NaCl → sodium chloride
  • MgO → magnesium oxide
  • CaBr₂ → calcium bromide

Use Greek prefixes to indicate the number of each atom. The first element usually does not get a prefix if there is only one atom of it.

2.3 Stock Notation for Transition Metals

Many transition metals can form ions with different charges. To specify which charge the metal carries, we place a Roman numeral in parentheses after the metal name.

  • FeCl₂ → iron(II) chloride - Fe is +2
  • FeCl₃ → iron(III) chloride - Fe is +3
  • Cu₂O → copper(I) oxide - Cu is +1
  • CuO → copper(II) oxide - Cu is +2

2.4 Oxides and Hydroxides

Oxides contain the O2− ion. Metal oxides are typically basic (they react with water to form bases): Na₂O + H₂O → 2 NaOH. Nonmetal oxides are typically acidic (they react with water to form acids): SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄.

Hydroxides contain the OH⁻ group. They are named as metal hydroxide: NaOH = sodium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂ = calcium hydroxide.

2.5 Naming Acids

Binary acids (hydrogen + nonmetal, dissolved in water): Use the prefix hydro- and the suffix -ic acid. HCl(aq) = hydrochloric acid; HBr(aq) = hydrobromic acid.

Oxyacids (hydrogen + polyatomic ion containing oxygen): If the ion ends in -ate, the acid ends in -ic acid. If the ion ends in -ite, the acid ends in -ous acid.

  • SO₄2− = sulfate → H₂SO₄ = sulfuric acid
  • SO₃2− = sulfite → H₂SO₃ = sulfurous acid
  • NO₃⁻ = nitrate → HNO₃ = nitric acid
  • NO₂⁻ = nitrite → HNO₂ = nitrous acid

Additional Polyatomic Ions

Some polyatomic ions beyond the common ones above appear frequently in medical and analytical chemistry:

  • Silicate (SiO₃2−): derived from silicic acid (H₂SiO₃). Sodium silicate (Na₂SiO₃), known as "water glass," is used in industrial adhesives and egg preservation.
  • Borate (BO₃3−): derived from boric acid (H₃BO₃). Since boron has only one common oxidation state (+3), only the -ate suffix is used.
  • Selenite (SeO₃2−) and selenate (SeO₄2−): selenium oxyanions that follow the same -ite/-ate pattern as sulfite and sulfate. Selenium is a biologically essential trace element.

2.8 Peroxides

Peroxides contain the O₂2− ion, in which each oxygen has an oxidation state of −I rather than the usual −II. Alkali metals form peroxides with the formula M₂O₂ (e.g., Na₂O₂, Li₂O₂), while alkaline earth metals form MO₂ (e.g., BaO₂, MgO₂). The most familiar peroxide is hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), used as an antiseptic and bleaching agent. If you see a group IIA metal with the formula MO₂, it is a peroxide (oxygen is −I), not an oxide of M4+.

2.9 Salts

A salt is an ionic compound formed from the reaction of an acid with a base. Salts are named by combining the cation name and the anion name: Na₂SO₄ = sodium sulfate; KNO₃ = potassium nitrate; CaCO₃ = calcium carbonate.

2.10 Acid Salts

When not all ionizable hydrogen atoms of a polyprotic acid are replaced by a metal cation, the result is an acid salt. These are named by placing "hydrogen" (with a Greek prefix if needed) between the cation and anion names:

  • NaH₂PO₄ → sodium dihydrogen phosphate (two H remain)
  • Na₂HPO₄ → disodium hydrogen phosphate (one H remains)
  • Na₃PO₄ → trisodium phosphate (no remaining H, a normal salt)
  • NaHCO₃ → sodium hydrogen carbonate (commonly called sodium bicarbonate)
  • Ca(HS)₂ → calcium hydrogen sulfide

The hydrogen carbonate ion (HCO₃⁻) is routinely called "bicarbonate" in clinical medicine.

2.11 Hydrates

Hydrates are crystalline salts that incorporate a fixed number of water molecules into their crystal lattice. They are named by appending a Greek prefix plus "hydrate" after the salt name:

  • CuSO₄·5H₂O → copper(II) sulfate pentahydrate (blue vitriol)
  • FeSO₄·7H₂O → iron(II) sulfate heptahydrate
  • MgCl₂·6H₂O → magnesium chloride hexahydrate
  • CaSO₄·½H₂O → calcium sulfate hemihydrate (plaster of Paris)

Common hydrate prefixes: mono- (1), di- (2), tri- (3), tetra- (4), penta- (5), hexa- (6), hepta- (7), deca- (10), dodeca- (12).

2.12 Basic Salts and Double Salts

Basic salts contain hydroxide ions alongside the primary anion. They are named "cation hydroxy-anion":

  • Mg(OH)Cl → magnesium hydroxychloride

Double salts contain two different cations or two different anions. All ions are listed alphabetically:

  • KMgF₃ → potassium magnesium fluoride (two cations)
  • CaCl(ClO) → calcium chloride hypochlorite (two anions)

In every case, the total positive charge must equal the total negative charge for the compound to be electrically neutral.

2.13 Thioacids and Thiosalts

When an oxygen atom in an oxoacid is replaced by a sulfur atom, the prefix "thio-" is added to the name:

  • Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) → thiosulfuric acid (H₂S₂O₃) by replacing one O with S
  • Sodium thiosulfate (Na₂S₂O₃) is used in photographic processing and as a cyanide antidote
  • Cyanic acid (HOCN) → thiocyanic acid (HSCN); its potassium salt KSCN is potassium thiocyanate

2.14 Polyacids

Polyacids contain more than one central atom of the same element within the anion. Important examples include:

  • Dichromic acid (H₂Cr₂O₈) → contains two chromium atoms; its potassium salt K₂Cr₂O₈ (potassium dichromate) is a strong oxidizing agent in analytical chemistry
  • Tetraboric acid (H₂B₄O₈) → contains four boron atoms; its sodium salt decahydrate Na₂B₄O₈·10H₂O is known as borax

Mnemonic: Acid Naming from Ion Suffix

"I ate (-ate) something -ic, and now I feel -ite -ous" - -ate ions form -ic acids; -ite ions form -ous acids.

Key Points - Chapter 2

  • Ionic compounds: name the metal first, then nonmetal with -ide ending.
  • Covalent compounds: use Greek prefixes (di-, tri-, etc.) and -ide ending.
  • Transition metals with variable charges use Stock notation: Roman numeral in parentheses.
  • Metal oxides are basic; nonmetal oxides are acidic.
  • -ate ion → -ic acid; -ite ion → -ous acid.
  • Binary acids: hydro___ic acid (HCl → hydrochloric acid).
  • Memorize the most common polyatomic ions (sulfate, nitrate, phosphate, carbonate, hydroxide, ammonium).

Test Yourself - Chapter 2

1. Name the compound Fe₂O₃.
Show Answer
Iron(III) oxide. Since oxygen is −2 and there are three oxygens (total −6), two iron atoms must provide +6 total, so each Fe is +3.
2. Write the formula for calcium phosphate.
Show Answer
Ca₃(PO₄)₂. Calcium is +2, phosphate is −3. To balance: 3 × (+2) = +6 and 2 × (−3) = −6.
3. What is the acid name for H₂SO₃(aq)?
Show Answer
Sulfurous acid. The sulfite ion (SO₃2−) ends in -ite, so the acid ends in -ous.

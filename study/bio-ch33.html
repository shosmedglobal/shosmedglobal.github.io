Chapter 33

Nervous System - The Central Nervous System

🧠Practice this chapterTest yourself with chapter-specific questions →

The brain is arguably the most complex organ in the known universe. Together with the spinal cord, it forms the central nervous system (CNS), which receives sensory information, integrates it, stores memories, generates thoughts and emotions, and sends motor commands to the body.

Embryonic Development of the Brain

The brain develops from the anterior end of the neural tube during embryogenesis. By the fourth week of development, three primary brain vesicles emerge: the prosencephalon (forebrain), mesencephalon (midbrain), and rhombencephalon (hindbrain). These further divide into five secondary vesicles: the telencephalon and diencephalon (from the forebrain), the mesencephalon (midbrain, unchanged), and the metencephalon and myelencephalon (from the hindbrain). The telencephalon becomes the cerebral hemispheres; the diencephalon becomes the thalamus and hypothalamus; the metencephalon gives rise to the pons and cerebellum; and the myelencephalon becomes the medulla oblongata.

Brain Regions

1. Brainstem

The brainstem connects the brain to the spinal cord and controls the most basic life-sustaining functions. From bottom to top, it consists of:

  • Medulla oblongata - controls vital autonomic reflexes: breathing rate, heart rate, blood pressure, swallowing, vomiting, and coughing. Damage here is often fatal.
  • Pons - serves as a relay station between the cerebrum and cerebellum. It also helps regulate breathing and sleep.
  • Midbrain (mesencephalon) - processes visual and auditory reflexes (e.g., turning your head toward a loud noise). Contains the substantia nigra (dopamine-producing region important for movement).

2. Cerebellum

Located at the back of the brain, below the cerebrum. The cerebellum coordinates voluntary movements, maintains balance and posture, and fine-tunes motor activity. It does not initiate movement but ensures that movements are smooth, accurate, and well-timed. Think of it as a quality-control center for movement. Damage leads to ataxia (uncoordinated, clumsy movement).

3. Diencephalon

Nestled between the brainstem and the cerebrum:

  • Thalamus - the brain's relay station. Almost all sensory information (except smell) passes through the thalamus on its way to the cerebral cortex. It filters and routes signals to the appropriate cortical area.
  • Hypothalamus - small but critical. Controls homeostasis: body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep-wake cycles, and the endocrine system (via the pituitary gland). It is the main link between the nervous and endocrine systems.
  • Epithalamus - a small structure posterior to the thalamus. Its most prominent component is the pineal gland, which secretes melatonin to regulate circadian rhythms. The epithalamus also contains the habenular nuclei, involved in reward processing, pain modulation, and stress responses.

4. Cerebrum

The largest part of the brain, divided into left and right hemispheres. The surface is covered by the cerebral cortex - a thin layer of gray matter (cell bodies) with deep folds (gyri = ridges, sulci = grooves) that increase surface area. Beneath the cortex is white matter (myelinated axons that connect different brain regions).

Each hemisphere has four lobes:

  • Frontal lobe - planning, decision-making, personality, voluntary movement (primary motor cortex), speech production (Broca's area)
  • Parietal lobe - processes touch, temperature, pain, pressure (primary somatosensory cortex)
  • Temporal lobe - hearing (auditory cortex), language comprehension (Wernicke's area), memory
  • Occipital lobe - visual processing (primary visual cortex)

Deep within the cerebrum are basal nuclei (basal ganglia) - clusters of gray matter that help regulate and coordinate voluntary movement. Dysfunction of the basal nuclei contributes to disorders like Parkinson's disease.

Corpus Callosum

A thick band of white matter (about 200 million axons) connecting the left and right hemispheres, allowing them to communicate and share information. If severed, the hemispheres operate independently ("split-brain").

Limbic System

A group of interconnected structures deep in the brain involved in emotion, motivation, and memory:

  • Amygdala - processes fear and other strong emotions
  • Hippocampus - essential for forming new long-term memories
  • Parts of the hypothalamus and thalamus also contribute

Functional Areas of the Cortex

  • Motor areas (frontal lobe) - primary motor cortex controls voluntary movement. Each point on the cortex controls a specific body part (mapped as the motor homunculus).
  • Sensory areas (parietal lobe) - primary somatosensory cortex receives touch and body sensations. Also mapped as a homunculus. Visual cortex (occipital), auditory cortex (temporal).
  • Association areas - large regions of cortex that integrate information from multiple senses, plan actions, and form thoughts. They make up most of the cortex.

Electroencephalography (EEG)

An electroencephalogram (EEG) records the electrical activity of the brain using electrodes placed on the scalp. It detects synchronized postsynaptic potentials from large populations of cortical neurons. Different brain wave patterns correspond to different states of consciousness: alpha waves (relaxed wakefulness), beta waves (alert, active thinking), theta waves (drowsiness, light sleep), and delta waves (deep sleep). EEG is clinically essential for diagnosing epilepsy, evaluating sleep disorders, and assessing brain death.

Spinal Cord Structure

The spinal cord runs from the brainstem down through the vertebral column. In cross-section:

  • Gray matter (butterfly/H-shaped center) - contains neuron cell bodies, interneurons, and synapses. Dorsal horns receive sensory input; ventral horns send motor output.
  • White matter (outer region) - myelinated axons organized into tracts (ascending tracts carry sensory information up to the brain; descending tracts carry motor commands down from the brain).
  • The spinal cord is protected by vertebrae, meninges (three membrane layers), and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
Key Points - Central Nervous System
  • Brainstem (medulla, pons, midbrain) controls vital autonomic functions and reflexes.
  • Cerebellum coordinates movement, balance, and posture (does not initiate movement).
  • Thalamus = relay station for sensory information. Hypothalamus = homeostasis and endocrine control.
  • Cerebral cortex (gray matter) is the site of conscious thought, sensation, and voluntary action.
  • Four lobes: frontal (motor, planning), parietal (sensory), temporal (hearing, language), occipital (vision).
  • Corpus callosum connects the two hemispheres. Limbic system handles emotion and memory.
  • Spinal cord: gray matter inside (cell bodies), white matter outside (tracts).

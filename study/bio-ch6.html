Chapter 6

Molecular Bases of Gene Expression — RNA and Proteins

6.1 The Central Dogma

The central dogma of molecular biology, formulated by Francis Crick, describes the flow of genetic information in cells: DNA → RNA → Protein. DNA serves as the permanent archive; it is transcribed into RNA (a temporary working copy), which is then translated into protein (the functional molecule that carries out the work). The one major exception is reverse transcription in retroviruses (RNA → DNA), but the general principle holds across all domains of life.

6.2 Transcription

Transcription is the process of copying a gene's DNA sequence into a complementary RNA molecule. It is carried out by RNA polymerase.

  1. Initiation: RNA polymerase binds to a specific DNA sequence called the promoter (in prokaryotes, the sigma factor of RNA polymerase recognizes the promoter; in eukaryotes, transcription factors help RNA polymerase II bind). The two DNA strands separate locally, and transcription begins.
  2. Elongation: RNA polymerase moves along the template strand (read 3' to 5') and synthesizes the RNA strand in the 5' to 3' direction. The RNA sequence is complementary to the template strand and identical to the coding strand (except with uracil instead of thymine).
  3. Termination: RNA polymerase reaches a terminator sequence. In prokaryotes, this may involve a hairpin loop in the RNA (intrinsic termination) or the rho protein (rho-dependent termination). The RNA is released.

6.3 RNA Types and Processing

The primary RNA transcript in eukaryotes (called pre-mRNA) must be processed before it can be translated:

  • 5' cap: A modified guanine nucleotide (7-methylguanosine) is added to the 5' end. This protects the mRNA from degradation and helps ribosomes recognize it.
  • 3' poly-A tail: A string of 100–250 adenine nucleotides is added to the 3' end by poly-A polymerase. This also protects against degradation and aids in mRNA export from the nucleus.
  • Splicing: Non-coding sequences called introns are removed, and the remaining coding sequences called exons are joined together by the spliceosome (a complex of small nuclear RNAs and proteins). This allows alternative splicing, where different combinations of exons produce different mRNA variants (and thus different proteins) from a single gene.

6.4 The Genetic Code

The genetic code is a set of rules by which the nucleotide sequence of mRNA is translated into the amino acid sequence of a protein. The code is read in groups of three nucleotides called codons. With four bases and three positions, there are 43 = 64 possible codons.

  • 61 codons specify amino acids (there are only 20 standard amino acids, so most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon — this is the degeneracy or redundancy of the genetic code).
  • 3 stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA) signal the end of translation.
  • 1 start codon (AUG) signals the beginning of translation and codes for methionine.

The genetic code is nearly universal — the same codons specify the same amino acids in almost all organisms, from bacteria to humans, providing powerful evidence that all life shares a common ancestor.

6.5 Translation

Translation occurs on ribosomes, which are composed of a large subunit and a small subunit, both made of rRNA and proteins. In eukaryotes, ribosomes have an 80S size (60S large + 40S small); in prokaryotes, 70S (50S + 30S).

Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are the enzymes that attach the correct amino acid to its corresponding tRNA. There is at least one synthetase for each amino acid, and the accuracy of this "charging" step is crucial for the fidelity of translation.

Translation proceeds in three phases:

  1. Initiation: The small ribosomal subunit binds the mRNA and positions itself at the start codon (AUG). The initiator tRNA (carrying methionine) base-pairs with AUG at the P site. The large subunit then joins, forming the complete ribosome.
  2. Elongation: A new aminoacyl-tRNA enters the A site (acceptor site), with its anticodon complementary to the mRNA codon. A peptide bond forms between the amino acid in the P site (peptidyl site) and the one in the A site — this reaction is catalyzed by the ribosome's rRNA (making the ribosome a ribozyme). The ribosome then translocates one codon forward: the tRNA in the P site moves to the E site (exit site) and leaves, the tRNA in the A site shifts to the P site, and the A site is vacant for the next tRNA. This cycle repeats.
  3. Termination: When the ribosome encounters a stop codon, no tRNA can bind. Instead, a release factor enters the A site, triggering release of the polypeptide chain. The ribosome subunits dissociate.

6.7 Gene Regulation: Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes

Prokaryotic Regulation: The Operon Model

In prokaryotes, genes with related functions are often grouped into operons — clusters of genes under the control of a single promoter. The lac operon in E. coli is the classic example: when lactose is absent, a repressor protein binds the operator region and blocks RNA polymerase. When lactose is present, it binds the repressor, causing it to release the operator, allowing transcription of the genes needed to metabolize lactose. This is negative regulation (removal of an inhibitor). Positive regulation involves an activator protein that enhances transcription (e.g., CAP protein in the presence of cAMP).

Eukaryotic Regulation

Eukaryotic gene regulation is far more complex and occurs at multiple levels:

  • Chromatin remodeling: Histone acetylation loosens chromatin (promotes transcription); methylation can silence genes.
  • Transcription factors: Activators and repressors bind enhancer and silencer regions, which can be thousands of base pairs from the promoter.
  • Post-transcriptional: Alternative splicing, mRNA stability, miRNA-mediated degradation.
  • Translational control: Regulation of when and how efficiently mRNA is translated.
  • Post-translational: Protein modification (phosphorylation, ubiquitination), folding, and degradation.

Key Points — Chapter 6

  • The central dogma: DNA is transcribed into RNA, which is translated into protein.
  • Eukaryotic pre-mRNA is processed with a 5' cap, 3' poly-A tail, and splicing (introns removed, exons joined).
  • The genetic code is triplet, degenerate, and nearly universal; AUG is the start codon; UAA, UAG, UGA are stop codons.
  • Translation involves initiation, elongation, and termination at ribosomes; tRNA delivers amino acids; the ribosome catalyzes peptide bond formation.
  • Protein structure has four levels: primary (sequence), secondary (helices/sheets), tertiary (3D fold), quaternary (multi-subunit assembly).
  • Prokaryotes regulate genes largely through operons; eukaryotes use multi-level regulation from chromatin to post-translational modification.

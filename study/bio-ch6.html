Chapter 6

Molecular Bases of Gene Expression — RNA and Proteins

6.1 The Central Dogma

The central dogma of molecular biology, formulated by Francis Crick, describes the flow of genetic information in cells: DNA → RNA → Protein. DNA serves as the permanent archive; it is transcribed into RNA (a temporary working copy), which is then translated into protein (the functional molecule that carries out the work). The one major exception is reverse transcription in retroviruses (RNA → DNA), but the general principle holds across all domains of life.

RNA Properties and the RNA World

RNA has remarkable properties beyond its role as an information carrier. Some RNA molecules can self-replicate (albeit imperfectly, introducing variability). Others function as enzymes called ribozymes — catalytic RNA molecules. The ribosome itself is fundamentally a ribozyme, with rRNA catalyzing peptide bond formation. The spliceosome similarly contains catalytic RNA (snRNA) components. Several coenzymes (NAD⁺, FAD, CoA) are nucleotide derivatives, suggesting RNA's ancient centrality in metabolism. These properties support the RNA world hypothesis for the origin of life.

6.2 Transcription

Transcription is the process of copying a gene's DNA sequence into a complementary RNA molecule. It is carried out by RNA polymerase.

  1. Initiation: RNA polymerase binds to a specific DNA sequence called the promoter. In prokaryotes, the sigma factor of RNA polymerase recognizes the promoter directly. In eukaryotes, the promoter often contains a TATA box (consensus sequence TATAAA, located about 25–30 base pairs upstream of the start site). The general transcription factor TFIID (specifically its TBP subunit — TATA-binding protein) recognizes and binds the TATA box, initiating assembly of the pre-initiation complex that recruits RNA polymerase II. Not all eukaryotic genes have TATA boxes; many housekeeping genes use alternative promoter elements. The two DNA strands separate locally, and transcription begins.
  2. Elongation: RNA polymerase moves along the template strand (read 3' to 5') and synthesizes the RNA strand in the 5' to 3' direction. The RNA sequence is complementary to the template strand and identical to the coding strand (except with uracil instead of thymine).
  3. Termination: RNA polymerase reaches a terminator sequence. In prokaryotes, this may involve a hairpin loop in the RNA (intrinsic termination) or the rho protein (rho-dependent termination). The RNA is released.

6.3 RNA Types and Processing

The primary RNA transcript in eukaryotes (called pre-mRNA) must be processed before it can be translated:

  • 5' cap: A modified guanine nucleotide (7-methylguanosine) is added to the 5' end. This protects the mRNA from degradation and helps ribosomes recognize it.
  • 3' poly-A tail: A string of 100–250 adenine nucleotides is added to the 3' end by poly-A polymerase. This also protects against degradation and aids in mRNA export from the nucleus.
  • Splicing: Non-coding sequences called introns are removed, and the remaining coding sequences called exons are joined together by the spliceosome (a complex of small nuclear RNAs and proteins). This allows alternative splicing, where different combinations of exons produce different mRNA variants (and thus different proteins) from a single gene.

This ensures that secretory proteins are correctly routed to the endomembrane system.

6.4 The Genetic Code

The genetic code is a set of rules by which the nucleotide sequence of mRNA is translated into the amino acid sequence of a protein. The code is read in groups of three nucleotides called codons. With four bases and three positions, there are 4³ = 64 possible codons.

  • 61 codons specify amino acids (there are only 20 standard amino acids, so most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon — this is the degeneracy or redundancy of the genetic code).
  • 3 stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA) signal the end of translation.
  • 1 start codon (AUG) signals the beginning of translation and codes for methionine.

The genetic code is nearly universal — the same codons specify the same amino acids in almost all organisms, from bacteria to humans, providing powerful evidence that all life shares a common ancestor.

6.5 Translation

Translation occurs on ribosomes, which are composed of a large subunit and a small subunit, both made of rRNA and proteins. Ribosome size is measured in Svedberg units (S), which reflect sedimentation rate during centrifugation rather than simple mass addition. Prokaryotic ribosomes are 70S (composed of 50S and 30S subunits, containing 23S, 16S, and 5S rRNA). Eukaryotic ribosomes are 80S (60S and 40S subunits, containing 28S, 18S, 5.8S, and 5S rRNA). This size difference is clinically important because many antibiotics specifically target 70S ribosomes, killing bacteria without harming human cells.

Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are the enzymes that attach the correct amino acid to its corresponding tRNA. There is at least one synthetase for each amino acid, and the accuracy of this "charging" step is crucial for the fidelity of translation.

Although there are 61 sense codons, cells typically contain only about 45 different tRNA species. This is possible because of wobble base pairing: the third position of the codon (3' end) and the first position of the anticodon (5' end) can form non-standard base pairs. For example, inosine (I) in the anticodon wobble position can pair with U, C, or A in the codon. This wobble flexibility explains why many synonymous codons that differ only in their third position are recognized by the same tRNA.

Translation proceeds in three phases:

  1. Initiation: The small ribosomal subunit binds the mRNA and positions itself at the start codon (AUG). The initiator tRNA (carrying methionine) base-pairs with AUG at the P site. The large subunit then joins, forming the complete ribosome.
  2. Elongation: A new aminoacyl-tRNA enters the A site (acceptor site), with its anticodon complementary to the mRNA codon. A peptide bond forms between the amino acid in the P site (peptidyl site) and the one in the A site — this reaction is catalyzed by the ribosome's rRNA (making the ribosome a ribozyme). The ribosome then translocates one codon forward: the tRNA in the P site moves to the E site (exit site) and leaves, the tRNA in the A site shifts to the P site, and the A site is vacant for the next tRNA. This cycle repeats.
  3. Termination: When the ribosome encounters a stop codon, no tRNA can bind. Instead, a release factor enters the A site, triggering release of the polypeptide chain. The ribosome subunits dissociate.

Polyribosomes and Open Reading Frames

Multiple ribosomes can translate a single mRNA molecule simultaneously, forming a polyribosome (polysome). This allows efficient production of many copies of the same protein from one mRNA. Each ribosome progresses along the mRNA from the 5' cap toward the 3' end, with the ribosome closest to the 5' end carrying the shortest nascent polypeptide. The concept of an open reading frame (ORF) — a stretch of codons from a start to a stop codon without interruption — is how potential protein-coding genes are identified in DNA sequences.

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Protein Synthesis

A key difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic gene expression is spatial organization. In prokaryotes, which lack a nuclear envelope, transcription and translation are coupled — ribosomes begin translating the mRNA while it is still being transcribed. In eukaryotes, the nuclear envelope physically separates transcription (in the nucleus) from translation (in the cytoplasm), creating an opportunity for RNA processing (capping, polyadenylation, splicing) between the two steps. This spatial separation enables additional regulatory checkpoints that prokaryotes lack.

Posttranslational Modifications

Proteins undergo numerous posttranslational modifications that regulate their activity, localization, and lifespan. Key modifications include phosphorylation (adding phosphate groups, often on serine, threonine, or tyrosine residues, controlling enzyme activity), glycosylation (adding sugar chains, important for secreted proteins and cell-surface recognition), ubiquitination (tagging proteins for degradation by proteasomes), acetylation (modifying lysine residues, important in histone regulation), methylation (of lysine or arginine residues), hydroxylation (critical for collagen stability), and sulfation (of tyrosine residues, affecting protein-protein interactions).

Insulin Processing as an Example

Insulin biosynthesis illustrates posttranslational processing. The gene encodes preproinsulin, which contains a signal peptide (removed during ER translocation), producing proinsulin. Proinsulin folds and forms disulfide bonds between its A and B chains. The connecting C-peptide is then excised by proteases in secretory vesicles, yielding mature insulin (two chains linked by disulfide bridges). This multi-step cleavage ensures proper folding before the active hormone is released. C-peptide levels are clinically measured to assess endogenous insulin production.

6.7 Gene Regulation: Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes

Prokaryotic Regulation: The Operon Model

In prokaryotes, genes with related functions are often grouped into operons — clusters of genes under the control of a single promoter. The lac operon in E. coli is the classic example: when lactose is absent, a repressor protein binds the operator region and blocks RNA polymerase. When lactose is present, it binds the repressor, causing it to release the operator, allowing transcription of the genes needed to metabolize lactose. This is negative regulation (removal of an inhibitor).

The lac operon also has a second layer of regulation through catabolite repression. Even when lactose is present (removing the repressor), full transcription requires the positive regulator CAP (Catabolite Activator Protein). CAP is only active when bound to cAMP, and cAMP levels are inversely related to glucose levels. When glucose is abundant, cAMP is low, CAP is inactive, and the lac operon is barely transcribed even if lactose is present — the cell prefers glucose. Only when glucose is scarce (high cAMP) does the CAP-cAMP complex bind near the promoter and facilitate strong RNAP binding, enabling maximal transcription. This ensures E. coli uses glucose before lactose. The lac operon demonstrates the remarkable speed of prokaryotic gene regulation: when induced, enzyme levels can increase approximately 1,000-fold within just 2–3 minutes.

The Trp Operon: A Repressible Operon

The tryptophan (trp) operon is the classic example of a repressible operon controlling an anabolic (biosynthetic) pathway. It contains five structural genes (trpE, D, C, B, A) encoding enzymes for tryptophan synthesis. The regulatory gene produces a repressor that is normally inactive. When tryptophan accumulates, it acts as a corepressor, binding to the repressor and activating it. The active repressor-corepressor complex then binds the operator, blocking transcription. This is a negative feedback system: the end product of the pathway shuts down the genes that make it, conserving cellular resources.

Eukaryotic Regulation

Eukaryotic gene regulation is far more complex and occurs at multiple levels:

  • Chromatin remodeling: Histone acetylation loosens chromatin (promotes transcription); methylation can silence genes.
  • Transcription factors: Activators and repressors bind enhancer and silencer regions, which can be thousands of base pairs from the promoter.
  • Post-transcriptional: Alternative splicing (one gene producing multiple protein variants by including different exon combinations — the human genome has roughly 20,000 genes but produces over 100,000 proteins partly through this mechanism), mRNA stability, miRNA-mediated degradation.
  • Translational control: Regulation of when and how efficiently mRNA is translated.
  • Post-translational: Protein modification (phosphorylation, ubiquitination), folding, and degradation.

Key Points — Chapter 6

  • The central dogma: DNA is transcribed into RNA, which is translated into protein.
  • Eukaryotic pre-mRNA is processed with a 5' cap, 3' poly-A tail, and splicing (introns removed, exons joined).
  • The genetic code is triplet, degenerate, and nearly universal; AUG is the start codon; UAA, UAG, UGA are stop codons.
  • Translation involves initiation, elongation, and termination at ribosomes; tRNA delivers amino acids; the ribosome catalyzes peptide bond formation.
  • Protein structure has four levels: primary (sequence), secondary (helices/sheets), tertiary (3D fold), quaternary (multi-subunit assembly).
  • Prokaryotes regulate genes largely through operons; eukaryotes use multi-level regulation from chromatin to post-translational modification.

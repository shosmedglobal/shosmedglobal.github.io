Chapter 11

Choosing a Journal and Navigating Submission

11.1 Journal Selection as Strategy

Choosing where to submit your manuscript is a strategic decision, not an afterthought. The right journal matches your paper's scope and quality level, reaches the audience you want, and has a timeline compatible with your needs. A poor choice wastes months in review cycles at journals that were never a realistic fit.

11.2 Factors to Evaluate

Scope and Audience

Does the journal publish work on your topic? Does its readership align with who would benefit from your findings? Read the journal's “Aims and Scope” statement on its website before investing time in formatting your manuscript for their specifications.

Impact Factor and Reputation

Higher-impact journals are more competitive but add greater weight to your CV. Be realistic: if this is your first manuscript, mid-tier specialty journals are appropriate targets. Aiming exclusively at top-tier journals and accumulating serial rejections wastes precious months.

Publication Costs

Many open-access journals charge article processing charges (APCs) ranging from zero to several thousand dollars. As a student, prioritize journals with no APC, fee waiver programs for trainees, or hybrid models where open access is optional rather than mandatory.

Review Speed

Some journals are significantly faster than others. If you need the publication before a specific deadline (like ERAS), review speed matters. Check journal review speed databases or ask colleagues about their recent experiences.

Acceptance Rate

Top journals may accept fewer than 10% of submissions. Match your manuscript's quality to realistic targets to avoid unnecessary delays.

11.3 Tools for Finding the Right Journal

  • Jane (Journal/Author Name Estimator): Free tool at jane.biosemantics.org. Paste your abstract and Jane suggests journals that have published similar content, ranked by relevance.
  • Elsevier Journal Finder: Similar concept, focused on Elsevier publications.

11.4 Understanding Journal Metrics

Metric What It Measures
Impact Factor (IF)Average citations per article over the previous two years. Higher = more prestigious, but varies dramatically by field.
CiteScoreSimilar to IF but calculated over three years and including all document types. Published by Scopus.
h-indexThe largest number h such that h articles have been cited at least h times.

Do not obsess over metrics. A relevant, reputable specialty journal is a perfectly appropriate home for solid work.

11.5 The Cover Letter

Every submission requires a cover letter. It should be concise and professional:

Sample Cover Letter Structure

Adapt this framework to each submission. A tailored letter signals professionalism.

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We respectfully submit our manuscript, “[Title],” for consideration in [Journal Name].

[One to two sentences summarizing the study design, population, and principal finding.]

[One to two sentences explaining why this journal's readership would benefit from the work and how it aligns with the journal's scope.]

This work is original and has not been published or submitted elsewhere. All listed authors satisfy the ICMJE authorship criteria and have approved the submitted version.

We declare no conflicts of interest relevant to this work.

Should it be helpful, we suggest the following potential reviewers with relevant expertise: [Two to three names with institutional affiliations and email addresses.]

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Corresponding Author], [Degree]
[Affiliation]
[Contact Email]

11.6 The Submission Process

  1. Select the journal and read the author guidelines completely.
  2. Format the manuscript to meet journal specifications (word limits, reference style, figure format).
  3. Create an account on the submission portal (typically Editorial Manager or ScholarOne).
  4. Upload files: manuscript, figures, tables, cover letter, conflict of interest forms, ICMJE forms, reporting guideline checklists.
  5. Enter metadata: author names, affiliations, abstract, keywords, suggested reviewers.
  6. Review the system-generated proof and submit.

11.7 The Post-Submission Timeline

Editorial review (1–2 weeks): The editor decides if the manuscript fits the journal. Many papers are desk-rejected at this stage without peer review.

Peer review (2–8 weeks, sometimes longer): Two to three field experts evaluate the manuscript and recommend: accept, minor revision, major revision, or reject.

Decision letter: You receive reviewer comments and an editorial decision.

Possible outcomes:

  • Accept as is: Extremely rare on first submission.
  • Minor revision: Small changes requested. Good news - respond promptly.
  • Major revision: Significant changes needed, but the journal is interested. Respond thoroughly.
  • Reject with invitation to resubmit: Substantial revisions needed, but the door is not closed.
  • Reject: Move to the next journal on your list.

11.8 Responding to Reviewer Comments

A thoughtful response letter can convert a major revision into an acceptance. Follow these principles:

  • Be respectful and professional regardless of the reviewer's tone.
  • Address every single comment with a numbered, point-by-point response.
  • Quote the specific changes you made in the manuscript, with page and line numbers.
  • If you disagree, explain your reasoning with evidence. Do not simply refuse.

11.9 Handling Rejection

Rejection is a normal part of the publication process. Even excellent manuscripts get rejected. The productive response is:

  1. Read the reviewer feedback carefully.
  2. Determine which criticisms can be addressed in a revision.
  3. Revise the manuscript incorporating the useful feedback.
  4. Submit to the next journal on your target list.
  5. Resubmit within one to two weeks of rejection to maintain momentum.

11.10 Identifying and Avoiding Predatory Journals

Predatory journals exploit the open-access model by charging fees without providing legitimate editorial services. Publishing in one damages your credibility.

Warning signs:

  • Unsolicited email invitations to submit manuscripts
  • Promises of publication within days (legitimate peer review takes weeks to months)
  • No identifiable editorial board, or board members unaware of their listing
  • Not indexed in PubMed, Scopus, or Web of Science
  • Website with grammatical errors or unprofessional design
  • Vague or suspiciously low article processing charges

Verification tools:

  • Think.Check.Submit (thinkchecksubmit.org) - a checklist for evaluating journal legitimacy
  • Beall's List - a regularly updated list of suspected predatory publishers
  • Check indexing in PubMed/MEDLINE, Scopus, or Web of Science
  • Check membership in the Directory of Open Access Journals (DOAJ)

Key Takeaways

  • Choose a journal that matches your manuscript's scope, quality, and timeline requirements
  • Use Jane to identify candidate journals based on your abstract
  • Write a professional, concise cover letter for every submission
  • Respond to reviewer comments thoroughly, respectfully, and point by point
  • Rejection is normal - revise incorporating useful feedback and resubmit promptly
  • Never publish in a predatory journal under any circumstances

Common Mistakes

  • Submitting without first reading the journal's author guidelines
  • Repeatedly targeting top-tier journals and accumulating months of avoidable delay
  • Writing a generic cover letter that does not explain why this journal is the right fit
  • Responding defensively or dismissively to reviewer criticism
  • Giving up after a single rejection instead of revising and resubmitting
  • Falling for predatory journal solicitation emails

Chapter 8

How to Conduct a Systematic Review

8.1 What Makes a Systematic Review Different

A systematic review is not simply a thorough literature review. It is a structured, reproducible synthesis of all available evidence on a specific clinical question, conducted according to a predefined protocol with explicit methods at every stage. Unlike a narrative review — where the author subjectively selects studies — a systematic review uses predetermined inclusion criteria, a comprehensive search strategy, and standardized quality assessment. This methodological rigor is what gives systematic reviews their credibility and their high position in the evidence hierarchy.

8.2 When Is a Systematic Review Appropriate?

A systematic review makes sense when:

  • Multiple studies have examined the same clinical question
  • There is uncertainty or conflicting evidence that a structured synthesis could clarify
  • A comprehensive summary would be clinically useful for practitioners or guideline developers

A systematic review is not appropriate when only one or two studies exist on the topic (there is nothing meaningful to synthesize) or when a recent, well-conducted systematic review already covers the same question comprehensively.

8.3 PROSPERO Registration

Before beginning any work, register your review protocol on PROSPERO (International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews). Registration is free and takes approximately 30 to 60 minutes. It serves three purposes:

  1. It demonstrates that your methodology was planned in advance (not adjusted to fit your findings).
  2. It prevents unnecessary duplication of effort by other researchers.
  3. Many journals now require PROSPERO registration for systematic review manuscripts.

You will receive a registration number (e.g., CRD42026XXXXXX) that you include in your manuscript.

8.4 Developing the Search Strategy

If possible, collaborate with a medical librarian. Librarians are experts in search strategy design, and many are willing to co-author the paper in recognition of their contribution.

Your search should cover at least two databases — PubMed and Embase are the standard minimum. For each database, construct a search combining:

  • MeSH terms (PubMed) or Emtree terms (Embase) for controlled vocabulary
  • Free-text keywords for terms not yet indexed
  • Boolean operators (AND, OR) to combine concept groups

Document every detail: the exact search string, the database used, the date of the search, and the number of results returned. This documentation must be reproducible — another researcher should be able to replicate your exact search.

8.5 Screening: A Two-Stage Process

Title and Abstract Screening

Two reviewers independently evaluate every title and abstract against your predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria. Use Rayyan (a free online tool) to manage this process. Rayyan allows both reviewers to screen independently, then compare decisions. Disagreements are resolved by discussion or by involving a third reviewer.

Full-Text Review

Articles that pass the initial screen are retrieved in full text. Both reviewers read each article completely and apply the inclusion criteria. Again, conflicts are resolved through discussion. At this stage, you must document the specific reason for every exclusion — this information populates your PRISMA flow diagram.

8.6 Data Extraction

Create a standardized extraction spreadsheet before beginning. Both reviewers independently extract the same information from each study:

  • Author, year, country
  • Study design
  • Population characteristics and sample size
  • Intervention or exposure details
  • Outcome measures and definitions
  • Key results (effect sizes, confidence intervals, p-values)
  • Quality indicators

Discrepancies between reviewers are resolved by discussion. Dual extraction reduces the risk of errors that could compromise your synthesis.

8.7 Quality Assessment (Risk of Bias)

Every systematic review must evaluate the methodological quality of each included study. The assessment tool you choose depends on the study designs in your review:

Study Design Quality Assessment Tool
Randomized controlled trialsCochrane Risk of Bias tool (RoB 2)
Non-randomized studies (cohort, case-control)Newcastle-Ottawa Scale
Diagnostic accuracy studiesQUADAS-2
Overall certainty of evidenceGRADE framework

8.8 Presenting Your Findings

Structure your results to include:

  • A PRISMA flow diagram documenting the screening process from identification through inclusion
  • A characteristics table summarizing all included studies
  • A narrative synthesis of findings organized by outcome
  • Forest plots if you pool data quantitatively (this crosses into meta-analysis territory, covered in Chapter 9)

8.9 PRISMA 2020 Compliance

PRISMA 2020 (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) is the current reporting standard. It consists of a 27-item checklist covering everything from title format to funding disclosure. Download the checklist, go through it item by item before submission, and confirm that each element is addressed in your manuscript. A large proportion of systematic review rejections occur because of PRISMA non-compliance — this is an entirely avoidable reason for rejection.

Key Takeaways

  • A systematic review follows strict, pre-defined methodology — it is not a casual literature survey
  • Register your protocol on PROSPERO before starting any work
  • Search at least two databases with a fully documented, reproducible strategy
  • Use two independent reviewers for screening, data extraction, and quality assessment
  • Follow the PRISMA 2020 checklist meticulously before submission
  • Typical timeline: three to six months from start to submission

Common Mistakes

  • Skipping PROSPERO registration before beginning the review
  • Searching only one database
  • Using a single reviewer for screening instead of two independent reviewers
  • Failing to document specific reasons for full-text exclusions
  • Omitting quality assessment of included studies
  • Not cross-checking the manuscript against the PRISMA checklist before submission

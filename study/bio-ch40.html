Chapter 40

Man and Environment (Ecology)

Ecology studies the relationships between organisms and their environment. Human activities have profoundly altered ecosystems worldwide — disrupting nutrient cycles, contaminating food chains, changing the atmosphere, and driving species to extinction. Understanding these impacts is essential for developing solutions.

Ecosystems and Nutrient Cycling

An ecosystem includes all the living organisms (biotic components) in an area along with the nonliving physical environment (abiotic components: water, soil, air, sunlight). Energy flows through ecosystems in one direction (from sunlight through producers to consumers to decomposers), but nutrients are recycled.

Key nutrient cycles include:

  • Carbon cycle — carbon moves between the atmosphere (as CO2), living organisms (incorporated into organic molecules through photosynthesis), and the earth (fossil fuels, limestone). Respiration and decomposition release CO2 back to the atmosphere.
  • Nitrogen cycle — atmospheric nitrogen (N2) is converted to usable forms by nitrogen-fixing bacteria. Plants absorb nitrates from soil. Animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants. Decomposers return nitrogen to the soil; denitrifying bacteria return N2 to the atmosphere.
  • Phosphorus cycle — phosphorus does not have a gaseous phase under normal conditions. It cycles from rocks (weathering releases phosphate) to soil to organisms and back. It is often a limiting nutrient for plant growth.

Agricultural Effects on Nutrient Cycles

Agriculture disrupts natural nutrient cycling in several ways:

  • Harvesting removes nutrients — unlike natural ecosystems where dead organisms decompose in place and return nutrients to the soil, crops are harvested and removed. This depletes soil nutrients over time.
  • Fertilizer application — to compensate for nutrient removal, farmers add artificial fertilizers (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium). While this supports crop growth, excess fertilizer often runs off into waterways.
  • Monoculture — growing a single crop depletes specific soil nutrients and reduces biodiversity.

Eutrophication of Lakes

When excess nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff, sewage, and detergents) enter a lake or other water body, a process called eutrophication occurs:

  1. Excess nutrients stimulate explosive growth of algae and aquatic plants (algal bloom).
  2. The dense surface algae block sunlight from reaching deeper plants, which die.
  3. When the algae eventually die, bacteria decompose the massive amount of dead organic matter.
  4. Bacterial decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen in the water.
  5. Oxygen levels plummet (hypoxia), suffocating fish and other aquatic organisms.
  6. The lake becomes a "dead zone" with low biodiversity.

Biological Magnification (Bioaccumulation)

Biological magnification (biomagnification) is the process by which certain toxic substances (such as heavy metals, DDT, PCBs, and mercury) become increasingly concentrated at higher levels of a food chain.

How it works: A toxic chemical enters the environment (e.g., a pesticide applied to crops). Organisms at the base of the food chain (like algae or small invertebrates) absorb small amounts. Because the chemical is not easily broken down or excreted, it accumulates in their tissues. When a predator eats many of these organisms, it concentrates all of their toxin loads in its own body. Top predators (eagles, large fish, humans) accumulate the highest concentrations, which can cause reproductive failure, neurological damage, or death.

Exam Tip: Biological magnification occurs because toxins are not metabolized or excreted efficiently, so they build up with each step in the food chain. The concentration increases at each trophic level, NOT because top predators are directly exposed to more toxin.

Carbon Dioxide Emissions and the Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process: certain gases in Earth's atmosphere (water vapor, CO2, methane, nitrous oxide) trap infrared radiation (heat) that would otherwise escape to space. This keeps Earth's surface warm enough to support life.

However, human activities have dramatically increased atmospheric CO2 concentrations, primarily through:

  • Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) for energy and transportation
  • Deforestation (removes trees that absorb CO2; burning wood releases CO2)
  • Industrial processes (cement production, chemical manufacturing)

The enhanced greenhouse effect leads to global warming — rising average surface temperatures — which causes climate change: altered weather patterns, melting glaciers and ice caps, rising sea levels, more extreme weather events, shifts in species ranges, and ocean acidification (CO2 dissolving in seawater lowers pH, harming coral reefs and shellfish).

Ozone Layer Depletion

The ozone layer is a region of the stratosphere (about 15–35 km altitude) with a relatively high concentration of ozone (O3). It absorbs most of the sun's harmful ultraviolet-B (UV-B) radiation, protecting living organisms from DNA damage, skin cancer, cataracts, and harm to phytoplankton.

Certain human-made chemicals, especially chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) (formerly used in refrigerants, aerosol sprays, and foam manufacturing), break down ozone molecules in the stratosphere. A single chlorine atom from a CFC molecule can destroy thousands of ozone molecules in a catalytic cycle.

The most dramatic ozone depletion occurs over Antarctica (the "ozone hole"), which was first identified in the 1980s. The Montreal Protocol (1987), an international agreement to phase out CFCs, has been highly successful, and the ozone layer is slowly recovering.

Do not confuse: The greenhouse effect (warming due to heat-trapping gases) is a separate issue from ozone depletion (thinning of the UV-blocking ozone layer). They have different causes and different consequences, even though both are atmospheric problems caused by human activity.

Biodiversity and Conservation

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life at all levels: genetic diversity within species, species diversity within an ecosystem, and ecosystem diversity across a region. Biodiversity is critical because:

  • Diverse ecosystems are more stable and resilient to disturbances.
  • Many species provide ecosystem services: pollination, water purification, soil formation, nutrient cycling.
  • Biodiversity is a source of food, medicine, and raw materials.

Current threats to biodiversity include:

  • Habitat destruction — the leading cause of species loss. Deforestation, urbanization, and conversion of land for agriculture destroy the habitats organisms need to survive.
  • Overexploitation — overhunting, overfishing, and over-harvesting deplete populations faster than they can reproduce.
  • Invasive species — non-native species introduced to new environments can outcompete, prey on, or bring disease to native species.
  • Pollution — chemical contaminants, plastic waste, oil spills, and noise/light pollution harm wildlife.
  • Climate change — shifts temperatures and weather patterns faster than many species can adapt or migrate.

Conservation strategies include establishing protected areas (national parks, marine reserves), restoring degraded habitats, captive breeding programs for endangered species, sustainable resource management, international agreements to limit pollution and overexploitation, and reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

Key Points — Man and Environment
  • Ecosystems cycle nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus) while energy flows one way.
  • Agriculture disrupts nutrient cycles by removing biomass and adding artificial fertilizers.
  • Eutrophication: excess nutrients cause algal blooms, oxygen depletion, and death of aquatic life.
  • Biomagnification: toxins become more concentrated at each trophic level of the food chain.
  • Enhanced greenhouse effect from CO2 emissions causes global warming and climate change.
  • CFCs deplete the stratospheric ozone layer; the Montreal Protocol successfully addressed this.
  • Biodiversity is threatened by habitat destruction, overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, and climate change.

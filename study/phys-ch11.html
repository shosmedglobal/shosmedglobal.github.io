Chapter 11

Vibrations, Waves, and Acoustics

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

x(t) = A · sin(ω · t) or x(t) = A · cos(ω · t)
  • A = amplitude (maximum displacement)
  • ω = 2*π*f = 2*π/T (angular frequency)
  • T = period, f = frequency = 1/T

At equilibrium: speed is maximum, acceleration is zero. At maximum displacement: speed is zero, acceleration is maximum.

Velocity and acceleration in SHM:

v_max = ω · A a_max = ω2 · A

Pendulum

T = 2 · π · √(L / g)

Period depends ONLY on L and g. Does NOT depend on mass or amplitude (small angles).

Worked Example: Length for T = 2 s?

  • 2 = 2(3.14)*√(L/10)
  • √(L/10) = 0.318 ⇒ L/10 = 0.101 ⇒ L = 1.01 m

Mass-Spring System

T = 2 · π · √(m / k)

Period depends on mass and spring constant, NOT on amplitude.

Energy in SHM

E_total = (1/2)*k*A2 = (1/2)*m*ω2*A2 KE + PE = constant throughout oscillation

Resonance

When driven at natural frequency, amplitude grows dramatically. Examples: pushing a swing, glass breaking from sound, soldiers breaking step on bridges.

Wave Equation

v = f · λ

v = wave speed, f = frequency, λ = wavelength.

Types of Waves

Transverse: Oscillation perpendicular to propagation. Examples: string waves, light, EM waves.

Longitudinal: Oscillation parallel to propagation. Examples: sound waves, pressure waves.

Wave Phenomena

Reflection: Wave bounces off boundary. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.

Refraction: Wave changes direction entering a medium where speed changes.

Interference:

  • Constructive: waves in phase (crest + crest), amplitudes add.
  • Destructive: waves out of phase (crest + trough), amplitudes subtract.
  • Path difference for constructive: n*λ (n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
  • Path difference for destructive: (n + 1/2)*λ

Interference in detail: When two coherent waves (same frequency, constant phase relationship) meet at a point, the resulting amplitude depends on the path difference Δ between the two waves. If Δ = n*λ (integer number of wavelengths), the waves arrive in phase and their amplitudes add, producing a maximum (constructive interference). If Δ = (n + 1/2)*λ, the waves arrive exactly out of phase and cancel, producing a minimum (destructive interference). For two waves of equal amplitude A, the resultant amplitude is 2A at constructive maxima and zero at destructive minima. Since intensity is proportional to amplitude squared, I_max = 4*I_single and I_min = 0. This principle applies universally to sound waves (noise-canceling headphones), water waves, light (thin films, anti-reflective coatings), and all other wave phenomena.

Diffraction: Waves spread through openings. Most noticeable when opening size is comparable to wavelength.

Polarization: Restricting transverse wave oscillation to one plane. Only transverse waves can be polarized. Sound (longitudinal) cannot be polarized.

Standing Waves

Formed by superposition of two identical waves traveling in opposite directions.

String fixed at both ends:

λ_n = 2L/n (n = 1, 2, 3, ...) f_n = n · v/(2L) (harmonics)

Pipe open at both ends: same as above.

Pipe closed at one end:

λ_n = 4L/n (n = 1, 3, 5, ... odd harmonics only) f_n = n · v/(4L)

Sound

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave. Requires a medium. Cannot travel through vacuum.

Speed of sound:

  • Air (20 C): approximately 343 m/s
  • Water: approximately 1480 m/s
  • Steel: approximately 5000 m/s
  • General: solids > liquids > gases

Sound characteristics:

  • Frequency determines pitch (high f = high pitch)
  • Amplitude determines loudness
  • Waveform/harmonics determine timbre

Sound Intensity and Level

Intensity: I = P / A (W/m2) Sound level: L = 10 · log_10(I / I_0) (in decibels, dB)

Where I_0 = 10-12 W/m2 (threshold of hearing).

Key rule: Every 10 dB increase = 10x intensity. Every 3 dB increase = 2x intensity.

Sound Pressure Level

Sound pressure is the local pressure deviation from ambient, caused by the sound wave. Sound pressure level (SPL) in dB:

SPL = 20 · log_10(p / p_0)

Where p_0 = 20 muPa (reference pressure).

Human Hearing Range

  • Frequency: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
  • Infrasound: below 20 Hz (elephants, earthquakes)
  • Ultrasound: above 20,000 Hz (bats, medical imaging, cleaning)

Doppler Effect

Source approaching: f_obs = f_source · v_sound / (v_sound - v_source) Source receding: f_obs = f_source · v_sound / (v_sound + v_source) Observer approaching: f_obs = f_source · (v_sound + v_obs) / v_sound Observer receding: f_obs = f_source · (v_sound - v_obs) / v_sound

Worked Example: Ambulance siren at 500 Hz approaching at 30 m/s (v_sound = 340 m/s):

  • f_obs = 500 · 340/(340 - 30) = 500 · 340/310 = 548 Hz (higher pitch)
Exam Tips:
- Pendulum period does NOT depend on mass.
- Spring-mass period does NOT depend on amplitude.
- Sound cannot travel through vacuum. Light can.
- Doppler effect changes observed frequency, not actual frequency emitted.
- Wave speed depends on the medium, not on frequency or amplitude.
- Polarization only works for transverse waves.

Chapter 16

Chromosome Disorders

🧠Practice this chapterTest yourself with chapter-specific questions →

While single-gene mutations affect individual genes, chromosome disorders involve changes to large segments of chromosomes or even entire chromosomes. These large-scale changes typically have severe clinical consequences because many genes are affected simultaneously.

Chromosome abnormalities are remarkably common in early pregnancy: they account for roughly 50% of all first-trimester spontaneous miscarriages, making them the leading cause of early pregnancy loss. Among liveborn infants, approximately 1 in 150 (about 0.6–1%) carries a clinically significant chromosomal abnormality, the most common being trisomy 21 (Down syndrome).

16.1 Chromosome Mutations (Structural Abnormalities)

Structural chromosome abnormalities result from breakage and incorrect rejoining of chromosome segments. They are classified as unbalanced (gain or loss of genetic material) or balanced (rearranged but no net gain or loss).

Unbalanced Structural Abnormalities

Deletion: A segment of a chromosome is lost. The consequences depend on the size of the deleted segment and which genes it contained. Example: Cri-du-chat syndrome results from a deletion on the short arm of chromosome 5 (5p-). Affected infants have a distinctive high-pitched cry, intellectual disability, and characteristic facial features.

Duplication: A segment of a chromosome is repeated, resulting in extra copies of the genes in that region. Duplications are generally less harmful than deletions because no genetic information is lost - but having extra copies can disrupt normal gene dosage.

Balanced Structural Abnormalities

Inversion: A segment of a chromosome is reversed (flipped 180 degrees). No genetic material is gained or lost, so carriers are usually phenotypically normal. However, during meiosis, inversions can lead to unbalanced gametes with deletions or duplications.

  • Paracentric inversion: Does not include the centromere.
  • Pericentric inversion: Includes the centromere.

Translocation: A segment of one chromosome is transferred to a nonhomologous chromosome.

  • Reciprocal translocation: Two nonhomologous chromosomes exchange segments. Carriers are usually phenotypically normal but may produce unbalanced gametes.
  • Robertsonian translocation: Two acrocentric chromosomes fuse at their centromeres, losing the short arms. The carrier has 45 chromosomes but is usually phenotypically normal. This is clinically important because Robertsonian translocations involving chromosome 21 can lead to familial Down syndrome.

16.2 Genome Mutations (Numerical Abnormalities)

Genome mutations involve changes in the total number of chromosomes. They usually arise from nondisjunction - the failure of chromosomes (or chromatids) to separate properly during meiosis.

Aneuploidy

An abnormal number of chromosomes that is NOT an exact multiple of the haploid number.

Trisomy (2n + 1 = 47 chromosomes): Three copies of one chromosome instead of two.

Condition Chromosome Karyotype Key Features
Down syndrome Trisomy 21 47,XX,+21 or 47,XY,+21 Intellectual disability, characteristic facial features, heart defects; risk increases with maternal age. Prenatal ultrasound may detect suggestive markers such as increased nuchal translucency, absent nasal bone, and cardiac defects; these findings are combined with maternal blood markers (PAPP-A, free beta-hCG) to calculate a risk score that may prompt confirmatory testing.
Edwards syndrome Trisomy 18 47,XX,+18 or 47,XY,+18 Severe intellectual disability, major organ malformations; most die within first year
Patau syndrome Trisomy 13 47,XX,+13 or 47,XY,+13 Severe defects including cleft lip/palate, polydactyly, brain malformations; most die within first month

Monosomy (2n − 1 = 45 chromosomes): Only one copy of a chromosome instead of two. Autosomal monosomy is almost always lethal.

Turner syndrome (45,X): The only viable monosomy in humans. Affected individuals are phenotypically female with short stature, webbed neck, shield-shaped chest, and ovarian dysgenesis (underdeveloped ovaries leading to infertility). Intelligence is typically normal.

Sex chromosome aneuploidies:

  • Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY): Phenotypically male. Tall stature, gynecomastia, small testes, reduced fertility, may have mild learning difficulties.
  • Triple X syndrome (47,XXX): Phenotypically female. Usually mild or no symptoms; may have tall stature and slightly increased risk of learning difficulties.
  • XYY syndrome (47,XYY): Phenotypically male. Tall stature, usually normal fertility and intelligence.

Polyploidy

An extra complete set of chromosomes:

  • Triploidy (3n = 69): Usually from fertilization by two sperm or failure to complete meiosis. Almost always lethal; miscarriage or death shortly after birth.
  • Tetraploidy (4n = 92): Four complete sets. Always lethal in humans. Common and normal in some plants.
MNEMONIC - Autosomal Trisomies

"Drinking age: 21, Voting age: 18, Unlucky 13"

21 = Down (most common, longest survival), 18 = Edwards, 13 = Patau (most severe, earliest death). The lower the chromosome number, the more genes are affected and the more severe the condition.

KEY POINTS - Chapter 16
  • Structural: deletions and duplications (unbalanced); inversions and translocations (balanced).
  • Robertsonian translocation fuses two acrocentric chromosomes, giving 45 chromosomes.
  • Nondisjunction during meiosis causes aneuploidy (trisomy or monosomy).
  • Down (trisomy 21), Edwards (trisomy 18), and Patau (trisomy 13) are the main autosomal trisomies.
  • Turner syndrome (45,X) is the only viable human monosomy (it involves a sex chromosome, not an autosome).
  • Polyploidy (3n or 4n) is lethal in humans.

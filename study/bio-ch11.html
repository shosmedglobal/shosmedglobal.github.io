Chapter 11

Genetics of the Prokaryotic Cell

Prokaryotes — bacteria and archaea — are structurally simpler than eukaryotic cells, yet they possess surprisingly sophisticated genetic mechanisms. Understanding how bacteria organize, regulate, and share their genetic information is critical for medicine, biotechnology, and evolutionary biology.

11.1 Bacterial Chromosome Structure

The bacterial genome is remarkably compact compared to the genomes of eukaryotes. Here are its main features:

  • Single circular chromosome: Most bacteria carry one circular DNA molecule, located in a region of the cytoplasm called the nucleoid. This is not surrounded by a membrane like a eukaryotic nucleus.
  • Supercoiling: The circular chromosome is extensively supercoiled (twisted upon itself) and associated with small proteins that help organize it. If you were to stretch out the DNA of E. coli, it would be roughly 1.5 millimeters long — about 1,000 times longer than the cell itself!
  • Relatively few noncoding sequences: Bacterial genomes are efficient. Most of the DNA encodes proteins. There are very few introns (unlike eukaryotes, where huge portions of DNA do not code for protein).
  • Plasmids: In addition to the main chromosome, bacteria often carry small, independently replicating circular DNA molecules called plasmids. These carry extra genes that can provide survival advantages, such as antibiotic resistance.
Nucleoid: The region within a prokaryotic cell where the chromosome is concentrated. It is not bounded by a membrane.

11.2 Gene Transfer in Bacteria

Bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission, creating clones of themselves. However, they can still gain genetic diversity through three mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer — the movement of genetic material between organisms that is not from parent to offspring.

A. Transformation

Transformation occurs when a bacterium picks up free DNA fragments from its environment. When bacteria die and lyse (burst open), their DNA is released into the surrounding medium. A nearby living bacterium that is competent (able to take up external DNA) can absorb these fragments and incorporate them into its own chromosome through recombination.

This was first demonstrated in 1928 by Frederick Griffith, who showed that a harmless strain of Streptococcus pneumoniae could become deadly after exposure to heat-killed virulent bacteria. The living cells had taken up DNA from the dead ones and transformed into a pathogenic form.

B. Transduction

Transduction is the transfer of bacterial DNA from one cell to another via a bacteriophage (a virus that infects bacteria). During viral replication inside a host bacterium, a piece of the bacterial chromosome may accidentally be packaged into a new virus particle. When this defective phage infects a new bacterium, it injects the previous host's DNA rather than its own viral genes. The recipient bacterium can then incorporate this DNA into its own chromosome.

  • Generalized transduction: Any gene from the donor bacterium may be transferred, because fragments of the host chromosome are randomly packaged.
  • Specialized transduction: Only specific genes near the site where the phage DNA was integrated are transferred. This occurs with lysogenic (temperate) phages that excise imprecisely.

C. Conjugation

Conjugation is direct cell-to-cell transfer of DNA through a physical bridge called a pilus (plural: pili). One bacterium (the donor, or F⁺ cell) extends a pilus to connect with a recipient cell (F− cell). A copy of the donor's plasmid (or sometimes chromosomal DNA) passes through this bridge to the recipient.

Conjugation requires direct physical contact and is the most efficient form of horizontal gene transfer. It can even occur between different species of bacteria, which is one reason antibiotic resistance can spread so rapidly.

11.3 F Plasmids and R Plasmids

F Plasmid (Fertility Plasmid)

The F plasmid contains genes that code for the formation of the sex pilus and the machinery needed for conjugation. Cells carrying the F plasmid are designated F⁺ (donor); those without are F− (recipient).

When the F plasmid integrates into the bacterial chromosome, the cell becomes an Hfr cell (High frequency of recombination). During conjugation, an Hfr cell can transfer large portions of its chromosome to the recipient, allowing transfer of chromosomal genes — not just the plasmid.

R Plasmid (Resistance Plasmid)

R plasmids carry genes conferring antibiotic resistance. A single R plasmid can carry resistance to multiple antibiotics simultaneously. Because R plasmids can be transferred by conjugation, antibiotic resistance spreads rapidly through bacterial populations — a major concern in modern medicine.

Clinical Significance

The spread of R plasmids between bacteria through conjugation is a primary driver of the global antibiotic resistance crisis. Multidrug-resistant bacteria (sometimes called "superbugs") often carry R plasmids with genes for resistance to several antibiotics at once.

11.4 Transposons (Jumping Genes)

Transposons are segments of DNA that can move from one location to another within a genome — or even jump between different DNA molecules (for example, from a plasmid to a chromosome). They were first described by Barbara McClintock in maize (corn) in the 1940s and 1950s.

Key features of transposons:

  • They encode an enzyme called transposase, which catalyzes their excision and reinsertion.
  • Insertion sequences (IS elements) are the simplest transposons: they contain only a transposase gene flanked by short inverted repeat sequences.
  • Complex transposons carry additional genes (such as antibiotic resistance genes) between the inverted repeats.
  • Transposons can disrupt genes when they insert into the middle of a coding sequence, potentially causing mutations.
  • They can also carry genes (like drug resistance) from plasmids to chromosomes and vice versa.

11.5 Operons: Gene Regulation in Prokaryotes

Bacteria need to respond rapidly to changes in their environment. If a nutrient suddenly becomes available, the bacterium should switch on the genes needed to use that nutrient. If the nutrient disappears, those genes should be turned off to conserve energy. This is accomplished through operons.

Operon: A cluster of functionally related genes that are transcribed together as a single mRNA (polycistronic mRNA) under the control of a single promoter. The operon includes: a promoter (where RNA polymerase binds), an operator (a regulatory switch), and the structural genes (the genes being regulated).

The Lac Operon (Inducible System)

The lac operon in E. coli controls the genes needed to metabolize lactose (milk sugar). It is an inducible operon — the genes are normally OFF and are turned ON only when lactose is present.

Components of the lac operon:

  • lacZ, lacY, lacA: Three structural genes that encode enzymes for lactose metabolism. lacZ produces beta-galactosidase (breaks lactose into glucose and galactose), lacY encodes a permease (transports lactose into the cell), and lacA encodes a transacetylase.
  • Operator (lacO): A short DNA sequence between the promoter and the structural genes where the repressor binds.
  • Promoter (lacP): Where RNA polymerase binds to begin transcription.
  • Regulatory gene (lacI): Located nearby, this gene encodes the lac repressor protein.

How it works:

  1. No lactose present (operon OFF): The lac repressor protein (produced constitutively by lacI) binds to the operator. This physically blocks RNA polymerase from moving past the promoter to transcribe the structural genes. The enzymes are not made.
  2. Lactose present (operon ON): Allolactose (a derivative of lactose) acts as an inducer. It binds to the repressor protein and changes its shape so it can no longer attach to the operator. RNA polymerase is free to transcribe the structural genes, and the enzymes are produced.
  3. Positive regulation by CAP: Even when lactose is present, the lac operon is transcribed at high levels only when glucose is absent. When glucose is low, cyclic AMP (cAMP) levels rise. cAMP binds to the Catabolite Activator Protein (CAP), and this CAP-cAMP complex binds upstream of the promoter, enhancing RNA polymerase binding and boosting transcription. This ensures that bacteria use glucose first (the preferred energy source) before turning to lactose.

The Trp Operon (Repressible System)

The trp operon controls genes for the synthesis of the amino acid tryptophan. It is a repressible operon — the genes are normally ON and are turned OFF only when tryptophan is abundant.

  • Tryptophan absent (operon ON): The repressor protein (produced by the trpR gene) is in an inactive form and cannot bind to the operator. RNA polymerase transcribes the structural genes, and the cell synthesizes tryptophan.
  • Tryptophan abundant (operon OFF): Tryptophan acts as a corepressor. It binds to the inactive repressor, activating it. The active repressor-corepressor complex then binds to the operator, blocking transcription. The cell stops making tryptophan because it already has enough.

11.6 Negative and Positive Gene Regulation

Negative regulation means a regulatory protein (repressor) inhibits transcription by binding to the operator. Both the lac and trp operons use negative regulation.

Positive regulation means a regulatory protein (activator) enhances transcription by helping RNA polymerase bind to the promoter. In the lac operon, the CAP-cAMP complex serves as a positive regulator.

Many genes are controlled by a combination of both negative and positive regulators, allowing fine-tuned responses to multiple environmental signals simultaneously.

KEY POINTS — Chapter 11
  • Bacteria have a single circular chromosome in the nucleoid plus optional plasmids.
  • Horizontal gene transfer occurs via transformation, transduction, and conjugation.
  • F plasmids enable conjugation; R plasmids spread antibiotic resistance.
  • Transposons are mobile DNA elements that can move within and between DNA molecules.
  • The lac operon is inducible (OFF by default, turned ON by lactose).
  • The trp operon is repressible (ON by default, turned OFF by tryptophan).
  • Negative regulation uses repressors; positive regulation uses activators like CAP.

Chapter 4

Energy

4.1 What Is Energy?

Energy is the capacity to do work or transfer heat. It cannot be created or destroyed — only converted from one form to another. This is the Law of Conservation of Energy (also the First Law of Thermodynamics).

4.2 Kinetic and Potential Energy

Kinetic energy (KE) is the energy of motion. A moving car, a flowing river, vibrating molecules — all possess kinetic energy. The formula is KE = ½mv².

Potential energy (PE) is stored energy due to position or arrangement. A book on a shelf has gravitational potential energy. Chemical bonds store chemical potential energy — when bonds break and reform during a reaction, this energy may be released or absorbed.

4.3 Heat and Temperature

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. Heat (q) is the transfer of energy between objects at different temperatures. Heat always flows from hotter to cooler objects.

4.4 Specific Heat Capacity

Different substances require different amounts of heat to raise their temperature. The specific heat capacity (c) is the energy needed to raise one gram of a substance by one degree Celsius.

q = m × c × ΔT

where q = heat (in joules), m = mass (in grams), c = specific heat (J/g·°C), and ΔT = change in temperature.

Water has an unusually high specific heat (4.18 J/g·°C), which is why large bodies of water moderate climate.

Heat Capacity vs. Specific Heat Capacity

Heat capacity (C) is the total amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of an entire object by 1 °C, measured in J/°C. It is related to specific heat by C = m × c, where m is the object's mass. For example, a 350 g aluminium pan with a specific heat of 0.897 J/(g·°C) has a heat capacity of 350 × 0.897 = 314 J/°C. This means 314 J raises the whole pan by one degree, regardless of how you distribute the heat.

Calorie-Joule Conversion

The SI unit of energy is the joule (J). The calorie (cal), still common in nutrition and older biochemistry texts, equals 4.184 J. When solving specific heat problems, make sure all energy values share the same unit. For instance, if a problem gives energy in calories and specific heat in J/(g·°C), convert calories to joules first by multiplying by 4.184.

Worked Example: Heating Water

Problem: How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 200 g of water from 20 °C to 80 °C?
Step 1: Identify values: m = 200 g, c = 4.18 J/g·°C, ΔT = 80 − 20 = 60 °C
Step 2: q = 200 × 4.18 × 60 = 50,160 J = 50.16 kJ
Answer: 50.16 kJ of heat energy is required.

4.5 Phase Changes

At the melting point, a solid becomes a liquid. At the boiling point, a liquid becomes a gas. During a phase change, temperature remains constant even though heat is being added (or removed). The added energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces rather than increasing kinetic energy.

4.6 Exothermic vs Endothermic Reactions

FeatureExothermicEndothermic
Energy flowReleases energy to surroundingsAbsorbs energy from surroundings
Sign of ΔHNegative (−)Positive (+)
Temperature of surroundingsIncreases (feels warm)Decreases (feels cold)
ExamplesCombustion, neutralizationMelting ice, photosynthesis

4.7 Enthalpy and Hess's Law

Enthalpy (H) is the total heat content of a system at constant pressure. We measure changes in enthalpy (ΔH) during reactions. Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the number of intermediate steps. This means we can add individual reaction enthalpies to find the overall ΔH for a process — extremely useful when a reaction is hard to measure directly.

Key Points — Chapter 4

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed (Law of Conservation of Energy).
  • Kinetic energy = energy of motion; potential energy = stored energy.
  • Heat (q) = m × c × ΔT. Water's specific heat is 4.18 J/g·°C.
  • Temperature stays constant during a phase change.
  • Exothermic reactions release energy (ΔH < 0); endothermic reactions absorb energy (ΔH > 0).
  • Hess's Law: total ΔH is independent of the pathway taken.

Mnemonic: Exo vs Endo

"EXit" — EXothermic means energy EXits the system. "ENter" — ENdothermic means energy ENters the system.

Chapter 39

Evolution of Humans

Humans did not appear suddenly on Earth. Our species, Homo sapiens, is the product of millions of years of evolution, sharing common ancestors with other primates. Understanding this history reveals how traits like upright walking, large brains, and language gradually emerged.

Timeline of Life on Earth

Earth formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago. The first single-celled life appeared about 3.5–3.8 billion years ago. Multicellular organisms arose roughly 600 million years ago.

The Paleozoic era (541–252 MYA) saw the colonization of land by plants, fungi, and animals. The Cambrian period (~541 MYA) witnessed an explosive diversification of animal body plans (the "Cambrian explosion"). The Mesozoic era (252–66 MYA) was dominated by dinosaurs but also saw the first mammals (~200 MYA) and the first flowering plants. A mass extinction event at 66 MYA — likely caused by an asteroid impact — eliminated the dinosaurs and opened ecological niches that mammals rapidly filled during the Cenozoic era. The primate lineage diverged approximately 65 million years ago.

Primate Evolution

Humans belong to the order Primates, which also includes lemurs, monkeys, and apes. Key primate traits include:

  • Forward-facing eyes providing stereoscopic (3D) vision — critical for judging distances in trees
  • Grasping hands with opposable thumbs
  • Large brains relative to body size
  • Extended parental care and complex social behavior
  • Nails instead of claws

An important early anthropoid is Aegyptopithecus, which lived approximately 29–35 million years ago in what is now Egypt's Fayum region. About the size of a small cat, it had forward-facing eyes, a relatively large brain for its body size, and features intermediate between prosimians and later anthropoids. It is considered close to the common ancestor of Old World monkeys and apes, providing key fossil evidence for the early diversification of the lineage that eventually led to humans.

Within primates, humans are most closely related to the great apes (chimpanzees, bonobos, gorillas, orangutans). Molecular evidence shows that humans and chimpanzees share approximately 98–99% of their DNA and diverged from a common ancestor about 6–7 million years ago. Humans did NOT evolve from modern chimpanzees; rather, both species descended from a shared ancestor that no longer exists.

Hominid Species Timeline

The term "hominid" (or hominin, more precisely) refers to species on the human branch of the evolutionary tree after the split from our common ancestor with chimpanzees. Here are key species:

Australopithecus (~4–2 million years ago)

Found in Africa. The famous "Lucy" fossil belongs to Australopithecus afarensis (~3.2 million years old). These hominins were bipedal (walked upright) but had small brains (~400–500 cm3, similar to chimpanzees). They retained some tree-climbing adaptations. Australopithecus is considered a likely ancestor or close relative of the genus Homo.

Homo habilis (~2.4–1.4 million years ago)

"Handy man." Brain size was larger (~600–700 cm3). Associated with the earliest known stone tools (Oldowan tools) — simple choppers and flakes used for cutting and scraping. Found in East Africa.

Homo ergaster (~1.9–1.4 million years ago)

Sometimes considered the African form of Homo erectus. Taller and more slender than earlier hominins with a brain size of about 700–900 cm3. Body proportions similar to modern humans, adapted for long-distance walking and running in open savanna environments. Associated with more sophisticated Acheulean stone tools (hand axes).

Homo erectus (~1.9 million–100,000 years ago)

"Upright man." The first hominin species to migrate out of Africa, spreading to Asia and Europe. Brain size ranged from about 900–1100 cm3. Evidence suggests they used fire and developed more advanced tools. Homo erectus survived for nearly 2 million years, making it one of the most successful hominin species.

Homo neanderthalensis (~400,000–40,000 years ago)

Neanderthals lived in Europe and western Asia. They had large brains (averaging ~1400 cm3, comparable to or larger than modern humans), stocky bodies adapted to cold climates, and sophisticated stone tools (Mousterian technology). They buried their dead, cared for the injured, and may have had some form of language. DNA evidence shows limited interbreeding with Homo sapiens; modern non-African humans carry about 1–4% Neanderthal DNA.

Denisovans (~400,000–50,000 years ago)

The Denisovans are an extinct group of archaic humans known primarily from DNA extracted from fossil fragments found in Denisova Cave, Siberia. Genomic analysis reveals they were a sister group to Neanderthals, having diverged from the lineage leading to modern humans approximately 400,000–500,000 years ago. Like Neanderthals, Denisovans interbred with Homo sapiens; modern Melanesian and Australian Aboriginal populations carry approximately 3–6% Denisovan DNA. Some Denisovan genetic contributions appear adaptive — for instance, the EPAS1 gene variant that helps Tibetan populations thrive at high altitudes.

Homo sapiens (~300,000 years ago–present)

"Wise man." Modern humans. Originated in Africa. Distinguished by a high, rounded skull, prominent chin, smaller brow ridges, and an average brain size of about 1400 cm3. Homo sapiens developed complex language, art, symbolic thought, and advanced technology, enabling cultural evolution that far outpaced biological evolution.

Key Evolutionary Traits

  • Bipedalism — walking on two legs was one of the earliest major changes, appearing in Australopithecus. It freed the hands for tool use and carrying food, improved energy efficiency for long-distance travel, and allowed better visibility over tall grass.
  • Brain size — increased dramatically from ~400 cm3 (Australopithecus) to ~1400 cm3 (Homo sapiens). Larger brains enabled tool making, problem solving, social cooperation, and language.
  • Tool use — progressed from simple stone flakes (Homo habilis) to complex blades, needles, and projectile weapons (Homo sapiens).
  • Language — complex, symbolic language is a uniquely human trait that enabled the transmission of knowledge across generations (cultural evolution).
  • Jaw and dental changes — throughout human evolution, the jaw became progressively shorter and the face flatter. Early hominins had large, projecting jaws with powerful chewing muscles. As diet changed — particularly with the adoption of cooking — jaw size decreased, the chin became more prominent (a uniquely Homo sapiens feature), and teeth became smaller. Third molars (wisdom teeth) became vestigial, often causing impaction in modern humans due to reduced jaw space.
  • Reduced sexual dimorphism — the physical difference between males and females decreased markedly. In gorillas, males are roughly twice the weight of females, reflecting intense male-male competition. In humans, males are only about 15–20% heavier than females. This reduction is associated with shifts toward pair-bonding, cooperative parenting, and reduced physical male-male competition, supporting extended parental investment and the growth of larger, more complex brains.

Cultural Evolution

Once Homo sapiens developed language and symbolic thought, cultural evolution became the primary driver of human change — far faster than biological evolution. Key milestones include: development of agriculture (~10,000 years ago, enabling permanent settlements and population growth), writing (~5,000 years ago, allowing cumulative knowledge preservation), the scientific revolution, and the industrial revolution. Cultural evolution allows humans to adapt to new environments within a single generation by passing knowledge socially rather than genetically.

Key Points — Evolution of Humans
  • Humans are primates, most closely related to chimpanzees (common ancestor ~6–7 million years ago).
  • Australopithecus: bipedal, small brain. Homo habilis: first tools. Homo erectus: first to leave Africa, used fire.
  • Neanderthals: large brains, cold-adapted, limited interbreeding with Homo sapiens.
  • Homo sapiens: originated in Africa ~300,000 years ago, with complex language and symbolic thought.
  • Key evolutionary trends: bipedalism (earliest), increasing brain size, tool sophistication, language.
  • The "Out of Africa" model is best supported by genetic and fossil evidence.
  • Cultural evolution (language, agriculture, writing) now drives human adaptation far faster than genetic change.

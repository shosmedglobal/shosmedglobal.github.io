Chapter 5

Literature Search Mastery

5.1 Why Basic Searching Is Not Enough

Most students approach PubMed by typing a few keywords into the search bar. For casual reading, this works. For research, it is inadequate. A rigorous literature search is the foundation of every credible study - it determines whether you find the right papers, identify the true state of the evidence, and avoid duplicating work that already exists. Mastering advanced search techniques is a skill that pays dividends across every project you undertake.

5.2 PubMed: Advanced Techniques

MeSH Terms

Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) are standardized indexing terms used by PubMed to categorize articles. When you search for “heart attack,” you may miss articles that use the term “myocardial infarction” instead. But the MeSH term Myocardial Infarction captures all indexed articles on that topic regardless of the specific words the authors chose. Access the MeSH database through PubMed's dedicated MeSH search page to find the correct controlled terms for your topic.

Boolean Operators

Three operators control how search terms interact:

  • AND narrows your search - both terms must appear in the result.
  • OR broadens your search - either term qualifies a result.
  • NOT excludes a term from your results.

Example: ("diffuse large B-cell lymphoma" OR "DLBCL") AND ("polatuzumab" OR "pola-R-CHP") AND "survival"

Filters

PubMed allows you to restrict results by publication date, article type (clinical trial, review, case report), language, species (human versus animal), and more. Use these to efficiently narrow down large result sets.

Field Tags

You can target specific parts of a citation using field tags: [ti] for title only, [tiab] for title and abstract, [au] for author, [jour] for journal name. For example: polatuzumab[ti] AND lymphoma[ti] finds only articles where both terms appear in the title.

Building a Comprehensive Search

The best approach combines MeSH terms with free-text keywords. Use OR to group synonyms within each concept, then connect concept groups with AND. This dual strategy catches both articles that have been formally indexed with MeSH terms and newer articles that have not yet been indexed.

5.3 Google Scholar

Google Scholar casts a wider net than PubMed, searching books, conference proceedings, theses, preprints, and more. It is particularly useful for:

  • Citation tracking - the “Cited by” feature lets you find every paper that has referenced a specific article, which is invaluable for building a literature map.
  • Finding gray literature - sources not formally indexed in PubMed.
  • Locating free PDFs - Google Scholar often links directly to free full-text versions.

Limitations: The indexing process is less transparent, non-peer-reviewed content is included, and search precision is lower than PubMed.

5.4 Cochrane Library

The Cochrane Library is the premier source for high-quality systematic reviews and meta-analyses. Before starting any systematic review, check Cochrane first. If a recent Cochrane review already covers your topic, you will need to find a different angle or a more focused question.

5.5 Embase

Embase overlaps substantially with PubMed but includes additional European and pharmacology-focused journals. For systematic reviews, current guidelines recommend searching both PubMed and Embase to ensure comprehensive coverage. Embase typically requires institutional access.

5.6 Building a Search Strategy Step by Step

  1. Define your PICO question clearly.
  2. Identify the two to four key concepts embedded in the question.
  3. For each concept, list all synonyms, alternative spellings, abbreviations, and corresponding MeSH terms.
  4. Combine synonyms within each concept using OR.
  5. Combine the concept groups using AND.
  6. Run the search and review the first 20 to 30 results for relevance.
  7. Refine: if results are too numerous, add more specific terms; if too few, broaden your synonyms.
  8. Document everything - database searched, exact search string, date, and number of results. This documentation is mandatory for systematic reviews and best practice for all research.

5.7 Reference Management

Over the course of your research career, you will accumulate hundreds of references. Managing them manually guarantees errors and wasted time. Set up a reference manager from day one.

Zotero (Recommended)

Zotero is free, open-source, and integrates with both Word and Google Docs. Install three components: the desktop application, the browser connector extension (which saves articles with a single click), and the word processor plugin (which inserts formatted citations as you write). When you encounter a relevant paper, click the Zotero icon in your browser toolbar to save it with complete metadata automatically.

Mendeley

Mendeley is another free option with a built-in PDF reader and annotation tool. It is owned by Elsevier and functions similarly to Zotero, though it has a smaller user community.

Organizing Your Library

Create separate folders for each project. Tag articles with relevant keywords. Read and annotate PDFs within the tool itself. When writing, use the word processor plugin to insert citations and auto-generate your bibliography in whatever format the journal requires.

5.8 Systematic Approaches to Reading

As your reading volume grows, you need a system for tracking what you have read and what you learned. Several approaches work well:

  • Annotated bibliography: A spreadsheet where each row contains a paper's citation, a two-to-three sentence summary of its key findings, and a note on how it relates to your question. Invaluable when writing your introduction and discussion.
  • Zotero notes: Attach notes directly to each reference. When you later need to cite a paper, your notes remind you exactly why it was relevant.
  • Tagging system: In Zotero or Mendeley, tag papers by subtopic (e.g., “efficacy,” “safety,” “mechanism,” “epidemiology”). This makes retrieving all papers on a specific aspect of your topic fast and reliable.
  • Critical appraisal notes: As you gain experience, get into the habit of noting each paper's strengths and weaknesses - sample size adequacy, potential biases, appropriateness of statistical methods. This skill is essential for writing discussion sections and conducting systematic reviews.

5.9 The PRISMA Flow Diagram

For systematic reviews, a PRISMA flow diagram is required. This diagram visually tracks how many records you identified, screened, assessed for eligibility, and ultimately included. The PRISMA 2020 statement provides a standardized template with specific boxes for each stage:

  1. Identification: Total records from each database plus any additional records from reference lists or citation searching. Report duplicates removed.
  2. Screening: Records screened at the title/abstract level and the number excluded (with reasons).
  3. Eligibility: Full-text articles assessed and the number excluded (with specific reasons for each exclusion).
  4. Included: Final count of studies in the qualitative synthesis, and if applicable, the quantitative synthesis (meta-analysis).

Free tools for creating the diagram include draw.io (diagrams.net), the editable template on the PRISMA website, or simple construction in PowerPoint or Google Slides.

Key Takeaways

  • Master PubMed's advanced features: MeSH terms, Boolean operators, field tags, and filters
  • Use Google Scholar for citation tracking and broader discovery beyond indexed journals
  • Set up Zotero for reference management from day one - do not wait
  • Document every search you run: database, search string, date, and result count
  • A rigorous, reproducible search strategy is the backbone of every credible study

Common Mistakes

  • Relying solely on basic keyword searches without incorporating MeSH terms
  • Failing to document search strategies as you go
  • Managing references manually in a Word document or spreadsheet instead of using dedicated software
  • Searching only one database when conducting a systematic review
  • Not checking for existing systematic reviews or meta-analyses before starting your own

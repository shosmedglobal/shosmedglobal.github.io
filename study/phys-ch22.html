De Broglie Waves

Matter Wave Hypothesis

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that ALL matter has wave-like properties. The wavelength of a particle is:

λ = h / p = h / (m · v)

Where:

  • λ = de Broglie wavelength (in meters)
  • h = Planck's constant = 6.626 · 10-34 J·s
  • p = momentum of the particle (in kg·m/s)
  • m = mass of the particle
  • v = velocity of the particle

Wave-Particle Duality

De Broglie's idea completed a beautiful symmetry in physics:

EntityWave NatureParticle Nature
LightElectromagnetic wave (interference, diffraction)Photon (photoelectric effect, Compton)
ElectronDe Broglie wave (electron diffraction)Particle (mass, charge, trajectory)

All matter exhibits both wave and particle properties. However, the wave nature is only noticeable for very small particles because h is so tiny (10-34).

Davisson-Germer Experiment

In 1927, Davisson and Germer fired electrons at a nickel crystal and observed a diffraction pattern -- exactly what would be expected if the electrons were behaving as waves with de Broglie's predicted wavelength. This was the first experimental confirmation of matter waves.

Why We Do Not See Wave Behavior in Daily Life

For a 1 kg ball moving at 1 m/s: λ = 6.626 · 10-34 / (1 · 1) = 6.6 · 10-34 m. This wavelength is astronomically smaller than any atom (10-10 m), so no wave effects can be observed.

For an electron (m = 9.1 · 10-31 kg) at typical speeds, the wavelength is on the order of angstroms (10-10 m), comparable to atomic spacing, making diffraction observable.

Application: Electron Microscope

The electron microscope exploits the short de Broglie wavelength of fast electrons. Since resolving power is limited by wavelength, and electron wavelengths can be much shorter than visible light wavelengths (~500 nm), electron microscopes can resolve much finer detail (down to ~0.1 nm for TEM).

The faster the electrons (higher voltage), the shorter their wavelength and the better the resolution.

Worked Example

Problem: An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of 100 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

Solution:

The electron gains kinetic energy: KE = e · V = 1.602 · 10-19 · 100 = 1.602 · 10-17 J

KE = (1/2) · m · v2, so v = √(2 · KE / m)

v = √((2 · 1.602 · 10-17) / (9.109 · 10-31))

v = √(3.517 · 1013)

v = 5.93 · 106 m/s

λ = h / (m · v) = 6.626 · 10-34 / (9.109 · 10-31 · 5.93 · 106)

λ = 6.626 · 10-34 / (5.402 · 10-24)

λ = 1.227 · 10-10 m = 0.123 nm

This is comparable to interatomic distances, which is why electron diffraction works.

Shortcut: For electrons accelerated through voltage V: λ (in nm) = 1.226 / √(V). Check: 1.226/√(100) = 1.226/10 = 0.123 nm. Matches.

Exam Tips:
- λ = h/(mv). Larger mass or higher speed means shorter wavelength.
- For electrons: λ(nm) = 1.226/√(V) where V is accelerating voltage in volts.
- Davisson-Germer experiment confirmed matter waves using electron diffraction on nickel.
- Electron microscopes use short electron wavelengths to achieve better resolution than optical microscopes.
- Wave behavior is only detectable for particles with very small mass (electrons, neutrons, atoms).

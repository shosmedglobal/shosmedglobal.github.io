Adiabatic Processes

What Is an Adiabatic Process?

An adiabatic process is a thermodynamic process in which no heat is exchanged between the system and its surroundings. Mathematically: Q = 0.

This does NOT mean the temperature stays constant -- quite the opposite. When a gas is compressed adiabatically, it heats up. When it expands adiabatically, it cools down. All the energy change comes from work done on or by the gas.

The Adiabatic Equation

For an ideal gas undergoing an adiabatic process:

p · V^γ = constant

Equivalently:

  • T · V^(γ - 1) = constant
  • T^γ · p^(1 - γ) = constant

Where γ (the adiabatic index or heat capacity ratio) is:

γ = Cp / Cv

Gas TypeγExample
Monatomic5/3 = 1.67He, Ne, Ar
Diatomic7/5 = 1.40N2, O2, air
Polyatomic~1.3CO2, H2O vapor

Adiabatic vs Isothermal on a pV Diagram

Both isothermal and adiabatic curves look like hyperbolas on a pV diagram, but the adiabatic curve is steeper because γ > 1. This makes physical sense: in an adiabatic compression, pressure rises faster because the gas also heats up (adding thermal pressure on top of the volume decrease).

  • Isothermal: pV = constant (slope proportional to 1)
  • Adiabatic: pV^γ = constant (slope proportional to γ, which is > 1)

Where Adiabatic Processes Occur

  • Rapid compression/expansion: When a process happens so fast that heat cannot escape (e.g., diesel engine compression ignites fuel)
  • Sound waves: Air compressions and rarefactions happen so quickly they are nearly adiabatic
  • Atmospheric convection: Rising air expands adiabatically and cools, which is why mountains are cold at the top
  • Insulated containers: Perfectly insulated systems undergo adiabatic changes by definition

First Law for Adiabatic Process

Since Q = 0, the first law gives:

ΔU = -W

(or equivalently, ΔU = Won, where Won is work done on the gas)

If the gas expands (does positive work), its internal energy decreases and it cools. If compressed (work done on gas), internal energy increases and it heats up.

Worked Example

Problem: Air (γ = 1.4) at initial pressure 100 kPa and volume 5 L is compressed adiabatically to a volume of 1 L. What is the final pressure?

Solution:

Using p1 · V1^γ = p2 · V2^γ:

100 · 51.4 = p2 · 11.4

We need 51.4. Using logarithms: ln(51.4) = 1.4 · ln(5) = 1.4 · 1.609 = 2.253, so 51.4 = e2.253 = 9.52.

(On an exam, you can estimate: 51 = 5 and 52 = 25, so 51.4 should be between 5 and 25, closer to 10. The exact value 9.52 fits.)

So: 100 · 9.52 = p2 · 1

p2 = 952 kPa

Check: The volume decreased by a factor of 5, and the pressure increased by a factor of about 9.5. In an isothermal compression by 5x, pressure would only increase 5x. The extra increase (9.5 vs 5) is because the gas also heated up.

Exam Tips:
- Adiabatic means Q = 0, NOT ΔT = 0 (that is isothermal).
- The adiabatic curve on a pV diagram is STEEPER than the isothermal curve.
- γ is always > 1 for any real gas.
- For adiabatic compression: temperature RISES. For adiabatic expansion: temperature FALLS.

Tissues

A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function. The human body has four fundamental tissue types: epithelial, connective, muscle, and nervous. Every organ is built from combinations of these four types.

Histological Staining: H&E

The hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stain is the most widely used method for examining tissues under the microscope. Hematoxylin binds to nucleic acids and stains cell nuclei blue-purple, while eosin stains cytoplasmic proteins and extracellular matrix in shades of pink, red, and orange. This combination provides excellent visualization of tissue architecture and is the standard stain used in histology courses and pathology laboratories.

Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial tissue (epithelium) covers body surfaces, lines internal cavities and organs, and forms glands. It acts as a barrier, protecting underlying tissues from physical damage, pathogens, and fluid loss. Epithelial cells sit on a thin non-cellular layer called the basement membrane.

Classification by Layers

  • Simple epithelium: A single layer of cells. Found where diffusion, absorption, or secretion occur (thin = efficient transport).
  • Stratified epithelium: Multiple layers stacked on top of each other. Found where protection from abrasion is needed (thick = durable).
  • Pseudostratified epithelium: Appears to have multiple layers because cell nuclei sit at different heights, but all cells touch the basement membrane. Found lining the respiratory tract.

Classification by Cell Shape

  • Squamous: Flat, scale-like cells (think: "squashed"). Ideal for diffusion and filtration.
  • Cuboidal: Cube-shaped cells. Specialized for secretion and absorption.
  • Columnar: Tall, column-shaped cells. Often have microvilli (for absorption) or cilia (for moving substances along the surface).
  • Transitional: Cells that can stretch and change shape. Found exclusively in the urinary system (bladder, ureters).

Gland Secretion Mechanisms

Exocrine glands are classified by how they release their products:

  • Merocrine (eccrine): Secretion is released by exocytosis without any loss of cell material. This is the most common mechanism (e.g., most sweat glands, salivary glands, pancreas).
  • Apocrine: The apical portion of the cell pinches off, carrying the secretory product with it. The cell then repairs itself. Example: lipid secretion by mammary glands.
  • Holocrine: The entire cell ruptures and disintegrates to release its contents; new cells continuously replace those lost. Example: sebaceous (oil) glands of the skin.

Connective Tissue

Connective tissue is the most diverse and widespread tissue type. It supports, connects, and separates other tissues. Unlike epithelium, connective tissue cells are spread apart within an extracellular matrix - a combination of protein fibers and ground substance.

Components of Connective Tissue

  • Cells: Fibroblasts (produce fibers and ground substance), macrophages (immune defense), mast cells (release histamine), adipocytes (store fat).
  • Fibers: Collagen fibers (strong, resist stretching), elastic fibers (allow tissue to snap back), reticular fibers (form delicate networks).
  • Ground substance: A gel-like material between cells and fibers, composed of water, glycoproteins, and proteoglycans.

Types of Connective Tissue

  • Loose (areolar) connective tissue: Soft, flexible packing material found beneath the skin and around organs. Contains all three fiber types loosely arranged.
  • Dense (fibrous) connective tissue: Tightly packed collagen fibers. Dense regular has parallel fibers (tendons, ligaments). Dense irregular has fibers in many directions (dermis of skin, organ capsules). A special form of dense regular connective tissue is the aponeurosis - a flat, broad, sheet-like tendon that attaches muscles to bones or to other muscles over a wide area, distributing force more evenly than a cord-like tendon (examples include the abdominal aponeuroses and the epicranial aponeurosis of the scalp).
  • Adipose tissue: Specialized for fat storage. Provides insulation, energy reserve, and cushioning around organs. Each cell is filled with a single large lipid droplet.
  • Cartilage: Firm but flexible tissue. Cells called chondrocytes sit in small spaces called lacunae within the matrix. Three types: hyaline (smooth, glassy; covers joint surfaces, trachea rings), elastic (external ear, epiglottis), and fibrocartilage (tough, shock-absorbing; intervertebral discs, knee menisci). Cartilage is avascular (no blood supply), which is why it heals slowly.
  • Bone (osseous tissue): The hardest connective tissue. Its matrix is mineralized with calcium phosphate crystals (hydroxyapatite), making it rigid. Cells called osteocytes reside in lacunae. Detailed bone structure is covered in Chapter 23.
  • Blood: A unique connective tissue with a liquid matrix called plasma. The "cells" are red blood cells (erythrocytes), white blood cells (leukocytes), and cell fragments called platelets (thrombocytes). Blood is covered in detail in Chapter 36.

Connective tissue also harbors plasma cells - terminally differentiated B lymphocytes that function as antibody-secreting factories. They are particularly abundant in connective tissue underlying mucosal surfaces and near sites of chronic infection, where they produce large quantities of immunoglobulins to neutralize specific pathogens.

Muscle Tissue

Muscle tissue is specialized for contraction - the ability to shorten and generate force. There are three types:

  • Skeletal (striated voluntary) muscle: Attached to bones. Cells are very long, cylindrical, and multinucleated (multiple nuclei per cell because they form from the fusion of many precursor cells). They show visible striations (alternating light and dark bands) under the microscope. Controlled voluntarily (you consciously decide to move).
  • Cardiac muscle: Found only in the heart wall. Cells are branched, striated, and have a single central nucleus. They are connected to each other by special junctions called intercalated discs, which allow electrical signals to pass rapidly between cells so the heart contracts as a coordinated unit. Involuntary.
  • Smooth muscle: Found in the walls of hollow organs (stomach, intestines, blood vessels, uterus, bladder). Cells are spindle-shaped (tapered at both ends), have a single central nucleus, and lack striations. Involuntary. Controls movements like peristalsis (pushing food through the digestive tract) and blood vessel diameter.

Skeletal Muscle Fiber Types

Skeletal muscle is not uniform - it contains a mix of fiber types adapted for different tasks:

  • Type I (slow-twitch) fibers: Rich in myoglobin and mitochondria, relying on aerobic metabolism. They resist fatigue and are suited for endurance activities (e.g., maintaining posture, long-distance running).
  • Type IIA (fast-twitch oxidative) fibers: An intermediate type combining moderate endurance with greater power output.
  • Type IIB (fast-twitch glycolytic) fibers: Rely primarily on anaerobic glycolysis, fatigue rapidly, but produce maximum force for short, powerful bursts (e.g., sprinting, heavy lifting).

The proportion of each fiber type varies between muscles and between individuals, contributing to differences in athletic performance.

Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is specialized for communication. It detects stimuli, processes information, and transmits signals throughout the body.

Neurons

The functional units of the nervous system. Each neuron has three parts:

  • Cell body (soma): Contains the nucleus and most organelles.
  • Dendrites: Branching extensions that receive incoming signals from other neurons or from sensory receptors.
  • Axon: A single long extension that carries the electrical signal (action potential) away from the cell body toward other neurons, muscles, or glands. Axons can be very long (up to 1 meter in leg nerves).

Glial Cells (Neuroglia)

Supporting cells that are approximately equal in number to neurons in the human brain. Key types:

  • Astrocytes: Star-shaped cells in the brain that support neurons, regulate the blood-brain barrier, and help control the chemical environment.
  • Oligodendrocytes: Form the myelin sheath around axons in the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord), speeding up signal transmission.
  • Schwann cells: Form the myelin sheath around axons in the peripheral nervous system.
  • Microglia: Immune cells of the central nervous system. They remove debris and defend against pathogens.

Mnemonic: Four Tissue Types - "Every Mother's Nurse"

Epithelial • Muscle • (connective is the) Mother of all tissues • Nervous

Or simply remember: CEMN = Connective, Epithelial, Muscle, Nervous - the four fundamental tissue types.

Key Points - Chapter 22

  • Four fundamental tissue types: epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous.
  • Epithelium is classified by layers (simple vs. stratified) and cell shape (squamous, cuboidal, columnar, transitional).
  • Connective tissue has cells spread within an extracellular matrix of fibers and ground substance. Includes loose CT, dense CT, cartilage, bone, blood, and adipose.
  • Three muscle types: skeletal (striated, voluntary, multinucleated), cardiac (striated, involuntary, intercalated discs), smooth (non-striated, involuntary).
  • Nervous tissue consists of neurons (signal transmission) and glia (support). Neurons have a cell body, dendrites, and an axon.
  • Cartilage is avascular and heals slowly. Bone is mineralized and rigid. Blood is a liquid connective tissue.

Chapter 36

Blood

Blood is the body's primary transport medium. This specialized connective tissue circulates through the cardiovascular system, delivering oxygen and nutrients, removing wastes, distributing hormones, and defending against infection. An average adult has about 5 liters of blood.

Functions of Blood

  • Transport - oxygen (from lungs to tissues), carbon dioxide (from tissues to lungs), nutrients (from gut to cells), hormones (from glands to targets), waste products (to kidneys and liver)
  • Defense - white blood cells fight infections; antibodies neutralize pathogens; clotting prevents blood loss
  • Regulation - maintains body temperature (distributing heat), pH (buffering systems), and fluid balance

Composition of Blood

If you place blood in a tube and centrifuge it (spin it at high speed), it separates into layers:

  • Plasma (top, ~55%) - the liquid portion. A straw-colored fluid that is about 90% water. Contains dissolved proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), electrolytes, nutrients, hormones, and waste products.
  • Buffy coat (thin middle layer, <1%) - white blood cells and platelets.
  • Red blood cells (bottom, ~45%) - the densest components.

The hematocrit is the percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells (normally about 42–47% for women, 45–52% for men).

Normal plasma protein concentration is 60–80 g/L. Albumin (~42 g/L, accounting for 55–60% of total plasma protein) maintains colloid osmotic pressure and transports hydrophobic substances. Globulins include alpha and beta globulins (transport proteins, complement factors) and gamma globulins (immunoglobulins/antibodies). Fibrinogen (~2–4 g/L) is the clotting precursor converted to insoluble fibrin during coagulation.

Serum is plasma minus clotting factors (particularly fibrinogen). When blood clots and the clot is removed, the remaining fluid is serum.

Cellular Elements of Blood

Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells, RBCs)

By far the most abundant cells in blood (~5 million per microliter). They are small, biconcave discs - this shape maximizes surface area for gas exchange. In mammals, mature RBCs lack a nucleus and most organelles, creating more room for hemoglobin.

Hemoglobin is the iron-containing protein that binds oxygen in the lungs and releases it in the tissues. Each hemoglobin molecule has four subunits, each containing one heme group with an iron atom. One hemoglobin can carry four oxygen molecules.

Adult hemoglobin (HbA) consists of two alpha and two beta globin chains. During fetal development, fetal hemoglobin (HbF) contains two alpha and two gamma chains instead, giving it a higher oxygen affinity than HbA. This allows the fetus to extract oxygen from maternal blood across the placenta. HbF is gradually replaced by HbA during the first six months after birth.

Sickle cell hemoglobin (HbS) results from a point mutation that replaces glutamic acid with valine at position 6 of the beta-globin chain. Under low-oxygen conditions, HbS polymerizes, distorting red blood cells into a rigid sickle shape that blocks capillaries, causing painful crises, organ damage, and hemolytic anemia. Heterozygous carriers (sickle cell trait, HbA/HbS) have partial resistance to Plasmodium falciparum malaria.

Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)

The ESR measures how quickly red blood cells settle to the bottom of a vertical tube in one hour. In inflammatory conditions, increased fibrinogen and immunoglobulins cause RBCs to aggregate into stacks called rouleaux, which sediment faster. An elevated ESR is a nonspecific marker of inflammation, infection, autoimmune disease, or malignancy.

Leucocytes (White Blood Cells, WBCs)

Far less numerous than RBCs (~5,000–10,000 per microliter). They are nucleated cells that defend the body against infection and disease. There are five main types (discussed in detail in Chapter 37):

  • Neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils (granulocytes)
  • Lymphocytes, monocytes (agranulocytes)

Thrombocytes (Platelets)

Not true cells - they are small fragments of large bone marrow cells called megakaryocytes. They lack nuclei. Platelets are essential for blood clotting: they adhere to damaged blood vessel walls and aggregate to form a temporary plug, then release chemicals that activate the clotting cascade.

Hematopoiesis

All blood cells originate from hematopoietic stem cells in the red bone marrow (found in flat bones like the pelvis, sternum, and ribs, and in the ends of long bones). These multipotent stem cells can differentiate into any type of blood cell.

Erythropoietin (EPO) is a hormone produced mainly by the kidneys in response to low blood oxygen levels (hypoxia). EPO stimulates the bone marrow to increase red blood cell production. This is a negative feedback system: more RBCs improve oxygen delivery, which reduces EPO production.

Other hematopoietic growth factors include thrombopoietin (mainly from the liver, drives platelet production), granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) (stimulates neutrophil production), and granulocyte-macrophage CSF (GM-CSF) (stimulates production of granulocytes and monocytes). These factors are used clinically to treat low blood cell counts after chemotherapy or bone marrow transplantation.

Blood Clotting (Hemostasis)

When a blood vessel is damaged, the body responds in three stages:

  1. Vascular spasm - the damaged vessel constricts to reduce blood flow.
  2. Platelet plug formation - platelets adhere to exposed collagen fibers in the vessel wall, become activated, and recruit more platelets to form a temporary plug.
  3. Coagulation cascade - a series of clotting factors (proteins in plasma) activate each other in a chain reaction, ultimately converting fibrinogen (soluble protein) into fibrin (insoluble protein threads). Fibrin threads weave through the platelet plug, creating a stable, reinforced clot.

Intrinsic and Extrinsic Coagulation Pathways

The coagulation cascade has two initiating pathways that converge on a common pathway. The extrinsic pathway is triggered by tissue factor (released from damaged tissue) activating Factor VII - it is fast. The intrinsic pathway begins when Factor XII is activated by contact with exposed collagen - it is slower. Both pathways converge at Factor X, which together with Factor V, calcium ions, and phospholipid forms prothrombinase. This enzyme converts prothrombin to thrombin, which then converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin.

Anticoagulants

Clotting can be prevented by several anticoagulant mechanisms:

  • Sodium citrate and EDTA chelate calcium ions, which are essential cofactors in the coagulation cascade, and are used to prevent clotting in blood collection tubes.
  • Heparin activates antithrombin III, which inhibits thrombin and Factor Xa.
  • Hirudin (originally from leeches) directly inhibits thrombin.
  • Coumarins (e.g., warfarin) inhibit vitamin K-dependent synthesis of Factors II, VII, IX, and X in the liver.
Clinical Connection - Hemophilia: Hemophilia is a genetic bleeding disorder caused by deficiency of specific clotting factors. Hemophilia A (most common) results from deficiency of Factor VIII; hemophilia B from deficiency of Factor IX. Both are X-linked recessive conditions, meaning they predominantly affect males. Patients experience prolonged bleeding, easy bruising, and dangerous internal bleeding, especially into joints. Treatment involves replacement of the missing clotting factor.
Key Points - Blood
  • Blood = plasma (55%, liquid) + cellular elements (45%, mostly RBCs).
  • RBCs are biconcave, anucleate (in mammals), and filled with hemoglobin for oxygen transport.
  • WBCs are nucleated cells of the immune system. Platelets are cell fragments for clotting.
  • All blood cells originate from stem cells in red bone marrow (hematopoiesis).
  • Erythropoietin (EPO) from the kidneys stimulates RBC production when oxygen is low.
  • Clotting: vascular spasm, platelet plug, coagulation cascade (fibrin mesh).
  • Hemophilia = X-linked recessive clotting factor deficiency.

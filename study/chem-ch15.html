Chapter 15

Reaction Kinetics

15.1 Rate of Reaction

Reaction kinetics studies how fast reactions occur (in contrast to equilibrium, which tells us how far). The rate of reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time.

Rate = −Δ[reactant] / Δt = +Δ[product] / Δt

15.2 Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  • Concentration: Higher concentration means more frequent collisions between reactant particles, increasing the rate.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature increases particle speed and collision energy. As a rough rule, a 10 °C increase roughly doubles the rate.
  • Surface area: Finely divided solids (powders) react faster than large chunks because more surface is exposed to collisions.
  • Nature of reactants: Ionic reactions in solution tend to be fast; reactions requiring bond breaking are slower.
  • Catalyst: A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy, increasing the rate without being consumed.

15.3 The Law of Mass Action (Guldberg-Waage Law)

For a general reaction aA + bB → products, the rate law has the form:

Rate = k[A]m[B]n

where k is the rate constant, and m and n are the reaction orders with respect to each reactant. The orders must be determined experimentally — they are not necessarily equal to the stoichiometric coefficients.

15.4 Activation Energy

Activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy that colliding molecules must possess for a reaction to occur. Even if molecules collide, the reaction only happens if the collision has enough energy and the correct orientation. The higher the activation energy, the slower the reaction at a given temperature.

15.5 Catalysts

A catalyst speeds up a reaction by lowering the activation energy. It participates in intermediate steps but is regenerated at the end — it is not consumed. Catalysts do not change the equilibrium position or the overall ΔH; they only affect the rate.

Enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins that catalyze specific biochemical reactions with extraordinary efficiency and selectivity. They work by binding substrates at an active site, stabilizing the transition state, and lowering Ea.

15.6 Relationship Between Kinetics and Equilibrium

For a reversible reaction at equilibrium, the forward rate equals the reverse rate. The equilibrium constant K is related to the ratio of the forward and reverse rate constants:

K = kforward / kreverse

A large K means the forward reaction is much faster than the reverse, favouring product formation.

Key Points — Chapter 15

  • Reaction rate measures concentration change per unit time.
  • Rate increases with higher concentration, temperature, and surface area.
  • Rate law: Rate = k[A]m[B]n — orders are determined experimentally.
  • Activation energy (Ea) is the energy barrier that must be overcome for a reaction to proceed.
  • Catalysts lower Ea without being consumed; enzymes are biological catalysts.
  • At equilibrium: K = kforward / kreverse.

Mnemonic: Factors Affecting Rate

"CCTSN" — Concentration, Catalyst, Temperature, Surface area, Nature of reactants. Think of it as "Can Cats Think So Nimbly?" to remember the five factors.

Test Yourself — Chapter 15

1. Why does grinding a solid reactant into powder increase the reaction rate?
Show Answer
Grinding increases the total surface area of the solid. More surface area means more particles are exposed and available for collisions with the other reactant, leading to more collisions per second and a faster rate.
2. A reaction has the rate law: Rate = k[A]²[B]. If [A] is doubled while [B] stays the same, by what factor does the rate change?
Show Answer
The rate changes by a factor of 2² = 4. Since rate is proportional to [A]², doubling [A] quadruples the rate.
3. How does an enzyme lower the activation energy of a reaction?
Show Answer
An enzyme binds the substrate at its active site, positioning the reactant molecules in an optimal orientation and stabilizing the transition state. This effectively creates an alternative reaction pathway with a lower energy barrier, allowing the reaction to proceed at body temperature.

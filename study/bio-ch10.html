Chapter 10

Biological Evolution

10.1 The Geological Time Scale

Earth's history is divided into a hierarchical series of time units: eons (the largest) → eras → periods → epochs. The major eons are the Hadean (4.5–4.0 bya), Archean (4.0–2.5 bya), Proterozoic (2.5–0.54 bya), and Phanerozoic (540 mya–present). The Phanerozoic eon, which covers the time since complex multicellular life diversified, is divided into three eras: Paleozoic, Mesozoic, and Cenozoic. Mass extinction events mark the boundaries between several of these divisions.

10.2 Macroevolution vs Microevolution

  • Microevolution: Changes in allele frequencies within a population over a relatively short time. This includes changes driven by mutation, natural selection, genetic drift, and gene flow.
  • Macroevolution: Large-scale evolutionary changes observed over long time periods, such as the origin of new species, the evolution of major new body plans, mass extinctions, and adaptive radiations.

Macroevolution is ultimately driven by microevolutionary processes accumulating over vast stretches of time, though punctuated events (mass extinctions, sudden environmental changes) can also play decisive roles.

10.3 Historical Perspectives on Evolution

Jean-Baptiste Lamarck (1744–1829)

Lamarck proposed that organisms evolve by inheritance of acquired characteristics: traits developed during an organism's lifetime would be passed to its offspring. His famous example was the giraffe: by stretching to reach high leaves, a giraffe's neck would grow longer, and this longer neck would be inherited. We now know this is incorrect — changes to an organism's body during its life are not passed to offspring through DNA. However, Lamarck deserves credit for being one of the first to propose that species change over time, rather than being fixed and immutable.

Charles Darwin (1809–1882)

Darwin proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection, published in his landmark work "On the Origin of Species" (1859). His reasoning:

  1. Individuals within a population show variation in their traits.
  2. Some of this variation is heritable.
  3. More offspring are produced than can survive (the struggle for existence).
  4. Individuals with traits better suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce (differential reproductive success, or "survival of the fittest").
  5. Over many generations, favorable traits accumulate in the population (descent with modification).

Darwin independently conceived these ideas alongside Alfred Russel Wallace, though Darwin's contribution is more widely known because of his extensive evidence and publication.

Hugo de Vries (1848–1935)

De Vries, working with evening primrose plants, proposed the mutation theory: evolution occurs through sudden, large-scale genetic changes (mutations) rather than through the gradual accumulation of small variations as Darwin suggested. While de Vries overestimated the role of large mutations (some of his "mutations" were actually chromosomal rearrangements), his work correctly identified mutation as a source of new genetic variation, which is essential for evolution.

10.4 Mechanisms of Evolution

Mutation

Mutations are random changes in DNA sequence. They are the ultimate source of all new genetic variation. Most mutations are neutral or harmful, but occasionally a mutation produces a beneficial trait that improves fitness. Mutations can be point mutations (substitutions, insertions, deletions), chromosomal mutations (duplications, inversions, translocations), or changes in chromosome number.

Genetic Recombination

Crossing over during meiosis creates new combinations of alleles on the same chromosome. Independent assortment produces new combinations of alleles on different chromosomes. Recombination does not create new alleles, but it creates new combinations that natural selection can act upon.

Gene Duplication

When a gene is accidentally duplicated (through unequal crossing over or whole-genome duplication), one copy can continue its original function while the other is free to accumulate mutations and potentially evolve a new function. Gene duplication is considered a major mechanism for the evolution of new genes and gene families. For example, the globin gene family (hemoglobin subunits) evolved through successive gene duplications and divergence from an ancestral globin gene.

Horizontal Gene Transfer

As discussed in Chapter 2, bacteria can transfer genes directly between cells (transformation, transduction, conjugation). This allows useful genes (such as antibiotic resistance) to spread rapidly through populations and even between species. Horizontal gene transfer complicates phylogenetic reconstruction and is a major driver of bacterial evolution.

10.5 Natural Selection and Artificial Selection

Natural selection is the differential survival and reproduction of individuals based on heritable variation. It can take several forms:

  • Directional selection: Favors one extreme of a trait distribution. Example: a population of moths becoming darker over time in a polluted environment.
  • Stabilizing selection: Favors the average value of a trait, selecting against extremes. Example: human birth weight (very low or very high birth weight is associated with higher mortality).
  • Disruptive selection: Favors both extremes over the average. This can lead to speciation.

Artificial selection is the intentional selection by humans of organisms with desired traits for breeding. Dogs, crop plants, and livestock have all been dramatically shaped by artificial selection. Darwin used artificial selection as an analogy to explain how natural selection works.

10.6 Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is the random change in allele frequencies that occurs in all populations, but has the greatest impact in small populations. Two special cases:

  • Bottleneck effect: A drastic reduction in population size (due to a disaster, for example) randomly eliminates alleles, reducing genetic diversity. The surviving population may not be genetically representative of the original population.
  • Founder effect: A small group of individuals colonizes a new area, carrying only a fraction of the original population's genetic variation. Allele frequencies in the new population may differ significantly from the source population. This explains why some rare genetic disorders are more common in certain isolated populations.

10.7 Species Concept and Speciation

The biological species concept defines a species as a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring, and that are reproductively isolated from other groups. This definition works well for most sexually reproducing organisms but is problematic for asexual organisms, fossils, and species that can hybridize.

Speciation is the formation of new species. The two main modes are:

  • Allopatric speciation: A population is split by a geographic barrier (a river, mountain range, or island colonization). Separated populations evolve independently, accumulating genetic differences until they can no longer interbreed if they come back into contact.
  • Sympatric speciation: New species arise within the same geographic area, without physical isolation. This can occur through polyploidy (especially in plants, where chromosome doubling during meiosis creates a new species that is reproductively isolated from its parent), habitat differentiation, or behavioral isolation.

10.8 Gene Pools

A gene pool is the total collection of all alleles of all genes in a population. Evolution can be defined as a change in the allele frequencies of a gene pool over time. The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium describes a theoretical non-evolving population — one in which allele frequencies remain constant from generation to generation. Five conditions must be met for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: (1) no mutation, (2) no gene flow (migration), (3) infinite population size (no genetic drift), (4) random mating, and (5) no natural selection. Since real populations never meet all five conditions, evolution is always occurring to some degree.

10.9 Phylogeny and Taxonomy

Phylogeny is the evolutionary history of a group of organisms, often represented as a branching tree diagram (phylogenetic tree or cladogram). Taxonomy is the science of classifying organisms. The modern hierarchical classification system (developed originally by Carl Linnaeus) groups organisms into increasingly inclusive categories: Species, Genus, Family, Order, Class, Phylum, Kingdom, Domain.

Modern taxonomy relies heavily on molecular data (DNA and protein sequences) to determine evolutionary relationships, supplementing traditional approaches based on morphology (physical form) and anatomy.

10.10 Radiometric Dating

Radiometric dating allows scientists to determine the absolute age of rocks and fossils by measuring the decay of radioactive isotopes. Each radioactive isotope decays at a constant rate, characterized by its half-life (the time it takes for half of the radioactive atoms to decay into daughter atoms). By measuring the ratio of parent to daughter isotopes in a sample, the age can be calculated. Common methods include:

  • Carbon-14 dating: Useful for organic material up to about 50,000 years old. ¹⁴C has a half-life of approximately 5,730 years.
  • Potassium-Argon dating: Useful for volcanic rocks millions to billions of years old. ⁴⁰K has a half-life of about 1.25 billion years.
  • Uranium-Lead dating: One of the most reliable methods for very old rocks (billions of years).

Note on taxonomy updates: The domestic dog is currently classified as Canis lupus familiaris (a subspecies of the gray wolf) rather than the older Canis familiaris. Bread mould (Rhizopus) was historically placed in the now-defunct phylum Zygomycota; molecular phylogenetics has reclassified it into Mucoromycota. The bacterial phylum formerly called Proteobacteria has been formally renamed Pseudomonadota following updated ICNP nomenclature. These changes illustrate that taxonomy is a living science, continuously refined as molecular data improve our understanding of evolutionary relationships.

10.12 Alternation of Generations in Plants

Plants exhibit a distinctive reproductive pattern called alternation of generations, in which two morphologically different multicellular phases alternate within the life cycle. Understanding this pattern is essential because it explains why meiosis in plants occurs at a different point in the life cycle than in animals.

The sporophyte generation is diploid (2n). Through meiosis, the sporophyte produces haploid spores. Each spore can develop (by mitosis) into the gametophyte generation, which is haploid (n). The gametophyte produces gametes (eggs and sperm) by mitosis (not meiosis, since it is already haploid). When an egg and sperm fuse at fertilization, the resulting diploid zygote grows by mitosis into a new sporophyte, completing the cycle.

A key contrast with animals: in animals, meiosis directly produces gametes. In plants, meiosis produces spores, and gametes arise from the gametophyte generation via mitosis. This distinction is a common source of confusion on entrance exams.

The relative prominence of each generation varies across plant groups:

  • In mosses (bryophytes), the gametophyte is the dominant, visible plant (the green leafy structure), while the sporophyte is small and dependent on the gametophyte for nutrition.
  • In ferns, the sporophyte is the large, familiar fern plant, while the gametophyte (called a prothallus) is a small, free-living, heart-shaped structure only a few millimeters across.
  • In seed plants (gymnosperms and angiosperms), the sporophyte is overwhelmingly dominant — it is the tree, bush, or herb you see. The gametophyte generation has become extremely reduced. In flowering plants, the male gametophyte is the pollen grain (typically 2–3 cells), and the female gametophyte is the embryo sac within the ovule (7 cells, including the egg cell). Pollination delivers pollen to the pistil, where it germinates and grows a pollen tube to reach the ovule for fertilization. The resulting seed contains the embryo (the next sporophyte generation), a nutrient supply (endosperm), and a protective seed coat.

Key Points — Chapter 10

  • Microevolution is change in allele frequencies within a population; macroevolution involves large-scale changes like speciation and extinction.
  • Darwin proposed natural selection: heritable variation + differential reproduction = adaptation over generations.
  • Mutation is the ultimate source of genetic variation; recombination and gene duplication create new combinations and functions.
  • Natural selection can be directional, stabilizing, or disruptive; artificial selection is human-directed.
  • Genetic drift (bottleneck effect, founder effect) causes random allele frequency changes, especially in small populations.
  • Speciation can be allopatric (geographic isolation) or sympatric (within same area, often via polyploidy).
  • Radiometric dating uses radioactive decay to determine absolute ages; the Hardy-Weinberg model describes a theoretical non-evolving population.
  • The classification hierarchy runs: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species.
  • Plants exhibit alternation of generations: a diploid sporophyte produces spores (via meiosis), which grow into a haploid gametophyte that produces gametes (via mitosis). In seed plants, the gametophyte is reduced to pollen grains and embryo sacs.

Chapter 16

Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

16.1 Oxidation Numbers

An oxidation number (oxidation state) is the hypothetical charge an atom would have if all bonds were completely ionic. Rules for assigning oxidation numbers:

  1. Free elements have oxidation number 0 (e.g., Na, O₂, Fe).
  2. Monoatomic ions have oxidation number equal to their charge (Na⁺ = +1, Cl⁻ = −1).
  3. Hydrogen is +1 in most compounds (−1 in metal hydrides like NaH).
  4. Oxygen is −2 in most compounds (−1 in peroxides like H₂O₂).
  5. Fluorine is always −1.
  6. The sum of all oxidation numbers in a neutral compound is 0.
  7. The sum of all oxidation numbers in a polyatomic ion equals the ion's charge.

16.2 Oxidation and Reduction

ProcessDefinitionOxidation Number ChangeElectrons
OxidationLoss of electronsIncreasesElectrons are lost
ReductionGain of electronsDecreasesElectrons are gained

An oxidizing agent causes oxidation in another species (and is itself reduced). A reducing agent causes reduction in another species (and is itself oxidized).

Mnemonic: OIL RIG / LEO GER

"OIL RIG" - Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

"LEO the lion says GER" - Loss of Electrons = Oxidation; Gain of Electrons = Reduction.

16.3 Balancing Redox Equations (Half-Reaction Method)

The half-reaction method breaks the overall reaction into two separate processes: one for oxidation and one for reduction. Here is the procedure for aqueous (acidic) solution:

  1. Split the unbalanced equation into two half-reactions.
  2. Balance all atoms except O and H.
  3. Balance O by adding H₂O.
  4. Balance H by adding H⁺.
  5. Balance charge by adding electrons (e⁻) to the more positive side.
  6. Make the electron count equal in both half-reactions (multiply if necessary).
  7. Add the two half-reactions and cancel species that appear on both sides.

Worked Example: Balancing a Redox Reaction

Problem: Balance the following reaction in acidic solution:

Fe2+ + MnO₄⁻ → Fe3+ + Mn2+

Step 1: Split into two half-reactions

Oxidation:Fe2+ → Fe3+
Reduction:MnO₄⁻ → Mn2+

Step 2: Balance the Fe half-reaction

Fe2+ → Fe3+ + e⁻

Fe goes from +2 to +3, so it loses 1 electron.

Step 3: Balance the Mn half-reaction (4 sub-steps)

a) Start: MnO₄⁻ → Mn2+

b) Balance O with water: MnO₄⁻ → Mn2+ + 4H₂O

c) Balance H with H⁺: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ → Mn2+ + 4H₂O

d) Balance charge with electrons:

Left side charge: (−1) + (+8) = +7

Right side charge: +2

Difference: 7 − 2 = 5, so add 5e⁻ to the left:

MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn2+ + 4H₂O

Step 4: Equalize electrons

The Fe half-reaction transfers 1 electron. The Mn half-reaction transfers 5 electrons.

Multiply the Fe half-reaction by 5:

5Fe2+ → 5Fe3+ + 5e⁻

Step 5: Add the two half-reactions and cancel electrons

5Fe2+ + MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → 5Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 4H₂O + 5e⁻

Final balanced equation:
5Fe2+ + MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ → 5Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 4H₂O

16.4 Reduction Potentials and the Electrochemical Series

Each half-reaction has a standard reduction potential (E°) measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE, assigned E° = 0.00 V). Species with more positive E° are stronger oxidizing agents (more easily reduced). Species with more negative E° are stronger reducing agents (more easily oxidized).

16.5 Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

A galvanic cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy using a spontaneous redox reaction. It consists of two half-cells connected by a salt bridge:

  • Anode (−): Oxidation occurs here. The more easily oxidized metal (more negative E°).
  • Cathode (+): Reduction occurs here. The more easily reduced species (more positive E°).
  • Salt bridge: Allows ion flow to maintain electrical neutrality.
  • Electrons flow through the external circuit from anode to cathode.

The cell voltage (electromotive force, EMF) is calculated as:

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode

A positive E°cell means the reaction is spontaneous.

Worked Example: Cell Voltage

Problem: Calculate the standard cell voltage for a Zn/Cu galvanic cell.
Step 1: Identify: Zn is oxidized (anode, E° = −0.76 V); Cu2+ is reduced (cathode, E° = +0.34 V).
Step 2: E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = (+0.34) − (−0.76) = +1.10 V
Answer: E°cell = +1.10 V (spontaneous).

Mnemonic: Anode and Cathode

"An Ox" and "Red Cat" - Anode = Oxidation; Reduction = Cathode. Also: Alphabetical order helps - A (anode) comes before C (cathode), O (oxidation) comes before R (reduction).

Key Points - Chapter 16

  • Oxidation = loss of electrons (oxidation number increases); reduction = gain of electrons (oxidation number decreases).
  • OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.
  • The half-reaction method: split, balance atoms, balance O (H₂O), balance H (H⁺), balance charge (e⁻), equalize electrons, combine.
  • More positive E° = stronger oxidizing agent; more negative E° = stronger reducing agent.
  • Galvanic cell: anode (oxidation, −) and cathode (reduction, +).
  • E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode. Positive E°cell = spontaneous reaction.

Show Answer

2. In the reaction Zn + Cu2+ → Zn2+ + Cu, identify what is oxidized, what is reduced, the oxidizing agent, and the reducing agent.
Show Answer
Zn is oxidized (0 → +2, loses electrons). Cu2+ is reduced (+2 → 0, gains electrons). The oxidizing agent is Cu2+ (it causes Zn to be oxidized). The reducing agent is Zn (it causes Cu2+ to be reduced).
3. Can silver (Ag) spontaneously reduce Fe2+ ions to Fe? (Use the table of reduction potentials.)
Show Answer
No. For Ag to reduce Fe2+ to Fe, Ag would need to be oxidized (Ag → Ag⁺ + e⁻, E° = −0.80 V since we reverse the reduction). The cathode would be Fe2+ + 2e⁻ → Fe (E° = −0.44 V). E°cell = (−0.44) − (+0.80) = −1.24 V. The negative value means the reaction is not spontaneous.

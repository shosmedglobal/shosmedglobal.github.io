Chapter 12

Genetics of the Eukaryotic Cell: Mendelian Genetics

Genetics is the branch of biology devoted to the study of heredity (how traits are transmitted from parents to offspring) and variability (the differences in traits among individuals within a population). Together, heredity and variability supply the raw material upon which evolution acts.

The science of genetics began in a monastery garden in the 1860s, where an Augustinian friar named Gregor Mendel performed systematic breeding experiments with pea plants. His discoveries laid the foundation for our entire understanding of heredity.

12.1 Gregor Mendel and His Experiments

Mendel chose the garden pea (Pisum sativum) for several practical reasons:

  • Pea plants have many clearly distinguishable traits (flower color, seed shape, plant height, etc.).
  • They can self-fertilize (the flower pollinates itself), making it easy to produce pure-breeding lines.
  • They can also be cross-pollinated manually by transferring pollen from one plant to another.
  • They have a short generation time and produce many offspring.

To understand how Mendel performed his crosses, it helps to know the anatomy of a pea flower. The female reproductive structure is the carpel, which contains ovules that develop into female gametes (eggs). The male structures are the stamens, whose anthers produce pollen grains containing male gametes. Mendel manually transferred pollen from the anthers of one plant to the carpel of another to create controlled crosses, preventing self-fertilization by removing the anthers before they matured.

Mendel studied seven different characters (observable features), each with two contrasting traits (variations of that character): seed color (yellow vs. green), seed shape (round vs. wrinkled), flower color (purple vs. white), flower position (axial vs. terminal), pod shape (inflated vs. constricted), pod color (green vs. yellow), and stem height (tall vs. dwarf). All seven happened to be located on different chromosomes (or far enough apart on the same chromosome) that they assorted independently — a fortunate coincidence that made Mendel's results clean and interpretable.

12.2 Key Terminology

Character: An observable feature of an organism (e.g., flower color, seed shape).

Trait: A specific variation of a character (e.g., purple flowers vs. white flowers).

Hybridization: The crossing of two organisms that differ in one or more heritable characters.

P generation: The parental generation (the original true-breeding parents).

F1 generation: The first filial generation (offspring of the P cross).

F2 generation: The second filial generation (offspring from crossing F1 individuals).

Genetic variability: The capacity of organisms to differ from one another in their heritable characteristics. It arises from mutations, recombination during meiosis, and random fertilization, and it is the essential raw material that enables populations to adapt to changing environments through natural selection.

12.3 Monohybrid Crosses and the Law of Uniformity

A monohybrid cross follows a single character. Mendel crossed true-breeding purple-flowered plants (PP) with true-breeding white-flowered plants (pp).

Result: All F1 offspring were purple. This became the Law of Uniformity: when two true-breeding parents differing in one character are crossed, all F1 offspring are uniform and resemble one parent. The trait that appears in F1 is called dominant; the one that disappears is recessive.

When Mendel allowed the F1 plants to self-fertilize, the F2 generation showed a 3:1 ratio (approximately three purple to one white). The recessive trait reappeared!

12.4 The Law of Segregation

Mendel's first law states: Each organism carries two alleles for each gene, and these alleles separate (segregate) during gamete formation so that each gamete carries only one allele.

In modern terms: during meiosis, homologous chromosomes (and therefore the alleles they carry) are separated into different gametes. At fertilization, two gametes fuse, restoring the pair of alleles.

12.5 The Law of Independent Assortment

Mendel's second law states: Genes for different characters assort independently of one another during gamete formation.

This means that the inheritance of one character (e.g., seed color) does not influence the inheritance of another character (e.g., seed shape), provided the genes are on different chromosomes.

Mendel demonstrated this with dihybrid crosses (tracking two characters simultaneously).

12.6 Dihybrid Crosses

Consider a cross between a plant that is homozygous dominant for both seed color (YY) and seed shape (RR) with one that is homozygous recessive for both (yyrr). The F1 generation is all YyRr (yellow, round seeds).

When two F1 individuals (YyRr) are crossed, each parent can produce four types of gametes: YR, Yr, yR, and yr. Using a 4x4 Punnett square, the predicted F2 phenotypic ratio is:

  • 9 Yellow, Round
  • 3 Yellow, Wrinkled
  • 3 Green, Round
  • 1 Green, Wrinkled

This 9:3:3:1 ratio is the hallmark of a dihybrid cross with independent assortment and complete dominance.

Figure 12.2 — 4x4 Punnett square for a dihybrid cross between two YyRr parents.

12.7 The Testcross

A testcross is a cross between an individual with a dominant phenotype (unknown genotype) and a homozygous recessive individual. The purpose is to determine whether the dominant individual is homozygous (AA) or heterozygous (Aa).

  • If all offspring show the dominant phenotype, the unknown parent was most likely homozygous dominant (AA).
  • If approximately half the offspring show the recessive phenotype, the unknown parent was heterozygous (Aa).

12.8 Thomas Hunt Morgan and Gene Linkage

In the early 1900s, Thomas Hunt Morgan worked with the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster and discovered that some genes do not follow independent assortment. Instead, genes located on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together — they are linked.

Drosophila melanogaster has a compact genome organized into just 4 pairs of chromosomes (2n = 8), including one pair of sex chromosomes (XX in females, XY in males). Combined with a generation time of only 10–14 days and the ability to produce hundreds of offspring per cross, these features made the fruit fly an ideal model organism for Morgan's genetic experiments.

Morgan found that linked genes do not always stay together. During meiosis, crossing over (homologous recombination) can separate linked genes. The further apart two genes are on a chromosome, the more likely crossing over will occur between them. This insight allowed geneticists to map genes onto chromosomes based on recombination frequencies.

Recombination frequency is measured in units called centimorgans (cM). One centimorgan equals a 1% recombination frequency between two loci. For instance, if two genes show 15% recombination, they are approximately 15 cM apart. Genes on different chromosomes (unlinked) recombine at a maximum frequency of 50%, which is indistinguishable from independent assortment.

Geneticists use recombination data to construct three types of chromosome maps. A linkage map orders genes along a chromosome based on recombination frequencies, with distances expressed in centimorgans. A cytological map is built from visible banding patterns on stained chromosomes observed under the microscope. A physical (sequence) map provides the most precise picture, locating genes by their actual DNA nucleotide positions as determined by genome sequencing.

Important Distinction

Independent assortment applies to genes on different chromosomes. Linkage applies to genes on the same chromosome. Crossing over partially overcomes linkage by generating recombinant gametes.

KEY POINTS — Chapter 12
  • Mendel used pea plants and studied seven characters with two contrasting traits each.
  • Law of Uniformity: F1 from true-breeding parents are all alike.
  • Law of Segregation: Two alleles separate during gamete formation; each gamete gets one allele.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes on different chromosomes sort independently.
  • Monohybrid cross of heterozygotes gives 3:1 phenotypic ratio.
  • Dihybrid cross of heterozygotes gives 9:3:3:1 ratio.
  • Testcross uses a homozygous recessive individual to reveal an unknown genotype.
  • Morgan showed that linked genes (same chromosome) deviate from independent assortment.

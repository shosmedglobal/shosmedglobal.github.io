Chapter 3

Cellular Biology — Eukaryotic Organisms

3.0 Microscopy: Seeing Cells

Understanding microscopy is essential for interpreting cell biology. Light microscopes magnify up to about 1,000x with a resolution limit of approximately 200 nm (set by the wavelength of visible light). Electron microscopes use beams of electrons with wavelengths of about 0.1–1 nm, achieving far higher resolution. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) sends the beam through ultra-thin sections to reveal internal ultrastructure, while scanning electron microscopy (SEM) bounces electrons off specimen surfaces to produce detailed three-dimensional surface images. The resolving power of a microscope is defined as the minimum distance between two points that can be distinguished as separate.

3.1 The Nucleus

The nucleus is the defining feature of eukaryotic cells (the name means "true nucleus"). It is surrounded by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope, which is studded with nuclear pores — large protein complexes that regulate the movement of molecules in and out of the nucleus. Inside, you find the cell's DNA organized into multiple linear chromosomes (unlike the single circular chromosome of prokaryotes), along with proteins and RNA. A dense region called the nucleolus is where ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is produced and ribosome subunits are partially assembled.

3.2 The Plasma Membrane

The plasma membrane is the boundary between the cell and the outside world. Its structure is described by the fluid mosaic model, proposed by Singer and Nicolson in 1972. The membrane is a fluid bilayer of phospholipids, with their hydrophilic (water-loving) heads facing outward and their hydrophobic (water-fearing) tails facing inward. Embedded in this lipid sea are various proteins:

  • Integral (transmembrane) proteins span the entire bilayer. They serve as channels, transporters, and receptors.
  • Peripheral proteins sit on the surface of the membrane, often involved in signaling or structural support.

Cholesterol (in animal cells) is interspersed among the phospholipids and acts as a fluidity buffer: it prevents the membrane from becoming too rigid in cold temperatures and too fluid in warm temperatures.

Phospholipid Structure and Membrane Fluidity

Each phospholipid molecule has a glycerol backbone linking a hydrophilic head (a phosphate group often attached to a polar molecule like choline, serine, or ethanolamine) to two hydrophobic fatty acid tails. One tail is typically saturated (straight) while the other is unsaturated (kinked due to a double bond). This amphipathic nature drives phospholipids to spontaneously form bilayers in aqueous environments, with tails facing inward and heads facing outward.

Membrane phospholipids are in constant motion. Lateral movement within one leaflet is rapid (approximately 107 times per second), while flip-flop between the two leaflets is extremely rare (roughly once per month) because the polar head must pass through the hydrophobic interior. Unsaturated fatty acid tails contain kinks from double bonds that prevent tight packing, increasing membrane fluidity. Cholesterol acts as a fluidity buffer: at high temperatures it restricts phospholipid movement and reduces fluidity, while at low temperatures it prevents close packing and maintains fluidity.

Functions of Membrane Proteins

Membrane proteins serve at least six major functions: (1) transport of molecules across the membrane via channels and carriers, (2) enzymatic activity (membrane-bound enzymes catalyzing reactions at the cell surface), (3) signal transduction (receptors converting extracellular signals to intracellular responses), (4) intercellular joining (forming junctions between adjacent cells), (5) cell-cell recognition (glycoproteins serving as identity markers), and (6) attachment to the cytoskeleton and extracellular matrix (anchoring the cell and maintaining shape).

The outer surface of the membrane is often decorated with carbohydrate chains (attached to proteins as glycoproteins or to lipids as glycolipids). This carbohydrate coat, called the glycocalyx, plays roles in cell recognition, protection, and adhesion.

The Na+/K+-ATPase (sodium-potassium pump) is a critical active transporter found in virtually all animal cells. Each cycle of the pump uses one ATP molecule to export 3 Na+ ions from the cell and import 2 K+ ions. This creates an electrochemical gradient: high Na+ outside and high K+ inside the cell. This gradient powers secondary active transport, maintains cell volume, and is essential for nerve impulse transmission. The unequal ion exchange (3 out, 2 in) also contributes to the resting membrane potential, making it an electrogenic pump.

3.3 The Endomembrane System

Eukaryotic cells contain an elaborate system of interconnected membrane compartments that work together to produce, modify, package, and transport proteins and lipids.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The ER is a vast network of membrane-enclosed channels and sacs (cisternae) extending from the nuclear envelope. It comes in two varieties:

  • Rough ER (RER): Studded with ribosomes on its outer surface, giving it a "rough" appearance. Ribosomes on the RER synthesize proteins destined for secretion, for insertion into membranes, or for delivery to lysosomes. These proteins enter the ER lumen, where they are folded and may receive initial sugar modifications.
  • Smooth ER (SER): Lacks ribosomes. Functions include lipid and steroid synthesis, carbohydrate metabolism, calcium storage, and detoxification of drugs and poisons. Liver cells are especially rich in SER.

Golgi Apparatus

The Golgi apparatus (or Golgi complex) is a stack of flattened membrane sacs called cisternae. It receives proteins from the ER (on its cis face, nearest the ER), further modifies them (adding or trimming sugar chains, phosphorylating them), sorts them, and dispatches them in vesicles from its trans face to their final destinations: the plasma membrane, lysosomes, or secretion outside the cell.

Lysosomes

Lysosomes are membrane-bound vesicles filled with powerful digestive enzymes (hydrolases) that operate at an acidic pH (around 5). They break down worn-out organelles (autophagy), foreign material taken in by endocytosis, and macromolecules. Think of them as the cell's recycling and waste-disposal centers. Defects in lysosomal enzymes cause storage diseases (such as Tay-Sachs disease) where undigested material accumulates in cells.

Peroxisomes

Peroxisomes are small membrane-bound organelles that contain oxidative enzymes. They break down fatty acids through beta-oxidation and detoxify harmful substances such as hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), which they convert to water and oxygen using the enzyme catalase. Peroxisomes are particularly abundant in liver and kidney cells.

3.4 Mitochondria and Chloroplasts

Mitochondria

Mitochondria are the powerhouses of the cell, where aerobic respiration occurs. Each mitochondrion has a double membrane: a smooth outer membrane and a highly folded inner membrane. The folds of the inner membrane are called cristae, and they dramatically increase the surface area for the electron transport chain and ATP synthase. The space enclosed by the inner membrane is the matrix, where the Krebs cycle takes place. Mitochondria have their own small circular DNA and their own ribosomes, evidence of their evolutionary origins.

Chloroplasts

Chloroplasts are found in plant cells and algae. Like mitochondria, they have a double membrane. Inside, a third membrane system forms flattened sacs called thylakoids, which are stacked into grana (singular: granum). The thylakoid membranes contain the photosynthetic pigments (chlorophyll) and the machinery for the light reactions. The fluid surrounding the thylakoids is the stroma, where the Calvin cycle occurs.

Plastids

Plastids are a family of plant organelles that all derive from undifferentiated proplastids. Chloroplasts (green, photosynthetic) are the most studied, but the family also includes leucoplasts (colorless, store starch and other nutrients) and chromoplasts (contain carotenoid pigments, giving color to fruits and flowers). Plastids can interconvert: for example, chloroplasts in a ripening tomato become chromoplasts as chlorophyll is replaced by carotenoids.

Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

Several key structural differences distinguish plant and animal cells. Plant cells have a rigid cellulose cell wall that provides structural support and prevents osmotic lysis, a large central vacuole (bounded by the tonoplast) that maintains turgor pressure, and chloroplasts for photosynthesis. Animal cells lack cell walls and chloroplasts, typically contain smaller vacuoles, and have centrioles (absent in most plant cells). Without a cell wall, animal cells placed in a hypotonic solution will swell and potentially burst (lysis), while plant cells become turgid but are protected by the wall.

Endosymbiotic Theory

The endosymbiotic theory, championed by Lynn Margulis, proposes that mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by an ancestral eukaryotic cell. Instead of being digested, these prokaryotes survived inside the host and developed a mutually beneficial relationship. Over evolutionary time, they became permanent organelles. Evidence supporting this theory includes: (1) both organelles have double membranes (the inner membrane from the original bacterium, the outer from the host's engulfing membrane), (2) both have their own circular DNA, (3) both have their own ribosomes that resemble bacterial ribosomes, (4) both replicate by binary fission-like division, and (5) their size is similar to bacteria.

3.5 Centrosomes and the Cytoskeleton

Centrosomes

The centrosome is the main microtubule-organizing center (MTOC) of animal cells. It consists of a pair of centrioles — cylindrical structures made of nine triplets of microtubules arranged in a ring. During cell division, centrosomes duplicate and move to opposite poles, where they organize the mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes. Plant cells lack centrioles but still form functional spindles.

The Cytoskeleton

The cytoskeleton is a dynamic network of protein filaments that gives the cell its shape, enables movement, organizes internal structure, and facilitates cell division. There are three main types:

Actin filaments are assembled from individual globular actin monomers (G-actin) that polymerize into long, helical filamentous actin polymers (F-actin). This polymerization is dynamic and reversible, allowing cells to rapidly reorganize their cytoskeleton. The growing end (plus end) polymerizes faster than the shrinking end (minus end), enabling directional extension of structures like pseudopodia during cell crawling.

3.6 Flagella and Cilia

Eukaryotic flagella and cilia share the same internal structure: a core arrangement called the axoneme, consisting of nine pairs (doublets) of microtubules arranged in a ring around two central microtubules — the 9+2 arrangement. The motor protein dynein generates the bending motion. Flagella are typically long and few (one or two per cell), producing a whip-like undulation (example: sperm cells). Cilia are short and numerous, beating in coordinated waves (example: lining of the respiratory tract, where they sweep mucus upward).

3.7 Muscle Contraction

Muscle contraction is driven by the interaction of two proteins: actin (thin filaments) and myosin (thick filaments). Myosin heads bind to actin filaments, undergo a conformational change powered by ATP hydrolysis, and pull the actin filaments inward. This is the sliding filament model. Each cycle of binding, pulling, and release shortens the muscle cell (sarcomere) slightly. Thousands of such cycles occurring simultaneously produce the force of muscle contraction.

Muscle contraction is regulated by two additional proteins on the thin filament. Tropomyosin is a long protein that wraps around actin and blocks myosin binding sites at rest. Troponin is a complex of three subunits that senses calcium levels. When calcium ions (Ca2+) are released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, they bind to troponin, causing a conformational change that shifts tropomyosin away from the binding sites, allowing myosin heads to attach to actin and initiate the power stroke.

3.8 Cell Surfaces and Intercellular Junctions

In multicellular organisms, cells must communicate and adhere to one another. Three major types of intercellular junctions are found in animal cells:

  • Tight junctions: Proteins create a seal between adjacent cells, preventing molecules from leaking through the space between cells. Found in the lining of the gut and bladder, where you need a watertight barrier.
  • Desmosomes: Act like rivets or spot welds, anchoring cells together via intermediate filaments. Provide strong mechanical connections. Abundant in tissues subject to stretching, like skin and heart muscle.
  • Gap junctions: Channels (connexons) that directly connect the cytoplasm of adjacent cells, allowing small molecules, ions, and electrical signals to pass between them. Important in cardiac muscle for coordinated contraction.

Each gap junction channel is formed by two half-channels called connexons, one contributed by each adjacent cell. Each connexon is built from six connexin protein subunits arranged around a central pore. When connexons from neighboring cells dock end-to-end, they create a continuous channel allowing direct passage of small molecules (up to about 1,000 Da) including ions, amino acids, sugars, and signaling molecules like cAMP. In cardiac muscle, gap junctions act as electrical synapses, enabling rapid coordinated contraction of the heart.

Plant cells have plasmodesmata — channels through the cell wall that connect neighboring cells, functionally analogous to gap junctions.

Key Points — Chapter 3

  • The nucleus houses DNA within a double-membrane envelope perforated by nuclear pores; the nucleolus produces rRNA.
  • The plasma membrane follows the fluid mosaic model: a phospholipid bilayer with embedded and peripheral proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates.
  • Transport across the membrane can be passive (diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion) or active (pumps, endocytosis, exocytosis).
  • The endomembrane system (ER → Golgi → lysosomes/vesicles) produces, modifies, and sorts proteins and lipids.
  • Mitochondria and chloroplasts likely originated from endosymbiotic bacteria (endosymbiotic theory).
  • The cytoskeleton (microtubules, microfilaments, intermediate filaments) provides structural support, enables movement, and organizes cell division.
  • Tight junctions seal, desmosomes anchor, and gap junctions connect adjacent animal cells.

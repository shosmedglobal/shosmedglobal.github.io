Chapter 23

Compton Scattering and X-ray Production

Compton Scattering (Compton Effect)

When a photon collides with a free (or loosely bound) electron, the photon loses energy and its wavelength increases. This is the Compton effect (1923), which demonstrated the particle nature of light.

Wavelength shift: Δ_λ = λ' - λ = (h / (m_e · c)) · (1 - cos(θ))

Where:

  • Δ_λ = change in wavelength
  • θ = scattering angle of the photon
  • h / (m_e · c) = Compton wavelength of the electron = 2.426 · 10-12 m = 0.00243 nm

Key features:

  • The shift depends ONLY on the scattering angle, not on the initial wavelength
  • Maximum shift at θ = 180° (backscattering): Δ_λ = 2 · h/(m_e · c) = 0.00486 nm
  • Zero shift at θ = 0° (no scattering)
  • The effect is most noticeable for X-rays and γ rays (short wavelengths) because the shift is a larger fraction of the original wavelength

X-ray Tube Principle

An X-ray tube produces X-rays by accelerating electrons to high speed and smashing them into a metal target (anode):

  1. A heated cathode (filament) emits electrons by thermionic emission
  2. A high voltage (typically 30-150 kV) accelerates electrons toward the anode (target)
  3. When fast electrons hit the metal target, X-rays are produced by two mechanisms:

Bremsstrahlung (braking radiation):

  • When electrons decelerate in the electric field of target nuclei, they emit a continuous spectrum of X-rays
  • Maximum photon energy = e · V (when all kinetic energy converts to one photon)
  • Minimum wavelength: λ_min = h · c / (e · V)

Characteristic X-rays:

  • When a fast electron knocks out an inner-shell electron from a target atom, outer electrons fill the vacancy and emit X-ray photons at specific energies
  • These appear as sharp peaks (spectral lines) on top of the continuous bremsstrahlung spectrum
  • The peak energies depend on the target material (hence "characteristic")

Medical Applications

  • Diagnostic X-rays: Low-to-medium energy, used for imaging (bones, chest, dental). Dense tissues (bone) absorb more X-rays and appear white.
  • CT (computed tomography): Multiple X-ray projections combined by computer to create cross-sectional images.
  • Radiation therapy: High-energy X-rays used to destroy cancer cells.

Worked Example

Problem: An X-ray tube operates at 50 kV. Find the minimum wavelength of X-rays produced.

Solution:

The maximum energy photon is produced when an electron converts all its kinetic energy into a single photon:

E_max = e · V = 1.602 · 10-19 · 50000 = 8.01 · 10-15 J

λ_min = h · c / E_max = (6.626 · 10-34 · 3 · 108) / (8.01 · 10-15)

λ_min = (1.988 · 10-25) / (8.01 · 10-15)

λ_min = 2.48 · 10-11 m = 0.0248 nm

Shortcut: λ_min (in nm) = 1.240 / V(in kV) = 1.240 / 50 = 0.0248 nm. Matches.

Exam Tips:
- Compton shift depends ONLY on angle θ, not on the type of material or initial wavelength.
- Maximum Compton shift is at 180° backscattering: Δ_λ = 4.86 pm.
- X-ray tube: higher voltage = shorter minimum wavelength = more penetrating X-rays.
- Bremsstrahlung gives continuous spectrum. Characteristic X-rays give line spectrum.
- λ_min(nm) = 1.240/V(kV) is a useful shortcut for X-ray tube problems.

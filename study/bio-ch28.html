Excretory System

Overview

The excretory (urinary) system removes metabolic waste products from the blood and helps maintain homeostasis by regulating fluid volume, electrolyte balance, blood pressure, and blood pH. The primary organs are the two kidneys, each connected to a ureter that carries urine to the urinary bladder, which stores urine until it is expelled through the urethra.

Kidney Structure

The kidneys are bean-shaped organs located in the posterior abdominal wall (behind the peritoneum = retroperitoneal). Each kidney is about 12 cm long. When cut open, three regions are visible:

  • Renal cortex: The outer region. Contains the glomeruli and convoluted tubules of the nephrons.
  • Renal medulla: The inner region. Contains cone-shaped structures called renal pyramids, which are formed by the loops of Henle and collecting ducts.
  • Renal pelvis: A funnel-shaped cavity that collects urine from the pyramids and channels it into the ureter.

Blood supply: The renal artery brings blood to each kidney (about 20-25% of cardiac output!). After filtration, cleaned blood exits via the renal vein.

The Nephron: The Functional Unit of the Kidney

Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons. The nephron is where urine is actually produced. It consists of two main parts: a filtering component and a tubular component.

Nephron Components

  • Renal corpuscle: Made of the glomerulus (a tuft of capillaries with high blood pressure) enclosed by Bowman's capsule (a double-walled cup). This is where filtration occurs.
  • Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): The most active part of the nephron for reabsorption. Recovers about 65-70% of filtered water, nearly all glucose and amino acids, sodium, potassium, bicarbonate, and other useful solutes. Cells here have numerous microvilli and mitochondria (for active transport).
  • Loop of Henle: A U-shaped structure that dips into the medulla. The descending limb is permeable to water (water leaves the tubule by osmosis, concentrating the filtrate). The ascending limb is impermeable to water but actively transports sodium, potassium, and chloride out of the tubule. This creates a concentration gradient in the medulla that is essential for producing concentrated urine.
  • Distal convoluted tubule (DCT): Fine-tunes the composition of the filtrate. Responds to hormones: aldosterone (from the adrenal cortex) increases sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion here. Parathyroid hormone (PTH) increases calcium reabsorption.
  • Collecting duct: Receives filtrate from multiple nephrons. Its permeability to water is regulated by antidiuretic hormone (ADH/vasopressin): when ADH is present, the collecting duct becomes more permeable to water, allowing more water to be reabsorbed (producing concentrated urine). When ADH is low (e.g., after drinking a lot of water), the collecting duct is less permeable, and dilute urine is produced.
Is the Collecting Duct Part of the Nephron?

Whether the collecting duct belongs to the nephron is debated across textbooks. In the strict anatomical definition, the nephron comprises the glomerulus, Bowman's capsule, proximal convoluted tubule, loop of Henle, and distal convoluted tubule. The collecting duct is embryologically distinct and receives filtrate from several nephrons. For exam purposes, be prepared for both conventions - some questions treat the collecting duct as part of the nephron, while others do not.

Types of Nephrons

The kidney contains two types of nephrons. Cortical nephrons (~85%) have short loops of Henle that barely enter the medulla and handle most routine filtration and reabsorption. Juxtamedullary nephrons (~15%) have long loops of Henle extending deep into the medulla and are critical for producing concentrated urine via the countercurrent multiplier mechanism.

Vasa Recta

Juxtamedullary nephrons are served by specialized capillaries called the vasa recta that run parallel to the loops of Henle. They form a countercurrent exchange system that maintains the medullary osmotic gradient: as blood flows down alongside the descending limb, it picks up solutes and loses water; as it flows back up alongside the ascending limb, the reverse occurs. This design prevents the blood from washing away the solute concentration that drives water reabsorption.

Mesangial Cells

Mesangial cells are specialized cells located between the capillary loops within the glomerulus. They provide structural support for the glomerular capillaries, can contract to regulate glomerular blood flow, phagocytose trapped molecules and immune complexes, and secrete mediators that influence filtration. Their dysfunction is implicated in several types of glomerulonephritis.

Three Key Processes of Urine Formation

ProcessWhat HappensWhereKey Details
1. Glomerular Filtration Blood pressure forces water and small solutes from blood through the glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule Renal corpuscle The filtrate contains water, glucose, amino acids, urea, ions - essentially blood plasma without proteins and blood cells. ~180 liters of filtrate are produced per day!
2. Tubular Reabsorption Useful substances are transported from the filtrate back into the blood PCT (most), Loop of Henle, DCT, Collecting duct ~99% of filtrate is reabsorbed. All glucose and amino acids, most water and ions are recovered. Only ~1-2 liters of urine are produced per day.
3. Tubular Secretion Additional waste substances are transported from the blood into the tubular fluid PCT and DCT Hydrogen ions (for pH regulation), potassium ions, certain drugs, and toxins are secreted. This is the reverse of reabsorption.

Urinary Tract

  • Ureters: Two muscular tubes (~25 cm each) that transport urine from the kidneys to the urinary bladder by peristalsis.
  • Urinary bladder: A hollow, distensible organ that stores urine (capacity ~500-700 mL). Its wall contains smooth muscle called the detrusor muscle. The lining is transitional epithelium that stretches as the bladder fills.
  • Urethra: A tube that carries urine from the bladder to the outside. In females, it is short (~4 cm) and serves only urinary function. In males, it is longer (~20 cm) and shared with the reproductive system. Two sphincters control urine release: the internal urethral sphincter (involuntary, smooth muscle) and the external urethral sphincter (voluntary, skeletal muscle).

Common Diseases of the Excretory System

  • Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis): Hard mineral deposits (often calcium oxalate) that form in the kidneys and can cause severe pain when passing through the urinary tract.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI): Bacterial infection of the urethra, bladder, or kidneys. More common in females due to the shorter urethra.
  • Glomerulonephritis: Inflammation of the glomeruli, often from autoimmune causes. Can impair filtration and lead to blood and protein in the urine.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): Progressive loss of kidney function over time. Common causes include diabetes and hypertension. In severe cases, dialysis or kidney transplantation may be necessary.
  • Diabetes insipidus: A condition (unrelated to diabetes mellitus) where ADH is either not produced or the kidneys do not respond to it, leading to production of very large volumes of dilute urine and extreme thirst.

Mnemonic: Nephron Segment Order

"Bowman's PCT Loops DCT to Collect"

Bowman's capsule → Proximal Convoluted Tubule → Loop of Henle → Distal Convoluted Tubule → Collecting Duct

Mnemonic: Three Processes of Urine Formation - "FRS"

Filtration (at the glomerulus) → Reabsorption (mostly at PCT) → Secretion (additional waste removal)

Think: "Filter, Reclaim, Secrete"

Key Points - Chapter 28

  • Kidneys filter ~180 L of blood per day but produce only ~1-2 L of urine (99% is reabsorbed).
  • The nephron has: renal corpuscle (glomerulus + Bowman's capsule), PCT, loop of Henle, DCT, collecting duct.
  • Three processes: filtration (pressure-driven), reabsorption (recovers useful substances), secretion (adds waste to filtrate).
  • ADH controls water reabsorption in the collecting duct; aldosterone controls sodium/potassium balance in the DCT.
  • The loop of Henle creates a medullary concentration gradient essential for urine concentration.
  • Common diseases: kidney stones, UTIs, glomerulonephritis, CKD. Diabetes insipidus involves ADH dysfunction.

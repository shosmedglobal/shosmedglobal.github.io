Chapter 13

Genetics in Humans

Applying genetics to humans presents unique challenges: we cannot perform planned crosses, generation times are long, and family sizes are small. Nevertheless, careful analysis of family histories (pedigrees) and modern molecular tools have revealed an enormous amount about human inheritance.

13.1 Key Genetic Terminology

Gene: A segment of DNA that encodes instructions for a functional product (usually a protein or RNA molecule).

Allele: An alternative version of a gene. Different alleles arise through mutation and can produce different traits.

Locus (plural: loci): The specific physical location of a gene on a chromosome.

Genome: The complete set of genetic information in an organism.

Genotype: The combination of alleles an individual carries for a particular gene (e.g., Aa).

Phenotype: The observable characteristic resulting from the genotype and environmental influences (e.g., brown eyes).

13.2 Gene Expression: Dominant vs. Recessive

Humans are diploid - we carry two copies of each gene (one from each parent). How these two alleles interact determines the phenotype.

  • Dominant allele: An allele whose effect is fully expressed in heterozygotes. Written with a capital letter (e.g., A).
  • Recessive allele: An allele whose effect is masked in heterozygotes and only expressed when two copies are present (homozygous). Written with a lowercase letter (e.g., a).

13.3 Homozygous vs. Heterozygous

Term Genotype Meaning
Homozygous dominant AA Two identical dominant alleles
Heterozygous Aa Two different alleles (one dominant, one recessive); also called a "carrier" for the recessive trait
Homozygous recessive aa Two identical recessive alleles; the recessive phenotype is expressed

13.4 Patterns of Dominance

A. Complete Dominance

The heterozygote is indistinguishable from the homozygous dominant individual. One allele completely masks the other. This is what Mendel observed in pea plants.

B. Incomplete Dominance

The heterozygote shows a blended, intermediate phenotype between the two homozygotes. Neither allele is fully dominant over the other.

Classic example: In snapdragon flowers, crossing a red-flowered plant (CRCR) with a white-flowered plant (CWCW) produces pink F₁ offspring (CRCW). The F₂ ratio is 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white (1:2:1).

C. Codominance

Both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously in the heterozygote. The phenotype is not a blend - both traits are clearly visible.

Incomplete Dominance vs. Codominance

Incomplete dominance: The heterozygote shows a blend (red + white = pink).

Codominance: The heterozygote shows both traits simultaneously (red and white spots together, not a blend).

13.5 ABO Blood Group System

The ABO blood group is a classic example of multiple alleles and codominance. There are three alleles for the ABO gene:

  • IA - codes for antigen A on red blood cells
  • IB - codes for antigen B on red blood cells
  • i - codes for no antigen (recessive to both IA and IB)

IA and IB are codominant with each other (both are expressed if present together), but each is dominant over i.

Universal donor: Type O (no antigens to trigger a reaction). Universal recipient: Type AB (no antibodies to attack donated blood).

Blood group frequencies vary among different populations, reflecting founder effects, genetic drift, and natural selection over thousands of years. In the U.S., Type O is the most common (roughly 44%), followed by Type A (about 42%), Type B (about 10%), and Type AB (about 4%). Among some indigenous peoples of the Americas, Type O approaches nearly 100%, while Type B is more prevalent in Central and East Asian populations than in European ones. These population-level frequency differences have practical applications in population genetics and forensic science.

13.6 Rh Blood Group System

The Rh system is determined by the presence or absence of the Rh antigen (D antigen) on red blood cells.

  • Rh-positive (Rh+): Has the Rh antigen. Genotype: DD or Dd.
  • Rh-negative (Rh−): Lacks the Rh antigen. Genotype: dd.

Rh is inherited as a simple dominant/recessive trait. The Rh factor is medically important during pregnancy: if an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus, her immune system may produce antibodies against the fetal red blood cells. This condition, called hemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN), can be prevented with an injection of Rh immunoglobulin (RhoGAM).

13.7 Polygenic and Quantitative Traits

Many human traits are not controlled by a single gene but are instead polygenic - influenced by the additive effects of multiple genes, often combined with environmental factors. These are called quantitative characters because they vary continuously across a range of values rather than falling into distinct categories (as Mendelian traits do). Common examples include height, skin color, blood pressure, and body weight.

When the distribution of a quantitative trait is plotted for a population, it typically follows a bell-shaped Gaussian (normal) distribution curve: most individuals cluster near the mean, with progressively fewer at either extreme. While monogenic (single-gene) disorders account for only about 1–2% of genetic conditions, multifactorial traits and disorders influenced by many genes plus environmental factors constitute the vast majority.

13.8 Gene Pool Concept

The gene pool is the total collection of all alleles present in a population at a given time. It is a concept central to population genetics (Chapter 19). When we describe a population genetically, we refer to the frequencies of alleles and genotypes within the gene pool.

KEY POINTS - Chapter 13
  • Genotype refers to the allele combination; phenotype is the observable trait.
  • Complete dominance: heterozygote looks like homozygous dominant.
  • Incomplete dominance: heterozygote is intermediate (blend).
  • Codominance: both alleles are expressed simultaneously (not blended).
  • ABO system: three alleles (IA, IB, i), codominance between IA and IB.
  • Rh factor is dominant/recessive; Rh incompatibility can cause HDN.
  • The gene pool represents all alleles in a population.
MNEMONIC - Blood Type AB

"AB = Accept Both, O = Offer to all"

AB is the universal recipient (accepts both antigens). O is the universal donor (offers to all, since it has no antigens).

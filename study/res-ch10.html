Chapter 10

Writing the Manuscript

10.1 The IMRaD Framework

The standard structure for original research articles is IMRaD: Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion. This format is used by the vast majority of medical journals and is what editors and reviewers expect. Deviating from it for original research is almost never appropriate. Understanding how each section functions - and what belongs where - is fundamental to producing a publishable manuscript.

10.2 Crafting an Effective Title

Your title is the first element that editors, reviewers, and readers encounter. It determines whether anyone clicks on your paper or scrolls past it. A strong title is specific, informative, and searchable.

Weak: “Outcomes in Lymphoma Patients” - too vague to be useful.

Strong: “Overall Survival Following Polatuzumab-R-CHP Versus R-CHOP in Newly Diagnosed DLBCL: A Single-Center Retrospective Analysis” - specific, includes the study design, and is findable through keyword searches.

Aim for under 20 words. Avoid abbreviations that are not universally recognized. Some journals have specific title formatting requirements, so always check the author guidelines.

10.3 Writing the Abstract

The abstract is often the only section most readers will see. For original research, use a structured format: Background, Methods, Results, Conclusion.

Include specific quantitative results: sample size, primary outcome values with confidence intervals or p-values, and the main conclusion. Never include data in the abstract that does not appear in the main text. The abstract must stand alone as a self-contained summary of your entire study.

10.4 The Introduction: The Funnel Approach

Structure your introduction like an inverted funnel - start broad and progressively narrow to your specific question:

Paragraph 1: Broad context. What is the disease or clinical problem? Why does it matter? (Two to four sentences.)

Paragraph 2: Current knowledge. What does the existing literature tell us? Summarize briefly. (Three to five sentences.)

Paragraph 3: The gap. What remains unknown, unresolved, or contradictory? (Two to three sentences.)

Final sentence: Your study's aim. “In this study, we aimed to evaluate...” This sentence signals to the reader exactly what to expect.

Keep the introduction concise - three to four paragraphs is typical. Do not write a comprehensive literature review here; save detailed comparisons for the Discussion.

10.5 The Methods Section

The Methods should be detailed enough that another researcher could replicate your study. Include:

  • Study design and setting: “This was a retrospective cohort study conducted at [institution] from [start date] to [end date].”
  • Participant selection: Inclusion and exclusion criteria, clearly stated.
  • Variables and outcomes: Define every variable, how it was measured, and your primary and secondary outcomes.
  • Data collection: How data were obtained (chart review, database query, etc.).
  • Statistical analysis: Which tests were used and why. Specify software and version. State your significance threshold (typically two-sided p < 0.05).
  • Ethics statement: IRB approval with protocol number.

10.6 The Results Section

Present results in an order that mirrors your Methods section:

  1. Descriptive data first. Patient demographics and baseline characteristics, typically in Table 1.
  2. Primary outcomes next. Highlight key findings in the text. Use tables and figures for detailed data. Do not present the same data in text, table, and figure format - each format should complement, not duplicate.
  3. Secondary outcomes. Follow the same pattern.
  4. Subgroup and sensitivity analyses. Present after the main findings.

Use tables for detailed numerical data (demographics, regression results). Use figures for visual patterns (Kaplan-Meier curves, forest plots, trend charts). Every table and figure should be interpretable on its own without reading the surrounding text.

10.7 The Discussion Section

The Discussion interprets your findings and places them in context. Structure it as follows:

Paragraph 1: Restate the main finding in one to two sentences. Interpret what it means - do not simply repeat the numbers from the Results section.

Paragraphs 2–4: Compare your results to existing literature. Where do they agree with previous studies? Where do they diverge? What might explain any discrepancies?

Clinical implications paragraph: What does this mean for patient care? Be specific but do not overstate your conclusions beyond what the data support.

Limitations paragraph: Every study has limitations. For retrospective studies, typical limitations include the retrospective design itself, selection bias, missing data, single-center experience, and inability to establish causation. Acknowledging limitations does not weaken your paper - it strengthens it by demonstrating intellectual honesty.

Conclusion: Two to four sentences summarizing the main finding and its significance. Avoid introducing new information. If appropriate, end with a statement about future research directions.

10.8 References

Use your reference manager (Zotero or Mendeley) to format citations according to the target journal's required style. Most medical journals use Vancouver (numbered) or AMA format. A typical original research article includes 25 to 50 references. Prioritize recent publications (within the last five to ten years) unless citing foundational older work. Verify that every in-text citation appears in the reference list and vice versa.

10.9 The Most Efficient Writing Order

Many researchers stare at a blank document and freeze. The following workflow makes writing manageable by starting with the most concrete sections:

  1. Methods first. Simply describe what you did. This gets words on the page and builds momentum.
  2. Results second. Create tables and figures first - they form the skeleton. Then write the narrative text around them.
  3. Introduction third. Now that you know your findings, you can craft an introduction that sets them up properly.
  4. Discussion fourth. Interpret your results, compare to existing literature, address limitations.
  5. Abstract fifth. It summarizes the entire paper, so write it only after everything else is complete.
  6. Title last. The perfect title often only becomes apparent after the paper is fully written.

Set daily writing goals of 300 to 500 words per session. Consistent incremental progress produces better writing than marathon weekend sessions.

10.10 Tips for Non-Native English Speakers

Academic English follows specific conventions. If you are writing in a second or third language, these strategies help:

  • Keep sentences short. Aim for 15 to 20 words on average. Complex sentences increase the risk of errors and reduce clarity.
  • Prefer active voice: “We analyzed the data using Cox regression” is clearer than “The data were analyzed using Cox regression by the authors.”
  • Watch article usage (a, an, the) - this is the most common source of grammatical errors for non-native speakers.
  • Maintain tense consistency: Methods and Results typically use past tense. Introduction and Discussion may use present tense for established facts.
  • Avoid colloquialisms: Replace “a lot of” with “numerous,” “looked at” with “examined,” “right away” with “immediately.”
  • Use tools: Grammarly (free tier) catches most grammatical issues. LanguageTool is a free open-source alternative.
  • Read published papers in your target journal. Pay attention to sentence structure, transitions, and how authors describe methods and interpret results. This passive exposure naturally improves your writing.

Key Takeaways

  • Follow the IMRaD structure without deviation for original research manuscripts
  • Your title and abstract determine whether the rest of your paper gets read
  • Use the funnel approach for introductions: broad context narrowing to your specific aim
  • Methods must be detailed enough that someone else could replicate your study
  • Present results logically with complementary text, tables, and figures (avoid duplication)
  • The Discussion interprets findings, compares to literature, and honestly acknowledges limitations

Common Mistakes

  • Writing an introduction that is excessively long or reads like a textbook chapter
  • Providing insufficient methodological detail for replication
  • Duplicating the same data across text, tables, and figures
  • Omitting a limitations paragraph from the Discussion
  • Overstating conclusions beyond what the data actually support
  • Submitting without having a native English speaker proofread the manuscript

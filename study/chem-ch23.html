Chapter 23

Ethers, Epoxides, Thiols, Sulfides, and Disulfides

Ethers

Ethers have the general formula R–O–R'. Two hydrocarbon groups are bonded to the same oxygen atom. They are named by listing the two alkyl groups in alphabetical order followed by "ether" (e.g., diethyl ether, methyl propyl ether). In IUPAC nomenclature, the smaller group + oxygen is treated as an "alkoxy" substituent (methoxy-, ethoxy-).

Properties: Ethers have low boiling points (no hydrogen bonding between ether molecules) and are relatively unreactive, making them excellent solvents. Diethyl ether is the classic laboratory solvent. However, ethers can form explosive peroxides when exposed to air over time.

Epoxides

Epoxides (oxiranes) are three-membered cyclic ethers. The small ring creates significant ring strain, making epoxides far more reactive than ordinary ethers. The simplest epoxide is ethylene oxide (oxirane).

Epoxides readily undergo ring-opening reactions with nucleophiles (water, alcohols, amines) because breaking the strained ring releases energy.

Thiols

Thiols (mercaptans) have the general formula R–SH. They are the sulfur analogs of alcohols. Named by adding "-thiol" to the parent name (e.g., ethanethiol).

  • Strong, unpleasant odor — propane-1-thiol contributes to the smell of onions, while allyl mercaptan (prop-2-ene-1-thiol) gives garlic its pungent aroma. Thiols are also added to odorless natural gas as a safety marker so leaks can be detected by smell.
  • Lower boiling points than corresponding alcohols (S–H bonds form much weaker hydrogen bonds than O–H).
  • More acidic than alcohols (sulfur is larger, stabilizes the negative charge better). When the –SH group is not the principal functional group in a compound's name, the prefix "sulfanyl-" is used (the older prefix "mercapto-" is still sometimes seen).
  • Easily oxidized to form disulfides.

Sulfides and Disulfides

Sulfides (thioethers) have the formula R–S–R'. They are the sulfur analogs of ethers.

Disulfides have the formula R–S–S–R'. They are formed by the oxidation of two thiol molecules:

2 R–SH → R–S–S–R + 2H⁺ + 2e−

This reaction is reversible (reduction breaks the disulfide bond back to thiols). Disulfide bonds are critically important in protein chemistry: the amino acid cysteine contains an –SH group, and two cysteine residues can form a disulfide bridge (–S–S–) that stabilizes the three-dimensional structure of proteins.

Key Points — Chapter 23

  • Ethers (R–O–R'): low reactivity, good solvents, low boiling points (no self-hydrogen bonding).
  • Epoxides: strained 3-membered ring; highly reactive to ring-opening.
  • Thiols (R–SH): sulfur analogs of alcohols; foul smell; more acidic than alcohols.
  • Disulfides (R–S–S–R): formed by oxidation of thiols; crucial for protein structure (cysteine bridges).

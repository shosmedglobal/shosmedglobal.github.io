Chapter 16

Surface Tension

Definition and Units

Surface tension (σ) is the force per unit length acting along the surface of a liquid, or equivalently, the energy per unit area of the surface:

  • σ = F / L (force per unit length, units: N/m)
  • σ = E / A (energy per unit area, units: J/m2)

These are equivalent: 1 N/m = 1 J/m2.

Surface tension arises because molecules at the surface have fewer neighbors than molecules in the bulk liquid, so they experience a net inward force. This makes the surface behave like a stretched elastic membrane.

Capillary Action

When a narrow tube is placed in a liquid, the liquid may rise or fall inside the tube:

h = (2 · σ · cos(θ)) / (ρ · g · r)

Where:

  • h = height of rise (positive) or depression (negative)
  • σ = surface tension of the liquid
  • θ = contact angle between liquid and tube wall
  • ρ = density of the liquid
  • g = gravitational acceleration
  • r = radius of the tube
Liquid-Tube PairContact AngleBehavior
Water in glassθ < 90°Rises (meniscus concave)
Mercury in glassθ > 90°Depresses (meniscus convex)

Laplace Pressure (Excess Pressure Inside Curved Surfaces)

A curved liquid surface creates a pressure difference between inside and outside:

  • Liquid droplet (one surface): Δ_p = 2 · σ / r
  • Soap bubble (two surfaces, inner and outer): Δ_p = 4 · σ / r
  • General curved surface: Δ_p = σ · (1/R_1 + 1/R_2) (where R_1, R_2 are principal radii of curvature)

Smaller bubbles/droplets have HIGHER internal pressure (since r is in the denominator).

Surfactants and Detergents

Surfactants are molecules that reduce surface tension. They have a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and a hydrophobic (water-fearing) tail. When added to water, they concentrate at the surface and reduce the energy cost of the interface.

Detergents are surfactants used for cleaning. They reduce water's surface tension, allowing it to wet surfaces better and penetrate fabrics. They also form micelles around grease droplets, suspending them in water.

Biological Relevance: Lung Surfactant

The alveoli (tiny air sacs) in the lungs are lined with a thin layer of fluid. Without surfactant, the surface tension would make small alveoli collapse (since smaller radius means higher Laplace pressure). Pulmonary surfactant (produced by Type II pneumocytes) reduces surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse and reducing the work of breathing.

In premature infants, insufficient surfactant production causes respiratory distress syndrome (RDS).

Worked Example

Problem: A glass capillary tube with inner radius 0.3 mm is placed vertically in water (σ = 0.073 N/m, ρ = 1000 kg/m3, contact angle θ = 0°). How high does the water rise?

Solution:

h = (2 · σ · cos(θ)) / (ρ · g · r)

h = (2 · 0.073 · cos(0)) / (1000 · 10 · 0.0003)

h = (2 · 0.073 · 1) / (3)

h = 0.146 / 3

h = 0.0487 m = 4.87 cm

Note: cos(0) = 1 for perfect wetting. The narrower the tube, the higher the rise.

Exam Tips:
- Soap bubble has TWO surfaces so Laplace pressure is 4*σ/r (double a droplet).
- Capillary rise is inversely proportional to tube radius: narrower tube = higher rise.
- Surfactants decrease surface tension.
- Water wets glass (θ < 90, concave meniscus). Mercury does not (θ > 90, convex meniscus).
- Lung surfactant is clinically critical -- premature babies may lack it.

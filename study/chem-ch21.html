Chapter 21

Aromatic Compounds (Arenes)

Benzene and Aromaticity

Benzene (C₆H₆) is the parent aromatic compound. Its structure puzzled chemists for decades because it has the formula of a highly unsaturated molecule (three double bonds) yet behaves as though it is remarkably stable and resists addition reactions.

The modern understanding: benzene is a planar, hexagonal ring in which six p-electrons are delocalized equally over all six carbon atoms. Instead of three alternating single and double bonds, there is a continuous cloud of electron density above and below the ring. This delocalization gives benzene extraordinary stability called aromatic stabilization energy (about 150 kJ/mol more stable than predicted for a hypothetical "cyclohexatriene").

To be aromatic, a compound must be: (1) cyclic, (2) planar, (3) fully conjugated (every atom in the ring has a p-orbital), and (4) have 4n+2 pi electrons (Huckel's rule). Benzene has 6 pi electrons (n=1), satisfying this criterion.

Naming Aromatic Compounds

Monosubstituted benzenes: the substituent name is added as a prefix to "benzene" (e.g., chlorobenzene, nitrobenzene). Some have retained common names: toluene (methylbenzene), styrene (vinylbenzene).

Disubstituted benzenes use the prefixes ortho- (1,2-), meta- (1,3-), and para- (1,4-) to indicate relative positions.

Phenyl vs. Benzyl: The phenyl group (C₆H₅–) is the benzene ring minus one hydrogen. The benzyl group (C₆H₅CH₂–) is phenyl plus a CH₂ bridge.

Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)

Because aromatic stability is so valuable, benzene prefers substitution reactions over addition. The electron-rich ring attacks an electrophile, temporarily losing aromaticity, then a proton is lost to restore the aromatic system.

Key Points - Chapter 21

  • Aromatic compounds have delocalized pi electrons and special stability (Huckel: 4n+2 pi electrons).
  • Benzene prefers substitution over addition to preserve aromaticity.
  • EAS reactions: nitration, halogenation, Friedel-Crafts alkylation/acylation, sulfonation.
  • Ortho (1,2), meta (1,3), para (1,4) describe relative positions on the ring.
  • Phenyl = ring only (C₆H₅–); Benzyl = ring + CH₂ (C₆H₅CH₂–).

Mnemonic: EAS Reactions

"Never Hesitate, Freddy's Superb" = Nitration, Halogenation, Friedel-Crafts, Sulfonation

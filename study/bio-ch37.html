Immune System

🧠Practice this chapterTest yourself with chapter-specific questions →

The immune system is the body's defense network against pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites) and abnormal cells (such as cancer). It ranges from simple physical barriers to highly specific cellular responses that can remember and rapidly respond to previously encountered threats.

Organs of the Immune System

  • Bone marrow - site of hematopoiesis; where all immune cells originate. B cells mature here.
  • Thymus - located behind the sternum. Where T cells mature and are "educated" to distinguish self from non-self. Most active during childhood; shrinks with age.
  • Lymph nodes - small bean-shaped structures distributed throughout the body along lymphatic vessels. They filter lymph and serve as meeting points where immune cells encounter antigens.
  • Spleen - the largest lymphoid organ. Filters blood, removes old/damaged RBCs, and houses immune cells that respond to blood-borne pathogens.

WBC Morphological Details

Under the microscope, white blood cells have distinctive features that help identify them in a blood smear:

  • Neutrophils have a multilobed nucleus (typically 2–5 lobes connected by thin chromatin strands) and pale pink cytoplasmic granules. They are the predominant cells in pus.
  • Monocytes are the largest circulating WBCs (16–20 micrometers) with a characteristic kidney-shaped (reniform) or horseshoe-shaped nucleus. They circulate for 24–36 hours before migrating into tissues to become macrophages.
  • Eosinophils have a distinctive bilobed nucleus and bright pink-orange cytoplasmic granules.
  • Lymphocytes are small cells whose large, round, darkly staining nucleus occupies most of the cell volume, leaving only a thin rim of cytoplasm.
  • Basophils have a bilobed or trilobed nucleus often obscured by their prominent dark purple granules.

Nonspecific (Innate) Defense

The innate immune system provides immediate, broad protection that does not distinguish between specific pathogens. It does not improve with repeated exposure.

First Line of Defense: Physical and Chemical Barriers

  • Skin - intact skin is the most important barrier. Keratinized epithelium is difficult for pathogens to penetrate. Sweat and sebum contain antimicrobial substances.
  • Mucous membranes - line the respiratory, digestive, urinary, and reproductive tracts. Mucus traps pathogens. Cilia in the airways sweep trapped particles upward.
  • Chemical defenses - stomach acid (pH ~2) kills most ingested microbes. Lysozyme in tears, saliva, and nasal secretions destroys bacterial cell walls.
  • Normal microbiota - the resident commensal bacteria of the skin, gut, and mucous membranes compete with pathogens for nutrients and attachment sites (competitive exclusion), produce antimicrobial substances such as bacteriocins and short-chain fatty acids, and help train the immune system. Disruption of normal flora - for example by broad-spectrum antibiotics - can lead to opportunistic infections such as Clostridioides difficile colitis.

Second Line of Defense: Internal Innate Responses

  • Phagocytes - neutrophils and macrophages (mature monocytes) engulf and digest pathogens through phagocytosis.
  • Natural Killer (NK) cells - lymphocytes that destroy virus-infected cells and tumor cells without prior sensitization.
  • Inflammation - when tissue is damaged or infected, local cells release histamine and other signaling molecules. This causes vasodilation (redness, warmth), increased capillary permeability (swelling from fluid leaking into tissue), and attraction of phagocytes to the site (chemotaxis). The four cardinal signs of inflammation are redness, heat, swelling, and pain.
  • Fever - elevated body temperature triggered by pyrogens (fever-inducing substances released by immune cells or pathogens). Moderate fever enhances immune function and inhibits some pathogen growth.
  • Complement system - a group of about 30 plasma proteins that, when activated, punch holes in bacterial membranes (lysis), coat pathogens to make them easier to phagocytize (opsonization), and attract immune cells to the infection site.
  • Interferons - signaling proteins released by virus-infected cells that help neighboring cells resist viral infection. Type I interferons (IFN-alpha and IFN-beta), produced by most nucleated cells, bind to receptors on adjacent cells and induce an "antiviral state" by activating genes that degrade viral RNA and inhibit viral protein synthesis. Type II interferon (IFN-gamma), produced mainly by T cells and NK cells, activates macrophages and enhances antigen presentation. Interferons serve as an important bridge between innate and adaptive immunity.

Specific (Adaptive) Defense

The adaptive immune system develops targeted responses against specific pathogens. It is slower to activate initially but generates immunological memory, allowing faster, stronger responses upon re-exposure. It relies on lymphocytes: T cells and B cells.

An antigen is any molecule (usually a protein or polysaccharide) that can be specifically recognized by the adaptive immune system. Each lymphocyte carries receptors for one specific antigen.

Cellular Immunity (T Cells)

T cells mature in the thymus and provide cell-mediated immunity - they directly attack infected or abnormal cells.

  • Helper T cells (CD4+) - the "commanders" of the immune response. When activated by an antigen-presenting cell (like a macrophage or dendritic cell), they release cytokines that activate other immune cells, including B cells and cytotoxic T cells. They do not kill pathogens directly.
  • Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) - the "killers." They directly destroy cells that are infected with viruses or have become cancerous. They recognize foreign antigens displayed on the surface of target cells and release perforins and granzymes that induce cell death (apoptosis).
  • Regulatory T cells - suppress immune responses after the threat is cleared, preventing excessive inflammation and autoimmune reactions.

Humoral Immunity (B Cells)

B cells mature in the bone marrow and provide antibody-mediated immunity - they produce soluble proteins called antibodies that circulate in body fluids ("humors").

When a B cell encounters its specific antigen and receives help from a helper T cell, it divides and differentiates into:

  • Plasma cells - factories that produce large quantities of antibodies (immunoglobulins). Each plasma cell secretes antibodies specific to one antigen.
  • Memory B cells - long-lived cells that "remember" the antigen. If the same pathogen returns months or years later, memory B cells rapidly produce antibodies, generating a faster and stronger secondary immune response.

Antibodies work by: binding to pathogens and neutralizing them (blocking their ability to infect cells), marking them for destruction by phagocytes (opsonization), activating the complement system, and clumping pathogens together (agglutination).

Vaccination

Vaccination is a form of artificial active immunity. A vaccine contains a weakened (attenuated) or killed pathogen, parts of a pathogen (subunit), or instructions for making a pathogen protein (mRNA vaccines). The immune system responds by producing antibodies and memory cells without causing the actual disease. If the real pathogen is encountered later, memory cells mount a rapid, effective response.

Arthropod Vectors of Infectious Disease

Many of the world's most serious infectious diseases are not transmitted directly from person to person but require an arthropod vector - an organism that carries and transmits pathogens from one vertebrate host to another. Understanding disease vectors is essential for public health and for understanding how the immune system encounters different types of pathogens.

Mosquitoes

Mosquitoes are the deadliest animals on Earth measured by the number of human deaths they cause. Female mosquitoes of different genera transmit distinct sets of pathogens: Anopheles species transmit malaria (Plasmodium); Aedes species (particularly Ae. aegypti) transmit dengue fever, Zika virus, yellow fever, and chikungunya; and Culex species transmit West Nile virus and several forms of encephalitis. Mosquito control - including insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, larviciding, and elimination of standing water - remains one of the most important public health interventions worldwide.

Ticks

Ticks (class Arachnida) are obligate blood-feeding ectoparasites with a slow feeding cycle lasting several days. Ixodes ticks transmit Borrelia burgdorferi, the spirochete bacterium causing Lyme disease (the most common tick-borne illness in the Northern Hemisphere), as well as the agents of babesiosis and tick-borne encephalitis. Because pathogen transmission typically requires prolonged tick attachment (often 36–48 hours for Lyme disease), prompt tick detection and removal is an effective preventive measure.

Lice

Lice are wingless, host-specific insects. Head lice (Pediculus humanus capitis) cause itching but do not transmit serious diseases. Body lice (Pediculus humanus corporis), however, are medically significant vectors of epidemic typhus (Rickettsia prowazekii), trench fever (Bartonella quintana), and louse-borne relapsing fever (Borrelia recurrentis). Body lice proliferate in conditions of overcrowding and poor hygiene, which is why these diseases have historically been associated with wartime, famine, and displacement of populations.

Fleas

Fleas are small, wingless, laterally compressed insects adapted for jumping and blood feeding. The rat flea (Xenopsylla cheopis) was the primary vector for bubonic plague (Yersinia pestis), which killed an estimated one-third of Europe's population during the Black Death pandemic of the 14th century. Fleas can also transmit murine typhus (Rickettsia typhi) and serve as intermediate hosts for the dog tapeworm (Dipylidium caninum).

Flies

Tsetse flies (Glossina species) in sub-Saharan Africa transmit Trypanosoma brucei, the protozoan that causes African sleeping sickness. Sandflies (family Phlebotomidae) transmit Leishmania parasites, causing leishmaniasis. Blackflies (Simulium species) transmit Onchocerca volvulus, the filarial nematode responsible for river blindness (onchocerciasis), which affects millions in tropical Africa and parts of Latin America. Common houseflies and blowflies can mechanically transmit enteric pathogens (such as Salmonella and Shigella) on their legs and mouthparts.

Key Points - Immune System
  • Innate immunity: barriers (skin, mucus), phagocytes, inflammation, fever, complement. Fast, nonspecific, no memory.
  • Adaptive immunity: T cells (cellular) and B cells (humoral). Slow initially, specific, generates memory.
  • Helper T cells coordinate the response; cytotoxic T cells kill infected cells; regulatory T cells dampen the response.
  • B cells differentiate into plasma cells (secrete antibodies) and memory cells (long-term protection).
  • Active immunity = body makes its own antibodies (lasting). Passive immunity = receives preformed antibodies (temporary).
  • Vaccination = artificial active immunity. Produces memory without disease.
  • Key arthropod vectors: mosquitoes (malaria, dengue, Zika), ticks (Lyme disease), body lice (epidemic typhus), fleas (plague), tsetse flies (sleeping sickness), sandflies (leishmaniasis).

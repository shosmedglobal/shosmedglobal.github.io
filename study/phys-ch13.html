Chapter 13

Atomic and Nuclear Physics

Atomic Structure

  • Atomic number (Z): number of protons. Defines the element.
  • Mass number (A): protons + neutrons. A = Z + N.
  • Nuclide: A specific nuclear species characterized by its atomic number Z and mass number A. Written as AZX (e.g., 126C is a nuclide of carbon with 6 protons and 6 neutrons). Each nuclide has a definite Z and A.
  • Isotopes: Nuclides with the same Z but different A (different number of neutrons). Example: 126C and 146C are both carbon isotopes.
  • Isobars: Nuclides with the same A but different Z. Example: 146C and 147N.

Bohr Model of the Atom

Think of energy levels like floors in a building: an electron can be on floor 1, floor 2, or floor 3, but never between floors. Electrons orbit the nucleus in discrete energy levels. Energy is quantized.

En = -13.6 / n2 eV (for hydrogen)

Where n = 1, 2, 3, ... (principal quantum number). n = 1 is the ground state.

Photon emission: When electron drops from nhigh to nlow:

Ephoton = Ehigh - Elow = hf = hc/λ

Consequences of the Pauli principle:

  • Determines the electron configuration of every element and the structure of the periodic table.
  • Explains why atoms have distinct sizes and chemical properties.
  • Prevents all electrons from collapsing into the lowest energy state, which is essential for the stability and diversity of matter.

Black Body Radiation

A black body is an idealized object that absorbs all incoming electromagnetic radiation and re-emits it in a characteristic spectrum that depends only on its temperature.

Wien's displacement law: The peak wavelength of emission shifts to shorter wavelengths as temperature increases:

λmax · T = b

Where b = 2.898 x 10-3 m·K (Wien's displacement constant).

Worked Example: The Sun's surface temperature is about 5800 K. At what wavelength does it emit most strongly?

  • λmax = 2.898 x 10-3 / 5800 = 5 x 10-7 m = 500 nm (green/yellow light)

Stefan-Boltzmann law: The total power radiated per unit area by a black body:

j = σ · T4

Where σ = 5.67 x 10-8 W/(m2·K4).

Total radiated power: P = σ · A · T4. Doubling the temperature increases radiated power by a factor of 16.

The ultraviolet catastrophe: Classical physics (Rayleigh-Jeans law) predicted that a black body should radiate infinite energy at short wavelengths. This catastrophic failure was resolved by Max Planck, who proposed that energy is emitted in discrete quanta (E = hf), founding quantum mechanics.

Key facts:

  • A black body at room temperature (300 K) emits mainly in the infrared.
  • As objects are heated, they glow red, then orange, then white, then blue-white.
  • Stars with higher surface temperatures appear blue; cooler stars appear red.

Absorption and Emission of Photons

When an electron in an atom jumps from a lower energy level to a higher one, it absorbs a photon whose energy exactly matches the energy gap. When it falls back to a lower level, it emits a photon of that same energy.

Ephoton = Eupper - Elower = h · f = h · c / λ

Absorption spectrum: Dark lines on a continuous spectrum (photons removed at specific wavelengths).

Emission spectrum: Bright lines on a dark background (photons emitted at specific wavelengths).

Each element has a unique set of energy levels and therefore a unique spectral fingerprint. This is the basis of spectroscopy, used to identify elements in distant stars, in laboratory samples, and in medical diagnostics.

Excited states: When an electron is in any energy level above the ground state (n > 1 for hydrogen), it is in an excited state. Excited states are unstable; the electron will spontaneously fall to a lower level, emitting a photon. The ground state (lowest energy) is stable.

Photoelectric Effect

Light striking a metal surface can eject electrons if the photon energy exceeds the work function:

Ephoton = hf = W + KEmax KEmax = hf - W

Where W = work function (minimum energy to eject electron).

Threshold frequency: f0 = W/h. Below this, no electrons are ejected regardless of intensity.

Analogy: Imagine the work function as a wall the electron must jump over. Each photon is like a single push. A stronger push (higher frequency) gets the electron over the wall with more speed. But 1000 weak pushes (high intensity, low frequency) cannot replace one strong push — the electron cannot "save up" energy from multiple photons.

Key observations:

  • Increasing intensity increases the NUMBER of ejected electrons (more photons), not their max KE.
  • Increasing frequency increases KE of ejected electrons.

Radioactive Decay

Α decay: Emits He-4 nucleus (2p + 2n). Z decreases by 2, A decreases by 4.

Β-minus decay: Neutron becomes proton + electron + antineutrino. Z increases by 1, A unchanged.

Β-plus decay: Proton becomes neutron + positron + neutrino. Z decreases by 1, A unchanged.

Γ decay: Nucleus emits high-energy photon. No change in Z or A.

Activity (decay rate): A = A0 · (1/2)^(t/thalf). Also A = λ·N where λ = ln(2)/thalf.

Decay constant (λ):

λ = ln(2) / thalf = 0.693 / thalf

The decay constant gives the probability per unit time that any individual nucleus will decay.

Exponential form of the decay law:

N(t) = N0 · e^(-λ·t) A(t) = A0 · e^(-λ·t)

This is equivalent to the half-life form but more useful for calculus-based problems.

Relationship between forms:

  • (1/2)^(t/thalf) = e^(-λ·t) -- these are the same, just different notation.
  • After one half-life: N = N0/2.
  • After time 1/λ (the mean lifetime τ): N = N0/e = 0.368·N0.

Units of activity:

  • 1 becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay per second
  • 1 curie (Ci) = 3.7 x 1010 Bq

Worked Example 1: Half-life = 6 hours, starting with 800 g. After 18 hours?

  • Number of half-lives: 18/6 = 3
  • Remaining: 800 · (1/2)3 = 100 g

Worked Example 2: A sample has activity 1200 Bq. After 20 minutes, activity is 150 Bq. Find half-life.

  • 150 = 1200 · (1/2)^(20/thalf)
  • 1/8 = (1/2)^(20/thalf)
  • (1/2)3 = (1/2)^(20/thalf)
  • 3 = 20/thalf ⇒ thalf = 20/3 = 6.67 minutes

Worked Example 3: A radioactive isotope has a half-life of 10 days and an initial activity of 5000 Bq. What is the decay constant, and what is the activity after 25 days?

  • λ = 0.693/10 = 0.0693 per day
  • A = 5000 · e^(-0.0693 x 25) = 5000 · e^(-1.733) = 5000 · 0.177 = 884 Bq
  • Alternatively: 25/10 = 2.5 half-lives, so A = 5000 · (1/2)2.5 = 5000/5.66 = 884 Bq

Worked Example 4: If a sample contains 1020 atoms of a radioactive isotope with thalf = 5 years, what is the activity?

  • λ = 0.693/(5 x 365.25 x 24 x 3600) = 0.693/(1.578 x 108) = 4.39 x 10-9 per second
  • A = λ · N = 4.39 x 10-9 x 1020 = 4.39 x 1011 Bq = 439 GBq

Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = Δm · c2

1 atomic mass unit (u) = 931.5 MeV/c2.

Nuclear Binding Energy

The mass of a nucleus is always LESS than the sum of the masses of its individual protons and neutrons. This difference is called the mass defect (Δm):

Δm = (Z · mproton + N · mneutron) - mnucleus

The "missing" mass has been converted into energy that holds the nucleus together. This energy is the nuclear binding energy:

Ebinding = Δm · c2

Binding energy per nucleon (Ebinding / A) indicates how tightly the nucleus is held together. Higher values mean more stable nuclei.

NuclideBinding energy per nucleon (approximate)
2 H (deuterium)~1.1 MeV
4 He (helium-4)~7.1 MeV
56 Fe (iron-56)~8.8 MeV (most stable)
235 U (uranium-235)~7.6 MeV

Key insight: Iron-56 sits at the peak of the binding energy curve. Nuclei lighter than iron can release energy by fusion (combining to climb the curve). Nuclei heavier than iron can release energy by fission (splitting to climb back toward the peak). This explains why both fusion and fission release energy.

Worked Example: Helium-4 has mass 4.0026 u. Two protons (2 x 1.0073 u) and two neutrons (2 x 1.0087 u) have combined mass 4.0320 u.

  • Δm = 4.0320 - 4.0026 = 0.0294 u
  • Ebinding = 0.0294 x 931.5 = 27.4 MeV
  • Per nucleon: 27.4 / 4 = 6.85 MeV/nucleon

Nuclear Reactions

Fission: Heavy nucleus splits into lighter ones. Releases energy. Used in reactors and bombs.

Fusion: Light nuclei combine into heavier ones. Releases even more energy per unit mass. Powers the Sun. Requires extreme temperatures (millions of kelvin) to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged nuclei.

Neutron Radiation

Neutrons are uncharged particles, so they are not deflected by electric or magnetic fields and do not ionize atoms directly. Instead, they interact with nuclei through nuclear collisions:

  • Fast neutrons (high energy) can knock protons out of hydrogen-containing materials, causing indirect ionization.
  • Thermal (slow) neutrons are readily captured by nuclei, triggering nuclear reactions (including fission of 235 U).
  • Neutrons are very penetrating because they have no charge and are not stopped by the electron clouds of atoms.

Shielding: Materials rich in hydrogen (water, paraffin, concrete) are effective at slowing neutrons because hydrogen nuclei have nearly the same mass as neutrons, so energy transfer in collisions is maximized.

Neutron sources: nuclear reactors, radioactive sources (e.g., californium-252), and particle accelerators.

Principle of a Nuclear Reactor

A nuclear reactor harnesses the energy from controlled nuclear fission. The key components:

1. Nuclear fuel: Typically enriched uranium (235 U) or plutonium (239 Pu). When 235 U absorbs a thermal neutron, it splits into two lighter nuclei plus 2-3 additional neutrons and approximately 200 MeV of energy per fission.

2. Moderator: Slows fast neutrons down to thermal speeds so they can be captured by more 235 U nuclei and sustain the chain reaction. Common moderators: water (H2O), heavy water (D2O), graphite. The moderator works because neutrons lose the most energy when they collide with particles of similar mass (hydrogen).

3. Control rods: Made of neutron-absorbing material (boron or cadmium). Inserting control rods absorbs neutrons and slows the reaction. Withdrawing them allows more neutrons to reach fuel and speeds it up. This is how the reaction rate is controlled.

4. Coolant: Carries thermal energy away from the reactor core to produce steam, which drives turbines for electricity. Often the same water that serves as moderator.

Chain reaction: Each fission event releases 2-3 neutrons. If exactly one of those neutrons triggers another fission, the reaction is self-sustaining (critical). If more than one triggers fission, the reaction accelerates (supercritical). If fewer than one, it dies out (subcritical). The criticality factor (k) equals the ratio of neutrons in one generation to the previous:

  • k = 1: critical (steady power output)
  • k > 1: supercritical (power increasing)
  • k < 1: subcritical (power decreasing)

Conservation Laws in Nuclear Reactions

  • Total charge (Z) is conserved
  • Total mass number (A) is conserved
  • Total energy is conserved (including mass-energy)
  • Total lepton number is conserved
  • Total baryon number is conserved

The Geiger-Mueller Counter

A Geiger-Mueller (GM) tube is a gas-filled detector used to measure ionizing radiation. It consists of a sealed cylindrical tube containing a low-pressure gas mixture (typically argon with a small amount of a halogen quench gas such as bromine or chlorine). A thin central wire serves as the anode, held at a high positive voltage of approximately 400-600 V relative to the outer cylindrical cathode.

Operating principle: When an α particle, β particle, or γ photon enters the tube (through a thin mica window for α/β, or directly through the wall for γ), it ionizes gas atoms along its path. The freed electrons accelerate toward the anode in the strong electric field, gaining enough energy to ionize additional atoms. This cascade, called a Townsend avalanche, produces a brief, intense discharge that registers as a voltage pulse in the external circuit.

Key characteristics:

  • Each detected event produces one pulse of similar size, regardless of the type or energy of the incoming radiation.
  • The GM counter therefore counts events but cannot distinguish particle type or measure energy (unlike scintillation or semiconductor detectors).
  • After each discharge, the quench gas absorbs UV photons that would otherwise restart the avalanche, ensuring a clean reset. The dead time (typically 100-200 microseconds) is the brief interval after each pulse during which the tube cannot detect another event.
  • GM counters detect α, β, and γ radiation, though α detection requires a very thin entrance window since α particles have limited penetrating power.

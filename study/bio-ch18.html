Chapter 18

DNA Technology

Modern DNA technology has revolutionized biology and medicine. From diagnosing genetic diseases to solving crimes to engineering new therapies, these tools are essential knowledge for any medical student.

18.1 DNA Isolation

Before any DNA technology can be applied, DNA must first be extracted from cells. The standard isolation protocol involves four steps:

  1. Cell lysis: Detergents and/or mechanical disruption break open cell membranes and nuclear envelopes, releasing cellular contents.
  2. Protein removal: Enzymes such as proteinase K digest proteins, or phenol-chloroform extraction separates proteins from nucleic acids.
  3. DNA precipitation: Adding cold ethanol or isopropanol makes DNA insoluble, causing it to form visible strands that can be spooled out of solution.
  4. Resuspension: The purified DNA is dissolved in sterile water or buffer, ready for downstream applications such as PCR or sequencing.

18.2 PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

PCR is a technique that amplifies (makes millions of copies of) a specific DNA segment in vitro (in a test tube). It was developed by Kary Mullis in 1983 and earned him the Nobel Prize.

What you need for PCR:

  • Template DNA: The DNA sample containing the target sequence.
  • Two primers: Short, synthetic, single-stranded DNA sequences (approximately 18-25 nucleotides) that are complementary to the flanking regions of the target. One primer binds to each strand.
  • Taq polymerase: A heat-stable DNA polymerase isolated from the thermophilic bacterium Thermus aquaticus. It survives the high temperatures used in PCR.
  • Deoxynucleotides (dNTPs): The building blocks (A, T, G, C) for synthesizing new DNA strands.

The three steps of each PCR cycle:

  1. Denaturation (~94-95°C): The double-stranded DNA is heated to separate (denature) into two single strands.
  2. Annealing (~50-65°C): The temperature is lowered to allow the primers to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the template strands.
  3. Extension (~72°C): Taq polymerase synthesizes new DNA strands by adding nucleotides to the primers, extending them along the template.

Each cycle doubles the amount of target DNA. After n cycles, there are approximately 2ⁿ copies. Typically 25-35 cycles are run, yielding billions of copies from a single starting molecule.

18.3 DNA Sequencing (Sanger Method Basics)

DNA sequencing determines the exact order of nucleotides in a DNA molecule. The Sanger method (chain-termination method) was the gold standard for decades:

  1. The target DNA is mixed with a primer, DNA polymerase, normal dNTPs, and small amounts of dideoxyNTPs (ddNTPs). Each ddNTP is labeled with a different fluorescent dye (one color per base).
  2. ddNTPs lack the 3'-OH group needed for the next nucleotide to be added. When a ddNTP is incorporated, the growing chain terminates.
  3. This produces DNA fragments of every possible length, each ending with a fluorescently labeled ddNTP.
  4. The fragments are separated by size using capillary electrophoresis. A laser reads the fluorescent labels as fragments pass a detector, revealing the sequence.

18.4 Restriction Enzymes and Restriction Digestion

Restriction enzymes (restriction endonucleases) are bacterial enzymes that cut DNA at specific recognition sequences (typically 4-8 base pairs long, palindromic). They are named after the bacteria they were isolated from (e.g., EcoRI from E. coli).

Restriction enzymes can produce:

  • Sticky ends: Staggered cuts that leave short single-stranded overhangs. These can base-pair with complementary sticky ends from another DNA fragment - extremely useful for cloning.
  • Blunt ends: Straight cuts that leave no overhangs.

Gel Electrophoresis in Detail

DNA fragments produced by restriction digestion (or other methods) are separated by gel electrophoresis. The gel (made of agarose or polyacrylamide) acts as a molecular sieve with tiny pores. Because DNA carries a negative charge (from its phosphate backbone), it migrates toward the positive electrode (anode) when an electric field is applied. Smaller fragments thread through the pores more easily and travel farther, so fragment size is inversely proportional to migration distance. After separation, fragments are visualized using fluorescent dyes such as ethidium bromide or SYBR Green, which intercalate into the DNA helix and glow under UV light.

RFLP Analysis for Mutation Detection

Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism (RFLP) analysis takes advantage of the fact that point mutations can create or destroy a restriction enzyme recognition site. If a mutation abolishes a site, the enzyme cannot cut at that position, producing one large fragment instead of two smaller ones. Conversely, a new mutation may create an additional site, generating extra smaller fragments. By comparing the banding patterns of digested normal and mutant DNA on a gel, geneticists can detect specific mutations - a technique historically important for diagnosing conditions such as sickle cell disease.

18.5 Reverse Transcription

Reverse transcriptase is an enzyme (originally from retroviruses) that synthesizes DNA from an RNA template. This produces complementary DNA (cDNA), which represents only the expressed genes (since it is made from mRNA, which has already been spliced to remove introns).

cDNA is useful because it can be cloned or amplified by PCR (using RT-PCR) to study gene expression.

18.7 Gene Cloning

Gene cloning involves inserting a gene of interest into a vector (such as a plasmid) and introducing the recombinant molecule into a host cell (usually bacteria) where it is replicated.

Steps in gene cloning:

  1. Cut both the gene of interest and the vector with the same restriction enzyme to produce compatible sticky ends.
  2. Mix the fragments together and use DNA ligase to join them, creating recombinant DNA.
  3. Introduce the recombinant vector into bacteria (transformation).
  4. Select for bacteria containing the recombinant vector (using antibiotic resistance markers on the plasmid).
  5. Grow the bacteria to produce many copies of the gene (cloning).

18.8 Gene Therapy

Gene therapy aims to treat or cure genetic disorders by introducing functional copies of genes into patient cells to replace defective ones.

  • Somatic gene therapy: Corrects genes in body (somatic) cells. Changes are not passed to offspring. This is the current focus of clinical gene therapy.
  • Germline gene therapy: Would correct genes in reproductive cells (eggs or sperm), meaning changes would be inherited. This is technically possible but ethically controversial and not currently permitted in clinical practice.

Common delivery methods include viral vectors (modified viruses that deliver therapeutic genes) and non-viral methods (electroporation, lipid nanoparticles).

18.9 Recombinant Protein Production

Recombinant DNA technology enables bacteria or yeast to manufacture medically important human proteins on an industrial scale. Notable examples include:

  • Recombinant Factor VIII for treating hemophilia A, replacing older plasma-derived products that carried a risk of viral contamination.
  • Recombinant human insulin for diabetes management, replacing porcine and bovine insulin preparations that occasionally caused immune reactions.
  • Recombinant human growth hormone for growth disorders, replacing cadaver-derived pituitary hormone that carried a risk of prion disease (Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease).

18.10 Cell Differentiation and Gene Expression

Every somatic cell in an individual carries the same complete genome, yet cells differ enormously in structure and function. This diversity arises because different cell types express different subsets of genes - a process called differential gene expression. A liver cell activates genes for albumin and detoxification enzymes while keeping muscle-specific genes silent; a muscle cell does the opposite, expressing actin and myosin genes. Transcription factors, epigenetic modifications (such as DNA methylation and histone modification), and cell-signaling pathways orchestrate which genes are turned on or off in each cell type.

18.11 Reproductive vs. Therapeutic Cloning

Reproductive cloning aims to produce a genetically identical copy of an entire organism. The technique - somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) - involves transferring the nucleus from a donor's somatic cell into an enucleated egg cell, then implanting the resulting embryo into a surrogate mother. The most famous example is Dolly the sheep (born 1996), the first mammal cloned from an adult somatic cell.

Therapeutic cloning uses the same nuclear transfer technique, but instead of implanting the embryo, it is cultured to the blastocyst stage and used to derive embryonic stem cells. These stem cells can potentially be directed to develop into replacement tissues genetically matched to the donor, avoiding immune rejection. Therapeutic cloning remains ethically controversial and is legally restricted in most countries.

18.12 The Ames Test for Mutagenicity

The Ames test is a widely used laboratory assay that screens chemicals for mutagenic potential. It employs specially engineered strains of Salmonella typhimurium that carry mutations preventing them from synthesizing the amino acid histidine (His⁻ mutants). These bacteria are exposed to the test chemical and plated on media lacking histidine. If the chemical induces reverse mutations that restore the ability to synthesize histidine, bacterial colonies will grow on the histidine-free plates, indicating that the substance is mutagenic. The test is rapid, inexpensive, and has strong predictive value for carcinogenicity, since the majority of known carcinogens are also mutagens.

KEY POINTS - Chapter 18
  • PCR amplifies specific DNA segments using denaturation, annealing, and extension cycles.
  • Sanger sequencing uses ddNTPs that terminate the growing chain at each base position.
  • Restriction enzymes cut DNA at palindromic sequences, producing sticky or blunt ends.
  • Reverse transcriptase converts RNA to cDNA.
  • Southern (DNA), Northern (RNA), Western (Protein) blots detect specific molecules.
  • Gene cloning uses vectors, restriction enzymes, and ligase to produce recombinant DNA.
  • Gene therapy introduces functional genes into patient cells; somatic therapy is the current clinical approach.

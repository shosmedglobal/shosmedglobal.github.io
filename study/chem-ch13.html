Chapter 13

Acids and Bases

13.1 Arrhenius Theory

Svante Arrhenius defined acids and bases based on what they produce in water:

  • Arrhenius acid: Produces H⁺ ions (protons) in aqueous solution. Example: HCl → H⁺ + Cl−
  • Arrhenius base: Produces OH− ions in aqueous solution. Example: NaOH → Na⁺ + OH−

This definition is limited to water-based (aqueous) solutions.

13.2 Bronsted-Lowry Theory

The Bronsted-Lowry model is broader:

  • Bronsted-Lowry acid: A proton (H⁺) donor.
  • Bronsted-Lowry base: A proton (H⁺) acceptor.

This definition works in any solvent, not just water. When an acid donates a proton, it becomes its conjugate base. When a base accepts a proton, it becomes its conjugate acid.

HCl + H₂O ⇌ Cl− + H₃O⁺

Here, HCl is the acid (donates H⁺), H₂O is the base (accepts H⁺), Cl− is the conjugate base, and H₃O⁺ is the conjugate acid.

13.3 Autoprotolysis of Water

Water can act simultaneously as both an acid and a base in a process called autoprotolysis (or self-ionization):

H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH−

The equilibrium constant for this reaction is the ionic product of water (Kw):

Kw = [H₃O⁺][OH−] = 1.0 × 10−14 at 25 °C

In pure water, [H₃O⁺] = [OH−] = 10−7 mol/L, giving a neutral pH of 7. This value is temperature-dependent; at body temperature (37 °C), Kw is slightly larger, so neutral pH is approximately 6.8.

13.5 Electrolytes

An electrolyte is a substance that dissociates into ions in water and conducts electricity. Strong acids, strong bases, and soluble salts are strong electrolytes. Weak acids and bases are weak electrolytes. Sugar and most organic molecules are nonelectrolytes (they dissolve but don't form ions).

13.6 Salts and Their Reactions

A salt results from an acid-base neutralization reaction:

acid + base → salt + water

The nature of the salt solution depends on the parent acid and base:

  • Strong acid + strong base → neutral salt (pH 7). Example: NaCl
  • Strong acid + weak base → acidic salt (pH < 7). Example: NH₄Cl
  • Weak acid + strong base → basic salt (pH > 7). Example: CH₃COONa

13.7 Inorganic Nomenclature

IUPAC rules for naming inorganic compounds follow a systematic pattern. The positive part (cation) is named first, followed by the negative part (anion).

Naming Ions

  • Monoatomic cations: element name + "ion." When multiple oxidation states exist, a Roman numeral in parentheses indicates the charge — Fe2+ = iron(II) ion, Fe3+ = iron(III) ion.
  • Monoatomic anions: root of element name + suffix "-ide" — Cl− = chloride, O2− = oxide, S2− = sulfide, H− = hydride.
  • Oxoanions use "-ate" for the higher oxidation state and "-ite" for the lower: SO₄2− = sulfate, SO₃2− = sulfite; NO₃− = nitrate, NO₂− = nitrite.
  • When four oxidation states exist, prefixes are added: hypo-...-ite (lowest), -ite, -ate, per-...-ate (highest). The chlorine series illustrates this: ClO− (hypochlorite), ClO₂− (chlorite), ClO₃− (chlorate), ClO₄− (perchlorate).

Naming Acids from Oxoanions

The corresponding acids follow the same pattern with "-ous acid" and "-ic acid" endings: HClO = hypochlorous acid, HClO₂ = chlorous acid, HClO₃ = chloric acid, HClO₄ = perchloric acid. Binary acids (no oxygen) are named as "hydro-...-ic acid" in aqueous solution: HCl(aq) = hydrochloric acid.

Binary Covalent Compounds

Compounds between two nonmetals use Greek prefixes to indicate atom counts: CO = carbon monoxide, CO₂ = carbon dioxide, N₂O = dinitrogen monoxide, P₂O₅ = diphosphorus pentoxide. The prefix "mono-" is omitted for the first element.

13.8 Hydrolysis of Salts

When salts dissolve in water, ions derived from weak acids or weak bases can react with water, shifting the pH away from neutral. This reaction is called hydrolysis.

  • Anion hydrolysis (from a weak acid): The anion acts as a base, accepting a proton from water. For example, sodium acetate solution is slightly alkaline because: CH₃COO− + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH−
  • Cation hydrolysis (from a weak base): The cation acts as an acid, donating a proton to water. For example, ammonium chloride solution is slightly acidic because: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺
  • Transition metal cation hydrolysis: Hydrated transition metal cations like [Fe(H₂O)₆]3+ are Brønsted acids that release protons from their coordinated water molecules: [Fe(H₂O)₆]3+ + H₂O ⇌ [Fe(H₂O)₅(OH)]2+ + H₃O⁺. This is why FeCl₃ solutions are noticeably acidic.

Salts of strong acids and strong bases (e.g., NaCl, KNO₃) do not hydrolyse, and their solutions remain neutral at pH 7. For salts formed from a weak acid and a weak base, the resulting pH depends on the relative strengths (Kₐ vs Kb values) of the parent acid and base.

Mnemonic: Bronsted-Lowry Acids and Bases

"Acids are Proton Pitchers; Bases are Proton Catchers" — Acids throw (donate) protons; bases catch (accept) them.

Key Points — Chapter 13

  • Arrhenius: acids produce H⁺; bases produce OH− in water.
  • Bronsted-Lowry: acids are proton donors; bases are proton acceptors.
  • Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one proton.
  • Strong acids/bases fully dissociate; weak ones partially dissociate.
  • Electrolytes dissociate into ions and conduct electricity in solution.
  • Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water.
  • Salt acidity/basicity depends on the strength of the parent acid and base.
  • Oxoanion naming: -ite (lower oxidation state), -ate (higher); hypo-/-per- prefixes extend the series.
  • Hydrolysis: anions of weak acids make solutions basic; cations of weak bases make solutions acidic.
  • Solubility: all nitrates soluble; most chlorides and sulfates soluble; most carbonates, phosphates, and hydroxides insoluble (except Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺ salts).

Test Yourself — Chapter 13

1. Identify the conjugate acid-base pairs in: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH−
Show Answer
Pair 1: NH₃ (base) and NH₄⁺ (conjugate acid). Pair 2: H₂O (acid) and OH− (conjugate base). NH₃ accepts a proton to become NH₄⁺; H₂O donates a proton to become OH−.
2. Will a solution of sodium acetate (CH₃COONa) be acidic, basic, or neutral?
Show Answer
Basic. Sodium acetate comes from a weak acid (acetic acid) and a strong base (NaOH). The acetate ion acts as a base in water, accepting protons and producing OH−, making the solution slightly basic.
3. Why is HNO₃ classified as a strong acid?
Show Answer
HNO₃ completely dissociates in water: HNO₃ → H⁺ + NO₃−. There is essentially no reverse reaction. Every molecule of HNO₃ produces an H⁺ ion, making it a strong acid.

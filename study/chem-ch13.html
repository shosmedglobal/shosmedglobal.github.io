13.1 Arrhenius Theory

Svante Arrhenius defined acids and bases based on what they produce in water:

  • Arrhenius acid: Produces H‚Āļ ions (protons) in aqueous solution. Example: HCl ‚Üí H‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ
  • Arrhenius base: Produces OH‚ĀĽ ions in aqueous solution. Example: NaOH ‚Üí Na‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ

This definition is limited to water-based (aqueous) solutions.

13.2 Bronsted-Lowry Theory

The Bronsted-Lowry model is broader:

  • Bronsted-Lowry acid: A proton (H‚Āļ) donor.
  • Bronsted-Lowry base: A proton (H‚Āļ) acceptor.

This definition works in any solvent, not just water. When an acid donates a proton, it becomes its conjugate base. When a base accepts a proton, it becomes its conjugate acid.

HCl + H‚āāO ⇌ Cl‚ĀĽ + H‚āÉO‚Āļ

Here, HCl is the acid (donates H‚Āļ), H‚āāO is the base (accepts H‚Āļ), Cl‚ĀĽ is the conjugate base, and H‚āÉO‚Āļ is the conjugate acid.

13.3 Autoprotolysis of Water

Water can act simultaneously as both an acid and a base in a process called autoprotolysis (or self-ionization):

H‚āāO + H‚āāO ⇌ H‚āÉO‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ

The equilibrium constant for this reaction is the ionic product of water (Kw):

Kw = [H‚āÉO‚Āļ][OH‚ĀĽ] = 1.0 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Āī at 25 ¬įC

In pure water, [H‚āÉO‚Āļ] = [OH‚ĀĽ] = 10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∑ mol/L, giving a neutral pH of 7. This value is temperature-dependent; at body temperature (37 ¬įC), Kw is slightly larger, so neutral pH is approximately 6.8.

13.5 Electrolytes

An electrolyte is a substance that dissociates into ions in water and conducts electricity. Strong acids, strong bases, and soluble salts are strong electrolytes. Weak acids and bases are weak electrolytes. Sugar and most organic molecules are nonelectrolytes (they dissolve but don't form ions).

13.6 Salts and Their Reactions

A salt results from an acid-base neutralization reaction:

acid + base ‚Üí salt + water

The nature of the salt solution depends on the parent acid and base:

  • Strong acid + strong base ‚Üí neutral salt (pH 7). Example: NaCl
  • Strong acid + weak base ‚Üí acidic salt (pH < 7). Example: NH‚āĄCl
  • Weak acid + strong base ‚Üí basic salt (pH > 7). Example: CH‚āÉCOONa

13.7 Inorganic Nomenclature

IUPAC rules for naming inorganic compounds follow a systematic pattern. The positive part (cation) is named first, followed by the negative part (anion).

Naming Ions

  • Monoatomic cations: element name + "ion." When multiple oxidation states exist, a Roman numeral in parentheses indicates the charge - Fe¬≤‚Āļ = iron(II) ion, Fe¬≥‚Āļ = iron(III) ion.
  • Monoatomic anions: root of element name + suffix "-ide" - Cl‚ĀĽ = chloride, O¬≤‚ąí = oxide, S¬≤‚ąí = sulfide, H‚ĀĽ = hydride.
  • Oxoanions use "-ate" for the higher oxidation state and "-ite" for the lower: SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ąí = sulfate, SO‚āÉ¬≤‚ąí = sulfite; NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ = nitrate, NO‚āā‚ĀĽ = nitrite.
  • When four oxidation states exist, prefixes are added: hypo-...-ite (lowest), -ite, -ate, per-...-ate (highest). The chlorine series illustrates this: ClO‚ĀĽ (hypochlorite), ClO‚āā‚ĀĽ (chlorite), ClO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ (chlorate), ClO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ (perchlorate).

Naming Acids from Oxoanions

The corresponding acids follow the same pattern with "-ous acid" and "-ic acid" endings: HClO = hypochlorous acid, HClO‚āā = chlorous acid, HClO‚āÉ = chloric acid, HClO‚āĄ = perchloric acid. Binary acids (no oxygen) are named as "hydro-...-ic acid" in aqueous solution: HCl(aq) = hydrochloric acid.

Binary Covalent Compounds

Compounds between two nonmetals use Greek prefixes to indicate atom counts: CO = carbon monoxide, CO‚āā = carbon dioxide, N‚āāO = dinitrogen monoxide, P‚āāO‚āÖ = diphosphorus pentoxide. The prefix "mono-" is omitted for the first element.

13.8 Hydrolysis of Salts

When salts dissolve in water, ions derived from weak acids or weak bases can react with water, shifting the pH away from neutral. This reaction is called hydrolysis.

  • Anion hydrolysis (from a weak acid): The anion acts as a base, accepting a proton from water. For example, sodium acetate solution is slightly alkaline because: CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ + H‚āāO ⇌ CH‚āÉCOOH + OH‚ĀĽ
  • Cation hydrolysis (from a weak base): The cation acts as an acid, donating a proton to water. For example, ammonium chloride solution is slightly acidic because: NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + H‚āāO ⇌ NH‚āÉ + H‚āÉO‚Āļ
  • Transition metal cation hydrolysis: Hydrated transition metal cations like [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ are Brønsted acids that release protons from their coordinated water molecules: [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ + H‚āāO ⇌ [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÖ(OH)]¬≤‚Āļ + H‚āÉO‚Āļ. This is why FeCl‚āÉ solutions are noticeably acidic.

Salts of strong acids and strong bases (e.g., NaCl, KNO‚āÉ) do not hydrolyse, and their solutions remain neutral at pH 7. For salts formed from a weak acid and a weak base, the resulting pH depends on the relative strengths (K‚āź vs Kb values) of the parent acid and base.

Common Exam Examples of Salt Hydrolysis

Na‚āāCO‚āÉ (sodium carbonate) produces a basic solution because CO‚āÉ¬≤‚ĀĽ is the conjugate base of the weak acid H‚āāCO‚āÉ and readily accepts protons from water. Similarly, Na‚āāSO‚āÉ (sodium sulfite) yields a basic solution because SO‚āÉ¬≤‚ĀĽ derives from the weak acid H‚āāSO‚āÉ. In contrast, NH‚āĄCl (ammonium chloride) gives an acidic solution because NH‚āĄ‚Āļ is the conjugate acid of the weak base NH‚āÉ and donates protons to water. Finally, Na‚āāSO‚āĄ (sodium sulfate) produces a neutral solution because both Na‚Āļ and SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ originate from a strong base and a strong acid, respectively, so neither ion undergoes hydrolysis.

Mnemonic: Bronsted-Lowry Acids and Bases

"Acids are Proton Pitchers; Bases are Proton Catchers" - Acids throw (donate) protons; bases catch (accept) them.

Key Points - Chapter 13

  • Arrhenius: acids produce H‚Āļ; bases produce OH‚ĀĽ in water.
  • Bronsted-Lowry: acids are proton donors; bases are proton acceptors.
  • Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one proton.
  • Strong acids/bases fully dissociate; weak ones partially dissociate.
  • Electrolytes dissociate into ions and conduct electricity in solution.
  • Neutralization: acid + base ‚Üí salt + water.
  • Salt acidity/basicity depends on the strength of the parent acid and base.
  • Oxoanion naming: -ite (lower oxidation state), -ate (higher); hypo-/-per- prefixes extend the series.
  • Hydrolysis: anions of weak acids make solutions basic; cations of weak bases make solutions acidic.
  • Solubility: all nitrates soluble; most chlorides and sulfates soluble; most carbonates, phosphates, and hydroxides insoluble (except Na‚Āļ, K‚Āļ, NH‚āĄ‚Āļ salts).

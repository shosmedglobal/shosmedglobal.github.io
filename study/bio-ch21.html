Chapter 21

Individual Development in Humans (Embryology)

Humans as Deuterostomes and Triblastic Organisms

Every animal falls into one of two major groups based on how its embryo develops. In protostomes (like insects and worms), the first opening that forms in the embryo becomes the mouth. In deuterostomes (like humans, other vertebrates, and starfish), the first opening becomes the anus, and the mouth forms second. The word "deuterostome" literally means "second mouth."

These two groups also differ in how their body cavity forms. In protostomes, the coelom arises by splitting of solid mesodermal masses (schizocoely), and cleavage is spiral and determinate (each cell's fate is fixed early). In deuterostomes, the coelom forms from outpocketings of the gut wall (enterocoely), and cleavage is radial and indeterminate — meaning that early blastomeres retain the ability to form a complete organism if separated, which is why identical twins are possible.

Humans are also triblastic, meaning our embryos develop three distinct layers of cells called germ layers. These three layers — ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm — give rise to every tissue and organ in the body. Simpler animals like jellyfish only have two layers (they are diploblastic), while the most primitive animals like sponges have no true layers at all.

Types of Body Cavities (Coelom)

A body cavity is an internal, fluid-filled space that surrounds and protects organs. There are three arrangements found across the animal kingdom:

  • Acoelomate: No body cavity at all. Organs are packed tightly within solid tissue. Example: flatworms.
  • Pseudocoelomate: A body cavity exists but it is only partially lined by mesoderm. Example: roundworms.
  • Coelomate (Eucoelomate): A true body cavity completely lined by mesoderm. This is the human arrangement. The coelom allows organs to move, grow, and function independently from the body wall.

In humans, the coelom is divided into specific compartments: the thoracic cavity (housing the heart and lungs), the abdominal cavity (housing digestive organs), and the pelvic cavity (housing reproductive and excretory organs).

Vertebrate and Mammalian Characteristics

Humans belong to the phylum Chordata and the class Mammalia. At some point in development, all chordates share four key features:

  1. Notochord: A flexible, rod-like support structure. In humans, this is replaced by the vertebral column during development, though remnants persist as the soft centers (nucleus pulposus) of intervertebral discs.
  2. Dorsal hollow nerve cord: Becomes the brain and spinal cord.
  3. Pharyngeal slits: In fish these become gills; in humans they transform into structures of the ear and throat during embryonic development.
  4. Post-anal tail: Present briefly in the human embryo, then regresses. The coccyx (tailbone) is its remnant.

As mammals, humans also display: hair or fur, mammary glands that produce milk, a diaphragm for breathing, three middle ear bones (malleus, incus, stapes), warm-bloodedness (endothermy), and a four-chambered heart. As placental mammals, humans nourish the developing embryo internally through a placenta.

Genetic Bases of Development

Hox Genes

Hox genes are a family of master-control genes that determine which body parts form at which locations along the head-to-tail axis. Think of them as an address system: they tell cells "you are in the head region, so build a brain" or "you are in the trunk region, so build ribs." A remarkable fact is that Hox genes are found in nearly all animals, from fruit flies to humans, and they are arranged on chromosomes in the same order as the body segments they control (this is called collinearity).

Humans have 39 Hox genes organized into four clusters (HOXA, HOXB, HOXC, HOXD) on four different chromosomes. Mutations in Hox genes can cause dramatic developmental abnormalities, such as limbs forming in the wrong position.

Morphogen Gradients

A morphogen is a signaling molecule that spreads through developing tissue and forms a concentration gradient — high concentration near the source, decreasing with distance. Cells read the local concentration of the morphogen and respond by activating different genes. This is how a uniform ball of cells can become a complex, patterned organism.

For example, the protein Sonic Hedgehog (Shh) forms a gradient that helps pattern the limbs (determining which side is the thumb and which is the pinky) and the nervous system (determining which cells become motor neurons vs. other neuron types).

Maternal Cytoplasmic Determinants

Before the embryonic genome activates (around the 4–8 cell stage in humans), early development is controlled by maternal determinants — proteins and mRNA molecules deposited in the egg during oogenesis. These cytoplasmic determinants are asymmetrically distributed, so when cleavage divides the egg, different daughter cells inherit different sets of determinants, initiating distinct developmental fates from the very first divisions.

Developmental Gene Hierarchy

Pattern formation in embryos follows a cascade of regulatory genes, best characterized in Drosophila: egg-polarity genes (such as bicoid) establish the anterior-posterior axis; gap genes divide the embryo into broad regions; pair-rule genes define repeating parasegment units; segment-polarity genes refine individual segment boundaries; and finally homeotic (Hox) genes assign a unique identity to each segment. Humans use homologous regulatory systems, though the specific details differ from those in insects.

Ontogenesis: An Overview of Human Development

Ontogenesis (also called ontogeny) refers to the entire developmental journey of an individual from fertilization to death. The early stages proceed as follows:

  1. Fertilization: A sperm cell fuses with an egg cell (oocyte) in the uterine tube, forming a single-celled zygote with 46 chromosomes.
  2. Cleavage: The zygote undergoes rapid mitotic divisions without growing in overall size. Human cleavage is holoblastic (the entire egg divides) because the human egg contains very little yolk. In contrast, birds and reptiles undergo meroblastic cleavage, where only a small disc of cytoplasm divides while the massive yolk remains undivided. After several divisions, the human embryo becomes a solid ball of cells called a morula (Latin for "mulberry," which it resembles).
  3. Blastulation: The morula develops a hollow, fluid-filled cavity, becoming a blastocyst. The blastocyst has an outer layer (trophoblast, which will help form the placenta) and an inner cell mass (which will become the embryo).
  4. Implantation: Around day 6-7, the blastocyst embeds itself into the uterine wall (endometrium).
  5. Gastrulation: Around week 3, cells rearrange dramatically to form three germ layers. This is arguably the most important event in early development. The rearrangement involves three characteristic morphogenetic movements: invagination (an epithelial sheet folds inward to form a pocket), convergent extension (cells rearrange to narrow and lengthen a tissue), and migration (individual cells detach and travel to new locations). These movements are powered by cytoskeletal rearrangements and changes in cell adhesion molecules.
  6. Neurulation: The ectoderm folds to create the neural tube, which will become the brain and spinal cord. As the neural tube closes, neural crest cells — a transient, multipotent population — migrate from its edges to diverse destinations throughout the embryo. Neural crest cells give rise to peripheral neurons and glia (including Schwann cells), adrenal medulla chromaffin cells, melanocytes, craniofacial cartilage and bone, and dentine of teeth.
  7. Organogenesis: The three germ layers differentiate into all major organ systems over weeks 3-8. By the end of week 8, the developing human transitions from being called an "embryo" to a "fetus."

Extraembryonic Membranes

Four membranes develop outside the embryo proper but are essential for its survival in amniotes (reptiles, birds, mammals):

  • Amnion: Surrounds the embryo and encloses it in protective amniotic fluid, cushioning against mechanical shock and preventing desiccation.
  • Chorion: The outermost membrane; facilitates gas exchange and, in mammals, contributes to the formation of the placenta.
  • Allantois: Stores metabolic waste products and participates in gas exchange. In humans, its blood vessels become the umbilical vessels.
  • Yolk sac: Provides early nutrition and is the initial site of blood cell formation (hematopoiesis) in the embryo.

The Three Germ Layers

After gastrulation, the embryo has three layers. Each gives rise to specific structures:

Germ LayerPositionStructures It Forms
Ectoderm Outermost layer Skin (epidermis), hair, nails, sweat glands, tooth enamel, the entire nervous system (brain, spinal cord, peripheral nerves), lens of the eye, inner ear, pituitary gland (anterior from oral ectoderm, posterior from neuroectoderm)
Mesoderm Middle layer Muscles (all types), bones, cartilage, connective tissue, circulatory system (heart, blood vessels, blood), kidneys, reproductive organs (gonads), dermis of skin, spleen, adrenal cortex
Endoderm Innermost layer Lining of the digestive tract, lining of the respiratory tract, liver, pancreas, thyroid gland (follicular epithelium), parathyroid glands, thymus, urinary bladder lining, lining of the proximal urethra, epithelium of the auditory (Eustachian) tube and tympanic cavity of the middle ear

Cell Differentiation

Differentiation is the process by which a generic, unspecialized cell becomes a specific cell type (such as a neuron, muscle cell, or red blood cell). Every cell in your body contains the same DNA, so differentiation happens not by changing which genes are present, but by changing which genes are turned on or off (gene expression).

Key concepts in differentiation:

  • Totipotent cells: Can become any cell type in the body PLUS the placenta. Only the zygote and cells of the very early embryo (up to about the 8-cell stage) are totipotent.
  • Pluripotent cells: Can become any cell type in the body but NOT the placenta. The inner cell mass of the blastocyst consists of pluripotent cells. These are what we call embryonic stem cells.
  • Multipotent cells: Can become several related cell types within one tissue family. For example, a blood stem cell can produce red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets, but cannot become a neuron.
  • Unipotent cells: Can only produce one cell type. For example, skin stem cells produce only new skin cells.

Differentiation is controlled by a combination of transcription factors (proteins that activate or silence genes), signaling molecules from neighboring cells, and epigenetic modifications (chemical tags on DNA or histones that affect gene activity without changing the DNA sequence).

Stem Cells: Types and Medical Applications

The potency hierarchy described above (totipotent, pluripotent, multipotent, unipotent) is a theoretical framework. In practice, two major categories of stem cells are used in medicine and research, and understanding their differences is essential.

Embryonic Stem Cells (ESCs)

Embryonic stem cells are isolated from the inner cell mass of the blastocyst (approximately 5 days post-fertilization). They are pluripotent, capable of differentiating into any cell type in the body (all three germ layers), though they cannot form extraembryonic tissues such as the placenta. This remarkable versatility makes ESCs enormously valuable for research and potentially for regenerative medicine. However, harvesting ESCs requires destruction of the embryo, which raises significant ethical concerns that have shaped legislation in many countries.

Adult (Somatic) Stem Cells

Adult stem cells persist in many tissues throughout the body, serving as an internal repair system that replaces cells lost to normal turnover, injury, or disease. They are typically multipotent, meaning they can give rise to several related cell types but are restricted to their tissue lineage. Key examples include:

  • Hematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow, which generate all blood and immune cell types — these are the basis of bone marrow transplantation, one of the longest-established and most successful stem cell therapies in medicine.
  • Mesenchymal stem cells in bone marrow, adipose tissue, and other connective tissues, which can differentiate into bone, cartilage, and fat cells.
  • Neural stem cells in specific brain regions (such as the subventricular zone and hippocampal dentate gyrus), which generate new neurons and glial cells.
  • Intestinal stem cells at the base of intestinal crypts, which continuously replenish the gut epithelium every 3–5 days.

Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs)

In 2006, Shinya Yamanaka and colleagues demonstrated that ordinary adult somatic cells (such as skin fibroblasts) could be reprogrammed back to a pluripotent state by introducing a defined set of four transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc). The resulting cells, called induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), behave similarly to embryonic stem cells in their ability to differentiate into virtually any cell type. Yamanaka shared the 2012 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine with John Gurdon for this breakthrough.

iPSCs offer two major advantages over ESCs: they sidestep the ethical controversy of embryo destruction, and they can be derived from a patient's own cells, meaning that tissues grown from iPSCs are genetically matched to the patient and face reduced risk of immune rejection. iPSC-based therapies are currently under investigation for conditions including macular degeneration, Parkinson's disease, heart failure, spinal cord injury, and type 1 diabetes. In 2014, the first clinical use of iPSC-derived cells occurred in Japan, where retinal pigment epithelial cells were transplanted into a patient with age-related macular degeneration.

Medically Important Helminths (Parasitic Worms)

Helminths are multicellular parasitic worms that infect hundreds of millions of people worldwide, particularly in tropical regions and areas with poor sanitation. They belong to two major phyla of medical importance: Platyhelminthes (flatworms) and Nematoda (roundworms). Helminth infections are a major focus of the WHO's Neglected Tropical Diseases program.

Flatworms (Platyhelminthes)

Recall from earlier in this chapter that flatworms are acoelomate organisms (no body cavity). Two classes are medically important:

Cestodes (tapeworms) are ribbon-like intestinal parasites. Taenia solium (the pork tapeworm) can reach 2–3 meters in the human intestine. Its body plan consists of a scolex (head with suckers and hooks for anchoring to the intestinal wall) and a long chain of flat segments called proglottids, each of which contains both male and female reproductive organs (tapeworms are hermaphroditic). Humans become infected by eating undercooked pork containing larval cysts (cysticerci). A more dangerous complication, neurocysticercosis, occurs when humans accidentally ingest T. solium eggs (rather than cysts): the hatched larvae migrate through the body and can form cysts in the brain, causing seizures and neurological damage. Neurocysticercosis is a leading cause of acquired epilepsy in many developing countries.

Trematodes (flukes) are leaf-shaped flatworms with complex life cycles typically involving a snail intermediate host. Schistosoma species cause schistosomiasis (bilharzia), one of the most significant parasitic diseases globally, affecting over 200 million people. Infective larvae (cercariae) are released from snails into freshwater and penetrate human skin on contact. Adult worms reside in blood vessels around the intestines or urinary bladder, and their eggs become trapped in tissues, provoking chronic granulomatous inflammation that can lead to liver fibrosis, portal hypertension, and bladder damage. Fasciola hepatica (the liver fluke) infects the bile ducts after ingestion of contaminated aquatic vegetation such as watercress.

Roundworms (Nematoda)

Roundworms are pseudocoelomate organisms with a cylindrical body plan. Several species are major human parasites:

  • Ascaris lumbricoides is the most common human intestinal roundworm, infecting an estimated 800 million people. Transmission is fecal-oral, through soil contaminated with eggs. Adults can grow up to 35 cm long in the small intestine. Heavy infections cause intestinal obstruction, particularly in children. Migrating larvae also pass through the lungs, causing transient pneumonitis (Loeffler syndrome).
  • Enterobius vermicularis (pinworm) is extremely common in children in temperate climates. Female worms migrate to the perianal region at night to deposit eggs, causing intense itching and enabling reinfection through the hand-to-mouth route.
  • Hookworms (Ancylostoma duodenale and Necator americanus) enter through the skin of bare feet, migrate through the lungs, and settle in the small intestine where they feed on blood. Chronic infection causes iron-deficiency anemia, which is particularly harmful to children and pregnant women.
  • Trichinella spiralis causes trichinosis, acquired by eating undercooked meat (especially pork) containing encysted larvae. After ingestion, larvae are released, mature in the intestine, and produce new larvae that migrate to skeletal muscle, forming painful cysts.
  • Wuchereria bancrofti causes lymphatic filariasis. Transmitted by mosquitoes, the adult worms live in lymphatic vessels and cause chronic lymphatic obstruction, which over years can lead to massive swelling of the limbs and genitalia known as elephantiasis.

Metamorphosis in Animal Development

While many animals develop directly from embryo to a smaller version of the adult body form, others undergo metamorphosis — a dramatic reorganization of body structure during post-embryonic development. Three patterns are recognized:

In ametamorphosis (direct development), the young organism hatches or is born as a miniature version of the adult, differing only in size and sexual maturity. It simply grows to reach full adult form. Most mammals, birds, and reptiles develop this way.

In incomplete metamorphosis (hemimetabolism), the egg hatches into a juvenile form called a nymph that resembles the adult in general body plan but lacks certain structures such as functional wings or mature reproductive organs. The nymph grows through a series of stages called instars, progressively developing adult features with each molt. Grasshoppers, dragonflies, and cockroaches are classic examples. In amphibians, the transformation from aquatic tadpole (with gills and a tail) to terrestrial adult frog (with lungs and limbs) represents an analogous form of incomplete metamorphosis regulated by thyroid hormones.

In complete metamorphosis (holometabolism), the organism passes through four distinct stages: egg → larva → pupa → adult (imago). The larval stage (caterpillar, maggot, or grub) looks entirely different from the adult and typically occupies a different ecological niche, which reduces competition between life stages. During the pupal stage, the larval body is extensively broken down and rebuilt into the adult form within a protective casing — one of the most remarkable transformations in biology. Butterflies, beetles, flies, ants, and bees all undergo complete metamorphosis. This developmental strategy is extraordinarily successful: insects with complete metamorphosis (the Holometabola) account for roughly 85% of all insect species.

Key Points — Chapter 21

  • Humans are deuterostomes (mouth forms second), triblastic (three germ layers), and coelomates (true body cavity lined by mesoderm).
  • Four chordate features: notochord, dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, post-anal tail.
  • Hox genes determine body-part identity along the head-to-tail axis; morphogens create concentration gradients that pattern tissues.
  • Development proceeds: zygote → morula → blastocyst → gastrulation (3 germ layers) → neurulation → organogenesis.
  • Ectoderm = skin + nervous system; Mesoderm = muscles + bones + blood; Endoderm = gut linings + liver + pancreas.
  • Differentiation depends on gene expression, not gene content. Potency decreases as cells specialize: totipotent → pluripotent → multipotent → unipotent.
  • Adult stem cells (e.g., hematopoietic stem cells) are multipotent tissue-repair cells. iPSCs, reprogrammed from adult cells using four transcription factors (Yamanaka, 2012 Nobel Prize), offer pluripotency without embryo destruction.
  • Major helminths: Taenia solium (tapeworm → neurocysticercosis), Schistosoma (blood fluke → schistosomiasis), Ascaris (roundworm), hookworms (anemia), Trichinella (muscle cysts).
  • Three metamorphosis types: ametamorphosis (direct), incomplete/hemimetabolism (nymph → adult), complete/holometabolism (larva → pupa → imago).

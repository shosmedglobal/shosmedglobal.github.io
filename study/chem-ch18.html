Chapter 18

Isomerism

Isomers are molecules that share the same molecular formula but differ in their structure or spatial arrangement. Isomerism is fundamental to understanding why two compounds with identical atomic compositions can have completely different properties.

Three precise terms describe different levels of molecular identity: Constitution specifies the type and arrangement of atoms and bonds without regard to spatial orientation. Conformation refers to the different spatial shapes a molecule can adopt by rotation about single bonds - conformers interconvert freely and cannot be isolated at room temperature. Configuration describes the fixed spatial arrangement around stereocentres or double bonds; changing it requires breaking and reforming covalent bonds.

Structural (Constitutional) Isomers

Structural isomers have the same molecular formula but different connectivity - atoms are bonded in different sequences. There are three subtypes:

Chain Isomers

Differ in the arrangement of the carbon skeleton. For example, C₄H₁₀ can be either butane (a straight chain of 4 carbons) or 2-methylpropane (a branched chain of 3 carbons with a methyl branch).

Position Isomers

Have the same carbon skeleton and the same functional group, but the functional group is attached at a different position. For example, 1-propanol (OH on carbon 1) versus 2-propanol (OH on carbon 2), both with formula C₃H₈O.

Functional Group Isomers

Have the same molecular formula but contain different functional groups entirely. For example, ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH, an alcohol) and dimethyl ether (CH₃OCH₃, an ether) both have the formula C₂H₆O.

Tautomers

Tautomers are a special type of structural isomer that exist in dynamic equilibrium and interconvert by the migration of a hydrogen atom. The most common type is keto-enol tautomerism, where a carbonyl compound (keto form) equilibrates with its enol form (C=C–OH). In nucleic acid bases such as uracil and guanine, the predominant keto (lactam) tautomer is essential for correct Watson-Crick base pairing. Rare enol (lactim) tautomers can cause mutagenic mispairings during DNA replication.

Naming Conventions: Substitutional vs. Radical Functional Principle

IUPAC names for organic derivatives can follow two conventions: the substitutional principle (parent hydrocarbon name + suffix, e.g., propanol, propanal, propanone) or the radical functional principle (alkyl name + functional class, e.g., propyl alcohol, dimethyl ketone, ethyl methyl ether). The substitutional approach is more universally applicable and is the modern IUPAC standard, but both appear in practice and on exams.

Stereoisomers

Stereoisomers have the same connectivity (same constitutional structure) but differ in the three-dimensional arrangement of their atoms. There are two major categories:

Geometric Isomers (cis-trans / E-Z)

Geometric isomerism occurs when rotation around a bond is restricted, most commonly at a carbon-carbon double bond. Because the π bond prevents rotation, groups attached to the double-bond carbons are locked in place.

  • cis (Z): Two identical (or higher-priority) substituents are on the same side of the double bond.
  • trans (E): Two identical (or higher-priority) substituents are on opposite sides of the double bond.

The E/Z system is more general than cis/trans and uses the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) priority rules. Each carbon of the double bond has two substituents; you rank them by atomic number. If the two higher-priority groups are on the same side, the isomer is Z (from German zusammen, meaning "together"). If they are on opposite sides, it is E (from entgegen, meaning "opposite").

Optical Isomerism (Chirality)

Optical isomerism arises when a molecule and its mirror image are not identical - just as your left and right hands are mirror images but cannot be superimposed. Such molecules are called chiral.

The most common source of chirality is a chiral center (stereocenter): a carbon atom bonded to four different groups. A molecule with one chiral center exists as two enantiomers - non-superimposable mirror images. Enantiomers have identical physical properties (melting point, boiling point, solubility) except that they rotate the plane of polarized light in opposite directions.

R/S Configuration

To assign the absolute configuration at a chiral center, use the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) rules:

  1. Rank the four substituents on the chiral center by atomic number of the atom directly attached to the center (higher atomic number = higher priority).
  2. If there is a tie, move outward along the chain until you find a difference.
  3. Orient the molecule so the lowest-priority group (usually H) points away from you.
  4. Trace a path from the highest-priority group to the second to the third. If the path is clockwise, the configuration is R (rectus). If counterclockwise, it is S (sinister).

Key Points - Chapter 18

  • Structural isomers: same formula, different connectivity (chain, position, or functional group isomers).
  • Geometric isomers: same connectivity, different arrangement around a double bond (cis/trans or Z/E).
  • E/Z uses CIP priority rules; Z = same side (zusammen), E = opposite side (entgegen).
  • Chirality: a molecule with a carbon bonded to 4 different groups has a non-superimposable mirror image.
  • R = clockwise priority path; S = counterclockwise (lowest priority group pointing away).
  • Enantiomers have identical physical properties but rotate polarized light in opposite directions.

Mnemonic: E and Z

"Z = Zame Zide" (same side) - "E = Enemies" (opposite sides)

Endocrine System (Hormonal Control)

Your body contains a silent communication network that works alongside the nervous system. The endocrine system uses chemical messengers called hormones, released into the blood, to coordinate growth, metabolism, reproduction, and responses to stress. Unlike the rapid, targeted signals of nerves, hormones travel slowly through the bloodstream but produce widespread, long-lasting effects.

What Is the Endocrine System?

The endocrine system is a collection of glands and hormone-producing tissues scattered throughout the body. Each gland synthesizes specific hormones and secretes them directly into the bloodstream (this is why they are called "ductless" glands, unlike exocrine glands that release their products through ducts). Hormones then travel to distant target cells, where they bind to specific receptors and trigger a biological response.

Chemical Classes of Hormones

Hormones fall into three main chemical categories, and the category determines how the hormone travels in blood, where its receptor is located, and how fast it acts.

1. Peptide and Protein Hormones

These are chains of amino acids — from short peptides (like ADH, 9 amino acids) to large proteins (like growth hormone, 191 amino acids). Because they are water-soluble, they dissolve freely in blood plasma. However, they cannot cross the lipid-rich cell membrane, so their receptors sit on the outside surface of the target cell. When the hormone binds its surface receptor, it activates an intracellular second messenger system (commonly cyclic AMP) that relays the signal inside the cell. These hormones generally act quickly (seconds to minutes).

Examples: insulin, glucagon, growth hormone, ADH, oxytocin, PTH.

2. Steroid Hormones

Steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol. Because they are lipid-soluble, they pass easily through the cell membrane. Their receptors are located inside the cell — often in the cytoplasm or nucleus. Once bound to the receptor, the hormone-receptor complex acts as a transcription factor, directly influencing which genes are turned on or off. This means steroid hormones change the cell's behavior by altering protein synthesis. They act more slowly (hours to days) but produce longer-lasting effects.

Examples: cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone.

3. Amino Acid Derivatives (Amine Hormones)

These are modified from a single amino acid, typically tyrosine or tryptophan. This group is diverse: some behave like peptide hormones (water-soluble, surface receptor), while others behave more like steroids (lipid-soluble, intracellular receptor).

  • Catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine) — derived from tyrosine; water-soluble; act on surface receptors; fast action.
  • Thyroid hormones (T3, T4) — derived from tyrosine but contain iodine atoms; lipid-soluble; cross the membrane and bind intracellular receptors; slow but prolonged action.
  • Melatonin — derived from tryptophan; involved in sleep-wake cycles.

The Hypothalamus-Pituitary Axis

The hypothalamus is a small region at the base of the brain that serves as the critical bridge between the nervous system and the endocrine system. It receives information from the brain (about stress, temperature, light, emotions) and converts it into hormonal signals. The hypothalamus controls the pituitary gland, a pea-sized structure hanging just below it, which is often called the "master gland" because its hormones regulate many other endocrine glands.

Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis)

The anterior pituitary is made of glandular tissue. The hypothalamus controls it through releasing hormones and inhibiting hormones, which travel via a special portal blood vessel system (the hypophyseal portal system). When a releasing hormone arrives, the anterior pituitary secretes the corresponding hormone into the general bloodstream.

Key anterior pituitary hormones:

  • Growth Hormone (GH) — promotes growth of bones and soft tissues
  • Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH) — stimulates the thyroid gland
  • Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH) — stimulates the adrenal cortex
  • Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH) — stimulates gamete production
  • Luteinizing Hormone (LH) — triggers ovulation; stimulates sex hormone production
  • Prolactin (PRL) — stimulates milk production in mammary glands

Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis)

The posterior pituitary is not actually a gland — it is an extension of nervous tissue from the hypothalamus. It stores and releases two hormones that are manufactured in the hypothalamus itself and transported down nerve cell axons for storage:

  • Oxytocin — stimulates uterine contractions during labor and milk ejection during breastfeeding
  • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH, vasopressin) — promotes water reabsorption in the kidneys, reducing urine volume

Feedback Loops

The endocrine system maintains balance through feedback mechanisms:

Negative feedback is by far the most common: when the level of a hormone (or the substance it controls) rises above normal, it signals back to reduce further hormone release. Think of a thermostat — when the room reaches the set temperature, the heater turns off. Most hormonal pathways use negative feedback.

Positive feedback is rare and amplifies a response rather than dampening it. Oxytocin during labor is a classic example: uterine contractions stimulate more oxytocin release, which causes stronger contractions, which stimulate even more oxytocin — until delivery occurs and the cycle breaks.

Major Endocrine Glands and Their Hormones

Thyroid Gland

Located in the neck, wrapped around the trachea. Produces:

  • T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine) — increase basal metabolic rate, promote growth and development, generate heat. Require iodine for synthesis. Regulated by TSH from the pituitary.
  • Calcitonin — lowers blood calcium by inhibiting osteoclasts (bone-resorbing cells) and promoting calcium deposition into bone.

Parathyroid Glands

Four tiny glands embedded in the back of the thyroid. Produce parathyroid hormone (PTH), which raises blood calcium by stimulating osteoclasts to release calcium from bone, increasing calcium reabsorption in the kidneys, and activating vitamin D (which enhances calcium absorption in the intestines).

Adrenal Glands

Sit on top of each kidney. Each gland has two distinct regions:

  • Adrenal cortex (outer layer) — produces steroid hormones:
    • Mineralocorticoids (aldosterone) — regulate sodium and potassium balance; promote sodium reabsorption in kidneys
    • Glucocorticoids (cortisol) — raise blood glucose, suppress inflammation, help cope with long-term stress
    • Androgens — weak male sex hormones (small amounts in both sexes)
  • Adrenal medulla (inner core) — produces catecholamines:
    • Epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine — the "fight or flight" hormones; increase heart rate, blood pressure, blood glucose, and redirect blood to muscles

From outside to inside, the cortex has three zones: Glomerulosa, Fasciculata, Reticularis

Pancreas (Endocrine Function)

The pancreas is both an exocrine gland (secreting digestive enzymes through ducts) and an endocrine gland. The endocrine portion consists of clusters of cells called the islets of Langerhans, which contain:

  • Beta cells — produce insulin, which lowers blood glucose by promoting glucose uptake into cells and glycogen synthesis in the liver
  • Alpha cells — produce glucagon, which raises blood glucose by promoting glycogen breakdown and gluconeogenesis in the liver
  • Delta cells — produce somatostatin, which inhibits both insulin and glucagon secretion

Gonads

  • Testes — produce testosterone (promotes male secondary sexual characteristics, spermatogenesis, muscle growth)
  • Ovaries — produce estrogen (promotes female secondary sexual characteristics, endometrial growth) and progesterone (maintains pregnancy, prepares endometrium)

Homeostasis of Blood Calcium

Blood calcium levels must stay in a narrow range (about 8.5–10.5 mg/dL) because calcium is essential for nerve signaling, muscle contraction, and blood clotting. Three hormones cooperate:

  • PTH (from parathyroids) — RAISES blood calcium. Released when calcium drops too low. Activates osteoclasts, reduces calcium loss in urine, activates vitamin D.
  • Calcitonin (from thyroid C cells) — LOWERS blood calcium. Released when calcium is too high. Inhibits osteoclasts, promotes calcium deposition in bone.
  • Vitamin D (calcitriol) — activated by PTH in the kidneys. Increases calcium absorption from the intestines.

PTH and calcitonin form an antagonistic pair, regulated by negative feedback based on blood calcium concentration.

Homeostasis of Blood Glucose

Blood glucose normally stays between about 70–110 mg/dL. Two pancreatic hormones work as antagonists:

  • After a meal (blood glucose rises): beta cells release insulin. Insulin signals cells (especially muscle and adipose tissue) to take up glucose. It also tells the liver to convert glucose into glycogen for storage. Blood glucose falls back to normal.
  • Between meals / fasting (blood glucose drops): alpha cells release glucagon. Glucagon tells the liver to break down glycogen into glucose (glycogenolysis) and to synthesize new glucose from amino acids (gluconeogenesis). Blood glucose rises back to normal.
Clinical Connection: In type 1 diabetes, the immune system destroys beta cells, so the body cannot produce insulin. In type 2 diabetes, cells become resistant to insulin's signal. Both result in chronically elevated blood glucose (hyperglycemia), which damages blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, and eyes.

Key Points — Endocrine System
  • Endocrine glands secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream (no ducts).
  • Peptide hormones act on surface receptors via second messengers (fast). Steroid hormones enter cells and alter gene expression (slow, long-lasting).
  • The hypothalamus links the nervous and endocrine systems; it controls the pituitary gland.
  • Most hormone regulation uses negative feedback: high hormone levels reduce further release.
  • PTH raises blood calcium; calcitonin lowers it. They are antagonists.
  • Insulin lowers blood glucose; glucagon raises it. They are antagonists.

Digestive System

Overview

The digestive system breaks down food into small molecules that can be absorbed into the blood and used by cells for energy, growth, and repair. It consists of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract (a continuous tube from mouth to anus) and accessory organs that assist digestion.

The Gastrointestinal Tract

Oral Cavity (Mouth)

Digestion begins here with both mechanical digestion (chewing, or mastication, breaks food into smaller pieces) and chemical digestion. Three pairs of salivary glands (parotid, submandibular, sublingual) produce saliva, which contains:

  • Salivary amylase (ptyalin): Begins the digestion of starch (a polysaccharide) into maltose (a disaccharide).
  • Lingual lipase: Begins the digestion of fats (a minor role in adults).
  • Mucus: Lubricates food for swallowing.
  • Lysozyme: An antimicrobial enzyme.

The tongue manipulates food during chewing and forms it into a ball called a bolus for swallowing. Teeth mechanically break down food: incisors cut, canines tear, premolars and molars crush and grind. Adults have 32 permanent teeth.

Pharynx and Esophagus

The pharynx connects the mouth to the esophagus. During swallowing, the epiglottis closes over the larynx to prevent food from entering the airway. The esophagus is a muscular tube (~25 cm long) that uses wavelike contractions called peristalsis to push the bolus toward the stomach. At the bottom, the lower esophageal sphincter (cardiac sphincter) prevents stomach acid from flowing back up (gastroesophageal reflux).

Stomach

A J-shaped muscular organ that stores food and mixes it with gastric juice. The stomach lining contains several types of cells:

  • Parietal cells: Secrete hydrochloric acid (HCl), creating the very acidic environment (pH ~1.5-3.5) needed to denature proteins and kill many bacteria. They also secrete intrinsic factor, which is essential for vitamin B12 absorption in the small intestine.
  • Chief cells: Secrete pepsinogen, an inactive enzyme precursor. HCl activates pepsinogen into pepsin, which begins the digestion of proteins.
  • Mucous cells: Secrete a thick layer of alkaline mucus that protects the stomach wall from being digested by its own acid and enzymes.
  • G cells: Secrete the hormone gastrin, which stimulates acid secretion.

The muscular contractions of the stomach walls mix the food with gastric juice, producing a creamy mixture called chyme. The pyloric sphincter at the stomach's exit controls the release of chyme into the small intestine.

Small Intestine

The longest part of the GI tract (~6 meters), the small intestine is the primary site of both chemical digestion and nutrient absorption. It has three regions:

  1. Duodenum (~25 cm): Receives chyme from the stomach, bile from the liver/gallbladder, and pancreatic juice from the pancreas. Most chemical digestion occurs here. The duodenal submucosa contains Brunner's glands, which secrete an alkaline, mucous fluid that neutralizes acidic chyme and provides an optimal pH for intestinal enzyme activity.
  2. Jejunum (~2.5 m): Primary site of nutrient absorption.
  3. Ileum (~3.5 m): Absorbs remaining nutrients, vitamin B12, and bile salts.

The inner wall of the small intestine has enormous surface area thanks to three levels of folding:

  • Circular folds (plicae circulares): Large, permanent folds of the mucosa.
  • Villi: Tiny finger-like projections (~1 mm) covering the folds. Each villus contains a network of capillaries (for absorbing sugars and amino acids into blood) and a central lacteal (a lymphatic vessel for absorbing fats).
  • Microvilli: Microscopic projections on the surface of each villus cell, forming the "brush border." Enzymes bound to the microvilli (brush border enzymes) complete the final stages of digestion.

Paneth cells reside at the base of the intestinal crypts (crypts of Lieberkuhn). They secrete antimicrobial peptides called defensins, along with lysozyme and phospholipase A2, forming a first line of innate immune defense in the gut and helping regulate the composition of the intestinal microbiome.

Mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) is scattered throughout the GI tract, with prominent aggregations called Peyer's patches in the ileum. These contain B and T lymphocytes that survey intestinal contents for pathogens, forming a critical component of the gut immune system.

Enteroendocrine Cells and GI Hormones

Scattered throughout the GI mucosa, enteroendocrine cells produce hormones that coordinate digestive activity:

  • Gastrin (from G cells in the stomach): Stimulates gastric acid secretion and promotes stomach motility.
  • Secretin (from S cells in the duodenum): Stimulates the pancreas to release bicarbonate-rich fluid, neutralizing acidic chyme.
  • Cholecystokinin (CCK) (from I cells in the duodenum): Stimulates gallbladder contraction and pancreatic enzyme secretion.

Large Intestine (Colon)

About 1.5 meters long and wider than the small intestine. Its main functions are:

  • Water and electrolyte absorption: Reclaims most of the remaining water from the indigestible residue.
  • Vitamin production: Houses billions of bacteria (gut microbiota) that synthesize vitamins K and some B vitamins.
  • Feces formation: Compacts undigested material into feces.

Regions: cecum (with the appendix), ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, and anal canal. The rectum stores feces until defecation. The anus has two sphincters: an internal one (involuntary smooth muscle) and an external one (voluntary skeletal muscle).

Accessory Organs

Pancreas

An organ with both exocrine and endocrine functions. Its exocrine portion produces pancreatic juice (~1.5 liters/day), which contains:

  • Pancreatic amylase: Digests starch into maltose.
  • Trypsin and chymotrypsin: Digest proteins into smaller peptides (secreted as inactive zymogens trypsinogen and chymotrypsinogen, activated in the duodenum).
  • Pancreatic lipase: The main enzyme for fat digestion; breaks triglycerides into fatty acids and monoglycerides.
  • Nucleases: Digest nucleic acids.
  • Bicarbonate (HCO₃¯): Neutralizes the acidic chyme arriving from the stomach, creating the slightly alkaline pH (~7-8) optimal for intestinal enzymes.

Liver

The largest internal organ (~1.5 kg). The liver performs over 500 functions, including:

  • Bile production: Bile contains bile salts that emulsify fats (break large fat droplets into smaller ones), increasing the surface area for lipase action. Bile is not an enzyme — it is an emulsifier.
  • Nutrient processing: Receives nutrient-rich blood from the small intestine via the hepatic portal vein. Converts glucose to glycogen for storage (and back again when needed), processes amino acids, synthesizes plasma proteins (albumin, clotting factors).
  • Detoxification: Breaks down drugs, alcohol, and metabolic waste products (including converting ammonia to urea for excretion by kidneys).
  • Storage: Stores vitamins A, D, E, K, B12, and iron.

The liver plays a central role in intermediary metabolism: after a meal it stores glucose as glycogen (glycogenesis); during fasting it breaks down glycogen to release glucose (glycogenolysis) and synthesizes glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors such as lactate, amino acids, and glycerol (gluconeogenesis). During prolonged fasting, the liver produces ketone bodies from fatty acids as an alternative fuel for the brain. It also synthesizes VLDL lipoproteins for fat transport, and detoxifies ammonia to urea via the ornithine (urea) cycle.

Gallbladder

A small sac that stores and concentrates bile produced by the liver. When fatty food enters the duodenum, the hormone cholecystokinin (CCK) stimulates the gallbladder to contract and release bile into the duodenum through the common bile duct.

Common Diseases of the Digestive System

  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD): Chronic backflow of stomach acid into the esophagus, causing heartburn and potential esophageal damage.
  • Peptic ulcers: Erosions in the stomach or duodenal lining, commonly caused by infection with Helicobacter pylori bacteria or prolonged use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs).
  • Hepatitis: Inflammation of the liver, most commonly caused by viral infections (hepatitis A, B, C) or by alcohol abuse. Can lead to cirrhosis (scarring) if chronic.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Chronic inflammatory conditions of the GI tract. Includes Crohn's disease (can affect any part of the GI tract, often the ileum; involves all layers of the intestinal wall) and ulcerative colitis (affects only the colon and rectum; involves only the mucosal layer).
  • Gallstones (cholelithiasis): Hardened deposits (usually cholesterol) that form in the gallbladder and can block bile flow, causing intense pain.
  • Celiac disease: An autoimmune reaction to gluten (a protein in wheat, barley, rye) that damages the villi of the small intestine, impairing nutrient absorption.

Mnemonic: Small Intestine Sections — "DJ Ileum"

Duodenum → Jejunum → Ileum (in order from stomach to large intestine)

Remember: Duodenum = "D" for Digestion (most chemical digestion), Jejunum = "J" for Juice absorption (main absorption site), Ileum = "I" for the "I catch whatever's left" (absorbs B12 and bile salts).

Key Points — Chapter 27

  • GI tract: mouth → pharynx → esophagus → stomach → small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum) → large intestine → rectum → anus.
  • Carbohydrate digestion begins in the mouth (amylase); protein digestion begins in the stomach (pepsin); fat digestion occurs mainly in the duodenum (bile + lipase).
  • The small intestine is the primary site of absorption. Villi and microvilli dramatically increase surface area.
  • Sugars and amino acids are absorbed into blood capillaries; fats are absorbed into lacteals (lymph).
  • The liver produces bile, processes nutrients, detoxifies, and stores vitamins. The pancreas provides digestive enzymes and bicarbonate.
  • Peptic ulcers are commonly caused by H. pylori or NSAIDs. IBD includes Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.

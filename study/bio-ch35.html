Chapter 35

Sense Organs

Organisms must gather information about their internal and external environments. Sense organs contain specialized receptor cells that convert various forms of energy (light, sound, pressure, chemicals, heat) into electrical nerve impulses that the brain can interpret. This conversion process is called sensory transduction.

Types of Sensory Receptors

Mechanoreceptors

Detect physical deformation — pressure, stretch, vibration, and movement. Examples include touch receptors in skin, muscle spindles (detect muscle stretch for proprioception), and hair cells in the inner ear (detect sound waves and body position).

Nociceptors (Pain Receptors)

Detect tissue damage or potentially harmful stimuli (extreme heat, intense pressure, certain chemicals). Pain signals alert the organism to danger. Nociceptors are free nerve endings found in skin and internal organs (except the brain itself, which has no pain receptors).

Thermoreceptors

Detect temperature changes. Found in the skin (peripheral thermoreceptors) and in the hypothalamus (central thermoreceptors that monitor blood temperature for thermoregulation). Different receptors respond to warmth versus cold.

Chemoreceptors

Detect specific chemical substances. Responsible for taste (gustatory receptors on the tongue detecting sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami) and smell (olfactory receptors in the nasal cavity, which can distinguish thousands of different odors). Internal chemoreceptors monitor blood oxygen, carbon dioxide, and pH.

Photoreceptors

Detect light energy. Found in the retina of the eye. There are two types:

  • Rods — highly sensitive to light; function in dim conditions; provide black-and-white vision. There are about 120 million rods per retina.
  • Cones — less sensitive; require brighter light; provide color vision and sharp detail. Three types of cones (red, green, blue) allow perception of the full color spectrum. About 6 million cones per retina, concentrated in the fovea.

Hearing: Structure of the Ear

The ear detects sound waves (vibrations in air) and also provides the sense of balance (equilibrium).

Outer Ear

The pinna (visible flap) collects sound waves and funnels them into the ear canal (external auditory canal) to the tympanic membrane (eardrum).

Middle Ear

Sound waves vibrate the eardrum, which transmits vibrations through three tiny bones called ossicles: the malleus (hammer), incus (anvil), and stapes (stirrup). These bones amplify the vibrations and transmit them to the oval window of the inner ear. The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear to the throat to equalize air pressure.

Inner Ear

Contains the cochlea (a snail-shaped, fluid-filled structure for hearing) and the vestibular apparatus (semicircular canals and otolith organs for balance).

Sound transduction in the cochlea:

  1. Vibrations from the stapes push on the oval window.
  2. Pressure waves travel through the fluid (perilymph) inside the cochlea.
  3. These waves cause the basilar membrane to vibrate.
  4. Hair cells sitting on the basilar membrane are bent by the vibrations. Inside the cochlea's organ of Corti, the hair cell stereocilia project upward into (or very near) the tectorial membrane, a gelatinous structure overlying the hair cells. When the basilar membrane vibrates, stereocilia are sheared against the relatively stationary tectorial membrane, mechanically opening ion channels.
  5. The resulting receptor potentials generate nerve impulses sent to the brain via the auditory nerve (cranial nerve VIII).

Different frequencies (pitches) cause maximum vibration at different positions along the basilar membrane: high-frequency sounds near the base, low-frequency sounds near the tip.

Three Canals of the Cochlea

A cross-section of the cochlea reveals three fluid-filled chambers:

  • Scala vestibuli (vestibular canal) — filled with perilymph, connected to the oval window. Sound vibrations enter here.
  • Scala media (cochlear duct) — filled with endolymph, containing the organ of Corti with the hair cells.
  • Scala tympani (tympanic canal) — filled with perilymph, ending at the round window.

Perilymph resembles extracellular fluid (high sodium, low potassium), while endolymph has an unusual composition similar to intracellular fluid (high potassium, low sodium). This ionic difference creates the endocochlear potential, essential for hair cell sensitivity. The round window is a membrane-covered opening that acts as a pressure-relief valve: as the stapes pushes the oval window inward, the round window bulges outward, allowing the cochlear fluid to oscillate in response to sound.

Vestibular System: Utricle and Saccule

In addition to the three semicircular canals (which detect rotational acceleration in three planes), the vestibular apparatus includes two otolith organs: the utricle and the saccule. The utricle detects linear acceleration and head tilt in the horizontal plane, while the saccule detects vertical acceleration (such as the sensation felt in an elevator). Both contain hair cells embedded in a gelatinous mass topped with tiny calcium carbonate crystals (otoliths). When the head moves, gravity or inertia shifts the otoliths, bending the hair cells and generating nerve signals about head position and movement.

Vision: Structure of the Eye

How Vision Works

  1. Light enters through the cornea (transparent outer layer that does most of the light bending/refraction).
  2. It passes through the pupil (opening in the iris). The iris (colored ring of muscle) controls pupil size to regulate the amount of light entering.
  3. The lens further focuses light onto the retina. Ciliary muscles change the shape of the lens (accommodation): flattened for distant objects, more rounded for close objects.
  4. Light passes through the vitreous humor (gel filling the eyeball) and strikes the retina at the back of the eye.
  5. Photoreceptors (rods and cones) in the retina absorb light and convert it into electrical signals.
  6. Signals are processed by retinal neurons (bipolar cells receive signals from photoreceptors and transmit them to ganglion cells, whose axons form the optic nerve; horizontal cells and amacrine cells provide lateral connections that enhance contrast and edge detection) and then travel via the optic nerve to the visual cortex in the occipital lobe of the brain.

The fovea is a small pit in the center of the retina densely packed with cones — this is the area of sharpest vision. At the fovea, the overlying neural layers are displaced laterally to give light direct access to the cones. The blind spot is where the optic nerve exits the retina; there are no photoreceptors here.

Phototransduction: The Chemistry of Seeing

Photoreceptors contain visual pigments made of the protein opsin bound to retinal (a vitamin A derivative). In the dark, retinal exists in the 11-cis conformation. When a photon of light strikes the pigment, 11-cis-retinal isomerizes to all-trans-retinal, triggering a shape change in opsin. In rods, this pigment complex is called rhodopsin. The activated opsin initiates a signal cascade involving the G-protein transducin, which ultimately closes sodium channels, causing the photoreceptor to hyperpolarize and change its neurotransmitter release to the downstream bipolar cell.

Key Points — Sense Organs
  • Sensory transduction: converting environmental stimuli into nerve impulses.
  • Mechanoreceptors (touch, pressure), nociceptors (pain), thermoreceptors (temperature), chemoreceptors (taste, smell), photoreceptors (light).
  • Ear: pinna collects sound; ossicles amplify it; cochlea converts vibrations to nerve signals via hair cells.
  • Eye: cornea and lens focus light onto the retina; rods for dim light, cones for color and detail.
  • Fovea = sharpest vision (dense cones). Blind spot = no receptors (optic nerve exit).

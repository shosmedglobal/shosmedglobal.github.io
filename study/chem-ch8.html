States of Matter – Liquids

8.1 The Liquid State

Liquids have a definite volume but no fixed shape - they take the shape of their container. Molecules in a liquid are close together (like solids) but can move past one another (like gases, though much more slowly). This gives liquids their characteristic ability to flow.

8.2 Vapor Pressure and Dynamic Equilibrium

Some molecules at the surface of a liquid have enough kinetic energy to escape into the gas phase (evaporation). In a closed container, gas molecules can also return to the liquid (condensation). When the rate of evaporation equals the rate of condensation, the system is in dynamic equilibrium. The pressure exerted by the vapour at this point is the vapor pressure.

Vapor pressure increases with temperature (more molecules have enough energy to escape). Liquids with high vapor pressures at room temperature are called volatile (e.g., acetone, diethyl ether).

8.3 Water and Hydrogen Bonding

Water is arguably the most important liquid for life. Its unusual properties arise largely from hydrogen bonding between molecules. Each water molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds (two as donor via its H atoms, two as acceptor via the lone pairs on O). This extensive hydrogen bonding network gives water its high boiling point (100 °C), high specific heat capacity, and high surface tension compared to similarly sized molecules.

8.4 Boiling Point

A liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals the external atmospheric pressure. At sea level (1 atm), water boils at 100 °C. At higher altitudes (lower atmospheric pressure), the boiling point is lower. At higher external pressures, the boiling point is higher (principle behind pressure cookers).

Stronger intermolecular forces lead to higher boiling points because more energy is needed for molecules to escape into the gas phase.

Cooking at High Altitude

A practical consequence of reduced boiling points at altitude is that food cooks more slowly. At approximately 2,000 metres elevation, water boils near 93 °C instead of 100 °C. Because cooking is a series of temperature-dependent chemical reactions, this 7 °C difference noticeably extends the time required to boil an egg or cook pasta. Pressure cookers compensate by raising the internal pressure above atmospheric, which raises the boiling point back above 100 °C.

8.5 Surface Tension

Molecules at the surface of a liquid experience unbalanced intermolecular forces - they are pulled inward and sideways but not outward. This creates a "skin" on the liquid surface called surface tension. Water has a high surface tension due to hydrogen bonding, which is why small insects can walk on water and water drops form spheres.

8.6 Surfactants

Surfactants (surface-active agents) are molecules with both a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and a hydrophobic (water-fearing) tail. They reduce surface tension by disrupting the hydrogen bonding at the surface. Soap and detergents are common surfactants.

8.7 Viscosity

Viscosity is a liquid's resistance to flow. Honey has high viscosity; water has low viscosity. Viscosity depends on intermolecular force strength, molecular size and shape, and temperature (viscosity decreases as temperature increases, because molecules move faster and overcome attractions more easily).

Key Points - Chapter 8

  • Liquids have fixed volume but take the shape of their container.
  • Vapor pressure is the pressure of vapour in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid; it increases with temperature.
  • A liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals external pressure.
  • Water's unique properties (high bp, high specific heat, high surface tension) come from extensive hydrogen bonding.
  • Surfactants reduce surface tension; they have hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts.
  • Viscosity measures resistance to flow; it decreases with increasing temperature.

Test Yourself - Chapter 8

1. Why does water boil at a lower temperature on top of a mountain?
Show Answer
Atmospheric pressure is lower at high altitude. A liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals external pressure, so lower external pressure means a lower temperature is sufficient for the vapor pressure to match it.
2. Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) has a lower boiling point than water despite having a larger molar mass. Why?
Show Answer
Water can form more hydrogen bonds per molecule (up to 4) than ethanol (which has one OH group and a nonpolar ethyl chain). The more extensive hydrogen bonding network in water requires more energy to break, giving it a higher boiling point.
3. What is dynamic equilibrium in the context of vapor pressure?
Show Answer
Dynamic equilibrium occurs in a closed container when the rate of evaporation from the liquid surface equals the rate of condensation from the vapour back to the liquid. Both processes continue, but the net amount of liquid and vapour remains constant. The vapour pressure at this point is the equilibrium vapor pressure.

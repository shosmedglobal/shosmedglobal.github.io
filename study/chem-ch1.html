Chapter 1

Atoms

1.1 What Is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest unit of an element that still retains the chemical properties of that element. Everything you can touch, see, or breathe is made of atoms. If you could zoom in far enough on a piece of iron, you would eventually reach individual iron atoms. Each element on the periodic table is defined by a unique type of atom.

1.2 Subatomic Particles

Every atom is composed of three types of subatomic particles:

The nucleus (centre of the atom) contains protons and neutrons, which together are called nucleons. The electrons orbit the nucleus in a region called the electron cloud. Because the electron is roughly 1836 times lighter than a proton, virtually all of an atom's mass is concentrated in its nucleus.

1.3 Atomic Number and Mass Number

The atomic number (Z) equals the number of protons in the nucleus. This number defines the element. Every carbon atom has exactly 6 protons; every oxygen atom has exactly 8.

The mass number (A) equals the total count of protons plus neutrons:

A = Z + N

where N is the number of neutrons. In a neutral atom (no overall charge), the number of electrons equals the number of protons.

Chemical Symbol Notation

We write an element's identity compactly as:

AZX

For example, 126C means carbon with 6 protons and 12 − 6 = 6 neutrons.

1.4 Isotopes

Atoms of the same element that differ in neutron count are called isotopes. They share the same atomic number but have different mass numbers. For instance, carbon has three naturally occurring isotopes: 12C (6 neutrons), 13C (7 neutrons), and 14C (8 neutrons, radioactive). Because isotopes of an element have the same number of protons and electrons, their chemical behaviour is virtually identical; only their mass differs.

The atomic mass listed on the periodic table is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes of that element.

1.5 Electron Configuration

Electrons do not orbit the nucleus in neat circles like planets. Instead, they occupy regions of space called orbitals. Each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons. Orbitals are grouped into subshells labelled s, p, d, and f, and subshells are grouped into shells (energy levels) numbered 1, 2, 3, and so on.

1.6 Three Rules for Filling Orbitals

Aufbau Principle (Building-Up Principle)

Electrons fill orbitals starting from the lowest available energy level and move upward. The filling order is not simply 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 3d because some higher-numbered subshells have lower energy than expected. The standard filling order is:

1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p → 7s → 5f → 6d → 7p

Figure 1.2 — The diagonal rule for determining orbital filling order

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in an atom can share the same set of four quantum numbers. In practical terms, each orbital can hold at most two electrons, and those two must have opposite spins (one spin-up, one spin-down).

Hund's Rule

When filling orbitals of equal energy (such as the three p orbitals), electrons enter each orbital singly first, all with the same spin direction, before any orbital gets a second electron. Think of it like passengers on a bus: everyone takes an empty seat before anyone sits next to a stranger.

Worked Example: Electron Configuration of Nitrogen (Z = 7)

Step 1: Nitrogen has 7 electrons to place.
Step 2: Fill in order: 1s² 2s² 2p³
Step 3: The three 2p electrons each occupy a separate p orbital (Hund's rule), all with the same spin.
Answer: 1s² 2s² 2p³

Worked Example: Electron Configuration of Iron (Z = 26)

Step 1: 26 electrons to place.
Step 2: Fill: 1s² 2s² 2p&sup6; 3s² 3p&sup6; 4s² 3d&sup6;
Step 3: Note that 4s fills before 3d (Aufbau principle).
Answer: 1s² 2s² 2p&sup6; 3s² 3p&sup6; 4s² 3d&sup6;   (or [Ar] 4s² 3d&sup6;)

Mnemonic: Remembering Orbital Capacity

"Smart People Don't Fail" — s(2), p(6), d(10), f(14). The max electrons double the number of orbitals: 1, 3, 5, 7 orbitals holding 2, 6, 10, 14 electrons.

Key Points — Chapter 1

  • Atoms consist of protons (+), neutrons (0) in the nucleus and electrons (−) in the electron cloud.
  • Atomic number (Z) = number of protons; this defines the element.
  • Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.
  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different neutron counts.
  • Electrons fill orbitals following the Aufbau principle (lowest energy first), the Pauli exclusion principle (max 2 per orbital, opposite spins), and Hund's rule (fill equal-energy orbitals singly first).
  • Orbital types: s (2e−), p (6e−), d (10e−), f (14e−).
  • Use the diagonal rule to remember the filling order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p ...

Both have 6 protons and 6 electrons (same chemical behaviour). Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons while Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons, giving them different mass numbers and different nuclear stability (C-14 is radioactive).

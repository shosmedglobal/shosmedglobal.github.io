Chapter 9

How to Conduct a Meta-Analysis

9.1 From Systematic Review to Quantitative Synthesis

A meta-analysis takes the systematic review one step further by statistically combining data from multiple studies to generate a single pooled estimate of effect. While a systematic review synthesizes evidence qualitatively (“most studies found X”), a meta-analysis provides a precise numerical answer (“the pooled odds ratio was 2.3, 95% CI 1.5–3.5”). This quantitative precision is what makes meta-analyses so influential in shaping clinical guidelines and medical decision-making.

9.2 When Is Statistical Pooling Appropriate?

A meta-analysis is justified when you have identified multiple studies examining the same question with sufficiently similar populations, interventions, and outcome definitions that combining their data statistically makes scientific sense.

A meta-analysis is not appropriate when the included studies are too dissimilar from each other - different populations, different interventions, or different outcome definitions - to the point where pooling them would produce a meaningless number. When studies are too heterogeneous, present a qualitative systematic review without statistical pooling instead.

9.3 Essential Statistical Concepts

Effect Sizes

The summary measure you use depends on the type of outcome:

Outcome Type Effect Size Measures
Binary (event vs. no event)Odds ratio (OR), risk ratio (RR), risk difference
ContinuousMean difference (MD), standardized mean difference (SMD)
Time-to-eventHazard ratio (HR)

Heterogeneity

Heterogeneity quantifies how much variability exists between study results beyond what would be expected from chance alone. The key metric is the I-squared (I²) statistic:

  • 0–25%: Low heterogeneity - studies are fairly consistent
  • 25–50%: Moderate heterogeneity
  • 50–75%: Substantial heterogeneity - interpret pooled results with caution
  • >75%: Considerable heterogeneity - pooling may not be appropriate

The Q statistic (Cochran's Q) tests whether heterogeneity is statistically significant. Both I² and Q should be reported in every meta-analysis.

Fixed-Effects vs. Random-Effects Models

A fixed-effects model assumes that all studies share a single true effect size - any observed variation is due to sampling error. A random-effects model assumes the true effect size varies between studies, reflecting genuine clinical or methodological differences.

In practice, most medical meta-analyses use the random-effects model because clinical studies are never truly identical in their populations, settings, or methods. When heterogeneity is very low, both models yield similar results, but the random-effects model is the safer default.

9.4 Subgroup Analysis and Sensitivity Analysis

Subgroup Analysis

Subgroup analysis tests whether the pooled effect differs across predefined categories - for example, by patient age group, disease severity, geographic region, or study quality. Critical rules:

  • Plan subgroup analyses in advance and document them in your protocol
  • Limit them to a small number of clinically meaningful comparisons
  • Unplanned subgroup analyses generate false positives and undermine credibility

Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity analysis tests the robustness of your results by examining whether they change when you:

  • Remove one study at a time (leave-one-out analysis)
  • Restrict to only high-quality studies
  • Switch between fixed-effects and random-effects models
  • Modify inclusion criteria slightly

If your main finding survives these perturbations, it is considered robust. If it changes substantially, that instability must be reported and discussed.

9.5 Assessing Publication Bias

Studies with positive or statistically significant results are more likely to be published than negative studies, creating a systematic distortion in the available evidence. You must assess and report this potential bias.

Funnel Plots

A funnel plot graphs each study's effect size (x-axis) against its precision, typically the standard error (y-axis). In the absence of bias, studies should scatter symmetrically around the pooled estimate, forming an inverted funnel shape. Visible asymmetry suggests possible publication bias or other small-study effects.

Egger's Test

Egger's regression test provides a statistical evaluation of funnel plot asymmetry. A significant result (p < 0.05) suggests potential publication bias. Note that this test has limited statistical power when fewer than 10 studies are included, so it should be interpreted cautiously in smaller meta-analyses.

9.6 Free Software for Meta-Analysis

RevMan (Review Manager)

Free software from the Cochrane Collaboration, designed specifically for systematic reviews and meta-analyses. It offers a point-and-click interface and automatically generates forest plots and funnel plots. This is the best starting point for beginners.

R with the meta and metafor Packages

More flexible and powerful than RevMan, capable of handling advanced analyses. The meta package is user-friendly for standard analyses; metafor offers greater customization. Both require basic R programming skills but have excellent documentation and tutorials available online.

9.7 Reading Forest Plots and Funnel Plots

Forest Plots

The standard visual output of a meta-analysis. Each horizontal row represents one study, showing its individual effect estimate as a square (sized proportionally to its weight in the analysis) with a horizontal line representing the confidence interval. At the bottom, a diamond shows the pooled summary estimate. A vertical line at the null value (1.0 for ratios, 0 for differences) marks “no effect.” If the diamond does not cross this line, the pooled result is statistically significant.

Funnel Plots

Symmetry around the pooled estimate suggests no publication bias. Asymmetry - especially missing small studies with null results in the lower corner - raises concern. Both RevMan and R generate funnel plots readily.

9.8 Reporting Standards

Follow the PRISMA guidelines for meta-analyses, which extend the standard checklist with items specific to quantitative synthesis. Your manuscript should report:

  • The pooled effect size with confidence intervals
  • Heterogeneity measures (I², Q statistic, p-value)
  • Results of all subgroup and sensitivity analyses
  • Publication bias assessment (funnel plot and Egger's test)

Key Takeaways

  • Only pool studies that are sufficiently similar in population, intervention, and outcome measurement
  • Use random-effects models as the default in most medical meta-analyses
  • Always assess and report heterogeneity using I² and the Q statistic
  • Plan subgroup and sensitivity analyses in advance and limit their number
  • Evaluate publication bias with funnel plots and Egger's test
  • RevMan is the easiest free tool for beginners; R offers greater analytical flexibility

Common Mistakes

  • Pooling studies that are too heterogeneous to combine meaningfully
  • Failing to assess or report heterogeneity
  • Running excessive unplanned subgroup analyses that inflate false-positive rates
  • Ignoring publication bias entirely
  • Using a fixed-effects model when a random-effects model is more appropriate
  • Omitting sensitivity analyses from the manuscript

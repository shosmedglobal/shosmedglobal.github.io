Skin System (Integumentary)

Overview

The skin is the largest organ of the human body, covering approximately 1.5-2 square meters and weighing about 4-5 kg. Together with its accessory structures (hair, nails, glands), it forms the integumentary system. The skin has two principal layers: the epidermis (outer) and the dermis (inner), resting on a layer of subcutaneous tissue (hypodermis) that is not technically part of the skin but anchors it to underlying structures.

Epidermis

The epidermis is made of stratified squamous keratinized epithelium. It is avascular (has no blood vessels) and receives nutrients by diffusion from the dermis. Its thickness varies from very thin on the eyelids (~0.05 mm) to very thick on the palms and soles (~1.5 mm).

Layers of the Epidermis (from deepest to most superficial)

  1. Stratum basale (basal layer): The deepest layer, a single row of cells resting on the basement membrane. Contains actively dividing stem cells that continuously produce new keratinocytes. Also contains melanocytes (produce melanin pigment that protects against UV radiation) and Merkel cells (touch receptors).
  2. Stratum spinosum (spiny layer): Several layers of keratinocytes connected by desmosomes (cell junctions). Contains Langerhans cells (dendritic cells of the immune system that detect and present antigens).
  3. Stratum granulosum (granular layer): 3-5 layers of cells that contain keratohyalin granules (precursors of the protein keratin). Cells begin to die as they move outward.
  4. Stratum lucidum (clear layer): A thin, translucent layer found only in thick skin (palms, soles). Consists of dead, flattened cells.
  5. Stratum corneum (horny layer): The outermost layer, composed of 20-30 layers of dead, flat, keratin-filled cells called corneocytes. These cells are constantly shed (desquamation) and replaced from below. This layer is the primary barrier against the external environment.

A keratinocyte takes about 4-6 weeks to travel from the basal layer to the surface and be shed.

Cell Types of the Epidermis

  • Keratinocytes: ~90% of epidermal cells. Produce keratin, the tough, waterproof protein that gives skin its protective quality.
  • Melanocytes: ~8% of epidermal cells. Located in the stratum basale. Produce melanin in organelles called melanosomes, which are transferred to surrounding keratinocytes. All humans have roughly the same number of melanocytes; differences in skin color are due to the amount, type, and distribution of melanin produced.
  • Langerhans cells: Immune cells that patrol the epidermis for pathogens.
  • Merkel cells: Sensory receptors for fine touch, located in the stratum basale.

Dermis

The dermis lies beneath the epidermis and is much thicker (1-4 mm). It is composed of connective tissue rich in collagen fibers (for strength) and elastic fibers (for flexibility). The dermis is vascular and contains blood vessels, lymphatic vessels, nerves, hair follicles, glands, and sensory receptors.

Two Layers of the Dermis

  • Papillary layer: The thin, superficial layer. Contains dermal papillae — finger-like projections that interlock with the epidermis, increasing the bond between the two layers. In the fingertips, these papillae create the unique patterns known as fingerprints. This layer also contains capillaries that nourish the epidermis and tactile receptors (Meissner's corpuscles for light touch).
  • Reticular layer: The thick, deeper layer. Contains dense irregular connective tissue with thick bundles of collagen and elastic fibers. This layer gives skin its strength, extensibility, and elasticity. It also houses hair follicles, sebaceous glands, sweat glands, and deeper nerve receptors.

Hypodermis (Subcutaneous Layer)

Not technically part of the skin but lies directly beneath the dermis. Composed mainly of adipose tissue and loose connective tissue. Functions include insulation, energy storage, shock absorption, and anchoring the skin to underlying muscle and bone.

Nerve Endings and Sensory Receptors

The skin is a major sensory organ, containing several types of receptors:

  • Free nerve endings: Detect pain (nociception), temperature (thermoreception), and crude touch. Found throughout the epidermis and dermis. Pain receptors are the most numerous.
  • Meissner's corpuscles: Detect light touch and texture changes. Located in the dermal papillae, especially abundant in fingertips, lips, and palms.
  • Pacinian (lamellar) corpuscles: Detect deep pressure and vibration. Located deep in the dermis and hypodermis.
  • Ruffini endings: Detect continuous pressure and skin stretching. Found in the dermis.
  • Merkel discs: Detect fine touch and sustained pressure. Found at the junction of the epidermis and dermis.
  • Krause end bulbs (Krause's corpuscles): Encapsulated nerve endings found primarily in the mucosa of the lips, tongue, and external genitalia. Historically considered cold receptors, their precise sensory modality is still debated and they may respond to low-frequency vibration or other mechanical stimuli.

Glands of the Skin

Sweat Glands (Sudoriferous Glands)

  • Eccrine sweat glands: Found over most of the body surface (especially palms, soles, forehead). Produce a watery, odorless sweat that is hypotonic relative to plasma, containing water with dissolved sodium (~0.9 g/L), potassium (~0.2 g/L), calcium, and trace amounts of zinc, copper, and iron, along with metabolic wastes such as urea and lactate. Their primary function is thermoregulation: as sweat evaporates from the skin surface, it carries heat away from the body, cooling it.
  • Apocrine sweat glands: Found mainly in the axillae (armpits) and groin. Produce a thicker, protein-rich secretion that is odorless when released but becomes odorous when broken down by skin bacteria. They become active at puberty and are stimulated by stress and sexual arousal.

Sebaceous (Oil) Glands

Associated with hair follicles. Secrete sebum, an oily substance that keeps skin and hair moisturized and flexible, and has mild antimicrobial properties. Overproduction of sebum can lead to acne.

Functions of the Skin

  1. Protection: Physical barrier against pathogens, UV radiation, chemicals, and mechanical damage. The acid mantle (slightly acidic pH ~4.5-6) inhibits bacterial growth.
  2. Thermoregulation: Sweating cools the body. Blood vessel dilation (vasodilation) in the dermis releases heat; constriction (vasoconstriction) conserves heat. Hair erection (goosebumps) is a vestigial response.
  3. Sensation: Detects touch, pressure, pain, temperature, and vibration through specialized receptors.
  4. Vitamin D synthesis: When ultraviolet B (UVB) radiation from sunlight hits the skin, it converts a cholesterol precursor (7-dehydrocholesterol) into pre-vitamin D3. This is further processed by the liver and kidneys into active vitamin D (calcitriol), which is essential for calcium absorption and bone health.
  5. Excretion: Small amounts of water, salt, urea, and other waste products are eliminated through sweat.
  6. Immune defense: Langerhans cells in the epidermis detect and present antigens to the immune system.

The Skin Microbiome

The skin harbors a diverse community of commensal microorganisms estimated at roughly 1,000 bacterial species from 19 phyla. The dominant phyla are Actinobacteria (including Cutibacterium/Propionibacterium), Firmicutes (including Staphylococcus), Proteobacteria, and Bacteroidetes. This commensal flora competes with pathogenic organisms for resources and space, contributing to the skin's innate defense.

Fitzpatrick Skin Phototypes

The Fitzpatrick classification categorizes skin into six types based on response to UV exposure, and is useful for assessing skin cancer risk and guiding UV exposure recommendations:

  • Type I: Always burns, never tans (very fair skin).
  • Type II: Usually burns, sometimes tans (fair skin).
  • Type III: May burn, usually tans (medium skin).
  • Type IV: Rarely burns, always tans (olive skin).
  • Type V: Very rarely burns (brown skin).
  • Type VI: Never burns (deeply pigmented/black skin).

Common Skin Diseases

  • Acne: Occurs when hair follicles become clogged with sebum and dead skin cells. Bacteria (Cutibacterium acnes) can infect clogged pores, causing inflammation. Most common during puberty due to hormonal changes increasing sebum production.
  • Eczema (atopic dermatitis): A chronic inflammatory condition causing itchy, red, dry patches. Often associated with allergies and a defective skin barrier.
  • Psoriasis: An autoimmune condition causing rapid overproduction of skin cells, leading to thick, scaly, silvery patches. Involves T-cell mediated inflammation.
  • Skin cancer: Three main types:
    • Basal cell carcinoma: The most common and least dangerous; arises from stratum basale; rarely metastasizes.
    • Squamous cell carcinoma: Arises from keratinocytes of the stratum spinosum; can metastasize if untreated.
    • Melanoma: The most dangerous; arises from melanocytes. Has a high potential to metastasize. Early detection (using the ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving) is critical.
  • Burns: Classified by depth: first-degree (epidermis only, like sunburn), second-degree (epidermis + part of dermis, blistering), third-degree (full thickness, destroying epidermis and entire dermis).

Key Points — Chapter 29

  • Skin has two main layers: epidermis (stratified squamous keratinized epithelium, avascular) and dermis (connective tissue, vascular).
  • Epidermis layers (deep to superficial): basale, spinosum, granulosum, lucidum (thick skin only), corneum.
  • Key cell types: keratinocytes (protection), melanocytes (pigment/UV defense), Langerhans cells (immune), Merkel cells (touch).
  • Dermis has a papillary layer (capillaries, fingerprints, Meissner's corpuscles) and reticular layer (collagen, glands, hair follicles, Pacinian corpuscles).
  • Skin functions: protection, thermoregulation (sweat, vasodilation/constriction), sensation, vitamin D synthesis, excretion, immunity.
  • Melanoma is the most dangerous skin cancer; use ABCDE criteria for detection.

Population Genetics

Population genetics is the study of genetic variation within populations and the forces that change allele frequencies over time. It provides the mathematical framework for understanding evolution at the genetic level.

19.1 The Modern Evolutionary Synthesis

The modern evolutionary synthesis (also called the neo-Darwinian synthesis) merged Darwin's theory of natural selection with Mendelian genetics. Population genetics as a formal discipline emerged in the 1930s through the mathematical work of Ronald Fisher, J.B.S. Haldane, and Sewall Wright, who demonstrated how natural selection could be modeled within Mendelian frameworks. By the 1940s, the full synthesis was consolidated, integrating these quantitative models with systematics, paleontology, and ecology into a unified theory of evolution. It established that:

  • Populations (not individuals) evolve.
  • Evolution is defined as a change in allele frequencies in a population over time.
  • Natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and mutation are the main forces driving evolutionary change.
  • Genetic variation is the raw material for evolution.

19.2 Genetic Structure of Populations

A population in genetics refers to a group of individuals of the same species that live in the same area and interbreed. Under the biological species concept (championed by Ernst Mayr), a species is defined as a group of populations whose members can interbreed in nature and produce viable, fertile offspring. This definition works well for sexually reproducing organisms but has limitations for asexual species, fossils, and organisms that can hybridize. The gene pool is the total collection of all alleles in the population.

We describe the genetic structure of a population using:

  • Allele frequency: The proportion of a specific allele among all alleles for that gene in the population. If there are two alleles (A and a), the frequency of A is called p and the frequency of a is called q. Since there are only two alleles: p + q = 1.
  • Genotype frequency: The proportion of individuals with a specific genotype (AA, Aa, or aa) in the population.

19.3 Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

The Hardy-Weinberg principle (1908) states that in a large, randomly mating population with no evolution occurring, allele and genotype frequencies will remain constant from generation to generation.

The Hardy-Weinberg equation for genotype frequencies:

p² + 2pq + q² = 1

Where:

  • p² = frequency of homozygous dominant individuals (AA)
  • 2pq = frequency of heterozygous individuals (Aa)
  • q² = frequency of homozygous recessive individuals (aa)
  • p = frequency of the dominant allele
  • q = frequency of the recessive allele
  • p + q = 1

The Five Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

For a population to be in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, ALL of the following must be true:

  1. No mutation: No new alleles are being created.
  2. Random mating: Individuals do not choose mates based on genotype.
  3. No natural selection: All genotypes are equally fit.
  4. Very large population size: No genetic drift (random fluctuations are negligible).
  5. No gene flow: No migration of individuals in or out of the population.

In reality, no population perfectly meets all five conditions. The Hardy-Weinberg model serves as a null hypothesis - a baseline against which real populations can be compared. If observed genotype frequencies deviate from Hardy-Weinberg predictions, one or more evolutionary forces must be acting.

MNEMONIC - Hardy-Weinberg Conditions

"No MSDRG" - No Mutation, No Selection, No Drift, Random mating, No Gene flow

19.4 Using Hardy-Weinberg: Worked Examples

Worked Example 1

Problem: In a population, 16% of individuals show the recessive phenotype for a given trait. Calculate the allele frequencies and the frequency of carriers.

Solution:

Step 1: The recessive phenotype corresponds to genotype aa. So q² = 0.16.

Step 2: q = the square root of 0.16 = 0.4.

Step 3: p = 1 − q = 1 − 0.4 = 0.6.

Step 4: Frequency of carriers (heterozygotes, Aa) = 2pq = 2(0.6)(0.4) = 0.48 (48%).

Step 5: Frequency of homozygous dominant (AA) = p² = (0.6)² = 0.36 (36%).

Check: 0.36 + 0.48 + 0.16 = 1.00. Correct!

Worked Example 2

Problem: Cystic fibrosis (autosomal recessive) affects approximately 1 in 2,500 newborns in a certain population. What fraction of the population are carriers?

Solution:

Step 1: q² = 1/2,500 = 0.0004.

Step 2: q = the square root of 0.0004 = 0.02.

Step 3: p = 1 − 0.02 = 0.98.

Step 4: Carrier frequency = 2pq = 2(0.98)(0.02) = 0.0392, or approximately 1 in 25 people.

This means that roughly 4% of the population carries the CF allele without showing symptoms.

Common Exam Strategy

On exams, you almost always start with q² (the frequency of the recessive phenotype), because this is the only genotype you can directly observe from the phenotype. Take the square root to get q, then calculate p, and finally compute 2pq for the carrier frequency.

KEY POINTS - Chapter 19
  • Evolution = change in allele frequencies in a population over time.
  • Hardy-Weinberg equation: p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (and p + q = 1).
  • Five conditions for equilibrium: no mutation, random mating, no selection, large population, no gene flow.
  • Hardy-Weinberg serves as a null hypothesis to detect evolutionary forces.
  • Start calculations with q² (recessive phenotype frequency), take the square root for q, then find p.

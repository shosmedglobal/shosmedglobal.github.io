Chapter 4

Choosing Your Research Question

4.1 Every Publishable Study Fills a Gap

A research question is not something you invent from thin air. It emerges from identifying something that the existing literature has not adequately answered, described, or resolved. Your job as a researcher is to find that gap and design a study that addresses it. The better defined the gap, the more likely your work will be published and cited.

4.2 Strategies for Identifying Gaps in the Literature

Mine the Discussion Sections

Every published paper includes a Discussion section where authors identify the limitations of their own work and suggest directions for future research. These suggestions are essentially research questions handed to you. Read recent papers in your area of interest and pay close attention to phrases like “further studies are needed,” “our study was limited by,” and “future work should examine.”

Examine Clinical Guidelines

Practice guidelines frequently highlight areas where evidence is weak or absent. When you see phrases like “limited evidence,” “expert opinion,” or “Grade C recommendation,” you are looking at a potential research opportunity.

Listen During Clinical Rotations

When an attending says “we do not really know the answer to that” or “the data on this are mixed,” a research question may be sitting right in front of you. Clinical uncertainty is the raw material of research.

Look for Contradictory Findings

When two studies on the same topic reach opposite conclusions, there is room for a new study - whether a larger retrospective analysis, a meta-analysis that reconciles the discrepancy, or a study in a different population.

Explore Unstudied Populations

A treatment well-studied in younger adults but not in elderly patients, or well-characterized in one country but not another, represents a clear gap worth investigating.

4.3 The PICO Framework

PICO is the standard framework for structuring clinical research questions. Using it forces you to be specific about what you are studying:

Component Meaning Example
P - PopulationWho are you studying?Patients with newly diagnosed DLBCL
I - InterventionWhat treatment, exposure, or variable?Polatuzumab vedotin added to R-CHOP
C - ComparisonWhat is the comparator?R-CHOP alone
O - OutcomeWhat are you measuring?Two-year overall survival

Not every study fits PICO perfectly. Descriptive studies and case reports may lack a comparison group. But the framework is invaluable for sharpening your thinking and ensuring your question is answerable.

4.4 Assessing Feasibility Before You Commit

A brilliant research question is worthless if you cannot execute the study. Before investing significant time, ask yourself:

  1. Do I have access to the necessary data? For retrospective studies, can you obtain medical records? For systematic reviews, can you access the relevant databases?
  2. Do I have enough time? If you need this on your ERAS application in nine months, a prospective study is not realistic.
  3. Do I have the required skills, or can I acquire them? If the study needs survival analysis and you have never run a statistical test, you will need time to learn or a collaborator who can fill that gap.
  4. Is the sample size adequate? A retrospective study with only eight patients is unlikely to yield meaningful results or attract a journal's interest.
  5. Does my mentor support this direction? Their experience in assessing what is practical and publishable is invaluable.
  6. Has this question been definitively answered recently? If a comprehensive study on this exact topic was published last year, you need a different angle.

4.5 Matching Your Question to a Study Design

The nature of your question determines the type of study you should conduct:

Your Question Sounds Like... Study Design
“What happened to patients who...”Retrospective cohort study
“What will happen to patients who...”Prospective cohort study
“What does the literature say about...”Systematic review / meta-analysis
“How common is...”Cross-sectional study
“Has this unusual case been reported...”Case report
“Can we improve this process...”Quality improvement project
“What are the risk factors for...”Case-control study
“Is treatment A better than B...”RCT (or retrospective comparison)

Practical advice for your first project: Aim for a case report (if you have an interesting clinical encounter), a retrospective study (if you have data access), or a systematic review (if you have time but no patient data). These three types offer the best balance of accessibility and publishability for students.

4.6 The Iterative Process of Refining Your Question

Your initial question will almost never survive contact with the literature unchanged. Refinement is iterative and normal:

  1. Start with a broad area of interest.
  2. Read 10 to 15 recent papers in that area.
  3. Identify what remains unanswered or poorly addressed.
  4. Draft a preliminary PICO question.
  5. Discuss it with your mentor.
  6. Run a focused literature search to confirm the gap still exists.
  7. Refine the question based on what is feasible with your resources.
  8. Finalize the question and write a brief study protocol.

This process typically takes one to three weeks. Resist the urge to rush it. A well-formulated question is the foundation of a successful study, and time invested here prevents frustration downstream.

Key Takeaways

  • Every publishable study addresses a specific, identifiable gap in the existing literature
  • Use the PICO framework to structure your question with precision
  • Feasibility matters just as much as novelty - a great question you cannot execute is worthless
  • Match your question to a study design that fits your available resources and timeline

Common Mistakes

  • Selecting a question that is too broad (“What causes cancer?”) or too narrow to be publishable
  • Failing to check whether the question has already been comprehensively answered
  • Ignoring feasibility constraints until you are deep into the project
  • Developing a question without consulting your mentor
  • Attempting to answer multiple questions in a single study rather than focusing on one primary outcome

Chapter 29

Heterocyclic Compounds

What Are Heterocycles?

Heterocyclic compounds are ring structures in which at least one atom in the ring is not carbon — typically nitrogen, oxygen, or sulfur. These "heteroatoms" change the chemical properties of the ring dramatically. Heterocycles are ubiquitous in biology: DNA bases, amino acids (histidine, tryptophan, proline), vitamins, and many drugs contain heterocyclic rings.

Five-Membered Heterocycles

Pyrrole

A five-membered aromatic ring with one nitrogen. The nitrogen's lone pair is part of the aromatic pi system (contributes 2 electrons to make the total 6 pi electrons, satisfying Huckel's rule). This means pyrrole is a very weak base (the lone pair is tied up in aromaticity). Four pyrrole units linked together form porphyrins — the ring system in hemoglobin and chlorophyll.

The Porphin Ring System

The parent structure underlying all porphyrins is porphin, a large planar macrocycle consisting of four pyrrole rings linked by four methine bridges (=CH–) at the alpha positions. Porphin and its substituted derivatives (collectively called porphyrins) are fully conjugated aromatic systems and therefore intensely coloured.

Heme is a specific porphyrin — protoporphyrin IX — with an Fe2+ ion chelated at the centre by coordinate bonds to the four pyrrole nitrogens. Iron in heme can form two additional coordinate bonds above and below the porphyrin plane. In haemoglobin and myoglobin, one of these axial positions is occupied by a histidine residue of the globin protein (the "proximal histidine"), and the other reversibly binds molecular oxygen (O₂). Haemoglobin is a heterotetrameric protein (α₂β₂) in which each of the four subunits carries one heme group, giving it the ability to carry up to four O₂ molecules cooperatively.

Related cyclic tetrapyrrole systems include chlorophyll (which chelates Mg2+ instead of Fe2+) and the corrin ring of vitamin B₁₂ (which chelates cobalt in various oxidation states (Co3+ in cyanocobalamin, Co⁺ in the supernucleophilic form) and has one fewer methine bridge than porphin). Degradation of heme produces the linear tetrapyrroles biliverdin (green) and bilirubin (yellow-orange), which are conjugated in the liver and excreted in bile. Elevated blood bilirubin causes the yellow discolouration of jaundice.

Pyrrolidine

Pyrrolidine is the fully saturated five-membered ring containing one nitrogen. Unlike pyrrole, pyrrolidine is not aromatic and behaves as a typical secondary amine base. It is found in the amino acid proline (where the ring includes the alpha-carbon) and in nicotine, the addictive compound in tobacco. Nicotine consists of a pyridine ring bonded to an N-methylpyrrolidine ring and acts as a stimulant by mimicking acetylcholine at nicotinic receptors.

Furan

A five-membered aromatic ring with one oxygen. Less aromatic than pyrrole; the oxygen donates a lone pair to the ring. Found in some natural flavoring compounds.

Imidazole

A five-membered ring with two nitrogens at positions 1 and 3. One nitrogen is "pyrrole-like" (lone pair in the ring) and one is "pyridine-like" (lone pair not in the ring, available for protonation). Imidazole is an important buffer around physiological pH. Found in the amino acid histidine, where it plays a key role in enzyme catalysis.

Indole

A bicyclic compound: a benzene ring fused to a pyrrole ring. Found in the amino acid tryptophan, whose decarboxylation and hydroxylation product serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine) is a neurotransmitter regulating mood, sleep, and appetite.

Biogenic Amines from Heterocyclic Amino Acids

Several heterocyclic amino acids are precursors to important signalling molecules. The amino acid histidine contains an imidazole ring; its decarboxylation product histamine is a major mediator of allergic reactions and a stimulant of gastric acid secretion. Tryptophan (indole ring) gives rise to serotonin, as noted above. These examples show how heterocyclic amino acids serve as the starting materials for biologically active amines.

Six-Membered Heterocycles

Pyridine

A six-membered aromatic ring with one nitrogen replacing a CH group. The nitrogen's lone pair is NOT part of the aromatic pi system (it is in the plane of the ring, in an sp² orbital), so pyridine is a reasonable base (unlike pyrrole). Found in vitamin B3 (niacin/nicotinic acid) and NAD⁺/NADH.

Pyrimidine

A six-membered ring with two nitrogens at positions 1 and 3. The three pyrimidine bases of nucleic acids are cytosine, thymine (in DNA), and uracil (in RNA).

Pyran

A six-membered ring with one oxygen. The saturated form (tetrahydropyran) is the structural basis of the six-membered ring form of many sugars (pyranose form of glucose).

Fused Ring System: Purine

Purine is a bicyclic compound: a pyrimidine ring fused to an imidazole ring. The two purine bases of nucleic acids are adenine and guanine. Caffeine, uric acid, and xanthine are also purine derivatives.

Uric Acid: Structure and Clinical Significance

Uric acid (2,6,8-trihydroxypurine) is the final oxidation product of purine catabolism in humans. The catabolic pathway proceeds as follows: adenine → hypoxanthine → xanthine, and guanine → xanthine. The enzyme xanthine oxidase then catalyses the final two oxidation steps (hypoxanthine → xanthine → uric acid).

Uric acid is a very weak diprotic acid (pKa1 ≈ 5.4). At physiological blood pH (7.4), it exists predominantly as the monosodium urate salt. Despite being a metabolic waste product, uric acid has significant antioxidant activity, scavenging reactive oxygen species in the blood.

Uric acid is only slightly soluble in water, and its solubility drops sharply at pH below 5.5. Adults typically excrete up to approximately 1 g of uric acid or urates daily in urine. When blood uric acid levels are elevated (hyperuricaemia), monosodium urate crystals can deposit in joints, causing the painful inflammatory condition gout. Urate crystals can also form in the renal collecting system at low urinary pH, producing urate kidney stones. The drug allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase and is used clinically to lower uric acid levels in gout management.

Key Points — Chapter 29

  • Heterocycles: ring compounds with at least one non-carbon atom (N, O, or S).
  • Five-membered: pyrrole (hemoglobin), furan, imidazole (histidine), indole (tryptophan).
  • Six-membered: pyridine (niacin/NAD), pyrimidine (C, T, U bases), pyran (sugar rings).
  • Purine = pyrimidine + imidazole fused. Adenine and guanine are purines.
  • Porphin: four pyrrole rings linked by methine bridges; heme = protoporphyrin IX + Fe2+.
  • Uric acid (2,6,8-trihydroxypurine) is the end product of purine catabolism; excess causes gout.
  • Pyrrole is a weak base (lone pair in pi system); pyridine is a stronger base (lone pair free).

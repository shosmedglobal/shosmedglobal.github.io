Heterocyclic Compounds

What Are Heterocycles?

Heterocyclic compounds are ring structures in which at least one atom in the ring is not carbon — typically nitrogen, oxygen, or sulfur. These "heteroatoms" change the chemical properties of the ring dramatically. Heterocycles are ubiquitous in biology: DNA bases, amino acids (histidine, tryptophan, proline), vitamins, and many drugs contain heterocyclic rings.

Five-Membered Heterocycles

Pyrrole

A five-membered aromatic ring with one nitrogen. The nitrogen's lone pair is part of the aromatic pi system (contributes 2 electrons to make the total 6 pi electrons, satisfying Huckel's rule). This means pyrrole is a very weak base (the lone pair is tied up in aromaticity). Four pyrrole units linked together form porphyrins — the ring system in hemoglobin and chlorophyll.

Furan

A five-membered aromatic ring with one oxygen. Less aromatic than pyrrole; the oxygen donates a lone pair to the ring. Found in some natural flavoring compounds.

Imidazole

A five-membered ring with two nitrogens at positions 1 and 3. One nitrogen is "pyrrole-like" (lone pair in the ring) and one is "pyridine-like" (lone pair not in the ring, available for protonation). Imidazole is an important buffer around physiological pH. Found in the amino acid histidine, where it plays a key role in enzyme catalysis.

Indole

A bicyclic compound: a benzene ring fused to a pyrrole ring. Found in the amino acid tryptophan and in the neurotransmitter serotonin.

Six-Membered Heterocycles

Pyridine

A six-membered aromatic ring with one nitrogen replacing a CH group. The nitrogen's lone pair is NOT part of the aromatic pi system (it is in the plane of the ring, in an sp² orbital), so pyridine is a reasonable base (unlike pyrrole). Found in vitamin B3 (niacin/nicotinic acid) and NAD⁺/NADH.

Pyrimidine

A six-membered ring with two nitrogens at positions 1 and 3. The three pyrimidine bases of nucleic acids are cytosine, thymine (in DNA), and uracil (in RNA).

Pyran

A six-membered ring with one oxygen. The saturated form (tetrahydropyran) is the structural basis of the six-membered ring form of many sugars (pyranose form of glucose).

Fused Ring System: Purine

Purine is a bicyclic compound: a pyrimidine ring fused to an imidazole ring. The two purine bases of nucleic acids are adenine and guanine. Caffeine, uric acid, and xanthine are also purine derivatives.

Fundamental heterocyclic ring systems found throughout biochemistry

Key Points — Chapter 29

  • Heterocycles: ring compounds with at least one non-carbon atom (N, O, or S).
  • Five-membered: pyrrole (hemoglobin), furan, imidazole (histidine), indole (tryptophan).
  • Six-membered: pyridine (niacin/NAD), pyrimidine (C, T, U bases), pyran (sugar rings).
  • Purine = pyrimidine + imidazole fused. Adenine and guanine are purines.
  • Pyrrole is a weak base (lone pair in pi system); pyridine is a stronger base (lone pair free).

Mnemonic: Purine Bases

"Pure As Gold" = Purines are Adenine and Guanine

Pyrimidines (the other three) are CUT: Cytosine, Uracil, Thymine.

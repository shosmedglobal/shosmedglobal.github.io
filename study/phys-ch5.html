Chapter 5

Fluids

Density

rho = m / V

Unit: kg/m^3. Water: 1000 kg/m^3.

Pressure

P = F / A

Unit: pascal (Pa) = N/m^2. Pressure is a scalar -- acts equally in all directions at a point.

Pascal's Law

A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally throughout:

F_1 / A_1 = F_2 / A_2

A small force on a small piston creates a large force on a large piston, but displacement is inversely proportional (work conserved).

Hydrostatic Pressure

P = P_surface + rho * g * h
  • Pressure depends only on depth, not container shape.
  • Pressure increases linearly with depth.
  • At the same depth in the same fluid, pressure is equal everywhere.

Atmospheric Pressure

P_atm = 101,325 Pa = 1 atm = 760 mmHg = 1013.25 hPa

Buoyancy (Archimedes' Principle)

F_b = rho_fluid * V_displaced * g

Floating condition: Object floats when rho_object < rho_fluid.

For a floating object:

rho_object / rho_fluid = V_submerged / V_total

Worked Example 1: A cube of side 0.1 m and mass 0.5 kg in water.

  • V = 0.001 m^3, rho = 500 kg/m^3
  • Since 500 < 1000 (water), it floats. Fraction submerged = 500/1000 = 50%.

Worked Example 2: Pressure at bottom of a 3 m deep pool?

  • P = 10^5 + 1000(10)(3) = 130,000 Pa = 1.3 x 10^5 Pa

Worked Example 3: A hydraulic lift has pistons of area 0.01 m^2 and 0.5 m^2. What force on the small piston lifts a 2000 kg car?

  • F_2 = m*g = 20,000 N
  • F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2 => F_1 = F_2 A_1/A_2 = 20,000 0.01/0.5 = 400 N

Equation of Continuity

For an incompressible fluid in a pipe:

A_1 * v_1 = A_2 * v_2

Where the pipe narrows, fluid speeds up. The product A*v = Q is the volume flow rate (m^3/s).

Bernoulli's Equation

P + (1/2)*rho*v^2 + rho*g*h = constant

This is conservation of energy per unit volume for fluids. Where speed increases, pressure decreases. Think of it this way: the fluid has a fixed "energy budget" at every point — if it spends more on speed, it has less left for pressure.

Applications: airplane lift, Venturi effect, carburetors, perfume atomizers.

Worked Example 4: Water flows through a horizontal pipe. At point 1: A_1 = 0.02 m^2, v_1 = 1 m/s, P_1 = 200,000 Pa. At point 2: A_2 = 0.01 m^2. Find v_2 and P_2.

  • Continuity: v_2 = A_1*v_1/A_2 = 0.02(1)/0.01 = 2 m/s
  • Bernoulli (h_1 = h_2): P_1 + (1/2)*rho*v_1^2 = P_2 + (1/2)*rho*v_2^2
  • 200,000 + 500(1) = P_2 + 500(4)
  • P_2 = 200,500 - 2000 = 198,500 Pa

Viscosity

Viscosity is internal friction in a fluid. Higher viscosity = thicker fluid = more resistance to flow.

Poiseuille's Law (laminar flow in a pipe):

Q = (pi * r^4 * Delta_P) / (8 * eta * L)

Flow rate depends on the 4th power of radius -- doubling the pipe radius increases flow 16-fold.

Surface Tension

Surface tension (sigma, in N/m) is the force per unit length along the surface of a liquid. It causes:

  • Droplets to be spherical (minimizing surface area)
  • Capillary action (rise/fall in narrow tubes)
  • Insects walking on water

Capillary rise: h = 2*sigma*cos(theta) / (rho*g*r), where theta is the contact angle and r is the tube radius.

Laminar vs Turbulent Flow

PropertyLaminarTurbulent
PatternSmooth, parallel layersChaotic, eddies
SpeedLowHigh
Reynolds numberRe < 2000Re > 4000
Energy lossLowerHigher
Pipe surfaceSmoothAny

Reynolds number: Re = rho*v*d / eta (where d is pipe diameter, eta is viscosity).

Exam Tips:
- Pressure depends on depth, NOT on total volume of fluid above.
- Buoyant force depends on volume DISPLACED, not total volume of object.
- A steel ship floats because the hull encloses air, making average density low.
- In Bernoulli: higher speed = LOWER pressure, counterintuitive.
- Doubling pipe radius increases Poiseuille flow by 16x (fourth power!).

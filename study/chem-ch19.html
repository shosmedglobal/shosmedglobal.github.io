Chapter 19

Reaction Types and Nomenclature

Four Fundamental Reaction Types

1. Substitution

An atom or group in a molecule is replaced by a different atom or group. The molecular skeleton remains intact. Example: when methane reacts with chlorine under UV light, one hydrogen is replaced by chlorine to produce chloromethane and HCl.

2. Addition

Two molecules combine to form a single product, with no atoms lost. This is characteristic of unsaturated compounds (those with double or triple bonds). The π bond breaks and new σ bonds form. Example: ethene + HBr → bromoethane.

3. Elimination

The reverse of addition: a single molecule loses atoms or groups to form a double or triple bond. Two substituents (often on adjacent carbons) depart, and a π bond forms between the carbons. Example: heating ethanol with sulfuric acid removes water (H and OH from adjacent carbons) to form ethene.

4. Rearrangement

Atoms within a molecule reorganize their connectivity to produce a structural isomer. No atoms are gained or lost - the molecular formula stays the same, but the structure changes.

IUPAC Nomenclature

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) created a systematic naming system so that every organic compound has one unambiguous name. The basic rules are:

  1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain that contains the principal functional group. This gives the parent name (meth- = 1C, eth- = 2C, prop- = 3C, but- = 4C, pent- = 5C, hex- = 6C, hept- = 7C, oct- = 8C, non- = 9C, dec- = 10C).
  2. Number the chain from the end nearest to the principal functional group (giving it the lowest possible number).
  3. Name substituents as prefixes (e.g., methyl-, ethyl-, chloro-, bromo-) with position numbers.
  4. Add the suffix corresponding to the principal functional group (see table below).
  5. Arrange prefixes in alphabetical order (ignoring multiplying prefixes like di-, tri-).

Priority of functional groups (highest priority gets the suffix; others become prefixes): Carboxylic acid > ester > amide > aldehyde > ketone > alcohol > amine > alkene > alkyne > alkane.

Common vs. Systematic Names: Many simple compounds have well-established common names that you must know alongside IUPAC names. Formaldehyde = methanal. Acetaldehyde = ethanal. Acetone = propanone. Acetic acid = ethanoic acid. Formic acid = methanoic acid.

Key Points - Chapter 19

  • Four reaction types: substitution (replace), addition (π bond breaks, atoms add), elimination (atoms leave, π bond forms), rearrangement (atoms reshuffle).
  • IUPAC naming: longest chain + number from end nearest functional group + alphabetical prefixes + appropriate suffix.
  • Functional group priority determines which group gets the suffix.
  • Know both IUPAC and common names for key small molecules.

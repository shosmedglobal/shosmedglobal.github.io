Chapter 10

Electromagnetic Induction and AC

Faraday's Law of Induction

EMF = -N · d(Φ)/dt

A changing magnetic flux induces an EMF. N = number of turns.

Lenz's Law

The induced current flows in a direction that OPPOSES the change in flux that produced it. (The minus sign in Faraday's law.)

Motional EMF (Voltage Induced in a Moving Wire)

When a straight conductor of length L moves with velocity v through a uniform magnetic field B, an EMF is induced:

EMF = B · L · v · sin(θ)

Where θ is the angle between v and B. When the wire moves perpendicular to B (θ = 90), this simplifies to:

EMF = B · L · v

This is a direct consequence of Faraday's law: as the wire moves, the magnetic flux through the circuit changes, inducing an EMF. Alternatively, you can derive it from the Lorentz force: the magnetic force on free electrons in the moving wire pushes them along the wire, creating a potential difference.

Worked Example: A 0.5 m wire moves at 2 m/s perpendicular to a 0.3 T magnetic field. What EMF is induced?

  • EMF = B · L · v = 0.3 · 0.5 · 2 = 0.3 V

This is the principle behind electric generators: rotating a coil in a magnetic field continuously changes the flux, producing alternating current.

Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (Generator)

For a conductor moving in a magnetic field: thumb = motion, index finger = field (B), middle finger = induced current direction.

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (Motor)

For force on a current in a magnetic field: index = field (B), middle = current (I), thumb = force/motion.

Alternating Current

V(t) = Vpeak · sin(ω · t) I(t) = Ipeak · sin(ω · t + φ)

RMS (Effective) Values

Vrms = Vpeak / √(2) = Vpeak · 0.707 Irms = Ipeak / √(2)

When someone says "220 V mains," they mean Vrms. Peak voltage = 220 · 1.414 = 311 V.

AC Power

P = Vrms · Irms · cos(φ)

Where cos(φ) is the power factor. For purely resistive loads, cos(φ) = 1.

Reactance and Impedance

ComponentReactancePhase
ResistorRV and I in phase
CapacitorXC = 1/(ω·C) = 1/(2·π·f·C)I leads V by 90°
InductorXL = ω·L = 2·π·f·LV leads I by 90°

Impedance (RLC series):

Z = √(R2 + (XL - XC)2) Vrms = Irms · Z

Resonance: When XL = XC, impedance is minimum (= R), current is maximum.

fresonance = 1 / (2·π·√(L·C))

Transformers

Vs / Vp = Ns / Np = Ip / Is
  • Vs, Vp = secondary and primary voltages
  • Ns, Np = number of turns
  • Step-up: Ns > Np (increases voltage, decreases current)
  • Step-down: Ns < Np (decreases voltage, increases current)
  • Power is conserved: Pp = Ps (ideal transformer)

Worked Example: A transformer with 100 primary turns and 500 secondary turns. Input: 220 V, 2 A. Find output voltage and current.

  • Vs = Vp · (Ns/Np) = 220 · (500/100) = 1100 V
  • Is = Ip · (Np/Ns) = 2 · (100/500) = 0.4 A
  • Check: Pp = 220(2) = 440 W, Ps = 1100(0.4) = 440 W. Confirmed.

Ohm's Law for AC Circuits

In a DC circuit, V = IR. In an AC circuit, the relationship is generalized using impedance:

Vrms = Irms · Z

Where Z is the impedance, which accounts for resistance and reactance.

For individual components:

  • Resistor: VR = I · R (voltage and current are in phase)
  • Inductor: VL = I · XL = I · ω · L (voltage leads current by 90°)
  • Capacitor: VC = I · XC = I / (ω · C) (current leads voltage by 90°)

Phase Relationships in AC (Detailed)

The phase angle φ between voltage and current in a series RLC circuit:

tan(φ) = (XL - XC) / R
  • If XL > XC: circuit is inductive, voltage leads current (φ > 0)
  • If XC > XL: circuit is capacitive, current leads voltage (φ < 0)
  • If XL = XC: resonance, φ = 0, circuit behaves as purely resistive

Memory aid: "ELI the ICE man" -- in an inductor (L), EMF (E) leads current (I). In a capacitor (C), current (I) leads EMF (E).

Series RLC Circuit (Detailed)

In a series RLC circuit, the same current flows through all components, but the voltages across each component have different phases:

Vtotal = √(VR2 + (VL - VC)2) Z = √(R2 + (XL - XC)2)

At resonance (XL = XC):

  • ω0 = 1/√(L·C), or f0 = 1/(2·π·√(L·C))
  • Impedance reaches minimum: Z = R
  • Current reaches maximum: Imax = V/R
  • VL and VC can each be much larger than the source voltage (voltage magnification)

Worked Example: A series RLC circuit has R = 100 ohm, L = 0.2 H, C = 50 muF, connected to 220 V (rms) at 50 Hz.

  • ω = 2·π·50 = 314 rad/s
  • XL = 314 x 0.2 = 62.8 ohm
  • XC = 1/(314 x 50 x 10-6) = 1/0.0157 = 63.7 ohm
  • Z = √(1002 + (62.8 - 63.7)2) = √(10000 + 0.81) = 100.004 ohm (nearly resonance!)
  • Irms = 220/100.004 = 2.2 A
  • Power = I2 · R = (2.2)2 · 100 = 484 W

Half-Wave and Full-Wave Rectification

Half-wave rectification uses a single diode to convert AC to pulsating DC:

  • The diode conducts only during the positive half-cycle of the AC input.
  • During the negative half-cycle, the diode blocks current.
  • Output: a series of positive pulses with gaps (zero voltage during blocked half-cycles).
  • Average output voltage: Vavg = Vpeak / π (approximately 0.318 x Vpeak).

Full-wave rectification (using four diodes in a bridge arrangement) allows current during both half-cycles, providing smoother DC output with Vavg = 2·Vpeak/π.

A smoothing capacitor is typically added in parallel with the output to fill in the gaps and produce a steadier DC voltage.

The Transistor Principle

A transistor contains two PN junctions arranged as either NPN or PNP. In an NPN transistor, a thin, lightly doped p-type base region sits between the n-type emitter and n-type collector. A small current injected into the base (Ib) allows a much larger current to flow from collector to emitter (Ic).

The current gain (β) is defined as:

β = Ic / Ib (typically 50-300) Ie = Ic + Ib (emitter current is the sum)

In a common-emitter configuration, the input signal is applied to the base-emitter junction and the amplified output is taken from the collector. A small variation in base current produces a large variation in collector current, enabling signal amplification.

Transistors operate in three regions: cutoff (both junctions reverse-biased, no current flows -- acts as open switch), saturation (both junctions forward-biased, maximum current -- acts as closed switch), and active region (base-emitter forward, base-collector reverse -- used for amplification).

This ability to act as both a switch and an amplifier makes the transistor the fundamental building block of all modern digital and analog electronics.

Self-Inductance

When current through a coil changes, the changing magnetic flux induces an EMF in the coil itself that opposes the change (back-EMF):

EMF = -L · dI/dt

Where L is the inductance in henrys (H).

Energy stored in an inductor:

E = (1/2) · L · I2

This is analogous to the energy in a capacitor: E = (1/2)·C·V2. In an LC circuit, energy oscillates between the inductor (magnetic) and capacitor (electric).

Exam Tips:
- RMS values, not peak values, are used in power calculations.
- A capacitor blocks DC but passes AC. An inductor passes DC but resists high-frequency AC.
- The minus sign in Faraday's law = Lenz's law (opposes the change).
- Transformers only work with AC, not DC (need changing flux).
- Ideal transformer conserves power: V·I is constant between primary and secondary.
- At resonance in RLC, impedance = R and current is maximum. This is the principle behind radio tuning.
- "ELI the ICE man" for phase relationships: E leads I in inductors, I leads E in capacitors.
- Half-wave rectification wastes half the AC cycle. Full-wave is more efficient.

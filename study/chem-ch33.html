Chapter 33

Saccharides (Carbohydrates)

Classification

Carbohydrates ("hydrates of carbon") are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or substances that yield them upon hydrolysis. The general formula for simple sugars is approximately (CH₂O)ₙ.

  • Monosaccharides: Single sugar units that cannot be hydrolyzed further. Examples: glucose, fructose, galactose.
  • Oligosaccharides: Short chains of 2–10 monosaccharides. Most important are disaccharides (2 units). Examples: sucrose, lactose, maltose.
  • Polysaccharides: Long chains of hundreds or thousands of monosaccharide units. Examples: starch, glycogen, cellulose.

Monosaccharides are further classified by:

  • Carbonyl type: Aldoses (aldehyde at C1) vs. ketoses (ketone, usually at C2).
  • Number of carbons: Trioses (3C), tetroses (4C), pentoses (5C), hexoses (6C).

Glucose is an aldo-hexose; fructose is a keto-hexose.

Structural Representations

Fischer Projection

A flat representation of a three-dimensional molecule: the carbon chain runs vertically (most oxidized carbon at top), horizontal bonds project toward the viewer, and vertical bonds project away. This is used to show the D/L configuration: if the OH on the highest-numbered chiral center (the penultimate carbon) points to the right, the sugar is D; if left, it is L. Almost all biologically relevant sugars are D-sugars.

Tollens (Open-Chain) Formula

Shows the full open-chain structure with all carbons, hydrogens, and functional groups explicitly drawn. Useful for showing oxidation states and identifying aldose vs. ketose.

Haworth Projection

When a monosaccharide cyclizes (the aldehyde or ketone reacts with an OH group on the same molecule), a ring forms. The Haworth projection shows this ring as a flat hexagon or pentagon viewed from the side, with substituents above or below the plane.

Isomerization in Carbohydrates

  • Aldose ↔ Ketose: Glucose (aldose) and fructose (ketose) can interconvert (e.g., enzymatically via glucose isomerase).
  • Pyranose ↔ Furanose: Six-membered ring (pyranose, from pyran) vs. five-membered ring (furanose, from furan). Glucose normally forms pyranose; fructose commonly forms furanose.
  • Alpha (α) and Beta (β) Anomers: When the ring closes, C1 (the anomeric carbon) becomes a new chiral center, creating two forms: α (C1-OH axial/down in Haworth) and β (C1-OH equatorial/up). In solution, they interconvert through mutarotation.
  • Epimers: Sugars that differ at only one chiral center. Glucose and galactose are C4 epimers. Glucose and mannose are C2 epimers.
  • D/L Enantiomers: Mirror images that differ at every chiral center. D-glucose and L-glucose are enantiomers. Biology uses D-sugars almost exclusively.

Reactions of Monosaccharides

  • Oxidation: Aldoses are reducing sugars - the open-chain aldehyde can be oxidized (positive Tollens'/Fehling's test). Oxidation of C1 gives an aldonic acid (e.g., gluconic acid); oxidation of C6 gives a uronic acid (e.g., glucuronic acid).
  • Reduction: Reduction of the carbonyl gives a sugar alcohol (alditol). Glucose → sorbitol (glucitol).
  • Esterification: OH groups can be esterified with phosphoric acid to form sugar phosphates, which are essential intermediates in glycolysis and other metabolic pathways (e.g., glucose-6-phosphate, fructose-1,6-bisphosphate). Sulfate esters of sugars also occur in proteoglycans, where sulfation of glycosaminoglycan chains increases their negative charge and water-binding capacity.
  • Glycosylation: The anomeric OH reacts with an alcohol (or another sugar's OH) to form a glycosidic bond. This is how disaccharides and polysaccharides are built. The bond can be α or β depending on the configuration at the anomeric carbon.

Inulin

Inulin is a storage polysaccharide found in plants such as chicory and Jerusalem artichoke. Unlike starch and glycogen (which are glucose polymers), inulin is a fructan - composed of fructose units linked by β(2→1) glycosidic bonds. In clinical medicine, inulin is used to measure glomerular filtration rate (GFR) because it is freely filtered by the kidney but neither reabsorbed nor secreted.

Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)

Glycosaminoglycans are unbranched heteropolysaccharides composed of repeating disaccharide units of an amino sugar (often N-acetylated) and a uronic acid (or galactose in keratan sulfate). Most GAGs are sulfated and carry a strong negative charge, enabling them to attract water and form viscous gels in the extracellular matrix. Major GAGs include:

  • Hyaluronic acid: found in synovial fluid and vitreous humour; the only non-sulfated GAG
  • Chondroitin sulfate: major component of cartilage
  • Heparan sulfate: on cell surfaces; heparin (a related molecule) is a clinical anticoagulant
  • Keratan sulfate: found in cornea and cartilage

Glycoproteins and Glycosylation

Glycoproteins are proteins with covalently attached oligosaccharide chains. Two types of linkage connect the sugar to the protein:

  • O-linked glycosylation: sugar attaches to the hydroxyl group of serine or threonine residues.
  • N-linked glycosylation: sugar attaches to the amide nitrogen of asparagine (within the consensus sequence Asn-X-Ser/Thr).

N-linked glycans include mannose-rich and complex subtypes. These oligosaccharide chains influence protein folding, stability, cell-cell recognition, and immune function. Most secreted and membrane proteins are glycoproteins.

Glucose Metabolism: Glycolysis and Aerobic Oxidation

While earlier sections describe carbohydrate structure, this section outlines how glucose is broken down for energy - a process central to both chemistry and medicine.

Glycolysis

Glycolysis is a ten-step metabolic pathway in the cytosol that converts one molecule of glucose (C₆) into two molecules of pyruvate (C₃). The net yield per glucose is 2 ATP (from substrate-level phosphorylation) and 2 NADH.

The pathway can be divided into two phases:

  • Energy investment phase (steps 1–5): glucose is phosphorylated to glucose-6-phosphate, rearranged to fructose-6-phosphate, phosphorylated again to fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, and then split into two three-carbon fragments (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate). This phase consumes 2 ATP.
  • Energy payoff phase (steps 6–10): each three-carbon unit is oxidised and converted to pyruvate, producing 2 NADH and 4 ATP (net gain: 2 ATP per glucose).

Anaerobic Fate of Pyruvate

Under anaerobic conditions (e.g., intense exercise, red blood cells), pyruvate is reduced to lactate by lactate dehydrogenase, regenerating NAD⁺ so that glycolysis can continue. The net energy yield under anaerobic conditions is only 2 ATP per glucose. Accumulation of lactate lowers tissue pH (lactic acidosis).

Aerobic Fate of Pyruvate

Under aerobic conditions, pyruvate enters the mitochondrion and undergoes oxidative decarboxylation to acetyl-CoA, catalysed by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (which requires thiamine/vitamin B₁ as a cofactor):

pyruvate + NAD⁺ + CoA–SH → acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH

Acetyl-CoA then enters the citric acid cycle (see Chapter 32) and the resulting NADH and FADH₂ feed into oxidative phosphorylation. The complete aerobic oxidation of one glucose yields approximately 30–32 ATP - roughly 15 times more than anaerobic glycolysis alone. This vast difference explains why aerobic organisms can sustain far greater energy demands.

Glycogen: Glucose Storage

Excess glucose can be stored as glycogen, a highly branched polymer of glucose with α(1→4) backbone linkages and α(1→6) branch points. Liver glycogen serves as a blood glucose reserve (released during fasting), while muscle glycogen provides fuel for local contraction. When energy is needed, glycogen is broken down by glycogen phosphorylase to release glucose-1-phosphate, which enters glycolysis.

Key Points - Chapter 33

  • Monosaccharides: aldoses vs. ketoses; D vs. L; α vs. β anomers.
  • Fischer projections show D/L; Haworth projections show ring form and α/β.
  • Epimers differ at one chiral center: glucose/galactose (C4), glucose/mannose (C2).
  • Glycosidic bonds connect sugars; α or β configuration matters enormously (digestibility).
  • Sucrose is NOT a reducing sugar (both anomeric carbons are locked). Lactose and maltose are reducing sugars.
  • Starch/glycogen (α-links, digestible) vs. cellulose (β-links, indigestible by humans).
  • Glycolysis: glucose → 2 pyruvate; net yield 2 ATP + 2 NADH (cytosol).
  • Anaerobic: pyruvate → lactate (2 ATP total). Aerobic: pyruvate → acetyl-CoA → citric acid cycle (~30–32 ATP total).
  • Glycogen is the storage form of glucose (liver for blood glucose, muscle for contraction).

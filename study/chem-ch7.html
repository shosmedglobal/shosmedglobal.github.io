7.1 Properties of Gases

Gases have no fixed shape or volume - they expand to fill any container. Gas molecules are widely spaced and move rapidly in random directions. Gases are highly compressible and exert pressure on the walls of their container through collisions.

7.2 Pressure

Pressure is force per unit area. Gas pressure results from billions of molecular collisions against surfaces each second.

P = F / A

Common pressure units: 1 atm = 101,325 Pa = 101.325 kPa = 760 mmHg = 760 torr.

7.3 Gas Laws

Boyle's Law (P–V at Constant T and n)

At constant temperature, the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to its pressure.

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

If you squeeze a gas (increase P), its volume decreases proportionally.

Charles's Law (V–T at Constant P and n)

At constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (in Kelvin).

V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

Gay-Lussac's Law (P–T at Constant V and n)

At constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Combined Gas Law

P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

where P = pressure (Pa or atm), V = volume (m³ or L), n = moles, R = gas constant (8.314 J/(mol·K) or 0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K)), T = absolute temperature (K).

Critical reminder: Always convert temperature to Kelvin before using any gas law! T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15.

Avogadro's Principle

Equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules. At standard temperature and pressure (STP: 0 °C, 1 atm), one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L.

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

The total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas:

Ptotal = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...

Each gas behaves independently as if the other gases were not present.

Worked Example: Ideal Gas Law

Problem: What volume does 2.0 mol of an ideal gas occupy at 25 °C and 1.0 atm?
Step 1: Convert T: 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 K
Step 2: Use PV = nRT → V = nRT/P
Step 3: V = (2.0)(0.08206)(298.15) / (1.0) = 48.9 L
Answer: The gas occupies approximately 48.9 L.

Worked Example: Boyle's Law

Problem: A gas occupies 5.0 L at 2.0 atm. What is its volume at 5.0 atm (constant T)?
Step 1: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Step 2: (2.0)(5.0) = (5.0)(V₂)
Step 3: V₂ = 10.0 / 5.0 = 2.0 L
Answer: 2.0 L. Volume decreases as pressure increases.

7.4 Air Composition and Breathing at Altitude

Dry air at sea level is approximately 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.93% argon, and 0.04% carbon dioxide by volume, with trace amounts of other gases. Using Dalton's Law at 101.3 kPa total pressure, the partial pressure of oxygen is 0.21 × 101.3 = 21.3 kPa (about 160 mmHg), and the partial pressure of nitrogen is about 79.2 kPa (600 mmHg).

At approximately 5,500 m elevation, total atmospheric pressure drops to around 380 mmHg - roughly half the sea-level value. The partial pressure of oxygen therefore falls to about 80 mmHg, which is insufficient for adequate oxygen transfer in the lungs of most unacclimatized individuals. This causes altitude sickness with symptoms of headache, nausea, and impaired cognition. Note also that exhaled air is saturated with water vapour at body temperature, contributing a partial pressure of approximately 47 mmHg.

Mnemonic: Gas Laws

"Boyle's is a Ball" - squeeze it (increase P), it gets smaller (decrease V). "Charles is a hot-air balloon" - heat it (increase T), it expands (increase V).

Key Points - Chapter 7

  • Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (inverse relationship at constant T).
  • Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (direct relationship at constant P).
  • Gay-Lussac's Law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (direct relationship at constant V).
  • Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT. At STP, 1 mol of gas = 22.4 L.
  • Dalton's Law: Ptotal = sum of all partial pressures.
  • Always use Kelvin for temperature in gas calculations!

Test Yourself - Chapter 7

1. A gas at 300 K occupies 10 L. If the temperature is raised to 600 K at constant pressure, what is the new volume?
Show Answer
Using Charles's Law: V₂ = V₁ × T₂/T₁ = 10 × 600/300 = 20 L. Doubling the absolute temperature doubles the volume.
2. A container holds O₂ at 400 mmHg and N₂ at 300 mmHg. What is the total pressure?
Show Answer
Ptotal = 400 + 300 = 700 mmHg (Dalton's Law).
3. How many moles of gas are in a 11.2 L container at STP?
Show Answer
At STP, 1 mol = 22.4 L. So n = 11.2 / 22.4 = 0.50 mol.

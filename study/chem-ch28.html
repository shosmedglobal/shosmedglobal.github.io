Chapter 28

Amines and Nitrocompounds

Amines

Amines are organic derivatives of ammonia (NH₃) in which one or more hydrogen atoms have been replaced by organic groups. They are named by listing the alkyl groups followed by "-amine" (e.g., methylamine, diethylamine) or by IUPAC rules using the suffix "-amine."

Note: The classification is based on how many carbons are attached to nitrogen, NOT the carbon bearing the nitrogen. This differs from alcohol classification!

Physical Properties

  • Small amines have fishy, unpleasant odors. Larger diamines have especially foul smells (putrescine, cadaverine).
  • Primary and secondary amines can form hydrogen bonds (N–H...N), giving them higher boiling points than alkanes but lower than alcohols of similar size.
  • Small amines are water-soluble.

Basicity

Amines are bases because the lone pair on nitrogen can accept a proton: R–NH₂ + H₂O ↔ R–NH₃⁺ + OH−.

Basicity order (in water): secondary alkylamine > primary alkylamine > ammonia > aromatic amine (aniline). Aromatic amines are weaker bases because the lone pair is delocalized into the ring.

Diazotization

Primary aromatic amines react with nitrous acid (HNO₂) at low temperatures (0–5°C) to form diazonium salts (Ar–N₂⁺). These are versatile intermediates: the N₂⁺ group can be replaced by –OH, –Cl, –Br, –CN, –H, or coupled with another aromatic ring to form azo dyes (brightly colored compounds).

Important Amines

  • Aniline (aminobenzene, C₆H₅NH₂): Simplest aromatic amine; starting material for dyes and pharmaceuticals.
  • Choline ((CH₃)₃N⁺CH₂CH₂OH): A quaternary ammonium compound; component of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine and of phospholipids (phosphatidylcholine).

Nitrocompounds

Nitrocompounds contain the nitro group (–NO₂). They are named with the prefix "nitro-" (e.g., nitromethane, nitrobenzene). The nitro group is strongly electron-withdrawing, which makes nitroaromatic compounds useful as explosives (TNT = 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene) and as starting materials for amine synthesis (nitro group can be reduced to amino group).

Key Points — Chapter 28

  • Amines are classified as 1°, 2°, 3° by number of carbons on nitrogen (not on the carbon bearing N).
  • Amines are bases (lone pair on N accepts protons). Alkylamines are stronger bases than aniline.
  • Diazotization of primary aromatic amines gives diazonium salts — versatile synthetic intermediates.
  • Choline is a quaternary ammonium compound important in neurotransmission and membrane lipids.
  • Nitro groups (–NO₂) are electron-withdrawing; can be reduced to amino groups (–NH₂).

Chapter 4

Work, Energy, and Simple Machines

Work

W = F · d · cos(θ)

Where θ is the angle between force and displacement.

  • Force parallel to displacement: W = Fd (θ = 0)
  • Force perpendicular to displacement: W = 0 (θ = 90)
  • Force opposes displacement: W = -Fd (θ = 180)

Unit: joule (J) = N·m

Power

P = W / t (average power) P = F · v (instantaneous power, when F parallel to v)

Unit: watt (W) = J/s. Also: 1 horsepower = 746 W.

Mechanical Efficiency

η = Wuseful / Wtotal = Poutput / Pinput

Efficiency is always less than 100% in real machines due to friction and other losses.

Kinetic Energy

KE = (1/2) · m · v2

Always positive or zero. Doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy.

Gravitational Potential Energy

PE = m · g · h

Where h is height above a chosen reference level.

Elastic Potential Energy (Spring)

PE_spring = (1/2) · k · x2

Where k is the spring constant and x is the compression/extension from equilibrium.

Young's Modulus (Modulus of Elasticity)

Young's modulus quantifies the stiffness of a solid material in the elastic (reversible) regime:

E = stress / strain = (F/A) / (ΔL / L0)

Here F is the applied force, A is the cross-sectional area, ΔL is the change in length, and L0 is the original length. The unit is the pascal (Pa), since both stress (F/A) and strain (ΔL/L0, dimensionless) combine to give Pa.

Typical values of Young's modulus:

MaterialE (approximate)
Steel~200 GPa
Copper~120 GPa
Cortical bone~15 GPa
Wood (along grain)~10 GPa
Rubber~0.01 GPa

A higher value means the material is stiffer and resists deformation more strongly. Young's modulus applies only within the elastic limit -- beyond that point, permanent (plastic) deformation occurs and Hooke's law no longer holds. The stress-strain curve is linear in the elastic region, with slope equal to E.

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

When only conservative forces (gravity, springs) do work:

KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2

When friction is present:

KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + Wfriction

Worked Example 1: A 2 kg ball dropped from 20 m. Speed at ground?

  • mgh = (1/2)mv2 ⇒ v2 = 2gh = 2(10)(20) = 400 ⇒ v = 20 m/s

Worked Example 2: A 5 kg block slides down a 10 m ramp from height 6 m. Friction = 8 N. Speed at bottom?

  • mgh = (1/2)mv2 + f·d
  • 5(10)(6) = 2.5·v2 + 8(10)
  • 300 = 2.5·v2 + 80 ⇒ v2 = 88 ⇒ v = 9.38 m/s

Worked Example 3: A 60 kg person climbs 5 m of stairs in 10 s. Power?

  • W = mgh = 60(10)(5) = 3000 J
  • P = 3000/10 = 300 W

Worked Example 4: A spring (k = 200 N/m) is compressed 0.1 m. A 0.5 kg ball is launched horizontally from a table 1.2 m high. How far from the table base does it land?

  • Spring PE = (1/2)(200)(0.01) = 1 J
  • KE = 1 J ⇒ (1/2)(0.5)v2 = 1 ⇒ v = 2 m/s (horizontal)
  • Time to fall: h = (1/2)g·t2 ⇒ 1.2 = 5t2 ⇒ t = 0.49 s
  • Range = v·t = 2(0.49) = 0.98 m

Torque and Equilibrium

Torque = F · d · sin(θ), where d is the distance from the pivot. Unit: N·m.

Static equilibrium requires:

  1. Sum of all forces = 0 (no translational acceleration)
  2. Sum of all torques = 0 (no rotational acceleration)

The Lever

F1 · d1 = F2 · d2

Mechanical advantage = d1/d2 = F2/F1.

Exam Tips:
- Work done by gravity on a horizontal surface is zero (perpendicular to displacement).
- Centripetal force does zero work (always perpendicular to velocity).
- Doubling speed quadruples KE and stopping distance.
- Mechanical energy is NOT conserved when friction is present.
- Efficiency can never exceed 100%.

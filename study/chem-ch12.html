Chapter 12

Chemical Equilibrium

12.1 Reversible Reactions

Many chemical reactions do not go to completion. Instead, they proceed in both forward and reverse directions simultaneously. We write such reactions with a double arrow (⇌):

A + B ⇌ C + D

12.2 Dynamic Equilibrium

When the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction, the system is at dynamic equilibrium. At this point, concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal). Both reactions continue occurring — it is "dynamic" because individual molecules keep reacting, even though the overall concentrations appear static.

12.3 The Equilibrium Constant (K)

For the general reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, the equilibrium constant is:

K = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b

Products go in the numerator; reactants in the denominator. Each concentration is raised to the power of its coefficient.

  • K >> 1: Products strongly favoured at equilibrium.
  • K << 1: Reactants strongly favoured at equilibrium.
  • K ≈ 1: Significant amounts of both present.

Pure solids and pure liquids are not included in the equilibrium expression (their concentrations are constant).

12.4 Le Chatelier's Principle

When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to counteract the disturbance and re-establish equilibrium.

Change AppliedSystem ResponseDirection of Shift
Increase concentration of a reactantSystem consumes excess reactantShifts right (toward products)
Decrease concentration of a productSystem produces more productShifts right
Increase pressure (for gases)System favours side with fewer moles of gasToward fewer gas moles
Increase temperature (exothermic rxn)System absorbs excess heatShifts left (reverse)
Increase temperature (endothermic rxn)System absorbs excess heatShifts right (forward)
Add a catalystSpeeds up both directions equallyNo shift (reaches equilibrium faster)

Worked Example: Le Chatelier's Principle

Reaction: N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g)   ΔH = −92 kJ (exothermic)
Q: What happens if we increase pressure?
Analysis: Left side: 1 + 3 = 4 moles of gas. Right side: 2 moles of gas. Higher pressure favours the side with fewer gas moles.
Answer: Equilibrium shifts right, producing more NH3.

Worked Example: Calculating K

Problem: For the reaction H2(g) + I2(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), at equilibrium [H2] = 0.10 M, [I2] = 0.10 M, [HI] = 0.80 M. Calculate K.
Step 1: K = [HI]² / ([H2][I2]) = (0.80)² / (0.10 × 0.10)
Step 2: K = 0.64 / 0.01 = 64
Answer: K = 64. Products are strongly favoured at this temperature.

Key Points — Chapter 12

  • Dynamic equilibrium: forward rate = reverse rate; concentrations are constant.
  • K = [products]coefficients / [reactants]coefficients.
  • K >> 1 favours products; K << 1 favours reactants.
  • Le Chatelier's principle: a stressed system shifts to oppose the change.
  • Increasing concentration of a reactant shifts equilibrium toward products.
  • Catalysts do not shift equilibrium; they only speed up reaching it.

Test Yourself — Chapter 12

1. For the endothermic reaction CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO2(g), what happens if temperature is increased?
Show Answer
The equilibrium shifts right (toward products) because the system absorbs the added heat, favouring the endothermic direction. More CaO and CO2 are produced.
2. Write the equilibrium expression for: 2SO2(g) + O2(g) ⇌ 2SO3(g)
Show Answer
K = [SO3]² / ([SO2]²[O2])
3. A catalyst is added to a reaction at equilibrium. Does the equilibrium shift?
Show Answer
No. A catalyst increases both the forward and reverse reaction rates equally. The equilibrium position stays the same; it is simply reached more quickly.

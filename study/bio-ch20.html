Chapter 20

Evolution of Populations

When any of the five Hardy-Weinberg conditions are violated, allele frequencies change — and evolution occurs. This chapter explores the five forces of microevolution, the types of natural selection, and how new species arise.

20.1 The Five Causes of Microevolution

Microevolution is evolutionary change within a population, measured as changes in allele frequencies over generations. Five main mechanisms drive it:

1. Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is the random change in allele frequencies due to chance events, especially in small populations. Unlike natural selection, drift is not adaptive — it has no "direction" and can cause beneficial alleles to be lost or harmful alleles to increase purely by chance.

Two important scenarios of genetic drift:

Bottleneck Effect: A dramatic reduction in population size (due to a natural disaster, epidemic, or other catastrophe) randomly eliminates a large proportion of individuals. The surviving population has a different allele frequency than the original population, purely by chance. Example: Northern elephant seals were hunted down to approximately 20 individuals in the 1890s. The current population, though recovered in numbers, has extremely low genetic diversity.

Founder Effect: A small group of individuals colonizes a new area, founding a new population. The allele frequencies in this small group are unlikely to be representative of the larger source population. Example: The Amish community in Pennsylvania descended from a small number of German founders. Certain rare genetic conditions (such as Ellis-van Creveld syndrome) are much more common in this community than in the general population.

Key Principle

Genetic drift has a stronger effect in smaller populations. In very large populations, random fluctuations average out and allele frequencies remain relatively stable.

2. Gene Flow (Migration)

Gene flow is the transfer of alleles between populations through the movement of individuals (or their gametes). When an individual migrates from one population to another and reproduces, they introduce new alleles into the recipient population.

Effects of gene flow:

  • Tends to reduce differences between populations (makes them more similar genetically).
  • Introduces new alleles that may increase genetic variation within the receiving population.
  • Can counteract the effects of natural selection or genetic drift.

3. Mutation

Mutation is the ultimate source of all new genetic variation. It introduces new alleles into a population. However, mutation rates are very low (approximately 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁹ per base pair per generation), so mutation alone changes allele frequencies very slowly. Mutation becomes important when combined with other evolutionary forces, especially natural selection.

4. Nonrandom Mating

Hardy-Weinberg assumes random mating. In reality, mating is often nonrandom:

Assortative mating: Individuals preferentially mate with others who are similar (positive assortative mating) or dissimilar (negative assortative mating) for certain traits. Positive assortative mating increases homozygosity; negative increases heterozygosity. Neither changes allele frequencies directly, but both alter genotype frequencies.

Inbreeding: Mating between closely related individuals. Inbreeding increases homozygosity throughout the genome, which increases the chance of expressing harmful recessive alleles. This is why recessive genetic disorders are more common in inbred populations.

5. Natural Selection

Natural selection is the differential survival and reproduction of individuals based on their phenotype. It is the only evolutionary force that consistently produces adaptation — the accumulation of traits that improve fitness in a given environment.

Fitness in evolutionary terms is the reproductive success of an individual relative to others in the population. The genotype that produces the most surviving offspring has the highest fitness.

Mechanism Random or Directed? Effect on Variation Produces Adaptation?
Genetic drift Random Reduces (especially in small populations) No
Gene flow Neither Can increase within populations; decreases between populations No
Mutation Random Increases (source of all new alleles) No (provides raw material only)
Nonrandom mating Directed by preference Changes genotype frequencies, not allele frequencies directly No (does not change allele frequencies)
Natural selection Directed by environment Can increase or decrease depending on type Yes

20.2 Types of Natural Selection

Natural selection acts on the distribution of phenotypes in a population in three main ways:

A. Stabilizing Selection

Selects against both extremes and favors the intermediate phenotype. This reduces phenotypic variation but does not shift the mean. Example: Human birth weight — babies that are too light or too heavy have higher mortality. Intermediate-weight babies have the best survival.

B. Directional Selection

Favors individuals at one extreme of the phenotypic range. This shifts the mean of the population toward one end. Example: Antibiotic resistance in bacteria — bacteria exposed to an antibiotic are selected for increasing resistance over time. Another example: Peppered moths during the Industrial Revolution became darker as pollution darkened tree bark.

C. Disruptive Selection

Favors both extremes and selects against the intermediate phenotype. This increases variation and can potentially split the population into two distinct groups. Example: African seed-cracker finches — birds with very large or very small beaks are successful at cracking different-sized seeds, while intermediate-beak birds are less efficient at either.

Selection Type Favors Effect on Mean Effect on Variation
Stabilizing Intermediate phenotype No change Decreases
Directional One extreme Shifts toward the favored extreme May decrease
Disruptive Both extremes May not change (or splits) Increases

20.3 Genetic Variation and Adaptive Evolution

Genetic variation is essential for evolution by natural selection. Without variation, there is nothing for selection to act upon. Sources of genetic variation include:

  • Mutation: The original source of all new alleles.
  • Sexual reproduction: Independent assortment and crossing over during meiosis create new combinations of alleles. Random fertilization adds further variation.
  • Gene flow: Introduction of alleles from other populations.

Adaptive evolution occurs when natural selection increases the frequency of alleles that improve fitness in a given environment. Over many generations, this leads to populations becoming progressively better suited to their environments.

Mechanisms that maintain genetic variation in populations include:

  • Heterozygote advantage: Heterozygotes have higher fitness than either homozygote. Example: Sickle cell trait (Aa) provides resistance to malaria while avoiding sickle cell disease. Both the A and a alleles are maintained in malarial regions.
  • Frequency-dependent selection: The fitness of a phenotype depends on how common it is. Rare phenotypes may have an advantage.
  • Balancing selection: A general term for selection that maintains multiple alleles in a population.

Clines and Geographic Variation

A cline is a gradual change in a trait or allele frequency across a geographic area, typically following an environmental gradient. Body size in many mammal species, for example, increases at higher latitudes (Bergmann's rule), forming a cline. A striking demonstration comes from the fruit fly Drosophila subobscura, which was accidentally introduced from Europe to the Americas in the late 1970s. Within just two decades, American populations independently evolved wing-length clines that mirrored European patterns — larger wings at higher latitudes — showing that natural selection can rapidly generate predictable geographic variation.

20.4 Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species — the process by which one population becomes two reproductively isolated populations that can no longer interbreed.

Allopatric Speciation

Allopatric ("different homeland") speciation occurs when a population is physically divided by a geographic barrier (mountain range, river, ocean, glacier, etc.). The separated populations evolve independently, accumulating genetic differences through mutation, genetic drift, and different selection pressures. Eventually, if they diverge enough, they can no longer interbreed even if the barrier is removed.

This is considered the most common mode of speciation. Example: The formation of the Isthmus of Panama separated marine organisms on either side, leading to the divergence of previously interbreeding populations.

Sympatric Speciation

Sympatric ("same homeland") speciation occurs without geographic separation. New species arise within the same area through mechanisms like:

  • Polyploidy: Especially in plants. Errors in cell division can produce individuals with extra sets of chromosomes. These polyploid individuals may be reproductively isolated from the parent population because crosses between diploid and polyploid organisms produce sterile offspring.
  • Habitat differentiation: Subpopulations specialize on different resources within the same area.
  • Sexual selection: Mate preferences diverge within a population.
Feature Allopatric Speciation Sympatric Speciation
Geographic separation Yes (required) No
Mechanism of isolation Physical barrier Polyploidy, habitat specialization, or behavioral
Frequency Most common Less common in animals, more common in plants
Speed Usually gradual Can be rapid (especially polyploidy)
KEY POINTS — Chapter 20
  • Five forces of microevolution: genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, nonrandom mating, natural selection.
  • Genetic drift: random, stronger in small populations. Includes bottleneck and founder effects.
  • Gene flow reduces differences between populations.
  • Mutation is the ultimate source of all genetic variation.
  • Natural selection is the only force that produces adaptation.
  • Three types of selection: stabilizing (favors middle), directional (shifts mean), disruptive (favors extremes).
  • Heterozygote advantage maintains both alleles in a population.
  • Allopatric speciation requires geographic barrier; sympatric does not.

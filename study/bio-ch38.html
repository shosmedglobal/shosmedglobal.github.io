Chapter 38

Nutrition

Every cell in your body requires energy to function and building materials for growth and repair. Nutrition is the science of how organisms obtain and use food. This chapter covers what the body needs, how it regulates appetite, and the essential nutrients that must come from the diet.

Nutritional Requirements

The body needs food for three main purposes:

  1. Chemical energy - fuel for cellular respiration (ATP production). Measured in kilocalories (kcal) or kilojoules (kJ).
  2. Biosynthesis - raw materials (carbon skeletons, amino acids, fatty acids) to build the body's own molecules: proteins, membranes, DNA, hormones.
  3. Essential nutrients - substances the body cannot synthesize and must obtain from food: essential amino acids, essential fatty acids, vitamins, and minerals.

Appetite Regulation and Homeostasis

The body tightly regulates food intake and energy balance through hormones:

  • Leptin - produced by adipose (fat) tissue. Circulating leptin levels are proportional to fat stores. Leptin signals the hypothalamus to reduce appetite and increase energy expenditure. When fat stores decrease, leptin falls, appetite increases.
  • Ghrelin - produced by the stomach. Stimulates hunger. Levels rise before meals and fall after eating.
  • Insulin - also acts on the hypothalamus as a long-term satiety signal.

Nutritional Imbalances

  • Overnourishment - consuming more calories than expended. Excess energy is stored as fat, leading to obesity, which increases risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers.
  • Undernourishment - consuming fewer calories than needed. The body breaks down its own tissues for energy, leading to muscle wasting, weakened immunity, and organ failure.
  • Malnourishment - deficiency of one or more essential nutrients, even if total calorie intake may be adequate. Can cause specific deficiency diseases (scurvy, rickets, anemia, etc.).

Metabolism of Macronutrients

Carbohydrates

The body's preferred energy source. Digested into monosaccharides (mainly glucose). Glucose enters glycolysis and cellular respiration to produce ATP. Excess glucose is stored as glycogen in the liver and muscles, or converted to fat.

Proteins

Digested into amino acids. Used primarily for building and repairing tissues, making enzymes, hormones, and antibodies. Amino acids can also be used for energy if carbohydrates and fats are insufficient. The nitrogen group is removed (deamination) and excreted as urea; the remaining carbon skeleton enters metabolic pathways.

Adult humans require nine essential amino acids that cannot be synthesized endogenously: histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine. Complete protein sources (containing all nine) include meat, fish, eggs, and dairy. Most individual plant proteins are "incomplete" (lacking one or more essential amino acids), but combining complementary plant sources - such as beans with rice, or hummus with pita - provides the full set. Infants additionally require arginine.

Fats (Lipids)

Digested into fatty acids and glycerol. Fats provide the most energy per gram (9 kcal/g vs. 4 kcal/g for carbohydrates and proteins). They are essential for cell membrane structure, hormone production, insulation, and absorption of fat-soluble vitamins. Stored in adipose tissue as triglycerides.

Vitamins

Vitamins are organic molecules needed in small amounts for normal metabolism. The body generally cannot synthesize them, so they must come from the diet.

Fat-Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K)

Absorbed with dietary fats, stored in the liver and adipose tissue. Because they are stored, they can accumulate to toxic levels if consumed in excess.

Water-Soluble Vitamins (B Complex, C)

Dissolve in water and are not stored in large amounts. Excess is excreted in urine, so they must be consumed regularly. Toxicity is rare.

Essential Minerals

Minerals are inorganic elements needed for various body functions:

  • Calcium (Ca) - bone and tooth structure, muscle contraction, nerve signaling, blood clotting
  • Iron (Fe) - component of hemoglobin and myoglobin; oxygen transport. Deficiency causes iron-deficiency anemia.
  • Sodium (Na) and Potassium (K) - electrolytes essential for nerve impulse transmission and fluid balance
  • Phosphorus (P) - bone structure; component of ATP, DNA, and cell membranes
  • Iodine (I) - required for thyroid hormone synthesis. Deficiency causes goiter (enlarged thyroid).
  • Zinc (Zn) - enzyme function, immune health, wound healing
  • Magnesium (Mg) - enzyme cofactor; muscle and nerve function
  • Sulfur (S) - component of amino acids methionine and cysteine; essential for disulfide bonds in proteins
  • Chloride (Cl) - major extracellular anion; maintains osmotic balance; required for HCl production in the stomach
  • Fluoride (F) - strengthens tooth enamel and bone mineral
  • Copper (Cu) - cofactor for enzymes involved in iron metabolism and connective tissue formation
  • Manganese (Mn) - needed for bone formation and certain enzyme functions
  • Cobalt (Co) - an integral component of vitamin B12
  • Selenium (Se) - part of glutathione peroxidase, an important antioxidant enzyme
  • Chromium (Cr) - enhances insulin signaling and glucose metabolism
  • Molybdenum (Mo) - cofactor for enzymes including xanthine oxidase

Work, Rest, and the Human Circadian Rhythm

Human physiology is governed by circadian rhythms - approximately 24-hour cycles coordinated by the body's master clock in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus. The SCN receives direct light input from the retina and synchronizes peripheral clocks throughout the body. These rhythms regulate the sleep-wake cycle, hormone secretion (cortisol peaks in the early morning, melatonin rises in the evening to promote sleep), core body temperature fluctuations, and metabolic activity.

A healthy daily regimen aligned with circadian biology - consistent sleep and wake times, regular meals, alternating periods of physical activity and rest - is essential for maintaining physiological and psychological health. Chronic disruption of circadian rhythms through shift work, irregular sleep schedules, excessive screen exposure before bed, or persistent jet lag is associated with increased risks of obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and impaired immune function.

Prolonged psychological stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, sustaining elevated cortisol levels beyond the normal morning peak. Chronic cortisol elevation suppresses immune function, raises blood pressure, promotes visceral fat accumulation, and impairs memory consolidation in the hippocampus. Conditions in which psychological factors contribute to or exacerbate physical disease are termed psychosomatic disorders and include stress-related hypertension, irritable bowel syndrome, tension headaches, and exacerbation of autoimmune conditions. Adequate sleep (7–9 hours for adults), regular physical exercise, and deliberate relaxation are as important for overall health as proper nutrition.

Substance Use Disorders and Addiction

Substance use disorder (the current DSM-5 term for what was historically called drug addiction) is a chronic, relapsing condition characterized by compulsive seeking and use of a substance despite harmful consequences. The underlying neurobiology centers on the brain's mesolimbic reward pathway, particularly dopaminergic neurons projecting from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens. Addictive substances hijack this circuit by causing abnormally large dopamine surges that powerfully reinforce drug-seeking behavior.

With repeated exposure, the brain adapts through neuroplastic changes: dopamine receptor density decreases (downregulation), and the baseline reward threshold rises. This produces tolerance (progressively larger doses are needed for the same effect) and withdrawal (unpleasant physical and psychological symptoms when the substance is absent), which together drive a self-perpetuating cycle of use.

Major Categories of Addictive Substances

  • Alcohol (ethanol) is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance globally. It acts as a CNS depressant, enhancing GABAA receptor activity and inhibiting NMDA glutamate receptors. Chronic heavy use causes hepatic steatosis progressing to cirrhosis, cardiomyopathy, peripheral neuropathy, and Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (thiamine deficiency-related brain damage). Alcohol use disorder is associated with increased risks of cancer (especially liver, esophageal, and oropharyngeal).
  • Nicotine (in tobacco) activates nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in the brain's reward circuitry, making it highly addictive. Tobacco smoking is the leading preventable cause of death worldwide, causing lung cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and cardiovascular disease.
  • Opioids (morphine, heroin, fentanyl, and prescription painkillers such as oxycodone) bind to mu-opioid receptors, producing intense analgesia and euphoria. They carry extreme addiction potential and high overdose risk due to respiratory depression.
  • Sedative-hypnotics including benzodiazepines (e.g., diazepam) and barbiturates enhance GABAergic inhibition. They are prescribed for anxiety and insomnia but produce physical dependence with chronic use, and withdrawal can be life-threatening (seizures).
  • Stimulants such as cocaine (blocks dopamine reuptake) and amphetamines (promote dopamine release) cause intense but short-lived euphoria, followed by craving and depressive crashes.
  • Caffeine, the most widely consumed psychoactive compound, is a mild adenosine receptor antagonist that produces alertness. It generates mild physical dependence (withdrawal headaches), but rarely produces the compulsive patterns seen with other substances.

The consequences of substance use disorders extend beyond individual health to encompass social harm: impaired occupational function, family disruption, increased rates of accidents and violence, and economic burden on healthcare systems. Treatment typically involves a combination of medical management (pharmacotherapy for withdrawal and craving, such as methadone or buprenorphine for opioid use disorder), behavioral therapy (cognitive-behavioral therapy, motivational interviewing), and sustained social support. Prevention through public health education - particularly targeting adolescents and young adults, who are the most vulnerable demographic - remains the most effective long-term strategy.

Key Points - Nutrition
  • Food provides energy (ATP), building materials (biosynthesis), and essential nutrients.
  • Leptin (from fat) suppresses appetite; ghrelin (from stomach) stimulates hunger.
  • Overnourishment = excess calories. Undernourishment = insufficient calories. Malnourishment = missing specific nutrients.
  • Carbohydrates and proteins = 4 kcal/g. Fats = 9 kcal/g (most energy-dense).
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are stored and can accumulate. Water-soluble vitamins (B, C) are not stored and need regular intake.
  • Key minerals: calcium (bones), iron (hemoglobin), iodine (thyroid), sodium/potassium (nerve function).

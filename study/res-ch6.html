Chapter 6

How to Write a Case Report

6.1 The Case Report as Your Entry Point

Case reports are where most student researchers begin, and for good reason. They require no complex statistics, no large datasets, and no IRB approval process (in most institutions). What they do require is a genuinely interesting clinical encounter, careful attention to detail, and the ability to place your case within the context of existing medical knowledge. A well-crafted case report demonstrates that you can identify clinically significant observations, review the literature, and write a publishable manuscript - skills that transfer to every other type of research.

6.2 Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Identify a Reportable Case

Not every interesting patient encounter warrants a publication. Ask yourself: would other clinicians benefit from knowing about this? A case report should contain at least one of these elements:

  • An atypical presentation of a recognized disease
  • A novel diagnostic approach or technique
  • An unexpected response to treatment (positive or negative)
  • A rare disease or condition with limited published descriptions
  • An important clinical lesson or teaching point
  • A previously unreported adverse drug reaction

Step 2: Obtain Consent and Navigate Ethics Review

Patient consent: Written informed consent from the patient (or their legal guardian) is required before you can publish any identifiable clinical information. Most journals require a signed consent form, and your institution likely has a standard template.

IRB considerations: Many institutions classify single-patient case reports as exempt from full IRB review, but policies vary. Check with your institution's ethics office. Some require a formal exemption letter, which you may need to include with your journal submission. When uncertain, submit an inquiry to the IRB - it is always better to ask.

Step 3: Collect All Clinical Data

Before writing, gather everything: presenting symptoms, medical history, physical examination findings, laboratory values, imaging results, pathology reports, treatments administered, and outcomes. Be thorough at this stage - going back to collect missing data after you have started writing is inefficient and sometimes impossible.

Step 4: Review the Literature

Search PubMed for previously published similar cases. You must understand what has been reported before so you can articulate precisely how your case is different or additive. This search also helps you identify which journals have published comparable reports - valuable intelligence for choosing your target journal.

Step 5: Write the Manuscript

Title: Be descriptive and specific. Include the key clinical finding and the diagnosis. Example: “Pembrolizumab-Induced Fulminant Myocarditis in a Patient with Metastatic Melanoma: A Case Report.”

Abstract (150–250 words, usually unstructured): Include brief background context (one to two sentences), a concise case summary (three to five sentences), and the main conclusion or clinical lesson (one to two sentences).

Introduction (one to two paragraphs): Provide background on the disease or clinical scenario. Explain why this case is worth reporting. Conclude with a statement of purpose: “We present a case of [condition] that demonstrates [key point].”

Case Presentation: Write a chronological narrative of the patient encounter. Include demographics (age, sex), chief complaint, relevant medical history, examination findings, diagnostic workup with specific values, treatment decisions, and clinical outcome. Use concrete numbers and dates. Include relevant images with all patient identifiers removed.

Discussion: This is the section that distinguishes an excellent case report from a mediocre one. Explain the significance of your case in the context of published literature. How does it compare to previously reported cases? What are the possible pathophysiological mechanisms? What clinical lessons should practitioners take away? What are the limitations of a single-case observation?

Conclusion: Two to three sentences summarizing the core message of your report.

Step 6: Apply the CARE Guidelines

The CARE (CAse REport) guidelines provide a standardized checklist for reporting case reports. Review this checklist before submission to confirm you have included all recommended elements. An increasing number of journals require explicit CARE compliance.

Step 7: Choose a Journal and Submit

For case reports, consider:

  • Specialty-specific case report journals (BMJ Case Reports, Journal of Medical Case Reports, Cureus)
  • Specialty journals that accept case reports as a manuscript type (verify in the journal's author guidelines)
  • Open-access journals with no or low article processing charges

6.3 Case Report Manuscript Template

Manuscript Structure Template

Title: [Key Finding] in [Patient Descriptor]: A Case Report

Abstract (150–250 words, unstructured)

Introduction (250–400 words)
• Paragraph 1: Background on the condition
• Paragraph 2: Why this case is novel; transition to case description

Case Presentation (500–1,000 words)
• Demographics and chief complaint
• Medical/surgical history
• Physical examination
• Diagnostic workup (labs, imaging, pathology)
• Treatment and clinical course
• Outcome and follow-up

Discussion (500–800 words)
• Comparison to existing literature
• Possible mechanisms
• Clinical implications and lessons
• Limitations of a single case

Conclusion (50–100 words)

References (15–30 citations typical)

Key Takeaways

  • A publishable case report must offer genuine novelty or clear educational value
  • Always secure written patient consent before beginning
  • The Discussion is what separates an outstanding case report from a forgettable one
  • Follow the CARE guidelines checklist rigorously
  • Realistic timeline: two to four weeks for writing, two to six months to reach publication

Common Mistakes

  • Reporting a case that has been well-described multiple times (always search the literature first)
  • Writing a superficial Discussion that merely restates the Case Presentation
  • Forgetting to obtain written patient consent before starting the manuscript
  • Including excessive irrelevant clinical detail that distracts from the core message
  • Submitting without reviewing the CARE guidelines checklist

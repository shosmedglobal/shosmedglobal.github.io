Chapter 15

Single-Gene Disorders

Single-gene disorders (also called Mendelian disorders or monogenic disorders) result from mutations in a single gene. Although individually rare, collectively they affect millions of people worldwide. Understanding how these mutations arise and how they are inherited is essential for medical genetics.

15.1 Types of Gene Mutations

A gene mutation is a permanent change in the DNA sequence of a gene. Mutations can be classified by the type of change they cause:

Point Mutations (Substitutions)

A single nucleotide is replaced by another. The effects depend on how the change affects the protein:

Type What Happens Effect on Protein Example
Missense One codon changed to code for a different amino acid Altered protein (may or may not affect function) Sickle cell disease (glutamic acid changed to valine)
Nonsense One codon changed to a premature stop codon Truncated (shortened) protein, usually nonfunctional Some forms of β-thalassemia
Silent Codon changes but still codes for the same amino acid No effect on protein (due to genetic code redundancy) GAA to GAG (both code for glutamic acid)

Insertions and Deletions

One or more nucleotides are added (insertion) or removed (deletion) from the DNA sequence.

  • If the number of nucleotides inserted or deleted is not a multiple of 3, a frameshift mutation occurs. This shifts the entire reading frame downstream, producing a completely different (and usually nonfunctional) protein.
  • If the number is a multiple of 3, amino acids are added or removed without shifting the frame (though the protein may still be abnormal).
Frameshift Mutations Are Devastating

Frameshift mutations are typically much more harmful than point mutations because they alter every codon downstream of the mutation, usually producing a completely nonfunctional protein.

Counting Nucleotides and the Reading Frame

A frameshift occurs specifically when the number of inserted or deleted nucleotides is not a multiple of three. Deleting one nucleotide causes a frameshift; deleting two consecutive nucleotides also causes a frameshift. However, deleting exactly three nucleotides removes one entire codon without shifting the downstream reading frame (although one amino acid is lost from the protein). This distinction between frameshifting and non-frameshifting indels is commonly tested on exams.

RNA Processing and Regulatory Mutations

RNA processing mutations affect splice sites - the boundaries between exons and introns. When a splice site is altered, normal mRNA splicing is disrupted, potentially causing exon skipping or intron retention, both of which produce an abnormal protein. Regulatory mutations occur in promoter regions, enhancers, or other non-coding regulatory elements. They alter the amount, timing, or tissue-specificity of gene expression without changing the protein's amino acid sequence itself.

Transposable Element Insertions

Mobile DNA elements such as L1 (LINE-1) and Alu (a SINE element) can jump into new genomic locations and disrupt gene function. L1 elements are roughly 6,000 base pairs long and encode their own reverse transcriptase, while Alu elements are shorter (~300 bp) and rely on L1 machinery to move. Insertions of these elements have been identified as causes of disorders such as hemophilia A and neurofibromatosis.

Dynamic Mutations (Trinucleotide Repeat Expansions)

Dynamic mutations involve the expansion of short tandem repeats (most commonly trinucleotide repeats such as CAG, CGG, or CTG) beyond a normal threshold. As the number of repeats grows across successive generations, the disease tends to appear earlier and with greater severity - a phenomenon called anticipation. Key examples include Huntington disease (CAG repeats in the huntingtin gene), Fragile X syndrome (CGG repeats in FMR1), and myotonic dystrophy (CTG repeats in DMPK).

How Common Are These Mutations?

Genetic disorders are broadly classified by their underlying mechanism: single-gene (Mendelian) disorders account for about 2% of known genetic conditions, chromosome disorders for less than 1%, and multifactorial disorders (multiple genes plus environmental factors) for roughly 60%. Mutation rates also differ by scale: point mutations occur at approximately 10⁻¹⁰ per base pair per cell division, chromosomal rearrangements at roughly 10⁻⁴ per cell division, and whole-chromosome missegregation events at about 10⁻² per cell division. This gradient explains why chromosome abnormalities are far more frequent than single-gene mutations in spontaneous miscarriages.

15.2 Inheritance Patterns

A. Autosomal Dominant Inheritance

Only one copy of the mutant allele is needed to cause the disorder. Affected individuals are typically heterozygous (Aa).

Pedigree characteristics:

  • The trait appears in every generation (no skipping).
  • Affected individuals have at least one affected parent.
  • Males and females are equally affected.
  • An affected heterozygote crossed with an unaffected individual gives a 50% chance of affected offspring.
  • Unaffected individuals do not transmit the trait.

Examples: Huntington disease, Marfan syndrome, achondroplasia (dwarfism), familial hypercholesterolemia, polydactyly.

B. Autosomal Recessive Inheritance

Two copies of the mutant allele are needed to cause the disorder. Affected individuals are homozygous recessive (aa). Carriers (Aa) are unaffected but can pass the allele to offspring.

Pedigree characteristics:

  • The trait often skips generations.
  • Affected individuals typically have unaffected (carrier) parents.
  • Males and females are equally affected.
  • Two carrier parents have a 25% chance of an affected child, 50% chance of a carrier child, and 25% chance of an unaffected noncarrier child.
  • More common in populations with consanguinity (mating between relatives).

Examples: Cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease, phenylketonuria (PKU), Tay-Sachs disease, albinism.

C. X-Linked Recessive Inheritance

The mutant gene is on the X chromosome. Males (XY) are affected much more frequently because they have only one X chromosome - a single recessive allele is sufficient to cause disease (they are hemizygous). Females need two copies to be affected.

Pedigree characteristics:

  • Many more males are affected than females.
  • Affected males receive the allele from their carrier mother.
  • The trait is never transmitted directly from father to son (fathers give sons the Y chromosome).
  • Carrier females pass the allele to 50% of their sons (who will be affected) and 50% of their daughters (who will be carriers).

Examples: Hemophilia A and B, red-green color blindness, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, G6PD deficiency.

D. X-Linked Dominant Inheritance

The mutant gene is on the X chromosome and is dominant - one copy is sufficient to cause disease in both males and females.

Pedigree characteristics:

  • Affected males transmit the condition to all daughters and no sons.
  • Affected heterozygous females transmit to 50% of all children.
  • More females are affected than males (but affected males are often more severely affected).
  • Some X-linked dominant conditions are lethal in males.

Examples: Rett syndrome, Fragile X syndrome (shows features of dominant with variable penetrance), vitamin D-resistant rickets.

15.3 Clinical Profiles of Important Single-Gene Disorders

The disease names listed in the inheritance categories above are the ones most frequently named on entrance exams. The profiles below give the gene, mutation type, biochemical defect, and characteristic clinical features for each. Reading them once with the inheritance categories in mind makes the patterns much easier to recall.

Cystic Fibrosis (autosomal recessive)

Cystic fibrosis is caused by loss-of-function mutations in the CFTR gene on chromosome 7, which encodes a chloride-channel protein in the apical membrane of epithelial cells. The most common mutation is ΔF508 — an in-frame deletion of three nucleotides that removes a single phenylalanine residue (position 508) from the protein. The misfolded channel is recognised by the endoplasmic reticulum quality-control system and degraded before reaching the cell surface. Without functional CFTR, chloride and water cannot be secreted normally onto epithelial surfaces, so airway and pancreatic mucus becomes thick and sticky. Clinical consequences include recurrent pulmonary infections (especially with Pseudomonas aeruginosa), pancreatic insufficiency with steatorrhoea, male infertility from congenital absence of the vas deferens, and elevated sweat chloride (the basis of the diagnostic sweat test, > 60 mmol/L).

Sickle Cell Disease (autosomal recessive)

Sickle cell disease is the textbook example of a missense mutation: a single A → T substitution in the sixth codon of the β-globin gene (HBB) on chromosome 11 changes the codon GAG to GTG. At the protein level, glutamic acid (a hydrophilic, negatively charged residue) is replaced by valine (a hydrophobic residue) at position 6 of the β-globin chain. The hydrophobic patch causes deoxygenated haemoglobin S (HbS) to polymerise into long fibres that distort the red blood cell into a rigid sickle shape. Affected homozygotes (HbSS) experience chronic haemolytic anaemia, painful vaso-occlusive crises, splenic autoinfarction, and increased risk of stroke. Heterozygotes (HbAS, sickle cell trait) are largely asymptomatic and have a survival advantage in regions where Plasmodium falciparum malaria is endemic — the classic example of balanced polymorphism maintained by heterozygote advantage.

Phenylketonuria (PKU) (autosomal recessive)

PKU results from loss-of-function mutations in the PAH gene encoding phenylalanine hydroxylase, the liver enzyme that converts the essential amino acid phenylalanine to tyrosine. With the enzyme absent, dietary phenylalanine accumulates in blood and is shunted into alternative pathways producing phenylpyruvate, phenyllactate, and phenylacetate — metabolites that are toxic to the developing brain. Untreated infants develop progressive intellectual disability, microcephaly, seizures, eczema, and a characteristic musty body odour. PKU is a paradigm for newborn screening: blood is collected by heel prick within the first days of life, and dietary intervention (a phenylalanine-restricted diet supplemented with tyrosine) begun within the first weeks of life prevents almost all neurological damage.

Tay–Sachs Disease (autosomal recessive)

Tay–Sachs disease is caused by mutations in the HEXA gene on chromosome 15, which encodes the α-subunit of β-hexosaminidase A. The deficient enzyme cannot hydrolyse GM2 ganglioside, which therefore accumulates inside lysosomes — particularly in neurons, which depend most heavily on lysosomal turnover of membrane lipids. Affected infants appear normal at birth but undergo progressive neurodegeneration starting around 3–6 months of age: loss of motor skills, an exaggerated startle response to sound, blindness with a pathognomonic cherry-red macular spot (the surrounding ganglion cells are pale and ballooned with stored lipid, while the avascular fovea retains its normal red colour), and death by 4–5 years. Carrier frequency is elevated (~1 in 30) in Ashkenazi Jewish, French Canadian, and Cajun populations, making targeted carrier screening highly effective.

Huntington Disease (autosomal dominant, dynamic mutation)

Huntington disease is caused by an expansion of a CAG trinucleotide repeat in exon 1 of the HTT gene on chromosome 4. CAG codes for glutamine, so the expansion produces a huntingtin protein with an abnormally long polyglutamine (polyQ) tract that misfolds, aggregates, and exerts a toxic gain-of-function effect — particularly on medium spiny neurons of the striatum. Repeat lengths of 26 or fewer are normal, 27–35 are intermediate (no disease but unstable in transmission), 36–39 show reduced penetrance, and 40 or more cause classic disease. Symptoms typically appear in the fourth or fifth decade and include progressive chorea (involuntary, dance-like movements), cognitive decline, and psychiatric disturbance. Repeat expansion tends to grow with each transmission, especially through the paternal line, so successive generations show earlier onset and worse severity — the phenomenon of genetic anticipation. Predictive genetic testing is available but is offered only with extensive counselling because no curative therapy yet exists.

Hemophilia A (X-linked recessive)

Hemophilia A is caused by loss-of-function mutations in the F8 gene on the long arm of the X chromosome (Xq28), which encodes coagulation factor VIII. Without factor VIII, the intrinsic pathway of the coagulation cascade fails to amplify thrombin generation. Affected males (hemizygous, XhY) suffer prolonged bleeding from minor injuries, spontaneous bleeding into joints (haemarthroses — classically affecting knees, elbows, and ankles), intramuscular haematomas, and intracranial bleeding. Female heterozygous carriers (XHXh) are usually asymptomatic, although a minority show mild bleeding tendency due to skewed X-inactivation. About 30% of cases arise from new mutations with no prior family history. The royal pedigree descended from Queen Victoria is the classic teaching example of X-linked inheritance. Treatment is replacement therapy with recombinant factor VIII; gene therapy delivering F8 cDNA via adeno-associated viral vectors is now in clinical use.

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (X-linked recessive)

Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) results from out-of-frame mutations in the DMD gene on Xp21 — the largest known human gene at 2.4 Mb — which encodes the cytoskeletal protein dystrophin. Dystrophin links the actin cytoskeleton of muscle fibres to the surrounding extracellular matrix via a transmembrane glycoprotein complex; without it, repeated cycles of contraction tear the sarcolemma and progressively destroy muscle fibres. Affected boys appear healthy at birth but show delayed motor milestones, calf pseudohypertrophy (replacement of muscle by fat and fibrous tissue), Gowers’ sign (pushing on the thighs to stand from the floor), and progressive proximal weakness. Loss of ambulation typically occurs before age 13, with death from respiratory failure or cardiomyopathy in the second or third decade. Becker muscular dystrophy is a milder, in-frame allelic variant that retains partial dystrophin function. Serum creatine kinase is markedly elevated and is the standard initial screening test.

Marfan Syndrome (autosomal dominant)

Marfan syndrome is caused by missense or loss-of-function mutations in the FBN1 gene on chromosome 15, encoding the connective-tissue glycoprotein fibrillin-1. Fibrillin-1 forms the scaffold of microfibrils in elastic tissues and also sequesters TGF-β signalling molecules; defective fibrillin therefore both weakens connective-tissue architecture and dysregulates TGF-β. Clinical features classically span three systems: skeletal (tall stature, arachnodactyly, pectus deformity, joint hypermobility, scoliosis), ocular (ectopia lentis — superior dislocation of the lens), and cardiovascular (mitral valve prolapse and, most dangerously, progressive dilatation of the aortic root with risk of dissection). Aortic dissection is the leading cause of premature death and the reason patients require lifelong echocardiographic surveillance. Inheritance is autosomal dominant with full penetrance but variable expressivity, and roughly 25% of cases arise from de novo mutations.

Pattern Recognition for Exam Questions

Three high-yield patterns connect each disease to its inheritance category:

  • Autosomal recessive disorders are often metabolic. CF (chloride transport), PKU (amino acid metabolism), Tay–Sachs (lysosomal storage), and sickle cell (haemoglobin) all share the recessive logic that one functional gene copy is enough to maintain a basic metabolic activity, so disease appears only when both copies fail.
  • Autosomal dominant disorders are often structural or gain-of-function. Marfan syndrome (defective scaffold protein produced from one allele disrupts fibre assembly) and Huntington disease (toxic polyQ aggregates) damage the cell even when a normal copy is also present.
  • X-linked recessive disorders affect mainly males. Hemophilia A and DMD both reflect the rule that hemizygous males express any defective X-linked allele directly, while carrier mothers are usually asymptomatic. Always check the pedigree for the classic giveaway: father-to-son transmission is impossible.
KEY POINTS - Chapter 15
  • Point mutations: missense (different amino acid), nonsense (premature stop), silent (no change).
  • Insertions/deletions not in multiples of 3 cause frameshift mutations.
  • Autosomal dominant: one mutant allele sufficient; appears every generation.
  • Autosomal recessive: two mutant alleles needed; often skips generations; carriers are unaffected.
  • X-linked recessive: mostly males affected; no father-to-son transmission.
  • X-linked dominant: affected males transmit to all daughters, no sons.
MNEMONIC - X-linked Recessive Pattern

"X marks the Mother" - carrier mothers pass X-linked recessive conditions to sons.

Remember: An affected father cannot give the condition to his sons (he gives them Y, not X).

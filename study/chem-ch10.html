Mixtures and Solutions

10.1 Types of Mixtures

A mixture contains two or more substances that are not chemically bonded to each other. Mixtures can be separated by physical means.

The Tyndall Effect

The Tyndall effect is the scattering of a light beam by colloidal particles (1–1000 nm). This makes a light beam visible as it passes through a colloid, distinguishing it from a true solution where particles are too small to scatter light. Everyday examples include a flashlight beam visible in fog and the slight bluish tinge of diluted milk. In clinical settings, lipemia (excess lipids in blood) makes serum turbid due to light scattering by lipoprotein particles.

10.2 Solutions and Aqueous Solutions

A solution consists of a solute (dissolved substance) and a solvent (the dissolving medium). When the solvent is water, we call it an aqueous solution. The principle governing dissolution is "like dissolves like": polar solvents dissolve polar and ionic solutes; nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes.

10.3 Solubility

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. A solution at maximum solubility is saturated. Below that level, it is unsaturated. A supersaturated solution contains more dissolved solute than the solubility limit at that temperature. It is metastable - adding a seed crystal or disturbing the solution can trigger rapid crystallization. Supersaturated sodium acetate solutions are used in reusable hand warmers: clicking a metal disc nucleates crystallization, releasing the heat of crystallization.

Solubility Product (Kₛₚ)

For sparingly soluble ionic compounds, the solubility product constant (Kₛₚ) describes the equilibrium between the solid and its dissolved ions. For example, for AgCl:

AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)    Kₛₚ = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻]

A small Kₛₚ means the compound is barely soluble.

Solubility of Gases (Henry's Law)

The solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid:

C = kH × P

where C is concentration, kH is Henry's law constant, and P is partial pressure. Each gas dissolves independently according to its own partial pressure. This explains why carbonated drinks go flat when opened - reducing the CO₂ pressure above the liquid decreases its solubility.

Decompression Sickness

Henry's Law also explains decompression sickness ("the bends"). At the increased pressure of deep water diving, more nitrogen dissolves in the blood and tissues. If the diver ascends too rapidly, the sudden pressure drop reduces nitrogen solubility and dissolved N₂ forms gas bubbles in the bloodstream and tissues. These bubbles can block blood vessels, causing severe pain, paralysis, or death. Treatment requires gradual decompression or recompression in a hyperbaric chamber.

Qualitative Solubility Rules for Ionic Compounds

Predicting whether an ionic compound dissolves in water is essential for identifying precipitates in double-replacement reactions:

  1. All alkali metal (Li⁺, Na⁺, K⁺) and ammonium (NH₄⁺) salts are soluble.
  2. All nitrates (NO₃⁻), acetates (CH₃COO⁻), and perchlorates (ClO₄⁻) are soluble.
  3. All chlorides, bromides, and iodides are soluble except those of Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, and Hg₂²⁺.
  4. All sulfates are soluble except those of Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺, Pb²⁺, and Hg₂²⁺. (CaSO₄ is slightly soluble.)
  5. Most hydroxides are insoluble except those of alkali metals and Ca²⁺, Sr²⁺, Ba²⁺ (which are moderately soluble).
  6. Most carbonates, phosphates, sulfites, and sulfides are insoluble except those of alkali metals and NH₄⁺.

10.4 Diffusion and Osmosis

Diffusion is the spontaneous movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration. It occurs in gases, liquids, and even across membranes.

Osmosis is the diffusion of water (or solvent) across a semipermeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration. The driving force is the tendency to equalize concentrations on both sides.

Osmotic pressure (π) is the pressure needed to prevent osmosis. It depends on solute concentration:

π = iMRT

where i = van 't Hoff factor (number of particles the solute splits into), M = molarity, R = gas constant, T = temperature in Kelvin.

Osmolarity

Osmolarity is the total molar concentration of all osmotically active particles in a solution, measured in osmol/L (or mosmol/L). For electrolytes that dissociate, osmolarity equals molarity multiplied by the number of ions produced per formula unit:

  • NaCl (2 ions) at 0.15 M → osmolarity = 0.30 osmol/L
  • CaCl₂ (3 ions) at 0.10 M → osmolarity = 0.30 osmol/L
  • Glucose (non-electrolyte, 1 particle) at 0.30 M → osmolarity = 0.30 osmol/L

Normal blood plasma has an osmolarity of approximately 285–310 mosmol/L. Physiological (normal) saline (0.9% w/v NaCl, approximately 154 mM) has an osmolarity of about 308 mosmol/L, which is isotonic with blood.

Isotonic, Hypotonic, and Hypertonic Solutions

These terms describe how a solution's osmolarity compares to that of intracellular fluid:

  • Isotonic: same osmolarity as the cell interior - no net water movement across the membrane. Red blood cells maintain their normal shape.
  • Hypotonic: lower osmolarity than the cell - water enters by osmosis, causing cells to swell and potentially burst. This bursting of red blood cells is called hemolysis.
  • Hypertonic: higher osmolarity than the cell - water exits, causing cells to shrivel. For red blood cells, this produces a scalloped appearance called crenation.

Dilution Calculations

Dilution relies on the principle that the amount of solute remains constant: c₁V₁ = c₂V₂. A clinical example: if 2 mL of a urine sample is diluted to a total volume of 200 mL (a 100-fold dilution), and the measured urea concentration in the diluted sample is 4.8 mmol/L, then the original urine urea concentration is 4.8 × 100 = 480 mmol/L.

10.5 Dialysis

Dialysis is the separation of dissolved substances across a semipermeable membrane based on particle size. Small molecules and ions pass through the membrane; large molecules (like proteins) cannot. This is the principle behind kidney dialysis machines, which remove waste products from the blood.

Key Points - Chapter 10

  • Solutions are homogeneous; colloids show the Tyndall effect; suspensions settle.
  • "Like dissolves like" - polar dissolves polar; nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.
  • Kₛₚ describes equilibrium solubility of sparingly soluble ionic compounds.
  • Henry's Law: gas solubility is proportional to its partial pressure (C = kHP).
  • Osmosis is solvent movement across a semipermeable membrane toward higher solute concentration.
  • Osmotic pressure: π = iMRT.
  • Dialysis separates small molecules from large ones using a semipermeable membrane.

Test Yourself - Chapter 10

1. Why does a carbonated drink fizz when you open the bottle?
Show Answer
Inside the sealed bottle, CO₂ is dissolved at high pressure (Henry's Law). When you open it, the pressure above the liquid drops, decreasing CO₂ solubility. The excess dissolved CO₂ escapes as bubbles.
2. Red blood cells placed in pure water will swell and burst (lyse). Explain why using osmosis.
Show Answer
Pure water is hypotonic relative to the cell interior. Water moves by osmosis across the cell membrane into the cell (toward the higher solute concentration inside). The influx of water increases cell volume until the membrane cannot stretch further, and the cell bursts.
3. The Kₛₚ of BaSO₄ is 1.1 × 10⁻¹⁰. Calculate the molar solubility.
Show Answer
BaSO₄ → Ba²⁺ + SO₄²&mⁱⁿus;. If solubility = s, then Kₛₚ = s × s = s². So s = √(1.1 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 1.05 × 10⁻⁵ mol/L.

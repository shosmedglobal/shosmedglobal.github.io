Mixtures and Solutions

10.1 Types of Mixtures

A mixture contains two or more substances that are not chemically bonded to each other. Mixtures can be separated by physical means.

10.2 Solutions and Aqueous Solutions

A solution consists of a solute (dissolved substance) and a solvent (the dissolving medium). When the solvent is water, we call it an aqueous solution. The principle governing dissolution is "like dissolves like": polar solvents dissolve polar and ionic solutes; nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes.

10.3 Solubility

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. A solution at maximum solubility is saturated. Below that level, it is unsaturated. Under special conditions, a supersaturated solution holds more solute than normal — it is unstable.

Solubility Product (Ksp)

For sparingly soluble ionic compounds, the solubility product constant (Ksp) describes the equilibrium between the solid and its dissolved ions. For example, for AgCl:

AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag+(aq) + Cl−(aq)    Ksp = [Ag+][Cl−]

A small Ksp means the compound is barely soluble.

Solubility of Gases (Henry's Law)

The solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid:

C = kH × P

where C is concentration, kH is Henry's law constant, and P is partial pressure. This explains why carbonated drinks go flat when opened — reducing the CO2 pressure above the liquid decreases its solubility.

10.4 Diffusion and Osmosis

Diffusion is the spontaneous movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration. It occurs in gases, liquids, and even across membranes.

Osmosis is the diffusion of water (or solvent) across a semipermeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration. The driving force is the tendency to equalize concentrations on both sides.

Osmotic pressure (π) is the pressure needed to prevent osmosis. It depends on solute concentration:

π = iMRT

where i = van 't Hoff factor (number of particles the solute splits into), M = molarity, R = gas constant, T = temperature in Kelvin.

10.5 Dialysis

Dialysis is the separation of dissolved substances across a semipermeable membrane based on particle size. Small molecules and ions pass through the membrane; large molecules (like proteins) cannot. This is the principle behind kidney dialysis machines, which remove waste products from the blood.

Key Points — Chapter 10

  • Solutions are homogeneous; colloids show the Tyndall effect; suspensions settle.
  • "Like dissolves like" — polar dissolves polar; nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.
  • Ksp describes equilibrium solubility of sparingly soluble ionic compounds.
  • Henry's Law: gas solubility is proportional to its partial pressure (C = kHP).
  • Osmosis is solvent movement across a semipermeable membrane toward higher solute concentration.
  • Osmotic pressure: π = iMRT.
  • Dialysis separates small molecules from large ones using a semipermeable membrane.

Test Yourself — Chapter 10

1. Why does a carbonated drink fizz when you open the bottle?
Show Answer
Inside the sealed bottle, CO2 is dissolved at high pressure (Henry's Law). When you open it, the pressure above the liquid drops, decreasing CO2 solubility. The excess dissolved CO2 escapes as bubbles.
2. Red blood cells placed in pure water will swell and burst (lyse). Explain why using osmosis.
Show Answer
Pure water is hypotonic relative to the cell interior. Water moves by osmosis across the cell membrane into the cell (toward the higher solute concentration inside). The influx of water increases cell volume until the membrane cannot stretch further, and the cell bursts.
3. The Ksp of BaSO4 is 1.1 × 10−10. Calculate the molar solubility.
Show Answer
BaSO4 → Ba2+ + SO42−. If solubility = s, then Ksp = s × s = s². So s = √(1.1 × 10−10) = 1.05 × 10−5 mol/L.

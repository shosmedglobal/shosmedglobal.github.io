Chapter 20

Alkanes, Alkenes, and Alkynes

Alkanes

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with the general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. They contain only C–C single bonds and C–H bonds. Alkanes are also called paraffins (from Latin "parum affinis" - little affinity), reflecting their low reactivity.

Naming Alkanes

The first four are named by historical convention: methane, ethane, propane, butane. From C5 onward, Greek/Latin number prefixes apply: pentane, hexane, heptane, octane, nonane, decane.

Carbon Hybridization

Carbon's ground-state electron configuration (1s² 2s² 2p²) has only two unpaired electrons, yet carbon always forms four bonds. This is explained by hybridization:

  • sp³ (alkanes): four equivalent orbitals pointing toward the corners of a tetrahedron, bond angles 109.5°.
  • sp² (alkenes, aromatics): three orbitals in a trigonal plane (120°) with one unhybridized p-orbital available for the π bond.
  • sp (alkynes): two linear orbitals (180°) with two unhybridized p-orbitals forming two π bonds.

Physical Properties

  • C1–C4: gases at room temperature.
  • C5–C17: liquids (petrol/gasoline and diesel range).
  • C18+: waxy solids.
  • Nonpolar: insoluble in water but soluble in nonpolar solvents.
  • Boiling points increase with molecular weight. Branching decreases boiling point (less surface area for London dispersion forces).

Health Effects of Alkanes

Alkane toxicity varies with molecular weight. Inhaling volatile alkane vapours (e.g., gasoline fumes) can damage lung tissue by dissolving cell membranes. Low molecular weight liquid alkanes strip protective oils from skin on prolonged contact, causing dryness and blistering. Conversely, high molecular weight alkanes (C20–C30 mixtures) are used in skin and hair lotions to replace natural oils. Purified mineral oil (a mixture of long-chain alkanes) has been used as a laxative.

Cycloalkane Details

Cyclopropane was historically used as a general anaesthetic owing to its narcotic properties at low concentrations. Cyclohexane, the most important cycloalkane, adopts a chair conformation as its most stable form, with hydrogens in either axial (perpendicular to the ring plane) or equatorial (roughly in the ring plane) positions. The polycyclic system cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene (also called sterane or gonane) is the parent carbon skeleton of all steroids, including cholesterol and steroid hormones.

Substituted cycloalkanes exhibit geometric isomerism because the ring prevents free rotation. In cis isomers, two substituents lie on the same side of the ring plane; in trans isomers they are on opposite sides. This is analogous to E/Z isomerism around double bonds in alkenes.

Reactions of Alkanes

Combustion: The most important alkane reaction. Complete combustion in excess oxygen produces CO₂ and H₂O and releases heat energy:

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O    (ΔH = −890 kJ/mol)

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces CO (carbon monoxide, toxic) or soot (carbon).

Halogenation (free radical substitution): Alkanes react with halogens (Cl₂, Br₂) in the presence of UV light or heat. The mechanism proceeds in three steps:

  1. Initiation: UV light breaks the Cl–Cl bond homolytically into two chlorine radicals: Cl₂ → 2Cl•
  2. Propagation: Cl• abstracts an H from the alkane to form HCl and a carbon radical. The carbon radical reacts with Cl₂ to form the chloroalkane and regenerate Cl•.
  3. Termination: Two radicals combine, ending the chain.

Alkenes

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ. The double bond consists of one sigma and one π bond, making alkenes far more reactive than alkanes.

Reactions of Alkenes

Markovnikov's Rule

When an unsymmetrical reagent (like HBr) adds to an unsymmetrical alkene, the hydrogen adds to the carbon of the double bond that already has more hydrogens, and the other group (Br) adds to the carbon with fewer hydrogens. In other words: "the rich get richer" - hydrogen goes to the carbon that already has more hydrogens.

Example: Propene + HBr → 2-bromopropane (not 1-bromopropane).

Addition of hydrogen (hydrogenation): H₂ adds across the double bond in the presence of a metal catalyst (Pt, Pd, or Ni), converting the alkene into an alkane.

Addition of halogens: Cl₂ or Br₂ add across the double bond. Br₂ in CCl₄ is used as a test for unsaturation - the brown bromine color disappears when it reacts with a double bond.

Addition of water (hydration): Water adds across the double bond in the presence of an acid catalyst, following Markovnikov's rule, to produce an alcohol.

Polymerization: Many alkene molecules join together to form long-chain polymers. Example: n molecules of ethylene form polyethylene (plastic).

Oxidation: Mild oxidation (KMnO₄, cold, dilute) breaks the π bond and adds two OH groups (forming a diol). Strong oxidation (hot KMnO₄) cleaves the double bond entirely.

Alkynes

Alkynes contain a carbon-carbon triple bond (one sigma + two π bonds). General formula: CₙH₂ₙ−₂. They undergo the same types of addition reactions as alkenes, but can add two equivalents of reagent.

Laboratory Preparation of Acetylene

A classic method for producing acetylene involves reacting calcium carbide with water: CaC₂ + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + C₂H₂. Calcium carbide is an ionic compound containing the carbide ion C₂²⁻. This reaction was historically used in miners' carbide lamps and continues to find application in some welding processes. The acetylene generated can serve as a versatile starting material for numerous organic syntheses.

Important Polymers from Alkene Polymerization

Many commercially vital plastics are produced by chain-growth polymerization of substituted ethenes:

  • Polyethylene (PE) - from ethene; packaging films and bottles
  • Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) - from vinyl chloride; pipes and flooring
  • Polypropylene (PP) - from propene; food containers and textiles
  • Polytetrafluoroethylene (Teflon/PTFE) - from tetrafluoroethene; non-stick coatings
  • Polystyrene (PS) - from styrene; insulation and disposable cups
  • Polymethyl methacrylate (Plexiglas/PMMA) - from methyl methacrylate; transparent panels
  • Polychloroprene (Neoprene) - from 2-chlorobuta-1,3-diene; synthetic rubber

Halogenated Hydrocarbons

Halogenated derivatives of hydrocarbons have wide-ranging applications. Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) and trichloroethylene served as industrial solvents. Freons (e.g., CCl₂F₂) were used as refrigerants until they were restricted for depleting the ozone layer. Halothane (CF₃CHClBr) remains in limited use as a general anaesthetic. DDT and lindane (hexachlorocyclohexane, C₆H₆Cl₆) are insecticides, and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) are persistent environmental pollutants. Many halogenated hydrocarbons have narcotic effects and can cause liver and nervous system damage.

Aromatic Hydrocarbon Toxicity

Benzene is a confirmed human carcinogen that specifically damages bone marrow and can cause leukaemia with chronic exposure. Toluene and xylenes produce narcotic and irritant effects but are less hazardous than benzene itself. Naphthalene (the active ingredient in mothballs) irritates skin and mucous membranes and can cause methemoglobinaemia and kidney damage. Benzo[a]pyrene, found in cigarette smoke and charred food, is a potent carcinogen that damages DNA after metabolic activation in the liver.

Key Points - Chapter 20

  • Alkanes (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂): saturated, low reactivity, undergo combustion and halogenation (radical substitution).
  • Alkenes (CₙH₂ₙ): one C=C double bond, reactive via addition reactions.
  • Alkynes (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂): one C≡C triple bond, even more reactive, can add two equivalents.
  • Markovnikov's rule: H adds to the carbon with more H atoms already.
  • Bromine water decolorization test distinguishes unsaturated from saturated hydrocarbons.

Mnemonic: Markovnikov's Rule

"The rich get richer"

The carbon with more hydrogens gets the hydrogen from HX addition.

Oxidation State of Carbon Chains: The Organic Oxidation Ladder

In inorganic chemistry, oxidation is tracked by integer oxidation numbers. In organic chemistry the same idea is expressed with a simpler – and exam-friendly – rule:

A carbon atom is more oxidised when it is bonded to more electronegative atoms (O, N, halogens) and to fewer hydrogens.

Adding an O–H or replacing C–H with C=O is oxidation. Adding hydrogen (or removing oxygen) is reduction. The classical "ladder" of carbon oxidation, using a one-carbon example, runs from most reduced (bottom) to most oxidised (top):

StepClassExample (C₁)H atoms on the CBonds to ODirection
1AlkaneCH₄ (methane)40most reduced ↓
2AlcoholCH₃OH (methanol)31 (single)
3Aldehyde / KetoneHCHO (methanal)1–22 (one double)
4Carboxylic acidHCOOH (methanoic acid)13
5Carbon dioxideCO₂04most oxidised ↑

Notice the trend: at each step one C–H bond is replaced by a C–O bond. The number of hydrogens on the carbon decreases, the number of bonds to oxygen increases, and the carbon becomes progressively more oxidised. The full sequence is the metabolic logic of, for example, ethanol oxidation in the liver: CH₃CH₂OH (ethanol) → CH₃CHO (acetaldehyde) → CH₃COOH (acetic acid) → CO₂ via the citric acid cycle.

Comparing degrees of unsaturation. The same logic explains why alkenes are more oxidised than alkanes, and alkynes are more oxidised than alkenes: each π bond costs the molecule two hydrogens. For a chain of fixed length:

  • Alkane (CₙH₂ₙ₂₂) — saturated, most hydrogen, most reduced.
  • Alkene (CₙH₂ₙ) — one π bond, two fewer H, more oxidised.
  • Alkyne (CₙH₂ₙ₂₂) — two π bonds, four fewer H, even more oxidised.
Exam Tip: Whenever a question asks which form of a carbon chain is "more oxidised," count hydrogens. Fewer hydrogens = more oxidised. Hydrocarbons (lots of C–H) are less oxidised than their alcohol, aldehyde, or carboxylic acid derivatives. Common distractors that reverse this relationship are wrong.

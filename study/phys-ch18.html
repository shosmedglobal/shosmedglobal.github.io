Chapter 18

Photoelectric Effect

🧠Practice this chapterTest yourself with chapter-specific questions →

The Phenomenon

When light shines on a metal surface, electrons may be ejected. This is the photoelectric effect. However, it only works if the light's frequency is high enough.

Einstein's Equation

Ephoton = W + KEmax

Or equivalently:

h · f = W + (1/2) · me · vmax2

Where:

  • h = Planck's constant = 6.626 · 10-34 J·s
  • f = frequency of incident light
  • W = work function of the metal (minimum energy needed to free an electron)
  • KEmax = maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons
  • me = electron mass = 9.109 · 10-31 kg

Threshold Frequency and Work Function

The threshold frequency f0 is the minimum frequency needed to eject electrons:

f0 = W / h

At f = f0, the electron barely escapes with zero kinetic energy. Below f0, no electrons are emitted regardless of light intensity.

MetalWork Function W (eV)Threshold Wavelength (nm)
Cesium2.1590 (visible, yellow)
Sodium2.3540 (visible, green)
Zinc4.3289 (UV)
Platinum5.6221 (UV)

Conversion: 1 eV = 1.602 · 10-19 J

Stopping Voltage

To measure KEmax, a reverse voltage Vs (stopping voltage) is applied to stop the fastest electrons:

KEmax = e · Vs

So: h · f = W + e · Vs

Why Classical Wave Theory Fails

Classical physics predicted:

  1. Any frequency should eject electrons if intensity is high enough -- WRONG
  2. Higher intensity should give electrons more energy -- WRONG
  3. There should be a time delay for dim light (energy must accumulate) -- WRONG

Einstein explained that light comes in discrete packets (photons), each with energy E = hf. A single photon either has enough energy (hf ≥ W) or it does not. Intensity affects the number of photons (and hence number of electrons), not the energy per photon.

Key Experimental Observations

ObservationClassical PredictionActual Result
Effect of frequencyAny frequency worksMust exceed f0
Effect of intensityMore energy per electronMore electrons, same energy
Time delayYes, for dim lightNo delay, even for dim light
KEmax vs frequencyNo dependenceLinear: KEmax = hf - W

Worked Example

Problem: Light of wavelength 400 nm strikes a metal with work function W = 2.0 eV. Find: (a) the energy of each photon in eV, (b) the maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons, (c) the stopping voltage.

Solution:

(a) Ephoton = h · f = h · c / λ

E = (6.626 · 10-34 · 3 · 108) / (400 · 10-9)

E = (1.988 · 10-25) / (4 · 10-7)

E = 4.97 · 10-19 J

Converting to eV: E = (4.97 · 10-19) / (1.602 · 10-19) = 3.1 eV

(Shortcut: E(eV) = 1240 / λ(nm) = 1240 / 400 = 3.1 eV)

(b) KEmax = Ephoton - W = 3.1 - 2.0 = 1.1 eV

(c) Vs = KEmax / e = 1.1 eV / e = 1.1 V

(Since KE is already in eV, the stopping voltage in volts equals the KE in eV.)

Exam Tips:
- Shortcut: E(eV) = 1240 / λ(nm). Memorize this for fast calculations.
- Intensity affects the NUMBER of emitted electrons, NOT their energy.
- Frequency (or wavelength) determines the ENERGY of each electron.
- Below threshold frequency: zero electrons emitted, no matter how intense the light.
- Stopping voltage in volts numerically equals KEmax in eV.

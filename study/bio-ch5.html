Chapter 5

Molecular Bases of Heredity — DNA

5.1 Historical Experiments: Proving DNA Is the Genetic Material

Griffith's Experiment (1928)

Frederick Griffith worked with two strains of Streptococcus pneumoniae: a virulent Smooth (S) strain with a polysaccharide capsule, and a harmless Rough (R) strain without one. He found that heat-killed S bacteria alone did not cause disease, and live R bacteria alone did not cause disease. But when he mixed heat-killed S bacteria with live R bacteria and injected them into mice, the mice died, and he recovered live S bacteria from them. Something from the dead S cells had "transformed" the R cells into virulent S cells. He called this a "transforming principle" but did not identify what it was.

Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty (1944)

These researchers set out to identify Griffith's transforming principle. They systematically destroyed different components of the heat-killed S extract (proteins, RNA, DNA) and tested which treatment eliminated the transforming ability. Only when they destroyed the DNA (using DNase enzyme) did transformation fail. Their conclusion: DNA is the transforming principle.

Hershey-Chase Experiment (1952)

Alfred Hershey and Martha Chase used bacteriophage T2, which injects its genetic material into bacteria. They labeled the phage's DNA with radioactive phosphorus (32P) and its protein coat with radioactive sulfur (35S) in separate experiments. After infection, they found that 32P (DNA) entered the bacterial cell, while 35S (protein) remained outside. This confirmed that DNA, not protein, is the genetic material.

5.2 DNA Structure

Nucleotides: The Building Blocks

DNA is a polymer built from repeating units called nucleotides. Each nucleotide has three components:

  1. A nitrogenous base: either a purine (adenine [A] or guanine [G], which have a double-ring structure) or a pyrimidine (cytosine [C] or thymine [T], which have a single-ring structure).
  2. A pentose sugar: deoxyribose in DNA (ribose in RNA).
  3. A phosphate group.
Nucleoside vs Nucleotide: A nucleoside is a base attached to a sugar (no phosphate). A nucleotide is a nucleoside plus one or more phosphate groups. Think: nucleotide = nucleoside + phosphate.

The Double Helix

Watson and Crick, building on Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography data, proposed in 1953 that DNA is a double helix: two antiparallel strands wound around each other. The sugar-phosphate backbones form the outer rails (connected by phosphodiester bonds between the 3' carbon of one sugar and the 5' carbon of the next). The bases point inward and pair through hydrogen bonds: A pairs with T via 2 hydrogen bonds; G pairs with C via 3 hydrogen bonds. This is Chargaff's rule: in any DNA molecule, the amount of A equals T, and the amount of G equals C.

The two strands are antiparallel: one runs 5' to 3', and the other runs 3' to 5'. This directionality is critical for understanding replication and transcription.

DNA Conformations

While B-DNA is the predominant form in living cells, DNA can adopt alternative conformations. A-DNA is a wider, more compact right-handed helix that forms under dehydrated conditions. Z-DNA is a left-handed helix with a zigzag backbone that can form in sequences rich in alternating purines and pyrimidines, and may play roles in gene regulation. Key B-DNA measurements that may appear on exams: the helix diameter is 2.37 nm (approximately 2 nm), the distance between adjacent base pairs is 0.33 nm, and one complete turn of the helix spans 3.40 nm (encompassing about 10.5 base pairs). The helix has two grooves of different widths: the major groove (wider, where many regulatory proteins bind to read the DNA sequence) and the minor groove (narrower).

DNA Denaturation and Hybridization

DNA denaturation (or melting) is the separation of the two strands of double-stranded DNA, achieved by heating or extreme pH. AT-rich regions denature first because they are held by only two hydrogen bonds per pair versus three for GC pairs. The melting temperature (Tm) of a DNA molecule increases with higher GC content. The reverse process, hybridization (or renaturation), occurs when complementary single strands re-associate upon cooling. These principles underlie many laboratory techniques including PCR, Southern blotting, and FISH.

Nucleotide Functions Beyond DNA and RNA

Nucleotides serve roles far beyond building nucleic acids. ATP and GTP are the primary energy currencies of the cell. Cyclic nucleotides (cAMP, cGMP) function as second messengers in signal transduction pathways. Several essential enzyme cofactors are nucleotide derivatives: coenzyme A (CoA) carries acyl groups in metabolism, FAD and FMN are electron carriers in redox reactions, and NAD+/NADP+ are critical electron carriers in catabolic and anabolic pathways respectively.

5.3 DNA Replication

DNA replication is semiconservative: each new DNA molecule consists of one original strand and one newly synthesized strand (this was demonstrated by the Meselson-Stahl experiment in 1958).

The process involves many enzymes working together:

  1. Helicase unwinds the double helix at the origin of replication, creating a replication fork (a Y-shaped structure).
  2. Single-strand binding proteins (SSBPs) stabilize the separated strands so they do not re-anneal.
  3. Topoisomerase relieves the torsional strain ahead of the replication fork by cutting and rejoining the DNA.
  4. Primase (an RNA polymerase) synthesizes a short RNA primer, providing a free 3'-OH group for DNA polymerase to extend from.
  5. DNA polymerase III (in prokaryotes) synthesizes new DNA in the 5' to 3' direction, reading the template strand 3' to 5'. It can only add nucleotides to an existing 3'-OH end.
  6. On the leading strand, synthesis is continuous (the fork opens in the direction of synthesis). On the lagging strand, synthesis is discontinuous — DNA is made in short segments called Okazaki fragments, each requiring its own RNA primer.
  7. DNA polymerase I removes the RNA primers and replaces them with DNA.
  8. DNA ligase seals the gaps (nicks) between Okazaki fragments, joining them into a continuous strand.

5.4 Chromatin and Chromosome Structure

In eukaryotic cells, DNA does not float free — it is associated with proteins called histones, forming a complex known as chromatin. The basic unit of chromatin is the nucleosome: approximately 147 base pairs of DNA wrapped about 1.7 times around a core of eight histone proteins (an octamer of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4, two of each). Nucleosomes are connected by linker DNA, and histone H1 helps compact nucleosomes into a more condensed fiber.

Chromatin exists in two forms:

  • Euchromatin: Loosely packed, transcriptionally active. Genes in euchromatin are being expressed or are accessible for expression.
  • Heterochromatin: Tightly packed, generally transcriptionally silent. Includes constitutive heterochromatin (always condensed, e.g., near centromeres) and facultative heterochromatin (can switch between condensed and relaxed states).

Constitutive heterochromatin is permanently condensed (found at centromeres and telomeres) and contains mostly repetitive sequences. Facultative heterochromatin is reversibly condensed and contains genes that are silenced in specific cell types or developmental stages. The classic example is X-inactivation in females: one of the two X chromosomes is randomly inactivated in each cell early in development, forming a densely packed Barr body visible at the nuclear periphery. This ensures dosage compensation between XX females and XY males.

Chromosome Ultrastructure

During cell division, chromatin condenses dramatically into visible chromosomes. The hierarchy of packaging: DNA wraps around nucleosomes → nucleosomes coil into a 30 nm fiber → the fiber forms loops attached to a protein scaffold → further coiling produces the compact metaphase chromosome. A metaphase chromosome has two identical copies (formed during S phase) called sister chromatids, joined at the centromere. Nonsister chromatids belong to the homologous chromosome from the other parent.

Chromosome Morphology

Chromosomes are classified by centromere position into three types: metacentric (centromere near the middle, arms roughly equal), submetacentric (centromere off-center, one arm noticeably shorter), and acrocentric (centromere near the end, with a very short p arm). By convention, the short arm is designated "p" (from French "petit") and the long arm "q." Human acrocentric chromosomes (13, 14, 15, 21, 22) have satellite stalks on their short arms containing nucleolus organizer regions (NORs), which encode ribosomal RNA genes.

Telomeres

Telomeres are repetitive nucleotide sequences (TTAGGG in humans, repeated thousands of times) that cap chromosome ends. They protect coding DNA from being lost during replication and prevent chromosome end-to-end fusions. Because DNA polymerase cannot fully replicate the 3' end of a linear chromosome (the "end replication problem"), telomeres shorten with each cell division. The enzyme telomerase, a reverse transcriptase with an RNA template, can extend telomeres but is normally active only in germ cells and stem cells. Most somatic cells lack telomerase activity, so their telomeres shorten progressively, contributing to cellular aging. Cancer cells frequently reactivate telomerase, enabling unlimited division.

Karyotypes

A karyotype is an organized display of an individual's chromosomes, arranged by size and shape. Humans have 46 chromosomes: 22 pairs of autosomes plus 2 sex chromosomes (XX in females, XY in males). Karyotyping can reveal chromosomal abnormalities such as trisomy (an extra chromosome, e.g., Down syndrome = trisomy 21) or structural rearrangements.

Key Points — Chapter 5

  • Griffith discovered transformation; Avery identified DNA as the transforming principle; Hershey-Chase confirmed DNA is the genetic material.
  • DNA nucleotides consist of a nitrogenous base (A, T, G, or C), a deoxyribose sugar, and a phosphate group.
  • Purines (A, G) have two rings; pyrimidines (C, T) have one ring. A pairs with T (2 H-bonds); G pairs with C (3 H-bonds).
  • Replication is semiconservative; DNA polymerase synthesizes 5' to 3'; the lagging strand is made in Okazaki fragments.
  • Nucleosomes (DNA + histones) are the basic unit of chromatin packaging.
  • Euchromatin is active and loosely packed; heterochromatin is silent and condensed.
  • A karyotype displays an organism's full chromosome complement; humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).

Mnemonic — Purines vs Pyrimidines

Purines are Pure As Gold (Adenine, Guanine — two rings). Pyrimidines: Cut the Tree — CUT = C, U (in RNA), T — one ring (like a small stump).

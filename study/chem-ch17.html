Introduction to Organic Chemistry

Why Carbon Is Special

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds. Out of more than 100 elements on the periodic table, carbon stands alone in its ability to form an almost limitless variety of molecular structures. Two properties make this possible:

Tetravalence: Carbon has four electrons in its outer shell and forms exactly four covalent bonds. This gives carbon the greatest possible number of bonding partners among the light elements, allowing it to bond to hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, halogens, and other carbon atoms simultaneously.

Catenation: Carbon atoms bond strongly to other carbon atoms, creating chains, branches, and rings of virtually any length. The C–C single bond is remarkably stable (bond energy about 348 kJ/mol), and carbon can also form double bonds (C=C) and triple bonds (C≡C) with itself.

Carbon forming single bonds (methane), double bonds (ethene), and triple bonds (ethyne)

Organic vs. Inorganic Matter

The composition of inorganic matter on Earth is dominated by oxygen (about 50%), silicon (25%), aluminium (7%), iron (5%), and calcium (3%). In contrast, living (organic) matter is built mainly from six elements: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur — abbreviated CHONPS.

Importantly, not all carbon-containing compounds are classified as organic. Carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), hydrogen cyanide (HCN), carbonic acid (H2CO3), carbonates, and cyanides are considered inorganic. Organic compounds are those in which carbon forms the structural backbone, typically bonded to hydrogen and other elements through covalent bonds.

Classification of Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are broadly divided into two families:

Hydrocarbons

Contain only carbon and hydrogen. These are the simplest organic molecules and serve as the backbone for all others. Examples: methane, ethane, benzene.

Hydrocarbon Derivatives

Hydrocarbons in which one or more hydrogen atoms have been replaced by a functional group containing elements such as O, N, S, or a halogen. Examples: ethanol, acetic acid, methylamine.

Key Terminology

Alkyl group
A fragment derived from an alkane by removing one hydrogen atom. Named by changing the "-ane" suffix to "-yl." For example, methane becomes methyl (CH₃–), ethane becomes ethyl (C₂H₅–).
Aryl group
A fragment derived from an aromatic compound by removing one hydrogen from the ring. The simplest is phenyl (C6H5–), derived from benzene. Related but distinct: the benzyl group (C6H5CH2–) has a –CH2– bridge between the ring and the attachment point, and the benzoyl group (C6H5CO–) is the acyl residue derived from benzoic acid. These three terms are commonly confused and must be distinguished on exams.
Divalent Residues
When two hydrogens are removed from a hydrocarbon, a divalent (two-bonded) residue is formed. Key examples: methylene (–CH2–) from methane, ethylene (–CH2–CH2–) from ethane, and vinylene (–CH=CH–) from ethene. These divalent groups appear as bridging units in larger molecules and in polymer nomenclature.
Aromatic compound
A cyclic, planar molecule with a continuous ring of overlapping p-orbitals and a special stability. Benzene is the prototype.
Functional group
An atom or group of atoms that gives a molecule its characteristic chemical behaviour. Examples: –OH (hydroxyl), –COOH (carboxyl), –NH₂ (amino).

Constitution, Conformation, and Configuration

These three terms describe different levels of molecular structure:

  • Constitution (also called connectivity): Which atoms are bonded to which. Two molecules with the same molecular formula but different constitutions are structural isomers.
  • Conformation: The different spatial arrangements a molecule can adopt by rotation around single bonds. Conformations interconvert freely at room temperature — you cannot isolate one from another.
  • Configuration: The fixed spatial arrangement of atoms around a stereocenter or double bond. Changing the configuration requires breaking and re-forming bonds.

Functional Group Naming Conventions

When naming organic compounds, students should know both suffix and prefix forms for major functional groups. Some additional naming conventions from the LF3 curriculum include: the prefix "mercapto-" (or the newer IUPAC "sulfanyl-") for the –SH group, "R-thio-" for sulfides, and "R-oxy-" for ethers. The prefix "keto-" is an older alternative to "oxo-" for carbonyl groups. Halogen substituents and nitro groups have only prefix forms (fluoro-, chloro-, bromo-, iodo-, nitro-) and never appear as suffixes.

Rearrangement Reactions

Rearrangement reactions produce a structural isomer of the starting material without any atoms being added or removed — the molecular formula stays the same but the connectivity changes. A clinically important example is keto-enol tautomerism, in which an enol form (containing C=C–OH) spontaneously converts to the more thermodynamically stable keto form (containing C=O). In metabolic pathways, enzymes catalyse such tautomeric shifts, for instance during the interconversion of pyruvate and its enol form.

Cyclic hydrocarbons are molecules whose carbon chain closes into a ring. Cycloalkanes (e.g., cyclohexane, C₆H₁₂) are saturated cyclic hydrocarbons with the general formula CnH2n.

Sigma and Pi Bonds

Every covalent bond in organic chemistry is either a sigma bond or a pi bond:

  • Sigma (σ) bond: Formed by head-on overlap of orbitals along the bond axis. This is the first bond formed between two atoms. It allows free rotation. Every single bond is a sigma bond.
  • Pi (π) bond: Formed by sideways overlap of p-orbitals above and below the bond axis. Pi bonds are the second and third bonds in double and triple bonds, respectively. They prevent free rotation around the bond.

A double bond = 1σ + 1π. A triple bond = 1σ + 2π.

Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Carbons

The degree of a carbon atom is determined by how many other carbon atoms are directly bonded to it:

  • Primary (1°) carbon: bonded to one other carbon
  • Secondary (2°) carbon: bonded to two other carbons
  • Tertiary (3°) carbon: bonded to three other carbons
  • Quaternary (4°) carbon: bonded to four other carbons (no hydrogen attached)

Chemical Formulas

  • Molecular formula: Shows the actual number of each type of atom. Example: C₂H₆O for ethanol.
  • Empirical formula: Shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms. Example: CH₂O is the empirical formula for both formaldehyde (CH₂O) and acetic acid (C₂H₄O₂), because both reduce to the same simplest ratio of C:H:O = 1:2:1. The molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms, while the empirical formula shows only the ratio.
  • Structural formula: Shows how atoms are connected. Can be drawn as a full Lewis structure, condensed (CH₃CH₂OH), or as a line-angle (skeletal) formula where carbon atoms are implied at vertices and line endpoints.

Key Points — Chapter 17

  • Carbon is tetravalent (always forms 4 bonds) and excels at catenation (forming chains with itself).
  • Organic compounds = hydrocarbons + hydrocarbon derivatives (which contain functional groups).
  • A sigma bond is head-on orbital overlap (allows rotation); a pi bond is sideways overlap (restricts rotation).
  • Double bond = 1σ + 1π; Triple bond = 1σ + 2π.
  • Constitution is connectivity; conformation is rotation around single bonds; configuration requires bond-breaking to change.
  • Primary/secondary/tertiary carbons are defined by the number of other carbons attached (1, 2, or 3).

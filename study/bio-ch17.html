Chapter 17

Genetic Disorders: Testing and Counseling

Genetic testing and counseling allow families to understand their risk for inherited conditions and make informed decisions. These fields have advanced dramatically with modern DNA technology.

17.1 Genetic Counseling: Purpose and Process

Genetic counseling is a communication process that helps individuals or families understand and adapt to the medical, psychological, and familial implications of genetic contributions to disease.

The genetic counseling process typically involves:

  1. Family history analysis: Constructing a detailed pedigree (family tree) covering at least three generations.
  2. Risk assessment: Calculating the probability that an individual carries a specific allele or that offspring will be affected.
  3. Education: Explaining the nature of the condition, its inheritance pattern, and available testing options.
  4. Discussion of options: Presenting reproductive options, testing procedures, and management strategies.
  5. Support: Providing psychological support and referrals to specialists and support groups.

Genetic counseling is nondirective: the counselor provides information and support but does not tell the family what decision to make.

Probability Rules in Genetics

Risk calculations in genetic counseling rely on two fundamental probability rules. The Rule of Multiplication states that the probability of two independent events both occurring equals the product of their individual probabilities. For example, if both parents are carriers (Aa) of a recessive disorder, the chance of a child being affected (aa) is ¼ × ¼ = ... actually, the chance is simply ¼ from the Punnett square. A better illustration: the probability of two carrier parents having two affected children in a row is ¼ × ¼ = 1/16. The Rule of Addition states that the probability of one or another mutually exclusive event equals the sum of their individual probabilities. From a cross of Aa × Aa, the probability of a heterozygous offspring is P(inheriting A from father, a from mother) + P(inheriting a from father, A from mother) = ¼ + ¼ = ½.

Gamete Diversity and the 2n Formula

For an organism heterozygous at n independently assorting genes, the number of genetically distinct gamete types it can produce is 2n. An individual heterozygous at 3 loci (AaBbCc) produces 23 = 8 different gamete types. This exponential increase is a major source of genetic variation during sexual reproduction and is important when calculating phenotypic ratios in complex crosses.

Disease Risk Estimation in Multifactorial Disorders

For multifactorial conditions (those involving multiple genes plus environmental contributions), counselors may estimate an individual's disease risk as the sum of familial risk (pF, based on the number and degree of affected relatives) and population risk (pP, the baseline prevalence in the general population). While this additive model is a simplification, it provides a practical framework for communicating risk to families when precise single-gene probabilities do not apply.

17.2 Carrier Recognition Methods

Identifying carriers (heterozygous individuals who do not show symptoms but can pass a recessive allele to offspring) is a cornerstone of genetic counseling.

  • Pedigree analysis: Tracing a condition through family members to identify likely carriers.
  • Biochemical tests: Measuring enzyme activity levels. Carriers of some recessive conditions (e.g., Tay-Sachs disease) have reduced enzyme activity compared to noncarriers, even though they are asymptomatic.
  • DNA-based testing: Directly analyzing DNA to detect specific mutations. This is the most definitive method. PCR and sequencing can identify carriers of known mutations with high accuracy.
  • Population-based carrier screening: Targeted screening of populations with higher carrier frequencies for specific conditions (e.g., Tay-Sachs in Ashkenazi Jewish populations, sickle cell trait in individuals of African descent).

17.3 Fetal Testing (Prenatal Diagnosis)

Amniocentesis

A needle is inserted through the mother's abdominal wall into the amniotic sac to withdraw a sample of amniotic fluid containing fetal cells. These cells can be cultured and used for karyotyping (to detect chromosome abnormalities) or DNA analysis (to detect gene mutations).

  • Typically performed at 15-20 weeks of pregnancy.
  • Results take approximately 2-3 weeks (time needed for cell culture).
  • Carries a small risk (approximately 0.1-0.3%) of miscarriage.

Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)

A small sample of chorionic villi (tiny finger-like projections of the placenta) is collected, either through the cervix (transcervical) or through the abdominal wall (transabdominal). Since chorionic villi are derived from the embryo, they share the same genetic makeup.

  • Can be performed earlier than amniocentesis: 10-13 weeks of pregnancy.
  • Results available faster (cells are already dividing rapidly).
  • Slightly higher miscarriage risk compared to amniocentesis (approximately 0.5-1%).
  • Cannot detect neural tube defects (which amniocentesis can, via alpha-fetoprotein levels).

17.4 Newborn Screening Programs

Many countries mandate screening of all newborns for specific treatable genetic conditions. The principle is that early detection allows early treatment, preventing irreversible damage.

Commonly screened conditions include:

  • Phenylketonuria (PKU): Inability to metabolize phenylalanine. Without a special diet, toxic buildup causes severe intellectual disability. Detected by a simple blood test (heel prick).
  • Congenital hypothyroidism: Insufficient thyroid hormone production. Early thyroid hormone replacement prevents intellectual disability and growth failure.
  • Sickle cell disease: Abnormal hemoglobin. Early detection allows preventive antibiotics and monitoring.
  • Cystic fibrosis: Detected by elevated immunoreactive trypsinogen (IRT) in blood.

17.5 Ethical Considerations

Genetic testing raises important ethical questions:

  • Informed consent: Patients must understand what is being tested and the possible implications before agreeing.
  • Privacy and confidentiality: Genetic information must be protected from misuse by employers, insurers, or others.
  • Genetic discrimination: Laws in many countries (such as GINA in the United States) prohibit discrimination based on genetic information.
  • Predictive testing: Testing for late-onset conditions (e.g., Huntington disease) raises questions about the right to know versus the right not to know.
  • Reproductive decisions: Prenatal testing results may influence decisions about pregnancy continuation, raising personal and societal ethical considerations.
KEY POINTS — Chapter 17
  • Genetic counseling is nondirective and involves pedigree analysis, risk assessment, education, and support.
  • Carriers can be identified by pedigree analysis, biochemical tests, or direct DNA testing.
  • Amniocentesis (15-20 weeks) and CVS (10-13 weeks) are the two main prenatal diagnostic procedures.
  • Newborn screening detects treatable conditions early (PKU, hypothyroidism, sickle cell, etc.).
  • Ethical considerations include informed consent, privacy, genetic discrimination, and reproductive autonomy.

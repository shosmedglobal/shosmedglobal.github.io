Reproductive System

Male Reproductive System

Testes

The testes (singular: testis) are the male gonads, located outside the body in the scrotum. This external position is crucial because sperm production (spermatogenesis) requires a temperature 2–3 degrees Celsius lower than core body temperature. Each testis contains hundreds of tightly coiled seminiferous tubules, where spermatogenesis occurs. Between the tubules, Leydig cells (interstitial cells) produce the hormone testosterone. Within the tubules, Sertoli cells (sustentacular cells) nourish and support developing sperm.

Sertoli cells form the blood-testis barrier through tight junctions between adjacent cells, dividing each seminiferous tubule into a basal compartment (containing spermatogonia) and an adluminal compartment (containing later-stage spermatocytes and spermatids). This barrier prevents immune cells and antibodies from reaching developing sperm, which express unique surface antigens that would otherwise trigger an autoimmune response. Sertoli cells also produce inhibin, a peptide hormone that selectively inhibits FSH secretion from the anterior pituitary through negative feedback, helping to fine-tune the rate of spermatogenesis without affecting LH levels.

Sperm Transport Pathway

Sperm travel through the following structures in order:

  1. Seminiferous tubules (where sperm are produced)
  2. Epididymis: A coiled tube on the surface of each testis where sperm mature (gain motility) and are stored. Maturation takes about 2-3 weeks.
  3. Vas deferens (ductus deferens): A muscular tube that propels sperm from the epididymis upward through the inguinal canal into the pelvic cavity.
  4. Ejaculatory duct: Formed where the vas deferens joins the duct of the seminal vesicle. Passes through the prostate gland and opens into the urethra.
  5. Urethra: The final passageway; carries semen (and urine at separate times) out through the penis.

Accessory Glands

Three glands contribute fluids that, together with sperm, make up semen:

  • Seminal vesicles: Produce about 60-70% of semen volume. Their secretion is rich in fructose (energy source for sperm), prostaglandins, and proteins.
  • Prostate gland: A walnut-sized gland surrounding the urethra just below the bladder. Produces about 25-30% of semen volume. Its secretion is slightly alkaline, helping to neutralize the acidic environment of the vagina. Contains enzymes and zinc.
  • Bulbourethral glands (Cowper's glands): Small glands that secrete a clear, alkaline mucus that lubricates the urethra and neutralizes residual acidity before ejaculation.

Female Reproductive System

Ovaries

The ovaries are the female gonads, located in the pelvic cavity on either side of the uterus. They have two functions: producing egg cells (oogenesis) and secreting hormones (estrogen and progesterone). At birth, the ovaries together contain about 1–2 million immature egg cells (primary oocytes). By puberty, roughly 300,000-400,000 remain, of which only about 400-500 will actually ovulate during a woman's reproductive lifetime.

Reproductive Tract

  • Uterine tubes (oviducts/Fallopian tubes): Extend from near each ovary to the uterus. The funnel-shaped end near the ovary has finger-like projections called fimbriae that catch the released egg during ovulation. The tubes are lined with ciliated epithelium and smooth muscle that propel the egg toward the uterus. Fertilization normally occurs in the ampulla (the widest part) of the uterine tube.
  • Uterus (womb): A thick-walled, pear-shaped muscular organ where the fertilized egg implants and develops. Its wall has three layers: the endometrium (inner lining, which thickens during the menstrual cycle and is shed during menstruation if no implantation occurs), the myometrium (thick smooth muscle layer that contracts during labor), and the perimetrium (outer serous covering). The lower narrow part is the cervix, which opens into the vagina.
  • Vagina: A muscular, elastic canal extending from the cervix to the exterior. Serves as the birth canal, the passageway for menstrual flow, and the receptacle for sperm during intercourse. Its lining is non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium with an acidic environment (pH ~3.8-4.5) that helps prevent infections.

Cervical Mucus Changes

The cervix produces mucus whose properties change dramatically during the menstrual cycle. Under estrogen dominance (around ovulation), cervical mucus becomes thin, watery, and stretchy (spinnbarkeit), facilitating sperm transport into the uterus. Under progesterone dominance (luteal phase), the mucus becomes thick, sticky, and acidic, forming a barrier that blocks sperm and bacteria. These changes can be observed clinically and are the basis of some natural family planning methods.

Hormonal Control of Reproduction

Reproduction is controlled by the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis:

  1. The hypothalamus releases gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH).
  2. GnRH stimulates the anterior pituitary gland to release two gonadotropins:
    • Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH): In females, stimulates the growth of ovarian follicles (and thus egg maturation) and estrogen production. In males, stimulates Sertoli cells and promotes spermatogenesis.
    • Luteinizing hormone (LH): In females, triggers ovulation and stimulates the formation of the corpus luteum (which produces progesterone). In males, stimulates Leydig cells to produce testosterone.
  3. The gonads produce sex hormones that exert negative feedback on the hypothalamus and pituitary, regulating the system:
    • Estrogen: Primary female sex hormone, produced mainly by the ovaries. Promotes development of female secondary sex characteristics (breast development, fat distribution), stimulates endometrial growth, and maintains bone density.
    • Progesterone: Produced by the corpus luteum (and later by the placenta during pregnancy). Prepares and maintains the endometrium for implantation, inhibits uterine contractions during pregnancy.
    • Testosterone: Primary male sex hormone, produced mainly by the Leydig cells of the testes. Drives spermatogenesis, promotes male secondary sex characteristics (deep voice, facial hair, muscle mass, bone growth).

The Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle averages about 28 days (though 21-35 days is considered normal). It involves coordinated changes in the ovary and the uterus:

Ovarian Cycle

  1. Follicular phase (days 1-13): FSH stimulates several ovarian follicles to begin developing. Usually, one dominant follicle emerges. As the follicle grows, it produces increasing amounts of estrogen.
  2. Ovulation (day ~14): The surge of estrogen triggers a spike in LH (the "LH surge"), which causes the dominant follicle to rupture and release the mature oocyte (egg) into the uterine tube.
  3. Luteal phase (days 15-28): The ruptured follicle transforms into the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone and estrogen. If the egg is not fertilized, the corpus luteum degenerates after about 10-12 days, progesterone drops, and menstruation follows.

Uterine (Endometrial) Cycle

  1. Menstrual phase (days 1-5): The functional layer of the endometrium is shed (menstruation), due to the drop in progesterone and estrogen.
  2. Proliferative phase (days 6-14): Rising estrogen stimulates the endometrium to regrow and thicken.
  3. Secretory phase (days 15-28): Progesterone from the corpus luteum causes the endometrium to become richly vascularized and to secrete nutrients, preparing for possible implantation of a fertilized egg.

Embryonic and Fetal Development Overview

If fertilization occurs, the resulting zygote begins dividing as it travels down the uterine tube. By about day 6-7, the blastocyst implants in the endometrium. Development is divided into two periods:

  • Embryonic period (weeks 1-8): All major organ systems are established (organogenesis). This is the most sensitive period for birth defects caused by teratogens (harmful substances like alcohol, certain drugs, and infections). By the end of week 8, the embryo is about 3 cm long and has recognizable human features.
  • Fetal period (weeks 9-38/40): Organs grow and mature. The fetus increases dramatically in size. Key milestones:
    • Week 12: Sex can be determined; skeletal ossification begins.
    • Week 20: Fetal movements (quickening) felt by mother; vernix caseosa (protective waxy coating) covers skin.
    • Week 24: Lungs begin producing surfactant; the fetus may be viable with intensive care if born prematurely.
    • Weeks 28-38: Rapid brain development, fat deposition, lung maturation.

The placenta develops from both maternal and embryonic tissues. It functions as the interface between mother and fetus, providing oxygen and nutrients, removing waste, producing hormones (hCG, estrogen, progesterone, human placental lactogen), and acting as a partial barrier to some pathogens (though not all).

Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) is produced by the trophoblast cells of the implanting blastocyst and later by the placenta. Its critical early function is to maintain the corpus luteum, preventing its degeneration and ensuring continued progesterone production until the placenta takes over hormone synthesis around weeks 8–12 of pregnancy. hCG levels rise rapidly in early pregnancy and are the hormone detected by standard pregnancy tests.

Common Reproductive Disorders

  • Endometriosis: A condition where endometrial tissue grows outside the uterus (on ovaries, uterine tubes, or pelvic surfaces), causing pain, inflammation, and potentially infertility.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): A hormonal disorder characterized by enlarged ovaries with small cysts, irregular menstruation, excess androgens (male hormones), and potential infertility. One of the most common endocrine disorders in women of reproductive age.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH): Noncancerous enlargement of the prostate gland, common in older men. Can compress the urethra and cause difficulty urinating.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Implantation of a fertilized egg outside the uterus, most commonly in the uterine tube. A medical emergency, as the tube can rupture.
  • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs): Include chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis, HPV (human papillomavirus, which can cause cervical cancer), HIV, and genital herpes.
  • Infertility: Affects approximately 10-15% of couples. Causes can be male (low sperm count, poor motility), female (ovulation disorders, tubal blockage), or combined.

Key Points - Chapter 30

  • Male: sperm are produced in seminiferous tubules, mature in the epididymis, and travel through the vas deferens, ejaculatory duct, and urethra. Semen = sperm + secretions from seminal vesicles, prostate, and bulbourethral glands.
  • Female: eggs mature in ovarian follicles, are released during ovulation, and travel through uterine tubes. Fertilization normally occurs in the ampulla of the uterine tube.
  • HPG axis: hypothalamus (GnRH) → anterior pituitary (FSH + LH) → gonads (sex hormones). Negative feedback regulates the cycle.
  • Menstrual cycle: follicular/proliferative phase (estrogen, endometrial growth) → ovulation (LH surge) → luteal/secretory phase (progesterone, endometrial preparation).
  • Embryonic period (weeks 1-8): organogenesis, most sensitive to teratogens. Fetal period (weeks 9-38): growth and maturation.
  • The placenta provides nutrient/gas exchange between mother and fetus and produces hormones (hCG, estrogen, progesterone).

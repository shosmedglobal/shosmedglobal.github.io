9.1 Early Earth Conditions

Earth formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago. The early atmosphere was very different from today's: it was reducing (lacking free oxygen) and likely contained water vapor, carbon dioxide, nitrogen, methane, ammonia, and hydrogen. Volcanic activity was intense, lightning storms were frequent, and ultraviolet radiation from the sun was harsh (no ozone layer yet). Oceans formed as the planet cooled. These conditions set the stage for the chemical reactions that would eventually give rise to life.

9.2 Chemical Evolution: From Simple Molecules to Complex Organic Compounds

The Oparin-Haldane hypothesis (proposed independently by Aleksandr Oparin and J.B.S. Haldane in the 1920s) suggested that simple organic molecules could have formed spontaneously in Earth's early reducing atmosphere, given energy sources like lightning and UV radiation. These molecules would have accumulated in the oceans, forming a "primordial soup" where increasingly complex chemical reactions could occur.

9.3 The Miller-Urey Experiment (1953)

Stanley Miller and Harold Urey designed an experiment to test the Oparin-Haldane hypothesis. They set up a closed system simulating early Earth conditions: a flask of water (representing the ocean) connected to a chamber containing methane, ammonia, hydrogen, and water vapor (representing the early atmosphere). Electrical sparks (simulating lightning) were discharged through the gas mixture. After a week, they analyzed the contents and found that numerous amino acids and other organic molecules had formed spontaneously. This was a landmark demonstration that the building blocks of life could arise from simple inorganic precursors under plausible early Earth conditions.

Later experiments using different atmospheric compositions (more CO₂ and N₂, less CH₄ and NH₃) still produced organic molecules, though in smaller quantities. Deep-sea hydrothermal vents have also been proposed as alternative sites for organic molecule synthesis.

9.4 Protobionts: Steps Toward Cells

Before true cells existed, simpler structures may have formed that had some cell-like properties:

  • Microspheres: Proteinoid droplets that form spontaneously when heated amino acid mixtures are cooled in water. They have a double-layer boundary, can absorb substances from the environment, and can even bud to form smaller spheres.
  • Liposomes: Spontaneously forming lipid bilayer vesicles when phospholipids are mixed with water. They can enclose molecules and selectively control what passes through their membrane. They are considered the most plausible model for early proto-cell membranes.
  • Coacervates: Clusters of charged organic molecules surrounded by a layer of water molecules. They can concentrate organic molecules from the surrounding solution.

These structures are collectively called protobionts - assemblages that show some, but not all, of the properties of living cells.

9.5 The RNA World Hypothesis

A central puzzle of the origin of life: DNA stores information but cannot catalyze reactions; proteins catalyze reactions but cannot store genetic information. Which came first?

The RNA world hypothesis proposes that RNA came first because it can do both - it can store information (like DNA) and catalyze chemical reactions (like proteins). Catalytic RNA molecules are called ribozymes. In modern cells, rRNA catalyzes peptide bond formation in the ribosome, supporting the idea that RNA once played a more central role. Under this scenario, early self-replicating RNA molecules gradually evolved; eventually, DNA took over information storage (being more stable) and proteins took over catalysis (being more versatile), leaving RNA in its current intermediary role.

9.6 Timeline of Evolution

  • ~4.5 billion years ago (bya): Earth forms.
  • ~4.0-3.8 bya: First organic molecules form; late heavy bombardment ends.
  • ~3.5 bya: Oldest fossil evidence of prokaryotic life (stromatolites - layered structures formed by cyanobacteria).
  • ~2.7-2.4 bya: Cyanobacteria begin oxygenic photosynthesis; oxygen accumulates in the atmosphere (the "Great Oxidation Event").
  • ~2.0-1.5 bya: First eukaryotic cells appear (endosymbiosis).
  • ~1.2 bya: First multicellular organisms.
  • ~540 million years ago (mya): Cambrian explosion - rapid diversification of animal body plans.

9.7 Evolution of Prokaryotic Metabolism

The earliest prokaryotes were likely anaerobic chemoheterotrophs that obtained energy from simple organic molecules in the primordial soup. As these food sources became scarce, some lineages evolved chemoautotrophy (using inorganic energy sources and fixing CO₂). Later, anoxygenic photosynthesis evolved (using light but not producing O₂), followed by oxygenic photosynthesis in cyanobacteria, which released O₂ as a byproduct. The accumulation of oxygen in the atmosphere was initially toxic to many anaerobes but eventually enabled the evolution of aerobic respiration, a far more efficient way to extract energy from organic molecules.

9.8 Endosymbiotic Theory (Revisited)

As covered in Chapter 3, the endosymbiotic theory explains how eukaryotic cells acquired mitochondria and chloroplasts. An ancestral archaeal-like host cell engulfed an aerobic bacterium (which became the mitochondrion). Later, some of these early eukaryotes also engulfed a photosynthetic cyanobacterium (which became the chloroplast). This gave rise to the plant and algal lineages.

9.9 The Phylogenetic Tree of Life

All known life on Earth can be organized into three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. Molecular phylogenetics (comparing ribosomal RNA sequences and other conserved genes) reveals that Archaea and Eukarya share a more recent common ancestor with each other than either does with Bacteria. The universal tree of life is rooted in a hypothetical Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA), which likely lived about 3.5–4 billion years ago.

9.10 Astrobiology: The Search for Life Beyond Earth

Astrobiology (also called exobiology) is the scientific study of whether life exists or could exist elsewhere in the universe. It is a natural extension of origin-of-life research: if we understand the conditions under which life arose on Earth, we can evaluate whether similar conditions exist elsewhere.

The essential requirements for life as we currently understand it include: liquid water (as a solvent for biochemical reactions), a source of energy (solar, chemical, or geothermal), and organic molecules (carbon-based building blocks). Scientists search for these conditions using two complementary approaches. Indirect methods analyze the chemical composition and atmospheric spectra of distant planets and moons through telescopes and spectroscopy - for instance, detecting atmospheric biosignature gases such as oxygen, methane, or phosphine. Direct methods include analyzing meteorites (the Murchison meteorite contained amino acids), examining samples returned by space probes, and deploying rovers equipped with instruments to test for organic molecules and signs of past or present water.

No definitive evidence of extraterrestrial life has been found to date. However, the discovery of extremophiles on Earth - organisms thriving in boiling hot springs, deep-sea hydrothermal vents, hypersaline lakes, Antarctic glacial ice, and intensely radioactive environments - has dramatically expanded scientific understanding of the physical and chemical limits within which life can persist. Within our solar system, the most promising candidates for harboring microbial life include Mars (evidence of past liquid water and seasonal methane fluctuations), Europa (a moon of Jupiter with a global subsurface liquid water ocean beneath its ice crust), and Enceladus (a moon of Saturn from which the Cassini spacecraft detected water vapor plumes containing organic molecules and molecular hydrogen, suggestive of hydrothermal activity on its ocean floor).

Key Points - Chapter 9

  • Early Earth had a reducing atmosphere with no free oxygen; energy sources included lightning, UV radiation, and volcanic heat.
  • The Miller-Urey experiment demonstrated that amino acids can form spontaneously under early Earth conditions.
  • Protobionts (microspheres, liposomes, coacervates) are non-living assemblages with some cell-like properties.
  • The RNA world hypothesis proposes that self-replicating RNA preceded DNA and proteins.
  • Cyanobacterial photosynthesis produced the oxygen that transformed Earth's atmosphere (Great Oxidation Event).
  • Eukaryotic cells arose through endosymbiosis of prokaryotic cells.
  • All life is classified into three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya.

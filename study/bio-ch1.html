Chapter 1

General Features and Energetics of Living Systems

1.1 What Makes Something "Alive"?

At first glance, this question seems obvious - a dog is alive, a rock is not. But biology demands precision, and the line between living and non-living is surprisingly tricky. Living systems share a set of fundamental characteristics that, taken together, distinguish them from everything else in the universe.

Organization

Every living thing is built from one or more cells. Cells are organized into tissues, tissues into organs, organs into organ systems, and organ systems into organisms. Even the simplest single-celled bacterium has an extraordinarily complex internal organization. Think of it this way: a random bag of carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and hydrogen atoms will never spontaneously arrange themselves into a functioning cell. Life maintains a breathtaking level of structural order.

More precisely, life is organized in a hierarchy of increasing complexity: molecules form organelles, which compose cells, which assemble into tissues, then organs, organ systems, and whole organisms. Beyond the individual, organisms form populations (members of the same species in one area), which interact in communities (multiple species), which together with their physical environment constitute ecosystems. All of Earth's ecosystems are contained within the biosphere.

Unicellular organisms demonstrate that a single cell can perform all the functions of life. An amoeba, for instance, moves using pseudopods (temporary cytoplasmic extensions), feeds by engulfing food into vacuoles via phagocytosis, regulates water balance with a contractile vacuole, and maintains distinct regions of endoplasm (inner, granular cytoplasm) and ectoplasm (outer, clear cytoplasm). Bacteria and many protists live successfully as single cells.

Homeostasis

Your body temperature hovers around 37 degrees Celsius regardless of whether you are standing in a snowstorm or sitting in a sauna. This ability to maintain a stable internal environment is called homeostasis. Living organisms constantly monitor their internal conditions (temperature, pH, ion concentrations, water balance) and make adjustments to keep everything within a narrow acceptable range. This typically involves negative feedback loops: when a variable drifts too far in one direction, the system pushes it back.

Animals can be classified by how they handle environmental variation. Regulators (such as endothermic mammals and birds) actively maintain internal parameters like body temperature at a constant level despite external fluctuations. Conformers (ectothermic animals like reptiles and many marine invertebrates) allow their internal conditions to fluctuate with the environment, conserving the energy that regulation would require.

While negative feedback maintains stability, positive feedback amplifies a response until a process is completed. A classic example is blood clotting: when a blood vessel is damaged, platelets aggregate at the site and release chemicals that recruit more platelets, accelerating clot formation in a self-reinforcing cascade. The process only stops when the vessel is sealed. Another example is oxytocin release during childbirth, where uterine contractions stimulate more oxytocin release, intensifying contractions until delivery.

Metabolism

Every cell in your body is running thousands of chemical reactions at any given moment. The sum total of all these reactions is called metabolism. Metabolism has two sides: catabolism breaks larger molecules into smaller ones, releasing energy (for example, breaking down glucose). Anabolism uses energy to build larger molecules from smaller building blocks (for example, synthesizing proteins from amino acids). Without metabolism, nothing else about life would be possible.

Growth and Development

Living organisms grow in an organized way, following genetic instructions. A fertilized human egg develops into a baby with trillions of specialized cells, not randomly, but according to a precise developmental program encoded in DNA.

Multicellular life depends on four essential cellular processes: proliferation (cell division to increase numbers), specialization (differentiation into distinct cell types), interaction (communication between cells to coordinate function), and movement (cell migration during development and tissue repair). Every multicellular organism begins as a single cell - it is essentially a clone that diversifies through these processes. The principle that "form fits function" applies at every level: the shape of a structure is closely tied to its role, from the biconcave disc of a red blood cell to the branching of a neuron.

Adaptation

Over generations, populations of organisms change in ways that make them better suited to their environment. This is adaptation through evolution by natural selection. Individual organisms can also adjust to their environment within their lifetime (acclimatization), but evolutionary adaptation is the larger story.

Response to Stimuli

Touch a hot stove and you pull your hand away instantly. A sunflower turns to face the sun. A bacterium swims toward a food source. All living things detect and respond to changes in their environment, whether those changes are light, temperature, chemicals, pressure, or countless other stimuli.

Reproduction

Life makes more life. This is perhaps the most fundamental feature. Without reproduction, life would end in a single generation. Organisms reproduce in remarkably diverse ways, as covered shortly.

Two Approaches to Biology

Biology can be approached from two complementary angles. The reductionist approach studies life at the molecular level, revealing that all organisms share fundamental biochemical mechanisms encoded in DNA. The evolutionary approach examines how diversity arises through processes like mutation, natural selection, and genetic drift. Together, these perspectives explain both the unity and the diversity of life.

Model Organisms

Biologists use model organisms to study fundamental processes that apply across species. Common in vivo models include the fruit fly (Drosophila melanogaster, used extensively in genetics), the nematode C. elegans (cell lineage and development), the African clawed frog Xenopus laevis (embryology), and the mouse Mus musculus (which shares roughly 85% protein-coding gene homology with humans). Plants are modeled using Arabidopsis thaliana and maize (Zea mays). In vitro models include permanent cell lines like HeLa cells (derived from a human cervical cancer) and primary cell cultures. Key microbial models include E. coli and Bacillus subtilis (bacteria), lambda phage and tobacco mosaic virus (viruses), and the yeasts Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Schizosaccharomyces pombe.

1.2 The Second Law of Thermodynamics and Biology

The second law of thermodynamics states that in any closed system, the total entropy (disorder) tends to increase over time. At first, this seems to contradict biology: how can life become more ordered and complex if the universe trends toward disorder?

The key word is closed system. Living organisms are open systems - they constantly exchange matter and energy with their surroundings. An organism can maintain and even increase its internal order, but only by increasing the disorder of its environment even more. When you eat food and metabolize it, you convert highly organized molecules into simpler waste products (carbon dioxide, water) and heat. The total entropy of the universe still increases, but your little corner of it stays beautifully organized - for a while.

Think of it like keeping your room clean. You can make your room very tidy, but only by expending energy (doing work) and generating waste and heat. The room gets more ordered; the universe as a whole gets slightly more disordered. Living organisms do the same thing on a molecular scale.

1.3 Types of Reproduction

Binary Fission and Asexual Reproduction

The simplest form of reproduction is binary fission, used by bacteria. The cell copies its single circular chromosome, elongates, and splits into two genetically identical daughter cells. This is fast and efficient but produces offspring that are genetic clones of the parent, limiting genetic diversity.

Bacterial Conjugation

Although bacteria usually reproduce asexually, they have a mechanism for exchanging genetic material called conjugation. A donor bacterium extends a thin protein tube called a pilus toward a recipient cell. Through this bridge, the donor transfers a copy of a small circular DNA molecule called a plasmid (and sometimes part of the main chromosome) to the recipient. This is not reproduction per se - it does not create a new organism - but it introduces genetic variation into the population, which is crucial for adaptation.

Parthenogenesis

Some animals can produce offspring from an unfertilized egg. This is called parthenogenesis (from the Greek for "virgin origin"). It occurs naturally in certain insects, reptiles, and fish. The offspring may be haploid or diploid depending on the species and mechanism. Since no second parent contributes DNA, genetic diversity is limited compared to sexual reproduction, although some parthenogenetic species have mechanisms that introduce a degree of genetic shuffling.

Strobilation

Strobilation is a form of asexual reproduction observed in jellyfish (class Scyphozoa). During this process, a sessile polyp undergoes transverse division to produce a stack of disc-shaped segments called ephyrae, each of which detaches and develops into a free-swimming medusa. Strobilation is one of several asexual strategies in the animal kingdom, alongside budding, fragmentation, and parthenogenesis.

Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of two specialized cells called gametes (typically a sperm and an egg) from two different parents. Each gamete is haploid (carrying half the full set of chromosomes), and their fusion produces a diploid offspring with a unique combination of genes from both parents. Sexual reproduction is the major engine of genetic diversity in eukaryotic organisms, and its molecular details are covered in Chapter 8.

Sexual reproduction can occur through two modes of fertilization. Autogamy (self-fertilization) occurs in hermaphroditic organisms that produce both egg and sperm, such as certain flatworms and many flowering plants. Allogamy (cross-fertilization) involves gametes from two different individuals and maximizes genetic diversity. Most animals use allogamy exclusively, while many plants can use both strategies depending on conditions.

Most sexually reproducing species are anisogamous, meaning their gametes differ in size. Males produce small, numerous, motile gametes (sperm or microspores), while females produce large, nutrient-rich, fewer gametes (ova or megaspores). This size asymmetry between male and female gametes is a defining feature of sexual dimorphism at the cellular level.

Interspecific Hybridization

Species boundaries, while real, are not always absolute. Closely related species can sometimes interbreed. A mule (male donkey crossed with a female horse) and a hinny (female donkey crossed with a male horse) are usually sterile because the parent species have different chromosome numbers (donkey 62, horse 64), preventing normal meiotic pairing. A liger (male lion crossed with a female tiger) and a tigon (male tiger crossed with a female lion) are similarly produced from crossing closely related species. Zebroids (such as the zonkey and zorse) also exist. These hybrids are frequently sterile and serve as exam-relevant examples illustrating reproductive isolation and species concepts.

1.4 Energetics of Living Systems

Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts - almost always proteins - that accelerate specific chemical reactions without being consumed. The human body contains approximately 10,000 different enzymes. Each enzyme acts on specific substrates (the molecules it transforms) and converts them into products. Enzyme specificity arises from the precise three-dimensional shape of its active site, which complements the substrate in a "lock and key" or "induced fit" manner.

Every activity of life requires energy. The universal energy currency in cells is a molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate). When a cell needs energy for any task - contracting a muscle, transporting an ion, building a protein - it breaks the bond between the second and third phosphate groups of ATP, releasing energy and producing ADP (adenosine diphosphate) and an inorganic phosphate group. The cell then regenerates ATP by reattaching a phosphate to ADP, using energy harvested from food molecules or sunlight.

Photosynthesis: Capturing Light Energy

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and certain bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. It takes place in the chloroplast and has two main stages:

Light-Dependent Reactions (in the thylakoid membranes): Chlorophyll and other pigments absorb photons of light. This energy is used to split water molecules (photolysis), releasing oxygen as a byproduct. The energy from light drives electrons through a series of protein complexes called the electron transport chain, generating ATP (by a process called photophosphorylation) and NADPH (a high-energy electron carrier). Think of the light reactions as charging up the cell's batteries (ATP and NADPH).

Light-Independent Reactions / Calvin Cycle (in the stroma): The ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions power the Calvin cycle, which fixes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into organic molecules. The enzyme RuBisCO catalyzes the first step, attaching CO₂ to a five-carbon sugar (RuBP). Through a series of reactions, the cycle ultimately produces glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), which can be used to build glucose and other sugars. For every three molecules of CO₂ fixed, one molecule of G3P exits the cycle; the rest are recycled to regenerate RuBP. It takes three turns of the cycle (fixing three CO₂) to produce one net G3P that exits the cycle, and six turns to produce one glucose (two G3P molecules combine).

Cellular Respiration: Extracting Energy from Glucose

Cellular respiration is, in a sense, the reverse of photosynthesis: it breaks down glucose (and other fuel molecules) to harvest the energy stored in their chemical bonds and convert it into ATP. The overall equation is: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy (as ATP). It has four main stages:

1. Glycolysis (cytoplasm): One molecule of glucose (6 carbons) is split into two molecules of pyruvate (3 carbons each). This yields a net gain of 2 ATP and 2 NADH. Glycolysis does not require oxygen - it is anaerobic.

2. Pyruvate Oxidation (mitochondrial matrix): Each pyruvate enters the mitochondrion and is converted into acetyl-CoA (2 carbons). One carbon is released as CO₂, and one NADH is generated per pyruvate. So from one glucose, this step produces 2 acetyl-CoA, 2 CO₂, and 2 NADH.

3. Krebs Cycle / Citric Acid Cycle (mitochondrial matrix): Each acetyl-CoA enters the cycle by combining with oxaloacetate (4 carbons) to form citrate (6 carbons). Through a series of reactions, two carbons are released as CO₂, and the cycle regenerates oxaloacetate. Per turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 GTP (equivalent to 1 ATP). Since each glucose produces 2 acetyl-CoA, the Krebs cycle turns twice per glucose: yielding 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, and 2 ATP (as GTP).

4. Electron Transport Chain and Oxidative Phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane): This is where the big payoff happens. NADH and FADH₂ donate their electrons to a series of protein complexes embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As electrons pass through these complexes, energy is released and used to pump hydrogen ions (protons) from the matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a concentration gradient. Protons then flow back through ATP synthase, a molecular turbine that uses this flow (chemiosmosis) to phosphorylate ADP into ATP. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor - it combines with electrons and protons to form water. This stage produces approximately 32 to 34 ATP per glucose (the exact number varies by cell type and conditions).

Total ATP yield per glucose: Approximately 36 to 38 ATP molecules (2 from glycolysis + 2 from Krebs + ~32-34 from oxidative phosphorylation).

Detailed ATP accounting: Glycolysis produces 4 ATP gross but invests 2, netting 2 ATP per glucose. The Krebs cycle generates 2 GTP (equivalent to 2 ATP) by substrate-level phosphorylation per glucose. In the electron transport chain, yields vary by carrier: each NADH from glycolysis yields approximately 2 ATP (due to shuttle costs across the mitochondrial membrane), while each mitochondrial NADH yields approximately 3 ATP, and each FADH₂ yields approximately 2 ATP. Note that these older textbook values (totaling 36–38 ATP) have been revised downward in modern biochemistry to approximately 30–32 ATP per glucose, accounting for updated P/O ratios and the cost of NADH shuttle systems.

Fermentation: When Oxygen Is Absent

When oxygen is not available, cells cannot run the electron transport chain. Glycolysis can still proceed, but the NADH it produces has nowhere to dump its electrons. Fermentation solves this problem by regenerating NAD+ so glycolysis can continue. There are two main types:

  • Lactic acid fermentation: Pyruvate is directly reduced to lactate. This occurs in your muscle cells during intense exercise and in certain bacteria (which is how yogurt is made). The net yield is only 2 ATP per glucose.
  • Alcoholic fermentation: Pyruvate is converted to ethanol and CO₂. This occurs in yeast and some bacteria, and is the basis for brewing beer and baking bread (the CO₂ makes dough rise). The net yield is also only 2 ATP per glucose.

Fermentation is far less efficient than aerobic respiration (2 ATP vs ~36-38 ATP), but it keeps the cell alive when oxygen is scarce.

1.5 Key Milestones in Biology and Medicine

Understanding the history of biological discovery provides context for modern concepts and is frequently tested in entrance examinations. The following milestones highlight key advances and the scientists behind them.

Foundations of Anatomy and Physiology

Aristotle (384–322 BC) is often called the Father of Biology. He systematically observed and classified over 500 animal species, pioneering empirical study of the natural world. Andreas Vesalius (1514–1564) revolutionized anatomy by performing meticulous human dissections. His masterwork De Humani Corporis Fabrica (1543) corrected centuries of errors inherited from Galen and established anatomy as a rigorous observational science. William Harvey (1578–1657) demonstrated that blood circulates continuously through arteries and veins in a closed loop, with the heart acting as a muscular pump - overturning the Galenic model that had dominated for nearly 1,500 years.

The Cell and the Microscope

Robert Hooke (1635–1703) examined thin slices of cork under a compound microscope in 1665 and described the small box-like compartments he observed as "cells" - giving biology its most fundamental term. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (1632–1723) built powerful single-lens microscopes and was the first person to observe living bacteria, protists, sperm cells, and red blood cells, earning the title Father of Microbiology. Jan Evangelista Purkyně (1787–1869), a Bohemian physiologist, coined the term "protoplasm" for the living contents of cells and made foundational contributions to histology and cell biology. He is recognized alongside Schleiden and Schwann as one of the founders of cell theory.

Classification and Evolution

Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778) established the binomial nomenclature system still used today, in which every species receives a two-part Latin name (genus + species). His hierarchical classification scheme laid the groundwork for modern taxonomy. Jean-Baptiste Lamarck (1744–1829) proposed one of the first broad theories of biological evolution, though his mechanism (inheritance of acquired characteristics) was later replaced by Darwinian natural selection. Charles Darwin (1809–1882) formulated the theory of evolution by natural selection, published in On the Origin of Species (1859), which remains the central organizing principle of biology.

Microbiology and Infectious Disease

Louis Pasteur (1822–1895) disproved spontaneous generation with his elegant swan-neck flask experiments, developed the pasteurization process, and created vaccines against rabies and anthrax. He is widely regarded as the Father of Microbiology (along with Koch). Robert Koch (1843–1910) established definitive links between specific bacteria and specific diseases - identifying the causative agents of anthrax (1876), tuberculosis (1882), and cholera (1883). He formulated Koch's postulates, the four criteria for proving that a particular microorganism causes a particular disease, which remain foundational in medical microbiology. Koch received the 1905 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. Alexander Fleming (1881–1955) discovered the antibacterial properties of the Penicillium mold in 1928, leading to the development of penicillin - the first antibiotic - and sharing the 1945 Nobel Prize.

Genetics and Molecular Biology

Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) discovered the fundamental laws of inheritance through his experiments with pea plants, though his work was not widely recognized until its rediscovery in 1900. Thomas Hunt Morgan (1866–1945) used Drosophila melanogaster to demonstrate that genes reside on chromosomes, establishing the chromosomal theory of inheritance (1933 Nobel Prize). Barbara McClintock (1902–1992) discovered transposable genetic elements ("jumping genes") in maize - mobile DNA sequences that can change position within the genome. Her work, initially met with skepticism, was vindicated by the 1983 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

Linus Pauling (1901–1994) determined the α-helix and β-sheet secondary structures of proteins and was the first to characterize a "molecular disease" when he identified the abnormal hemoglobin in sickle cell anemia (1949). James Watson and Francis Crick elucidated the double-helix structure of DNA in 1953 (1962 Nobel Prize, shared with Maurice Wilkins), building on crucial X-ray crystallography data from Rosalind Franklin. Har Gobind Khorana and Marshall Nirenberg deciphered the genetic code - determining which nucleotide triplets specify which amino acids - earning them the 1968 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (shared with Robert Holley).

Physiology and Behavior

Ivan Pavlov (1849–1936) demonstrated conditioned reflexes in his famous experiments with dogs, showing that learned associations can trigger automatic physiological responses such as salivation. This work earned him the 1904 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine and founded the study of behavioral physiology. Alexis Carrel (1873–1944) pioneered techniques for vascular suturing and organ transplantation, receiving the 1912 Nobel Prize, and made early advances in tissue culture that enabled cells to be grown outside the body for research.

Key Points - Chapter 1

  • Living organisms share seven key characteristics: organization, homeostasis, metabolism, growth, adaptation, response to stimuli, and reproduction.
  • Organisms are open systems that maintain internal order by increasing entropy in their surroundings, consistent with the second law of thermodynamics.
  • ATP is the universal energy currency of cells; it is spent (ATP → ADP + Pᵢ) and regenerated continuously.
  • Photosynthesis has two stages: light reactions (thylakoids → ATP + NADPH + O₂) and the Calvin cycle (stroma → G3P using CO₂).
  • Cellular respiration has four stages: glycolysis (2 ATP), pyruvate oxidation, Krebs cycle (2 ATP), and the electron transport chain (~32-34 ATP).
  • Fermentation (lactic acid or alcoholic) regenerates NAD+ in the absence of oxygen, yielding only 2 ATP per glucose.
  • Bacterial conjugation, parthenogenesis, and sexual reproduction are distinct strategies for generating or recombining genetic material.

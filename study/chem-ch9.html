9.1 Properties of Solids

Solids have both a fixed shape and a fixed volume. Their particles are packed closely together in relatively fixed positions, vibrating in place but not translating freely. Solids can be classified as crystalline or amorphous.

9.2 Crystalline vs Amorphous Solids

FeatureCrystallineAmorphous
Particle arrangementOrdered, repeating 3D latticeDisordered, no long-range order
Melting pointSharp, well-definedGradual softening over a range
ExamplesNaCl, diamond, ice, quartzGlass, rubber, plastic, tar

9.4 Melting Point

The melting point is the temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid. It reflects the strength of forces between particles. Covalent network solids (like diamond, which melts at ~3550 °C) have the highest melting points. Molecular solids (like ice, 0 °C) have the lowest among crystalline types.

9.5 Sublimation

Sublimation is the direct transition from solid to gas without passing through the liquid phase. Dry ice (solid CO₂) is a familiar example - it turns directly into gaseous CO₂ at atmospheric pressure. Iodine crystals also sublime readily, producing a purple vapour. The reverse process (gas → solid) is called deposition.

Key Points - Chapter 9

  • Crystalline solids have ordered lattices and sharp melting points; amorphous solids lack long-range order and soften gradually.
  • Four types of crystalline solids: ionic, molecular, covalent network, metallic.
  • Melting point reflects the strength of interparticle forces.
  • Covalent network solids have the highest melting points; molecular solids have the lowest.
  • Sublimation is the direct solid-to-gas transition (e.g., dry ice, iodine).

Test Yourself - Chapter 9

1. Glass softens gradually when heated rather than melting at a sharp temperature. Is it crystalline or amorphous?
Show Answer
Amorphous. The lack of long-range order means there is no single temperature at which all bonds break simultaneously, so it softens over a range.
2. Why is diamond one of the hardest known materials?
Show Answer
Diamond is a covalent network solid where every carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement extending throughout the entire crystal. Breaking diamond requires breaking many strong covalent bonds, which requires enormous energy.
3. Why can metals be bent and shaped (malleable) while ionic crystals shatter?
Show Answer
In metals, layers of cations can slide over each other while remaining embedded in the delocalized electron sea, which adapts to the new arrangement. In ionic crystals, shifting layers brings like-charged ions next to each other, creating repulsion that causes the crystal to crack.

3.1 Measurements in Chemistry

Chemistry is a quantitative science. To describe matter and its changes, we use measurable quantities - numbers associated with units. The international system of units (SI, from the French Systeme International) provides the standard.

3.3 The Mole

The mole (mol) is the chemist's counting unit. One mole of anything contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ items (Avogadro's number, NA). This number is chosen so that one mole of carbon-12 atoms has a mass of exactly 12 grams. The mole bridges the microscopic world (atoms, molecules) and the macroscopic world (grams, litres).

3.4 Mass and Volume

Mass measures the amount of matter in an object (unit: kg or g). Volume measures the space an object occupies (unit: m³ or the more practical litre, L, where 1 L = 10⁻³ m³).

3.5 Density and Specific Gravity

Density (ρ) = mass / volume    [g/mL or kg/m³]

Density tells you how much matter is packed into a given space. Water at 4 °C has a density of 1.00 g/mL - this serves as a reference. Specific gravity is the ratio of a substance's density to the density of water at 4 °C. Because it is a ratio, specific gravity has no units.

3.6 Temperature Scales

Chemistry uses three temperature scales:

  • Celsius (°C): Water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.
  • Kelvin (K): The SI unit. 0 K is absolute zero (−273.15 °C), the lowest possible temperature.
  • Fahrenheit (°F): Used mainly in the US. Not needed for this exam.
T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15

3.7 Unit Conversions

Converting between units uses multiplication by conversion factors that equal 1. For example, to convert 250 mL to litres: 250 mL × (1 L / 1000 mL) = 0.250 L.

In clinical lab reports, hormone and drug levels are commonly expressed in picomoles per litre (pmol/L) or picograms per millilitre (pg/mL). Drug doses often use micrograms (abbreviated mcg or μg).

Worked Example: Converting Between SI Prefixes (μmol ↔ mmol)

Why this matters: clinical labs report concentrations in whichever prefix produces a "convenient" number, and questions on the entrance exam routinely ask you to read one unit and recognise the equivalent value in another. The trick is that two adjacent prefixes – milli (10⁻³) and micro (10⁻⁶) – differ by a factor of 1000. Therefore 1 mmol = 1000 μmol, or equivalently 1 μmol = 0.001 mmol.

Problem: A patient’s serum uric acid is 430 μmol/L. Express this in mmol/L.

Solution by dimensional analysis:

430 μmol/L × (1 mmol / 1000 μmol) = 0.430 mmol/L

Sanity check: millimoles are the larger unit, so the same amount of substance must be a smaller number in mmol than in μmol. 430 → 0.430 satisfies this. If your conversion gave 43 000 mmol/L or 43.0 mmol/L, you moved the decimal point the wrong way.

Reverse direction: a hormone level of 0.012 mmol/L equals 0.012 × 1000 = 12 μmol/L.

Rule of thumb: to go from μmol → mmol, divide by 1000 (move the decimal three places to the left). To go from mmol → μmol, multiply by 1000 (move the decimal three places to the right). The same rule applies to mass units (μg ↔ mg), volume units (μL ↔ mL), and any other prefix pair separated by a factor of 1000.

3.8 Pressure Units

One standard atmosphere of pressure equals 101,325 Pa (101.325 kPa), which also equals 760 mmHg (also called 760 Torr). In clinical medicine, blood pressure is reported in mmHg (e.g., 120/80 mmHg), while blood gas partial pressures may appear in either kPa or mmHg. The conversion factor is 1 mmHg = 133.3 Pa.

3.9 Energy Units

The SI unit of energy is the joule (J). The calorie (cal), an older unit still widely used in nutrition, is defined as the energy required to raise 1 g of water by 1 °C. The precise conversion is 1 cal = 4.184 J. One kilocalorie (1 kcal = 1 "Calorie" with a capital C on food labels) equals 4.184 kJ. Approximate energy values of nutrients: carbohydrates and proteins provide about 4 kcal/g (17 kJ/g), while fats provide about 9 kcal/g (37 kJ/g).

3.10 The Atomic Mass Unit

The atomic mass unit (u), also called the dalton (Da), equals exactly 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom, or 1.661 × 10⁻²⁷ kg. The molar mass in g/mol and the relative molecular mass in Da always have the same numerical value. For example, water has a relative molecular mass of 18.02 Da and a molar mass of 18.02 g/mol. In biochemistry, protein masses are commonly expressed in kilodaltons (kDa) - hemoglobin, for instance, has a mass of approximately 64.5 kDa.

3.11 Volume Equivalences

The relationships 1 mL = 1 cm³ and 1 L = 1 dm³ = 10⁻³ m³ are fundamental conversion factors used throughout chemistry and medicine. Because 1 mL equals 1 cm³, density expressed in g/mL is numerically identical to density in g/cm³. These equivalences are essential for any calculation involving solution preparation or drug dosing.

Key Points - Chapter 3

  • SI base units: kg (mass), m (length), s (time), K (temperature), mol (amount).
  • 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number).
  • Density = mass / volume. Water's density is 1.00 g/mL.
  • T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. Always use Kelvin in gas law calculations.
  • Specific gravity is dimensionless: density of substance / density of water.
  • Use conversion factors (dimensional analysis) to switch between units.

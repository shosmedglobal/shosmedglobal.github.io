Anatomical Planes and Directional Terms

Before studying the skeletal system, it is helpful to understand the standard planes and directions used to describe body structures:

  • Sagittal (median) plane: Divides the body into left and right halves.
  • Coronal (frontal) plane: Divides the body into front (anterior) and back (posterior).
  • Transverse (axial) plane: Divides the body into upper (superior) and lower (inferior).

Key directional terms include: superior/inferior (up/down), anterior/posterior (front/back), medial/lateral (toward/away from midline), and proximal/distal (nearer to/farther from the trunk).

Functions of the Skeleton

The skeletal system is not merely a rigid frame. It serves six important functions:

  1. Support: Provides a structural framework that supports the body and maintains its shape.
  2. Protection: Shields vital organs. The skull protects the brain, the rib cage guards the heart and lungs, and the vertebral column encloses the spinal cord.
  3. Movement: Bones act as levers; when muscles attached to them contract, they produce movement at joints.
  4. Mineral storage: Bones are the body's reservoir of calcium and phosphorus. These minerals can be released into the blood when needed.
  5. Blood cell production (hematopoiesis): Red bone marrow, found inside certain bones, produces red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.
  6. Energy storage: Yellow bone marrow stores fat as an energy reserve.

Bone Structure

Macroscopic Structure

A typical long bone (like the femur) has the following parts:

  • Diaphysis: The long shaft in the middle. Made of compact bone surrounding a medullary cavity filled with yellow marrow.
  • Epiphysis: The rounded ends. Made of spongy bone covered by a thin layer of compact bone. The articular surface is coated with hyaline cartilage for smooth joint movement.
  • Metaphysis: The transition zone between diaphysis and epiphysis. In growing bones, this contains the epiphyseal plate (growth plate), where lengthwise bone growth occurs.
  • Periosteum: A tough, fibrous membrane covering the outer surface of bone (except at joints). Contains blood vessels, nerves, and cells for bone repair and growth in thickness.
  • Endosteum: A thin membrane lining the inner surface of bone (medullary cavity).

Bone Cells

  • Osteoblasts: Build new bone by secreting the organic matrix (osteoid) which then mineralizes. They are bone-forming cells.
  • Osteocytes: Mature bone cells trapped within lacunae. They maintain the bone matrix and sense mechanical stress.
  • Osteoclasts: Large cells that break down (resorb) bone, releasing minerals into the blood. Bone is constantly being remodeled by the balance between osteoblast and osteoclast activity.

The inorganic component of bone is primarily hydroxyapatite, with the formula Ca₅(PO₄)₃OH (sometimes written Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂ for the unit cell). This crystalline mineral provides hardness and rigidity, while the organic collagen matrix provides tensile strength and slight flexibility. Bone is a dynamic tissue: approximately 5–10% of the skeleton is replaced each year through the coordinated action of osteoclasts (resorbing old bone) and osteoblasts (depositing new bone). This constant remodeling allows bones to adapt to mechanical stress, repair microdamage, and help regulate calcium homeostasis.

Teeth: The Hardest Tissues

Tooth enamel is the hardest tissue in the human body, composed of roughly 96% hydroxyapatite crystals by weight. Because enamel is non-living and acellular, it cannot regenerate once damaged. Dentin, the tissue beneath enamel, is the second hardest tissue and is living - it contains cellular processes of odontoblasts within microscopic tubules, allowing limited repair and sensitivity.

Axial Skeleton (80 bones)

The axial skeleton forms the central axis of the body:

  • Skull (22 bones): The cranium (8 bones: frontal, 2 parietal, 2 temporal, occipital, sphenoid, ethmoid) protects the brain. The facial bones (14 bones, including 2 maxillae, the mandible, 2 zygomatic, 2 nasal, and others) form the structure of the face. The mandible is the only movable skull bone. Cranial bones are joined by fibrous joints called sutures: the coronal suture (between frontal and parietal bones), sagittal suture (between the two parietal bones), lambdoid suture (between parietal and occipital bones), and squamous sutures (between temporal and parietal bones). In newborns, membrane-covered gaps at suture intersections called fontanelles ("soft spots") allow the skull to compress during birth and accommodate rapid brain growth; the anterior fontanelle closes by about 18 months and the posterior fontanelle by about 2 months of age.
  • Vertebral column (26 bones in adults): 7 cervical vertebrae (neck; C1 is the atlas, C2 is the axis), 12 thoracic vertebrae (attach to ribs), 5 lumbar vertebrae (lower back, largest and strongest), sacrum (5 fused vertebrae), coccyx (3-5 fused vertebrae, the tailbone). The column has four natural curves: cervical and lumbar lordosis (concave posteriorly) and thoracic and sacral kyphosis (concave anteriorly). Exaggerated kyphosis produces a "hunchback" appearance; exaggerated lumbar lordosis causes "swayback"; and scoliosis is an abnormal lateral curvature of the spine. These curves help distribute weight and absorb shock during walking and running.
  • Thoracic cage: 12 pairs of ribs plus the sternum. Ribs 1-7 are "true ribs" (attach directly to the sternum via costal cartilage). Ribs 8-10 are "false ribs" (attach indirectly). Ribs 11-12 are "floating ribs" (no sternal attachment at all). The sternum has three parts: manubrium, body, and xiphoid process.
  • Hyoid bone: A U-shaped bone in the neck that does not articulate with any other bone. It anchors the tongue and assists in swallowing.

Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones)

The appendicular skeleton consists of the limbs and the girdles that attach them to the axial skeleton:

  • Pectoral (shoulder) girdle: 2 clavicles (collarbones) and 2 scapulae (shoulder blades). This girdle is lightweight and allows great range of motion but is relatively unstable.
  • Upper limb (30 bones each side): Humerus (upper arm), radius and ulna (forearm - radius is on the thumb side), 8 carpal bones (wrist), 5 metacarpals (palm), and 14 phalanges (fingers: 2 in the thumb, 3 in each other finger).
  • Pelvic girdle: Two hip bones (each formed by the fusion of the ilium, ischium, and pubis). The pelvic girdle is stronger and more stable than the shoulder girdle because it must support the weight of the upper body. The female pelvis is wider and has a larger pelvic inlet to accommodate childbirth.
  • Lower limb (30 bones each side): Femur (thigh bone, the longest and strongest bone), patella (kneecap), tibia and fibula (leg - tibia is the larger, weight-bearing bone on the medial side), 7 tarsal bones (ankle, including the calcaneus or heel bone), 5 metatarsals (foot), and 14 phalanges (toes).

Muscle Naming Conventions

Skeletal muscles are named according to several descriptive criteria:

  • Fiber direction: rectus (parallel to midline), oblique (diagonal), transverse (perpendicular to midline).
  • Relative size: maximus (largest), minimus (smallest), longus (long), brevis (short).
  • Shape: deltoid (triangular), trapezius (trapezoid-shaped).
  • Number of origins: biceps (two heads), triceps (three heads), quadriceps (four heads).
  • Location: frontalis (near the frontal bone), tibialis (near the tibia).
  • Action: flexor, extensor, abductor, adductor.

Common Diseases of the Skeletal System

  • Osteoporosis: A condition where bone density decreases, making bones fragile and prone to fractures. Most common in postmenopausal women due to decreased estrogen (which normally inhibits osteoclast activity). Prevention includes adequate calcium and vitamin D intake and weight-bearing exercise.
  • Osteoarthritis: Degenerative wear-and-tear of articular cartilage in joints, leading to pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility. Most common in the knees, hips, and hands. Incidence increases with age.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: An autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks the synovial membrane of joints, causing inflammation, swelling, and eventual joint destruction. Unlike osteoarthritis, it often affects both sides symmetrically and can occur at any age.
  • Fractures: Breaks in bone. Types include simple (clean break), compound (bone pierces skin), comminuted (bone shattered into fragments), stress (tiny cracks from repetitive force), and greenstick (incomplete break, common in children whose bones are more flexible).
  • Scoliosis: Abnormal lateral curvature of the spine, most commonly diagnosed in adolescents.

Key Points - Chapter 23

  • Six functions of the skeleton: support, protection, movement, mineral storage, blood cell production, energy storage.
  • Compact bone is dense with osteons; spongy bone is porous with trabeculae. Both work together for strength with minimal weight.
  • Three bone cell types: osteoblasts (build), osteoclasts (resorb), osteocytes (maintain).
  • Three joint types: fibrous (immovable), cartilaginous (slightly movable), synovial (freely movable).
  • Axial skeleton (80 bones): skull, vertebral column, rib cage. Appendicular skeleton (126 bones): limbs + girdles.
  • Osteoporosis = reduced bone density; osteoarthritis = cartilage wear; rheumatoid arthritis = autoimmune joint attack.

Chapter 31

Proteins: Amino Acids and Protein Structure

Amino Acids

Proteins are built from 20 standard proteinogenic amino acids. Each has the same basic structure: a central (alpha) carbon bonded to (1) an amino group (–NH₂), (2) a carboxyl group (–COOH), (3) a hydrogen atom, and (4) a variable side chain (R group) that determines the identity and properties of each amino acid.

All 20 amino acids (except glycine) are chiral — the alpha-carbon has four different substituents. In biology, proteins are made exclusively from L-amino acids.

Selenocysteine: The 21st Amino Acid

Selenocysteine contains selenium instead of sulfur, with a selenol (–SeH) functional group in place of the thiol. It is encoded by the UGA codon (normally a stop codon) through a special insertion mechanism requiring a selenocysteine insertion sequence (SECIS element) in the mRNA. Selenocysteine is found in the active site of glutathione peroxidase and other selenoproteins involved in antioxidant defence.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Applied to Amino Acids

The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (pH = pKa + log [base form]/[acid form]) predicts the protonation state of each ionizable group at any given pH. When pH is greater than the pKa, the deprotonated (conjugate base) form predominates. Key pKa values for ionizable amino acid side chains are:

Amino Acid Properties

Amphoteric Nature and Zwitterions

Amino acids are amphoteric — they can act as both acids and bases. At physiological pH, the amino group is protonated (–NH₃⁺) and the carboxyl group is deprotonated (–COO−). This doubly charged species is called a zwitterion. Because of the internal charge neutralization, amino acids have high melting points and good water solubility.

Isoelectric Point (pI)

The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which the amino acid carries no net electric charge. At this pH, the molecule exists predominantly as a zwitterion. Below the pI, the amino acid has a net positive charge; above the pI, it has a net negative charge. For neutral amino acids, pI is roughly the average of pKa1 (carboxyl) and pKa2 (amino). For acidic or basic amino acids, the pI is the average of the two pKa values closest to the zwitterion form.

Post-Translational Modifications

After proteins are synthesized on ribosomes, certain amino acid residues can be chemically modified. In collagen, proline is hydroxylated to 3-hydroxyproline and 4-hydroxyproline (a reaction requiring vitamin C as a cofactor), and lysine to 5-hydroxylysine. Allysine, formed by oxidative deamination of lysine side chains, participates in covalent cross-links that give collagen its exceptional tensile strength. Deficiency of vitamin C impairs proline hydroxylation, leading to the weakened connective tissue of scurvy.

Metabolic Reactions of Amino Acids

Amino acids undergo several key metabolic transformations:

  • Decarboxylation removes the carboxyl group to produce biogenic amines: histidine → histamine, tryptophan → tryptamine, glutamate → GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid).
  • Transamination transfers the amino group from an amino acid to an alpha-keto acid, catalysed by aminotransferases using pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) as a cofactor.
  • Oxidative deamination removes the amino group as ammonia, connecting amino acid catabolism to the urea cycle.

The Peptide Bond

Amino acids link together through peptide bonds — a special type of amide bond formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next, with loss of water:

–CO–NH– (peptide bond)

The peptide bond has partial double-bond character due to resonance, making it planar and rigid (no free rotation). This has major consequences for protein structure.

Levels of Protein Structure

Primary Structure

The linear sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain, read from the N-terminus to the C-terminus. This sequence is encoded by DNA and determines all higher levels of structure.

Secondary Structure

Local, regular folding patterns stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone N–H and C=O groups:

  • Alpha-helix (α-helix): A right-handed coil where each backbone C=O hydrogen-bonds to the N–H four residues ahead. The side chains project outward. (Note: collagen has a distinct secondary structure — each chain forms a left-handed helix, steeper than the alpha-helix, with approximately 3 residues per turn. Three such chains then wind around each other into a right-handed triple helix (tropocollagen), stabilized by interchain hydrogen bonds. Glycine must occupy every third position (Gly-X-Y repeat) to fit inside the tight triple helix.)
  • Beta-sheet (β-sheet): Extended polypeptide strands lying side by side, connected by hydrogen bonds between strands. Can be parallel (same direction) or antiparallel (opposite directions).

Tertiary Structure

The complete three-dimensional folding of a single polypeptide chain. Stabilized by multiple interactions between side chains:

  • Disulfide bonds (covalent S–S bridges between cysteine residues)
  • Hydrogen bonds (between polar side chains)
  • Ionic bonds / salt bridges (between oppositely charged side chains)
  • Hydrophobic interactions (nonpolar side chains cluster away from water)
  • Van der Waals forces (weak, short-range attractions)

Quaternary Structure

The arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits into a functional complex. Not all proteins have quaternary structure. Example: hemoglobin has four subunits (2 alpha, 2 beta).

Colloidal Properties of Proteins

Proteins in solution form colloidal dispersions characterised by viscosity, sedimentation behaviour, and light scattering (Tyndall effect). Plasma proteins generate colloidal-osmotic (oncotic) pressure of approximately 25 mmHg, which is essential for maintaining fluid distribution between blood and interstitial tissues. A decrease in plasma albumin (e.g., in liver disease or malnutrition) lowers oncotic pressure and causes oedema (fluid accumulation in tissues).

Proteins can be precipitated by adding high concentrations of neutral salts such as ammonium sulfate, a technique called salting out. The salt strips the hydration shell from the protein surface, causing it to aggregate and precipitate without denaturation. This is widely used as an initial step in protein purification.

Denaturation

Denaturation is the loss of the three-dimensional structure (secondary, tertiary, and quaternary) without breaking peptide bonds. The primary structure remains intact, but the protein unfolds and loses biological function. Caused by: heat, extreme pH, organic solvents, heavy metal ions, or detergents.

Key Points — Chapter 31

  • 20 standard amino acids; classified by side chain: nonpolar, polar uncharged, positive, negative.
  • Amino acids are amphoteric; exist as zwitterions at physiological pH.
  • pI = pH of zero net charge.
  • Peptide bond is an amide bond with partial double-bond character (planar, rigid).
  • Four structural levels: primary (sequence), secondary (α-helix, β-sheet), tertiary (3D fold), quaternary (subunit assembly).
  • Denaturation = loss of 3D structure; primary structure remains.

Mnemonic: Positively Charged Amino Acids

"Lazy Kings Have Riches" = Lysine (K), Histidine (H), Arginine (R) are the basic (positively charged) amino acids.

Mnemonic: Negatively Charged Amino Acids

"Acidic D and E" = Aspartate (D) and Glutamate (E) are acidic (negatively charged).

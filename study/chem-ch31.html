Chapter 31

Proteins: Amino Acids and Protein Structure

Amino Acids

Proteins are built from 20 standard proteinogenic amino acids. Each has the same basic structure: a central (alpha) carbon bonded to (1) an amino group (–NH₂), (2) a carboxyl group (–COOH), (3) a hydrogen atom, and (4) a variable side chain (R group) that determines the identity and properties of each amino acid.

All 20 amino acids (except glycine) are chiral - the alpha-carbon has four different substituents. In biology, proteins are made exclusively from L-amino acids.

Selenocysteine: The 21st Amino Acid

Selenocysteine contains selenium instead of sulfur, with a selenol (–SeH) functional group in place of the thiol. It is encoded by the UGA codon (normally a stop codon) through a special insertion mechanism requiring a selenocysteine insertion sequence (SECIS element) in the mRNA. Selenocysteine is found in the active site of glutathione peroxidase and other selenoproteins involved in antioxidant defence.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Applied to Amino Acids

The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (pH = pKₐ + log [base form]/[acid form]) predicts the protonation state of each ionizable group at any given pH. When pH is greater than the pKₐ, the deprotonated (conjugate base) form predominates. Key pKₐ values for ionizable amino acid side chains are:

At physiological pH (7.4), Asp and Glu side chains are deprotonated (negatively charged), Lys and Arg are protonated (positively charged), and His is near its pKₐ - making it an effective buffer at physiological pH and a versatile catalytic residue in enzymes.

Amino Acid Properties

Amphoteric Nature and Zwitterions

Amino acids are amphoteric - they can act as both acids and bases. At physiological pH, the amino group is protonated (–NH₃⁺) and the carboxyl group is deprotonated (–COO−). This doubly charged species is called a zwitterion. Because of the internal charge neutralization, amino acids have high melting points and good water solubility.

Isoelectric Point (pI)

The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which the amino acid carries no net electric charge. At this pH, the molecule exists predominantly as a zwitterion. Below the pI, the amino acid has a net positive charge; above the pI, it has a net negative charge. For neutral amino acids, pI is roughly the average of pKa1 (carboxyl) and pKa2 (amino). For acidic or basic amino acids, the pI is the average of the two pKa values closest to the zwitterion form.

Post-Translational Modifications

After proteins are synthesized on ribosomes, certain amino acid residues can be chemically modified. In collagen, proline is hydroxylated to 3-hydroxyproline and 4-hydroxyproline (a reaction requiring vitamin C as a cofactor), and lysine to 5-hydroxylysine. Allysine, formed by oxidative deamination of lysine side chains, participates in covalent cross-links that give collagen its exceptional tensile strength. Deficiency of vitamin C impairs proline hydroxylation, leading to the weakened connective tissue of scurvy.

Metabolic Reactions of Amino Acids

Amino acids undergo several key metabolic transformations:

  • Decarboxylation removes the carboxyl group to produce biogenic amines: histidine → histamine, tryptophan → tryptamine, glutamate → GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid).
  • Transamination transfers the amino group from an amino acid to an alpha-keto acid, catalysed by aminotransferases using pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B₆) as a cofactor.
  • Oxidative deamination removes the amino group as ammonia, connecting amino acid catabolism to the urea cycle.

The Peptide Bond

Amino acids link together through peptide bonds - a special type of amide bond formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next, with loss of water:

–CO–NH– (peptide bond)

The peptide bond has partial double-bond character due to resonance, making it planar and rigid (no free rotation). This has major consequences for protein structure.

Levels of Protein Structure

Primary Structure

The linear sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain, read from the N-terminus to the C-terminus. This sequence is encoded by DNA and determines all higher levels of structure.

Secondary Structure

Local, regular folding patterns stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone N–H and C=O groups:

  • Alpha-helix (α-helix): A right-handed coil where each backbone C=O hydrogen-bonds to the N–H four residues ahead. The side chains project outward. (Note: collagen has a distinct secondary structure - each chain forms a left-handed helix, steeper than the alpha-helix, with approximately 3 residues per turn. Three such chains then wind around each other into a right-handed triple helix (tropocollagen), stabilized by interchain hydrogen bonds. Glycine must occupy every third position (Gly-X-Y repeat) to fit inside the tight triple helix.)
  • Beta-sheet (β-sheet): Extended polypeptide strands lying side by side, connected by hydrogen bonds between strands. Can be parallel (same direction) or antiparallel (opposite directions).

Tertiary Structure

The complete three-dimensional folding of a single polypeptide chain. Stabilized by multiple interactions between side chains:

  • Disulfide bonds (covalent S–S bridges between cysteine residues)
  • Hydrogen bonds (between polar side chains)
  • Ionic bonds / salt bridges (between oppositely charged side chains)
  • Hydrophobic interactions (nonpolar side chains cluster away from water)
  • Van der Waals forces (weak, short-range attractions)

Quaternary Structure

The arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits into a functional complex. Not all proteins have quaternary structure. Example: hemoglobin has four subunits (2 alpha, 2 beta).

Colloidal Properties of Proteins

Proteins in solution form colloidal dispersions characterised by viscosity, sedimentation behaviour, and light scattering (Tyndall effect). Plasma proteins generate colloidal-osmotic (oncotic) pressure of approximately 25 mmHg, which is essential for maintaining fluid distribution between blood and interstitial tissues. A decrease in plasma albumin (e.g., in liver disease or malnutrition) lowers oncotic pressure and causes oedema (fluid accumulation in tissues).

Proteins can be precipitated by adding high concentrations of neutral salts such as ammonium sulfate, a technique called salting out. The salt strips the hydration shell from the protein surface, causing it to aggregate and precipitate without denaturation. This is widely used as an initial step in protein purification.

Denaturation

Denaturation is the loss of the three-dimensional structure (secondary, tertiary, and quaternary) without breaking peptide bonds. The primary structure remains intact, but the protein unfolds and loses biological function. Caused by: heat, extreme pH, organic solvents, heavy metal ions, or detergents.

Enzyme Classification: The Seven EC Classes

The International Union of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology (IUBMB) classifies all enzymes into seven major classes based on the type of reaction they catalyse. Each enzyme receives a systematic EC number (Enzyme Commission number). The original six classes (EC 1–6) have been used since 1961, and a seventh class (EC 7, translocases) was added in 2018 for enzymes that catalyse the movement of ions or molecules across membranes. The six classes most relevant for general chemistry are:

Digestive Enzymes

The gastrointestinal tract relies on specific hydrolases (EC 3) to break down dietary macronutrients into absorbable units. Many digestive proteinases are secreted as inactive zymogens (proenzymes) to prevent self-digestion of the secreting cells.

Protein Digestion

  • Pepsin (stomach, pH optimum 1.5–2): secreted as pepsinogen; cleaves peptide bonds within protein chains (an endopeptidase).
  • Trypsin and chymotrypsin (small intestine, pH optimum ~8): secreted by the pancreas as trypsinogen and chymotrypsinogen; continue protein digestion in the alkaline intestinal environment.
  • Carboxypeptidases (cleave from the C-terminus) and aminopeptidases (cleave from the N-terminus) convert oligopeptides to free amino acids.

Fat Digestion

Pancreatic lipase hydrolyses triacylglycerols into fatty acids and monoacylglycerols. Lipase requires prior emulsification of dietary fat into small droplets by bile acids (synthesised from cholesterol in the liver and stored in the gallbladder).

Carbohydrate Digestion

Alpha-amylase, present in both saliva and pancreatic juice, hydrolyses the α(1→4) glycosidic bonds in starch, producing maltose and dextrins. Disaccharidases on the intestinal brush border (maltase, lactase, sucrase) then cleave disaccharides into monosaccharides for absorption.

Nitrogen Waste Products

When amino acids are catabolised, the first step is removal of the α-amino group by transamination or oxidative deamination, producing highly toxic ammonia (NH₃). The body must rapidly detoxify ammonia through several pathways:

  • Urea (NH₂–CO–NH₂): In the liver, ammonia is converted to urea via the urea cycle (Krebs–Henseleit cycle), consuming 3 ATP equivalents per molecule of urea produced. Urea is non-toxic, highly water-soluble, and is the major nitrogenous waste product in mammals, excreted by the kidneys in urine.
  • Creatinine: Formed by spontaneous non-enzymatic cyclisation of creatine phosphate in muscle. Its urinary excretion rate is relatively constant and proportional to muscle mass, making it a valuable clinical marker for assessing kidney function (glomerular filtration rate, GFR).
  • Uric acid: The end product of purine (adenine, guanine) catabolism. Produced by xanthine oxidase and excreted in urine. Excess uric acid can precipitate as urate crystals, causing gout (see Chapter 29 for structural details).

Clinical note: Elevated blood urea nitrogen (BUN) or creatinine levels typically indicate impaired renal function. The BUN-to-creatinine ratio helps differentiate pre-renal, renal, and post-renal causes of kidney dysfunction.

Hormones: Classification and Mechanisms of Action

Hormones are signalling molecules released by endocrine glands that regulate metabolism and physiology in distant target tissues. They are classified by chemical structure and solubility into two broad groups with distinct mechanisms:

Hydrophilic Hormones (Peptides, Proteins, Amino Acid Derivatives)

These hormones cannot cross the lipid bilayer of the cell membrane. They bind to cell-surface receptors, triggering intracellular second messenger cascades (e.g., cyclic AMP, Ca²⁺, inositol trisphosphate) that amplify the signal and produce rapid cellular responses.

  • Insulin (pancreatic β-cells): lowers blood glucose by promoting cellular glucose uptake and glycogen synthesis.
  • Glucagon (pancreatic α-cells): raises blood glucose by stimulating glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.
  • Adrenaline (epinephrine) (adrenal medulla): "fight-or-flight" response; increases heart rate, blood glucose, and blood pressure.
  • Growth hormone (anterior pituitary): stimulates growth and protein synthesis.

Hydrophobic Hormones (Steroids and Thyroid Hormones)

These hormones can diffuse through the cell membrane. They bind to intracellular receptors (often nuclear receptors) that act as transcription factors, directly modulating gene expression. Their effects are typically slower in onset but longer-lasting than those of hydrophilic hormones.

  • Cortisol (adrenal cortex): glucocorticoid; anti-inflammatory, raises blood glucose, protein catabolism.
  • Aldosterone (adrenal cortex): mineralocorticoid; promotes Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ excretion in the kidney.
  • Testosterone and estradiol: sex steroids controlling reproductive development and function.
  • Thyroxine (T₄) and triiodothyronine (T₃): thyroid hormones that regulate basal metabolic rate. Although amino acid derivatives, they are lipophilic and use intracellular (nuclear) receptors.

Translation: Protein Synthesis on Ribosomes

Translation is the process by which the nucleotide sequence of messenger RNA (mRNA) is decoded to assemble a polypeptide chain. It occurs on ribosomes (complexes of ribosomal RNA and proteins) and proceeds in three stages:

1. Initiation

The small ribosomal subunit binds to the mRNA and locates the start codon (AUG), which encodes methionine. An initiator methionyl-tRNA base-pairs with AUG, and the large ribosomal subunit joins to form the complete ribosome.

2. Elongation

The ribosome reads the mRNA in the 5′→3′ direction, one codon (three nucleotides) at a time. Each incoming aminoacyl-tRNA pairs its anticodon with the complementary mRNA codon. The ribosome then catalyses peptide bond formation, transferring the growing polypeptide chain to the newly arrived amino acid. The ribosome advances one codon along the mRNA after each amino acid is added. Each amino acid is first activated by covalent attachment to its specific tRNA (a reaction requiring ATP).

3. Termination

When a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) is reached, no corresponding tRNA exists. Instead, release factors bind to the stop codon, causing the completed polypeptide to be released and the ribosome to dissociate from the mRNA.

The genetic code is degenerate (redundant): there are 64 possible codons but only 20 standard amino acids (plus stop signals), so most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon. The code is nearly universal across all known life forms.

Key Points - Chapter 31

  • 20 standard amino acids; classified by side chain: nonpolar, polar uncharged, positive, negative.
  • Amino acids are amphoteric; exist as zwitterions at physiological pH.
  • pI = pH of zero net charge.
  • Peptide bond is an amide bond with partial double-bond character (planar, rigid).
  • Four structural levels: primary (sequence), secondary (α-helix, β-sheet), tertiary (3D fold), quaternary (subunit assembly).
  • Denaturation = loss of 3D structure; primary structure remains.
  • Six enzyme classes (EC 1–6): oxidoreductases, transferases, hydrolases, lyases, isomerases, ligases. EC 7 (translocases) added 2018.
  • Digestive enzymes: pepsin (stomach), trypsin/chymotrypsin (intestine), pancreatic lipase, α-amylase.
  • Nitrogen waste: urea (major, from amino acid catabolism), creatinine (kidney marker), uric acid (purine catabolism).
  • Hydrophilic hormones → surface receptors → second messengers. Hydrophobic hormones → intracellular receptors → gene expression.
  • Translation: mRNA decoded on ribosomes; start codon AUG; stop codons UAA, UAG, UGA; genetic code is degenerate.

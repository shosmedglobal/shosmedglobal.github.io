Chapter 24

Carbonyl Compounds: Aldehydes and Ketones

The Carbonyl Group

The carbonyl group (C=O) is one of the most important functional groups in organic chemistry and biochemistry. It consists of a carbon double-bonded to oxygen. The C=O bond is polar because oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, making the carbon electrophilic (electron-poor) and the oxygen nucleophilic (electron-rich).

Reactions

Oxidation

Aldehydes are easily oxidized to carboxylic acids. This distinguishes them from ketones. Tollens' test: Ag⁺ in ammonia is reduced to metallic silver (a mirror forms on the glass) by an aldehyde. Fehling's test: Cu²⁺ (blue) is reduced to Cu₂O (brick-red precipitate) by an aldehyde.

Reduction

Both aldehydes and ketones are reduced by NaBH₄ or LiAlH₄: aldehydes → primary alcohols; ketones → secondary alcohols.

Nucleophilic Addition

The electrophilic carbon of the carbonyl attracts nucleophiles. Key examples:

  • Addition of water: Forms a geminal diol (hydrate). Usually unfavorable thermodynamically except for formaldehyde.
  • Addition of alcohol: One equivalent forms a hemiacetal (or hemiketal for ketones). A second equivalent (with acid catalyst) forms an acetal (or ketal). Acetals are important in carbohydrate chemistry (glycosidic bonds).
  • Addition of nitrogen compounds: Reaction with primary amines forms imines (Schiff bases); with hydroxylamine forms oximes; with hydrazine forms hydrazones.

Keto-Enol Tautomerism

A carbonyl compound with at least one hydrogen on the α-carbon (the carbon adjacent to C=O) can exist in two forms that interconvert: the keto form (C=O with H on alpha-C) and the enol form (–C=C–OH). These are called tautomers (not resonance structures, because atoms actually move). In most cases, the keto form predominates at equilibrium.

Aldol Condensation

When an aldehyde (or ketone) with an alpha-hydrogen reacts with a base, the α-carbon becomes nucleophilic (as an enolate). This enolate attacks the carbonyl carbon of another molecule, forming a beta-hydroxy aldehyde (an "aldol"). On heating, water is eliminated to give an alpha,beta-unsaturated carbonyl compound. This reaction is important for building larger carbon skeletons from smaller molecules.

Important Named Compounds

  • Formaldehyde (methanal, HCHO): Simplest aldehyde. In aqueous solution, it exists almost entirely as its hydrate, methanediol (CH₂(OH)₂), because there is no steric hindrance at the carbonyl carbon. A 37% aqueous formaldehyde solution is called formalin and is used as a tissue preservative and disinfectant.
  • Acetaldehyde (ethanal, CH₃CHO): Metabolite of ethanol in the body.
  • Benzaldehyde (C₆H₅CHO): Almond scent; simplest aromatic aldehyde.
  • Glyceraldehyde (2,3-dihydroxypropanal): Simplest aldose sugar; reference compound for D/L nomenclature.
  • Acetone (propanone): Simplest ketone; common solvent; ketone body in diabetes.
  • Dihydroxyacetone: Simplest ketose sugar.
  • Quinones: Cyclic diketones derived from aromatic diols by oxidation. Ubiquinone (coenzyme Q) is vital in electron transport.
  • Aromatic ketones: Ketones with a phenyl group are called phenones (suffix -ophenone), and those with a naphthyl group are naphtones (suffix -naphtone). Common examples include acetophenone (methyl phenyl ketone) and benzophenone (diphenyl ketone).

Key Points - Chapter 24

  • Aldehydes (R–CHO): terminal carbonyl; easily oxidized; give positive Tollens'/Fehling's tests.
  • Ketones (R–CO–R'): internal carbonyl; resist oxidation.
  • Key reaction: nucleophilic addition to the electrophilic carbonyl carbon.
  • Keto-enol tautomerism: interconversion between C=O + alpha-H and C=C–OH forms.
  • Aldol condensation joins two carbonyl compounds to make a larger molecule.

"ALDehydes give A Lovely Display" - silver mirror (Tollens') and red precipitate (Fehling's)

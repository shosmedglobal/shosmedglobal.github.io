Chapter 2

Cellular Biology — Prokaryotic Organisms

2.1 What Are Prokaryotes?

Prokaryotes are the oldest, smallest, and most abundant organisms on Earth. The word "prokaryote" means "before the nucleus" — these cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus. Their DNA floats freely in a region of the cytoplasm called the nucleoid. Despite their apparent simplicity, prokaryotes are remarkably successful and occupy virtually every habitat on the planet, from boiling hot springs to Antarctic ice.

Prokaryotes first appeared approximately 3.5 billion years ago, making them by far the oldest and most successful form of life. They dominate every habitat on Earth and are essential for biogeochemical cycling. The human body harbors an estimated 500–1,000 bacterial species across the skin, oral cavity, and gastrointestinal tract. Despite this enormous diversity, only a small minority of prokaryotic species are pathogenic.

The earliest prokaryotes were almost certainly anaerobic heterotrophs, feeding on organic molecules formed abiotically in the primordial environment. As oxygen accumulated in the atmosphere from cyanobacterial photosynthesis, many obligate anaerobes were driven to extinction or retreated to oxygen-free niches (such as deep sediments and the animal gut), while others evolved into facultative anaerobes or obligate aerobes.

2.2 Phylogenetic Division: Bacteria vs Archaea

Prokaryotes are divided into two fundamentally different domains: Bacteria and Archaea. Although they look similar under a microscope, their molecular biology reveals deep differences:

Interestingly, molecular studies suggest that archaea are more closely related to eukaryotes than bacteria are, despite looking more like bacteria on the outside. This is a reminder that appearances can be deceiving in biology.

A key comparison for exams: archaeal membranes use isoprenoid lipids linked to glycerol by ether bonds (more heat-stable), while bacterial and eukaryotic membranes use fatty acids linked by ester bonds. Bacteria initiate translation with formylmethionine (fMet), while both Archaea and Eukaryotes use unmodified methionine — one of several features showing Archaea are molecularly closer to Eukaryotes than to Bacteria.

2.3 Genomic Organization

A typical bacterial cell has a single circular chromosome located in the nucleoid region. Unlike eukaryotic DNA, this chromosome is not wrapped around histone proteins (though some bacteria and most archaea do have histone-like proteins). In addition, many bacteria carry small, circular, self-replicating DNA molecules called plasmids. Plasmids often carry genes that confer advantages like antibiotic resistance, the ability to break down unusual nutrients, or toxin production. Plasmids can be transferred between bacteria, making them important agents of horizontal gene transfer.

2.4 Shapes of Bacteria

Bacteria come in three main shapes, and knowing these is essential:

  • Cocci (singular: coccus) — spherical. Examples: Staphylococcus (grape-like clusters), Streptococcus (chains).
  • Bacilli (singular: bacillus) — rod-shaped. Examples: Escherichia coli, Bacillus subtilis.
  • Spirilla (singular: spirillum) — spiral or corkscrew-shaped. Includes tightly coiled spirochetes like Treponema pallidum (the agent of syphilis).

Some bacteria deviate from these classic shapes (comma-shaped vibrios, for example), but cocci, bacilli, and spirilla cover the vast majority.

2.5 The Bacterial Cell Wall

Almost all bacteria have a rigid cell wall outside the plasma membrane that gives them their shape and protects against osmotic lysis. The key structural component is peptidoglycan (also called murein), a mesh-like polymer of sugars cross-linked by short peptide chains.

The Gram stain, developed by Hans Christian Gram in 1884, divides bacteria into two major groups based on their cell wall structure:

The lipopolysaccharide (LPS) layer of gram-negative bacteria is clinically important: the lipid A component of LPS is an endotoxin that triggers powerful immune responses and can cause septic shock.

Students should know which common pathogens are Gram-positive versus Gram-negative. Important Gram-positive genera include Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Bacillus, Clostridium, and Corynebacterium. Important Gram-negative genera include Escherichia, Salmonella, Shigella, Neisseria, Helicobacter, Pseudomonas, and Haemophilus. Knowing the Gram status helps predict antibiotic susceptibility and toxin type (Gram-negative bacteria tend to produce endotoxin from LPS; Gram-positive bacteria more often produce exotoxins).

2.6 Movement

Many bacteria move using flagella — long, whip-like protein structures that rotate like propellers. The bacterial flagellum is fundamentally different from the eukaryotic flagellum: it is made of the protein flagellin, it rotates (rather than undulating), and it is powered by a proton gradient across the membrane rather than by ATP. The arrangement of flagella varies: a single flagellum at one pole (monotrichous), tufts at one or both poles (lophotrichous or amphitrichous), or flagella covering the entire surface (peritrichous).

The bacterial flagellum is a remarkably efficient molecular machine consisting of three parts: a helical filament (made of flagellin protein), a hook (flexible coupling), and a basal body (rotary motor embedded in the cell envelope). The motor is powered by the proton motive force, spinning the filament like a propeller at up to 1,000 revolutions per second. Some bacteria can cover 100 times their body length per second — proportionally faster than any animal on Earth.

Spirochete Motility

Spirochetes have a unique motility system. Instead of external flagella, they possess endoflagella (axial filaments) that run within the periplasmic space between the outer membrane and the cell wall. Rotation of these internal filaments causes the entire cell to corkscrew through viscous environments like mucus and connective tissue. This distinctive motility contributes to the pathogenicity of spirochetes like Borrelia burgdorferi (Lyme disease) and Treponema pallidum (syphilis).

2.7 Population Growth

Bacteria reproduce by binary fission: the cell copies its chromosome, grows in size, and divides into two identical daughter cells. Under ideal conditions, some species can divide every 20 minutes.

When bacteria are grown in a closed environment (like a flask of broth), their population follows a predictable growth curve with four phases:

  1. Lag phase: Bacteria adapt to the new environment; they are metabolically active but not yet dividing rapidly.
  2. Log (exponential) phase: Bacteria divide at their maximum rate; the population doubles at regular intervals.
  3. Stationary phase: Resources become limiting and waste products accumulate; the rate of cell division equals the rate of cell death, so the population size stabilizes.
  4. Death (decline) phase: Cells die faster than they divide; the population shrinks.

2.8 Genetic Variability: Transformation and Endospores

Bacterial Transformation

Some bacteria can take up free DNA from their environment and incorporate it into their own genome. This process is called transformation. It was famously demonstrated by Frederick Griffith in 1928, when he showed that dead pathogenic bacteria could transfer their virulence to living nonpathogenic bacteria (more on this in Chapter 5). Transformation is one of three mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer in bacteria (along with conjugation and transduction).

Transduction

Transduction is the transfer of bacterial genes by bacteriophages. In generalized transduction, random fragments of bacterial chromosomal DNA are accidentally packaged into phage particles during lytic replication and delivered to new host cells. In specialized transduction, the prophage excises imprecisely from the bacterial chromosome, carrying adjacent host genes along with viral DNA. Specialized transduction transfers only genes near the prophage integration site, while generalized transduction can transfer any bacterial gene.

Endospores

Certain bacteria (notably Bacillus and Clostridium species) can form endospores when environmental conditions become hostile (lack of nutrients, extreme temperatures). An endospore is an incredibly tough, dormant structure formed inside the cell. It is surrounded by a thick coat and can survive boiling, radiation, desiccation, and chemical disinfectants for years, centuries, or even longer. When favorable conditions return, the endospore germinates and resumes normal cell growth. This is not reproduction — one cell forms one endospore, which later becomes one cell again.

Relationship to Oxygen

Prokaryotes are also classified by their relationship to oxygen. Obligate aerobes require oxygen for aerobic respiration and cannot survive without it. Facultative anaerobes can use oxygen when available but switch to fermentation in its absence, giving them metabolic flexibility. Obligate anaerobes are killed by oxygen (which generates toxic reactive oxygen species they cannot detoxify) and rely exclusively on fermentation or anaerobic respiration using alternative electron acceptors like nitrate or sulfate.

2.10 Nitrogen Fixation, Symbiosis, and Pathogenicity

Nitrogen Fixation

Nitrogen gas (N2) makes up 78% of Earth's atmosphere, but most organisms cannot use it directly. Certain prokaryotes possess the enzyme nitrogenase, which converts N2 into ammonia (NH3), a form that organisms can incorporate into amino acids and nucleotides. This is nitrogen fixation, and it is biologically essential. Notable nitrogen fixers include free-living soil bacteria (Azotobacter) and symbiotic bacteria (Rhizobium) that live in root nodules of legume plants.

Symbiosis

Prokaryotes engage in various symbiotic relationships with other organisms:

  • Mutualism: Both organisms benefit. Example: Rhizobium in legume root nodules (the bacteria get sugars, the plant gets fixed nitrogen).
  • Commensalism: One organism benefits, the other is unaffected. Example: bacteria on human skin that feed on dead cells without harming or helping us.
  • Parasitism: One organism benefits at the expense of the other. Pathogenic bacteria fall into this category.

Pathogenicity

Pathogenic bacteria cause disease through various mechanisms: producing exotoxins (secreted toxic proteins), releasing endotoxins (LPS from gram-negative cell walls upon lysis), invading and damaging tissues, or triggering harmful immune responses. Virulence factors include adhesins (for attachment), capsules (to evade phagocytosis), and enzymes that degrade host tissues.

More specifically, endotoxin (the lipid A component of LPS in Gram-negative outer membranes) is heat-stable and triggers severe immune responses including fever and septic shock. Exotoxins are heat-labile secreted proteins produced by both Gram-positive and Gram-negative species; classic examples include botulinum toxin (Clostridium botulinum) and diphtheria toxin (Corynebacterium diphtheriae). The Gram-negative outer membrane provides an additional barrier against many antibiotics and host immune defenses. Opportunistic pathogens are organisms that normally reside harmlessly in or on the body but cause disease when the immune system is compromised.

Major Bacterial Pathogens

Students should recognize major bacterial pathogens and their diseases. Key associations include: Clostridium tetani (tetanus), C. botulinum (botulism), Bacillus anthracis (anthrax), Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB), M. leprae (leprosy), Vibrio cholerae (cholera), Yersinia pestis (plague), Bordetella pertussis (whooping cough), Corynebacterium diphtheriae (diphtheria), Borrelia burgdorferi (Lyme disease), Treponema pallidum (syphilis), Neisseria gonorrhoeae (gonorrhea), N. meningitidis (meningitis), Rickettsia prowazekii (epidemic typhus), and Francisella tularensis (tularemia). Many of these are Gram-positive spore-formers (Clostridium, Bacillus) or obligate intracellular pathogens (Rickettsia).

The 2010 Haiti cholera outbreak illustrates how natural disasters can create conditions for bacterial epidemics. Following a devastating earthquake, contamination of water supplies led to rapid spread of Vibrio cholerae, ultimately infecting nearly 800,000 people and killing over 9,000. This case underscores the importance of water sanitation, hygiene infrastructure, and oral cholera vaccination in disease prevention.

2.11 Archaea: Extremophile Groups

The domain Archaea includes three major groups defined by their ecological niches. Methanogens produce methane by reducing CO2 with H2; they are strict anaerobes found in wetlands, animal intestines, and sewage treatment plants. Extreme halophiles require very high salt concentrations and inhabit environments like the Great Salt Lake and Dead Sea. Extreme thermophiles thrive at temperatures of 60–80 degrees Celsius or higher, typically near hydrothermal vents and hot springs. These groups were initially classified using 16S ribosomal RNA sequence comparisons.

2.12 Bacteria in Research and Technology

Bacteria serve as invaluable research tools and have important industrial applications. E. coli is the most widely used prokaryotic model organism for molecular biology and genetic engineering. Pseudomonas species are used in bioremediation, breaking down environmental pollutants including petroleum and pesticides. The food industry depends on lactic acid bacteria (such as Lactobacillus) for yogurt and cheese production through lactic fermentation.

2.13 Cyanobacteria: Biology and Ecological Significance

Cyanobacteria (once misleadingly called "blue-green algae") are prokaryotes that perform oxygenic photosynthesis using chlorophyll a — the same primary photosynthetic pigment found in plants. Unlike eukaryotic photosynthesizers, cyanobacteria lack chloroplasts; their photosynthetic membranes (thylakoids) lie free within the cytoplasm. This is powerful evidence for the endosymbiotic origin of chloroplasts: according to the endosymbiotic theory, modern plant and algal chloroplasts descended from ancient cyanobacteria engulfed by a eukaryotic host cell.

In addition to chlorophyll a, cyanobacteria carry distinctive accessory pigments: phycocyanin (blue), phycoerythrin (red), and beta-carotene (orange-yellow). The blend of these pigments produces the characteristic blue-green color, though individual species may appear green, brown, red, or nearly black depending on pigment ratios.

Cyanobacteria occur as unicellular, filamentous, or colonial forms. Filamentous genera such as Anabaena and Oscillatoria form chains of cells. Some filamentous species contain specialized thick-walled cells called heterocysts, which create an anaerobic microenvironment for nitrogen fixation — the conversion of atmospheric N2 into ammonia (NH3). This capability makes cyanobacteria critically important for soil and aquatic fertility, particularly in rice paddies.

Cyanobacteria rank among the most ecologically important organisms in Earth's history. Fossil evidence (stromatolites — layered rock structures built by cyanobacterial mats) indicates they were the first organisms to perform oxygenic photosynthesis, beginning approximately 2.7 billion years ago. The oxygen they released gradually accumulated in the atmosphere during the Great Oxidation Event (~2.4 billion years ago), which drove many obligate anaerobes to extinction while making aerobic respiration — and eventually complex multicellular life — possible.

2.14 Medical Mycology: Fungi of Medical Importance

Although fungi are eukaryotes and not prokaryotes, they are introduced here because a solid understanding of infectious disease requires knowledge of fungal pathogens alongside bacteria. Fungi are eukaryotic organisms that obtain nutrients by absorbing organic matter from their environment. They lack chlorophyll and cannot photosynthesize. Their cell walls contain chitin (not cellulose as in plants and not peptidoglycan as in bacteria). Fungi may be unicellular (yeasts) or multicellular (molds and mushrooms), and they reproduce primarily by spores.

The body of a multicellular fungus consists of thread-like filaments called hyphae, which collectively form a mycelium. Yeasts are the exception: they are single-celled fungi that reproduce by budding, in which a small daughter cell pinches off from the parent cell.

Key Fungal Pathogens

Candida albicans is a commensal yeast that normally inhabits skin and mucosal surfaces without causing disease. When the immune system is weakened or when normal bacterial flora is disrupted (for instance, by broad-spectrum antibiotic use), Candida can overgrow and cause infections termed candidiasis. Oral candidiasis (thrush) manifests as white patches on the tongue and buccal mucosa. Vulvovaginal candidiasis is among the most common fungal infections in women. In severely immunocompromised patients (such as those with AIDS or those receiving chemotherapy), Candida can invade the bloodstream and cause life-threatening systemic candidiasis.

Aspergillus species are ubiquitous environmental molds found in soil, decaying vegetation, and indoor dust. Most people inhale Aspergillus spores daily without harm. However, in immunocompromised individuals — organ transplant recipients, patients on chemotherapy, or those with severe neutropenia — the fungus can cause invasive aspergillosis, a serious and often fatal lung infection that may spread to other organs. Aspergillus flavus also produces aflatoxins, potent carcinogens found in contaminated grain and peanuts that are linked to liver cancer.

Dermatophytes are a group of fungi that colonize keratinized tissues — skin, hair, and nails. They cause superficial infections commonly called ringworm (tinea), despite having no connection to worms. Important presentations include tinea pedis (athlete's foot), tinea corporis (body ringworm), tinea capitis (scalp ringworm), and onychomycosis (nail infection). The three principal genera are Trichophyton, Microsporum, and Epidermophyton.

Other Notable Fungi

Penicillium is the mold from which Alexander Fleming isolated penicillin in 1928 — the discovery that launched the antibiotic era. Some Penicillium species are used in cheese production (for example, Penicillium roqueforti in blue cheese). The genus Amanita includes some of the most toxic mushrooms known; Amanita phalloides (the death cap) contains amatoxins that inhibit RNA polymerase II, leading to fatal hepatic failure. Claviceps purpurea (ergot) is a parasitic fungus that infects rye grain and produces toxic alkaloids. Ingestion of ergot-contaminated grain causes ergotism, a condition characterized by intense vasoconstriction leading to gangrene of the extremities, as well as neurological symptoms including hallucinations and convulsions. Ergotism caused devastating outbreaks in medieval Europe.

2.15 Medically Important Protozoa

Protozoa are single-celled eukaryotic organisms. While many are free-living in aquatic habitats, several species are obligate parasites responsible for some of the most devastating human diseases worldwide. Protozoa have historically been classified by their mode of locomotion, though modern molecular phylogenetics has revised many of these groupings. The medically important genera are nonetheless often still taught using the traditional categories.

Sporozoa (Apicomplexa)

Plasmodium is the causative agent of malaria, one of the deadliest infectious diseases in human history. The parasite is transmitted by the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. After entering the bloodstream, Plasmodium sporozoites first invade liver cells (hepatocytes), where they multiply silently. Merozoites then emerge and invade red blood cells, where they replicate further before causing the cells to rupture. This cyclical destruction of erythrocytes produces the hallmark recurring fevers and chills. Five species infect humans; Plasmodium falciparum is the most lethal. According to the WHO, malaria kills over 600,000 people annually, predominantly children under five in sub-Saharan Africa.

Toxoplasma gondii causes toxoplasmosis. The definitive host is the domestic cat, which sheds oocysts in its feces. Humans acquire infection by ingesting oocysts from contaminated soil or cat litter, or by eating undercooked meat containing tissue cysts. In healthy immunocompetent individuals, the infection is usually asymptomatic or causes mild flu-like symptoms. However, infection during pregnancy is dangerous because the parasite can cross the placenta and cause congenital toxoplasmosis — severe birth defects including hydrocephalus, intracranial calcifications, and chorioretinitis. In immunocompromised patients (particularly those with AIDS), reactivation of latent Toxoplasma can cause fatal encephalitis.

Flagellates

Trypanosoma brucei, transmitted by tsetse flies (Glossina species) in sub-Saharan Africa, causes African trypanosomiasis (sleeping sickness). The disease progresses from an early hemolymphatic stage (fever, swollen lymph nodes) to a late meningoencephalitic stage with disrupted sleep-wake cycles, confusion, and ultimately death if untreated. Trypanosoma cruzi, transmitted by triatomine bugs ("kissing bugs") in Latin America, causes Chagas disease (American trypanosomiasis), which can lead to chronic cardiomyopathy and digestive tract dysfunction (megacolon, megaesophagus).

Giardia lamblia (also called Giardia intestinalis or G. duodenalis) is among the most common intestinal parasites worldwide. Transmission occurs through contaminated water (the fecal-oral route). The trophozoite has a distinctive pear shape with two nuclei, four pairs of flagella, and a ventral adhesive disc that attaches to intestinal epithelium. Giardiasis presents as profuse, foul-smelling watery diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and bloating. Giardia forms environmentally resistant cysts that can survive standard water chlorination.

Leishmania species, transmitted by sandfly bites, cause leishmaniasis. The disease manifests in three major forms: cutaneous leishmaniasis (skin ulcers at the bite site), mucocutaneous leishmaniasis (progressive destruction of nasal and oral mucosa), and visceral leishmaniasis (kala-azar), which affects the liver, spleen, and bone marrow and is fatal if untreated. Leishmania parasites survive and multiply inside host macrophages, evading the immune response.

Trichomonas vaginalis is a flagellated protozoan that causes trichomoniasis, the most common non-viral sexually transmitted infection worldwide. It infects the urogenital tract and produces vaginal discharge, itching, and irritation. Notably, Trichomonas lacks mitochondria and instead contains modified organelles called hydrogenosomes that produce energy anaerobically.

Amoebae (Sarcodina)

Entamoeba histolytica causes amoebic dysentery — severe diarrhea with bloody, mucus-containing stools. Transmission is via the fecal-oral route through contaminated water and food, and the disease is most prevalent in tropical regions with inadequate sanitation. The parasite invades the intestinal wall using proteolytic enzymes and can occasionally disseminate to the liver, forming an amoebic liver abscess.

Ciliates

Balantidium coli is the largest protozoan parasite of humans and the only ciliate known to cause human disease. It infects the large intestine and can cause diarrhea ranging from mild to severe dysentery. Pigs are the primary reservoir, and transmission occurs through the fecal-oral route.

Key Points — Chapter 2

  • Prokaryotes lack a membrane-bound nucleus; their DNA is in the nucleoid region.
  • Bacteria and Archaea are the two domains of prokaryotes, differing in membrane chemistry, cell wall composition, and molecular machinery.
  • Gram-positive bacteria have thick peptidoglycan and no outer membrane; gram-negative bacteria have thin peptidoglycan plus an outer membrane with LPS.
  • Binary fission produces two genetically identical cells; the population growth curve has lag, log, stationary, and death phases.
  • Horizontal gene transfer occurs through transformation, conjugation, and transduction.
  • Endospores are dormant survival structures, not a form of reproduction.
  • Nitrogen fixation by prokaryotes converts atmospheric N2 into biologically usable ammonia.
  • Cyanobacteria were the first oxygenic photosynthesizers; their activity led to the Great Oxidation Event and made aerobic life possible.
  • Medically important fungi include Candida (candidiasis), Aspergillus (invasive lung infection), and dermatophytes (ringworm/tinea).
  • Key protozoan pathogens: Plasmodium (malaria), Toxoplasma (congenital infections), Trypanosoma (sleeping sickness, Chagas disease), Giardia (waterborne diarrhea), Leishmania (cutaneous and visceral disease), Entamoeba (amoebic dysentery).

Chapter 34

Controlling the Internal Environment

Life depends on maintaining a stable internal environment despite constantly changing external conditions. This chapter examines how animals regulate body temperature, water balance, and the disposal of metabolic wastes — all critical aspects of homeostasis.

Thermoregulation

Body temperature affects the rate of all biochemical reactions. Enzymes work best within a narrow temperature range, so organisms must regulate body temperature or risk metabolic dysfunction.

Mechanisms of Heat Exchange

Heat moves between an organism and its environment by four physical processes:

  • Conduction — direct transfer of heat between objects in physical contact. Example: heat lost through the soles of your feet when standing on cold ground.
  • Convection — transfer of heat by the movement of air or water over the body surface. A breeze cools you because it replaces warm air next to your skin with cooler air.
  • Radiation — emission of electromagnetic (infrared) energy. Your body constantly radiates heat to cooler surroundings. You also absorb radiant heat from the sun or a fire.
  • Evaporation — heat is lost when water evaporates from the body surface (sweating, panting). Evaporation is the only cooling mechanism that works when the air temperature is higher than body temperature.

In endotherms, the hypothalamus acts as the thermostat. When body temperature rises, it triggers vasodilation (widening blood vessels near the skin to release heat) and sweating. When body temperature drops, it triggers vasoconstriction (narrowing skin blood vessels to conserve heat), shivering (muscle contractions generate heat), and increased metabolic rate.

Water and Mineral Balance

Cells require a stable concentration of water and dissolved solutes. The movement of water across membranes follows osmosis — water moves from regions of lower solute concentration to regions of higher solute concentration. If the body loses too much water, cells shrink; if it gains too much, cells swell.

Osmoregulation in Different Environments

  • Freshwater organisms — their body fluids are more concentrated than the surrounding water. Water constantly enters by osmosis, and salts are lost by diffusion. They must excrete large volumes of dilute urine and actively absorb salts through gills or skin.
  • Saltwater (marine) organisms — the ocean is more concentrated than their body fluids (for bony fish). They constantly lose water by osmosis and gain excess salt. They must drink seawater, absorb water in the gut, and actively excrete salt through specialized gill cells.
  • Terrestrial organisms — face constant water loss through evaporation (skin, lungs). They conserve water through waterproof skin coverings, concentrated urine, and behavioral strategies (being active at night).

Homeostasis of Blood Osmolarity, Pressure, and Volume

The kidneys are the primary regulators of blood composition:

  • ADH (antidiuretic hormone) — released when blood osmolarity is high (dehydration). Makes kidney collecting ducts more permeable to water, so more water is reabsorbed and urine becomes concentrated.
  • Aldosterone — released when blood pressure/volume is low. Promotes sodium reabsorption in the kidneys; water follows sodium, increasing blood volume.
  • Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) — when blood pressure drops, the kidneys release renin, which initiates a cascade leading to angiotensin II (a powerful vasoconstrictor) and aldosterone release, both of which raise blood pressure.

Nitrogenous Waste Products

When amino acids and nucleic acids are broken down, nitrogen-containing waste must be excreted. Different organisms produce different forms:

Waste ProductToxicityWater NeededEnergy CostOrganisms

UreaModerateModerateModerateMammals, amphibians, sharks

Mammals convert ammonia to urea in the liver (via the urea cycle). This requires energy but produces a less toxic molecule that can be safely concentrated in urine. Birds and reptiles convert nitrogen waste to uric acid, which requires even more energy but can be excreted with almost no water loss — an adaptation for conserving water.

Key Points — Controlling the Internal Environment
  • Heat exchange: conduction (contact), convection (fluid movement), radiation (electromagnetic), evaporation (water loss).
  • Ectotherms rely on external heat; endotherms generate internal heat. Endothermy is metabolically costly.
  • The hypothalamus is the body's thermostat, triggering sweating/vasodilation (hot) or shivering/vasoconstriction (cold).
  • Osmoregulation depends on environment: freshwater animals excrete water; marine fish drink water and excrete salt.
  • ADH controls water reabsorption; aldosterone controls sodium reabsorption; RAAS regulates blood pressure.
  • Nitrogenous waste: ammonia (toxic, needs water), urea (moderate), uric acid (least water needed).

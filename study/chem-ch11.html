Chapter 11

Quantitative Relationships in Chemical Reactions

11.1 The Mole Concept Revisited

As introduced in Chapter 3, one mole contains 6.022 × 1023 particles (Avogadro's number, NA). The molar mass of a substance is the mass of one mole in grams, numerically equal to its atomic or molecular mass in atomic mass units (u).

  • Molar mass of C = 12.01 g/mol
  • Molar mass of H2O = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol
  • Molar mass of NaCl = 22.99 + 35.45 = 58.44 g/mol
n = m / M

where n = moles, m = mass in grams, M = molar mass in g/mol.

11.2 Concentration

Molarity (Molar Concentration)

c = n / V

where c = molarity (mol/L or M), n = moles of solute, V = volume of solution in litres.

Mass Percentage

w% = (msolute / msolution) × 100%

11.3 Dilution

c1V1 = c2V2

When you add solvent to a solution, the amount of solute stays the same, but the concentration decreases.

Worked Example: Dilution

Problem: How much water must be added to 100 mL of 2.0 M HCl to make a 0.50 M solution?
Step 1: c1V1 = c2V2 → (2.0)(100) = (0.50)(V2)
Step 2: V2 = 200 / 0.50 = 400 mL total
Step 3: Water to add = 400 − 100 = 300 mL
Answer: Add 300 mL of water.

11.4 Balanced Chemical Equations

A balanced equation has the same number of each type of atom on both sides. Coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) indicate the mole ratios of reactants and products. For example:

2H2 + O2 → 2H2O

This tells us 2 moles of hydrogen react with 1 mole of oxygen to produce 2 moles of water.

11.5 Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry uses the mole ratios from balanced equations to calculate quantities of reactants and products.

Worked Example: Mass-to-Mass Stoichiometry

Problem: How many grams of water are produced when 8.0 g of H2 react with excess O2?
Equation: 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O
Step 1: Moles of H2 = 8.0 g / 2.016 g/mol = 3.97 mol
Step 2: Mole ratio: 2 mol H2 produces 2 mol H2O, so 3.97 mol H2 → 3.97 mol H2O
Step 3: Mass of H2O = 3.97 × 18.02 = 71.5 g
Answer: 71.5 g of water are produced.

11.6 Limiting Reagent

The limiting reagent is the reactant that runs out first, stopping the reaction. The other reactant(s) are in excess. To find the limiting reagent, convert all reactant amounts to moles and compare with the stoichiometric ratio from the balanced equation.

Worked Example: Limiting Reagent

Problem: 10 g of H2 and 10 g of O2 react. Which is the limiting reagent?
Equation: 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O
Step 1: Moles of H2 = 10 / 2.016 = 4.96 mol; Moles of O2 = 10 / 32.00 = 0.3125 mol
Step 2: According to the equation, 2 mol H2 requires 1 mol O2. So 4.96 mol H2 would need 2.48 mol O2.
Step 3: We only have 0.3125 mol O2, which is far less than 2.48 mol needed.
Answer: O2 is the limiting reagent. It will be consumed first, and excess H2 will remain.

11.7 Converting Between Molarity and Percent Concentration

Interconverting molarity and mass percent requires the solution's density. To convert X M to weight percent: (1) calculate the mass of solute in 1 L of solution (= X × MW grams), (2) find the total mass of 1 L of solution (= density × 1000 mL), (3) percent = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100.

Worked Example: Molarity to Percent

Problem: Convert 3.0 M HCl (density 1.05 g/mL, MW = 36.5 g/mol) to weight percent.
Step 1: Mass of HCl in 1 L = 3.0 × 36.5 = 109.5 g
Step 2: Mass of 1 L solution = 1.05 × 1000 = 1050 g
Step 3: w% = (109.5 / 1050) × 100 = 10.4%
Answer: 10.4% HCl by mass.

11.8 Mass Concentration

Mass concentration expresses solute mass per unit volume of solution (e.g., g/L, mg/dL, μg/mL). It is the most common reporting format in clinical laboratories. To convert between mass concentration and molar concentration, divide by the molar mass.

Worked Example: Clinical Unit Conversion

Problem: Convert blood glucose of 108 mg/dL to mmol/L (MW of glucose = 180 g/mol).
Step 1: 108 mg/dL = 1080 mg/L = 1.080 g/L
Step 2: n = 1.080 / 180 = 0.006 mol/L = 6.0 mmol/L
Answer: 6.0 mmol/L.

11.9 Clinical Calculation Practice

Medical chemistry requires fluency in switching between concentration units. A useful exercise: convert total cholesterol of 220 mg/dL to mmol/L given MW = 386.7 g/mol. Solution: 220 mg/dL = 2200 mg/L = 2.200 g/L; n = 2.200 / 386.7 = 0.00569 mol/L = 5.69 mmol/L. Another common calculation: the total mass of sodium in 3.0 L of plasma at 142 mmol/L is 0.142 mol/L × 3.0 L × 23 g/mol = 9.8 g.

11.10 Activity Series of Metals

The activity series ranks metals by their tendency to lose electrons (be oxidized). A metal higher in the series can displace any metal below it from a solution of its salt:

Li > K > Ba > Ca > Na > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Pt > Au

Metals above hydrogen can displace H2 from dilute acids (e.g., Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl2 + H2). Metals below hydrogen (Cu, Ag, Au, Pt) cannot react with dilute acids to produce hydrogen gas.

11.11 Types of Chemical Reactions

Inorganic reactions are commonly classified into four types:

  1. Combination (synthesis): two or more substances form one product. Example: 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO
  2. Decomposition: one substance breaks into two or more. Example: 2KClO3 → 2KCl + 3O2
  3. Single replacement: one element displaces another from a compound. Example: Zn + CuCl2 → ZnCl2 + Cu
  4. Double replacement (metathesis): cations exchange between two compounds, usually forming a precipitate, gas, or water. Example: AgNO3 + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO3

Key Points — Chapter 11

  • n = m / M connects mass to moles.
  • Molarity: c = n / V (mol/L).
  • Dilution formula: c1V1 = c2V2.
  • Balanced equations provide mole ratios for stoichiometric calculations.
  • Stoichiometry pathway: grams → moles → mole ratio → moles → grams.
  • The limiting reagent is used up first and determines the maximum product yield.

Mnemonic: Stoichiometry Steps

"Grams to Moles, Mole to Mole, Moles to Grams" — The three-step bridge for every stoichiometry problem: convert to moles, use the ratio, convert back.

1. What is the molarity of a solution containing 5.85 g of NaCl (M = 58.44 g/mol) dissolved in enough water to make 500 mL of solution?
Show Answer
n = 5.85 / 58.44 = 0.100 mol. c = 0.100 / 0.500 = 0.200 M.
2. How many grams of CO2 are produced from burning 12.0 g of carbon? (C + O2 → CO2)
Show Answer
n(C) = 12.0 / 12.01 = 0.999 mol. The 1:1 mole ratio gives 0.999 mol CO2. Mass = 0.999 × 44.01 = 44.0 g of CO2.
3. You dilute 50 mL of 6.0 M H2SO4 to a final volume of 300 mL. What is the new concentration?
Show Answer
c2 = c1V1 / V2 = (6.0)(50) / 300 = 1.0 M.

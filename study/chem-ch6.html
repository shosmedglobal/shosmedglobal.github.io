Chapter 6

Chemical Bonding

6.1 Why Atoms Bond

Atoms bond because the resulting compound is at a lower energy state than the separate atoms. In other words, bonded atoms are more stable. There are three primary types of strong chemical bonds: ionic, covalent, and metallic.

6.2 Ionic Bonds

An ionic bond forms when one atom transfers electrons to another. Typically a metal transfers electrons to a nonmetal. The metal becomes a cation (+) and the nonmetal becomes an anion (−). The opposite charges attract, holding the ions together. Example: Na donates one electron to Cl, forming Na+Cl−.

Ionic compounds form crystal lattices, have high melting points, conduct electricity when dissolved or molten, and are usually soluble in water.

6.3 Covalent Bonds

A covalent bond forms when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This usually occurs between two nonmetals.

Bond TypeShared PairsExampleBond Strength
Single bond1 pair (2 e−)H–H, C–HWeakest of the three
Double bond2 pairs (4 e−)O=O, C=OIntermediate
Triple bond3 pairs (6 e−)N≡N, C≡CStrongest of the three

As bond order increases, bond length decreases and bond energy increases.

6.4 Electronegativity and Bond Polarity

When two atoms with different electronegativities share electrons, the shared pair is pulled closer to the more electronegative atom. This creates a polar covalent bond with partial charges: δ+ on the less electronegative atom and δ− on the more electronegative atom.

  • ΔEN = 0: Nonpolar covalent (e.g., H2, Cl2)
  • 0 < ΔEN < 1.7: Polar covalent (e.g., H–Cl, H–O)
  • ΔEN > 1.7: Ionic (e.g., Na–Cl)

6.5 Lewis Structures

Lewis structures (dot diagrams) show how valence electrons are arranged in a molecule. To draw one:

  1. Count total valence electrons from all atoms.
  2. Place the least electronegative atom in the centre (hydrogen is always terminal).
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds (each bond = 2 electrons).
  4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs on outer atoms first, then on the central atom.
  5. If the central atom lacks an octet, convert lone pairs on adjacent atoms into double or triple bonds.

6.6 VSEPR Theory and Molecular Shapes

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory predicts molecular geometry. Electron groups (bonds and lone pairs) around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves to be as far apart as possible.

6.7 Intermolecular Forces

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are attractions between molecules (not within them). They are much weaker than covalent or ionic bonds but determine physical properties like boiling point, melting point, and solubility.

ForceStrengthOccurs BetweenExample
London dispersion (van der Waals)WeakestAll molecules; stronger with larger electron cloudsAr—Ar, CH4
Dipole-dipoleMediumPolar moleculesHCl—HCl
Hydrogen bondStrongest IMFH bonded to N, O, or F interacting with lone pair on N, O, or FH2O—H2O
Note: Hydrogen bonds are crucial in biology — they hold DNA strands together, shape protein structure, and give water its unique properties.

Key Points — Chapter 6

  • Ionic bonds: electron transfer between metal and nonmetal.
  • Covalent bonds: electron sharing between nonmetals (single, double, triple).
  • Polar covalent bonds have unequal sharing due to electronegativity difference.
  • Lewis structures show valence electron arrangement in molecules.
  • VSEPR predicts shape based on electron group repulsion around the central atom.
  • IMF strength: London dispersion < dipole-dipole < hydrogen bonds.
  • Hydrogen bonds require H–F, H–O, or H–N bonds and are vital in biochemistry.

Test Yourself — Chapter 6

1. What is the molecular geometry of NH3? Why is it not tetrahedral?
Show Answer
NH3 has a trigonal pyramidal shape. Although there are 4 electron groups around nitrogen (3 bonds + 1 lone pair), the geometry describes only atom positions. The lone pair occupies one position of the tetrahedron, pushing the three H atoms into a pyramid shape.
2. Explain why HF has a much higher boiling point than HCl, despite HCl having a larger molar mass.
Show Answer
HF forms strong hydrogen bonds (H bonded to the highly electronegative F), while HCl can only form weaker dipole-dipole interactions (Cl is not electronegative enough for hydrogen bonding). The stronger intermolecular forces in HF require more energy to overcome, resulting in a higher boiling point.
3. Draw the Lewis structure of CO2 and predict its shape.
Show Answer
CO2 has 16 valence electrons (4 from C + 6 from each O). Carbon is the central atom with double bonds to each oxygen: O=C=O. Each oxygen has 2 lone pairs. Shape: linear (180°), because there are 2 electron groups around carbon with no lone pairs.

Chapter 26

Respiratory System

External vs. Internal Respiration

It is important to distinguish between two levels of respiration:

  • External respiration: The exchange of gases between the lungs and the blood. Oxygen moves from the air in the alveoli into the blood; carbon dioxide moves from the blood into the alveoli to be exhaled.
  • Internal (cellular) respiration: The exchange of gases between the blood and the body's cells. Oxygen moves from the blood into cells (where it is used in mitochondria for aerobic metabolism); carbon dioxide, a waste product of metabolism, moves from cells into the blood.

Both types of exchange occur by simple diffusion down concentration gradients (gases move from areas of higher partial pressure to areas of lower partial pressure).

Anatomy of the Respiratory System

Upper Respiratory Tract

  • Nasal cavity: Warms, moistens, and filters incoming air. Lined with mucous membrane and ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium. Tiny hairs (vibrissae) trap large particles. The mucus traps smaller particles and pathogens; the cilia sweep the mucus toward the throat (the "mucociliary escalator"). Connected to the nasal cavity are four pairs of paranasal sinuses — air-filled cavities within the frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, and sphenoid bones. They lighten the skull, warm and humidify inhaled air, and contribute to voice resonance. Inflammation of these sinuses (sinusitis) is one of the most common medical complaints.
  • Pharynx (throat): A shared passageway for air and food. Divided into nasopharynx, oropharynx, and laryngopharynx.
  • Larynx (voice box): Connects the pharynx to the trachea. Contains the vocal cords (vocal folds) — two folds of mucous membrane that vibrate when air passes between them, producing sound. Also contains the epiglottis, a flap of cartilage that covers the larynx during swallowing to prevent food from entering the airway.

Lower Respiratory Tract

  • Trachea (windpipe): A tube about 10–12 cm long. Its wall has three layers: a mucosa (pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium with goblet cells), a submucosa containing seromucous glands that secrete mucus and serous fluid, and an outer layer reinforced by 16–20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings. The open posterior side of each ring faces the esophagus and is bridged by smooth muscle (the trachealis), allowing the esophagus to expand during swallowing. The ciliated epithelium continues to sweep mucus and trapped particles upward.
  • Bronchi: The trachea divides into the right and left primary (main) bronchi, each entering a lung. The right bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical (so aspirated foreign objects more commonly enter the right lung). Bronchi further divide into secondary (lobar) bronchi, then tertiary (segmental) bronchi.
  • Bronchioles: Smaller airways that lack cartilage. Their walls contain smooth muscle that can constrict (bronchoconstriction, as in asthma) or relax (bronchodilation). Bronchioles continue dividing until they become terminal bronchioles, then respiratory bronchioles.
  • Alveoli: Tiny, thin-walled air sacs (about 300 million in both lungs) where gas exchange occurs. Each alveolus is surrounded by a dense network of capillaries. The barrier between air and blood (the respiratory membrane) is extremely thin (about 0.5 micrometers), consisting of the alveolar epithelium (type I pneumocytes), a shared basement membrane, and the capillary endothelium. Type II pneumocytes in the alveolar wall secrete surfactant, a substance that reduces surface tension and prevents alveoli from collapsing.

The Pleura

Each lung is enclosed in a double-layered serous membrane called the pleura. The visceral pleura adheres directly to the lung surface, while the parietal pleura lines the inner chest wall. Between them lies the pleural cavity, which contains a thin film of pleural fluid. This fluid reduces friction during breathing and creates surface tension that keeps the lungs expanded against the chest wall. Loss of negative intrapleural pressure (as occurs in a pneumothorax) allows the lung to collapse.

Respiratory Mucus Composition

The respiratory tract produces approximately 1 liter of mucus per day. This viscous secretion contains water, mucin glycoproteins (which give it gel-like properties), shed epithelial cells, leukocytes, lysozyme (an antibacterial enzyme), immunoglobulin A (IgA), and lactoferrin (an iron-binding antimicrobial protein). The mucociliary escalator transports this mucus toward the pharynx, where it is typically swallowed.

Mechanics of Breathing

Breathing is driven by pressure differences between the atmosphere and the inside of the lungs:

Inspiration (Breathing In)

An active process requiring muscle contraction:

  1. The diaphragm contracts and flattens (moves downward).
  2. The external intercostal muscles contract, lifting the ribs upward and outward.
  3. These actions increase the volume of the thoracic cavity.
  4. According to Boyle's Law, when volume increases, pressure decreases. The pressure inside the lungs (intrapulmonary pressure) drops below atmospheric pressure (this is called negative pressure).
  5. Air flows into the lungs from the atmosphere (from high pressure to low pressure).

Expiration (Breathing Out)

Normally a passive process during quiet breathing:

  1. The diaphragm and external intercostals relax.
  2. The elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall causes the thoracic cavity to decrease in volume.
  3. Intrapulmonary pressure rises above atmospheric pressure (positive pressure).
  4. Air is pushed out of the lungs.

During forced expiration (exercise, coughing), the internal intercostals and abdominal muscles actively contract to push air out more forcefully.

Gas Exchange Mechanism

Gas exchange depends on partial pressure gradients. In the alveoli, the partial pressure of oxygen (pO₂ ~104 mmHg) is higher than in the deoxygenated blood arriving from the pulmonary arteries (~40 mmHg), so oxygen diffuses from alveoli into blood. The partial pressure of CO₂ is higher in the blood (~46 mmHg) than in the alveoli (~40 mmHg), so CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli to be exhaled.

In the tissues, the reverse occurs: pO₂ is higher in blood than in cells, so oxygen diffuses into cells. pCO₂ is higher in cells than in blood, so CO₂ diffuses into blood.

Carbon dioxide is transported in the blood in three ways: dissolved in plasma (~7%), bound to hemoglobin (~23%), and as bicarbonate ions (HCO₃¯) (~70%), which is the most common form. The conversion of CO₂ to bicarbonate is catalyzed by the enzyme carbonic anhydrase inside red blood cells.

The Bohr Effect and Oxygen Unloading

The Bohr effect describes how decreased blood pH (increased H+ concentration) and elevated CO2 reduce hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen, promoting oxygen release in metabolically active tissues that need it most. Other factors that shift the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve to the right (favoring unloading) include elevated temperature and increased 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG), a glycolytic intermediate produced within red blood cells. Conversely, alkaline pH, low CO2, and low temperature shift the curve leftward, enhancing oxygen binding in the lungs.

Common Respiratory Diseases

  • Asthma: A chronic condition where bronchioles become inflamed and constrict in response to triggers (allergens, exercise, cold air). This narrows the airways, causing wheezing, shortness of breath, and coughing. Treated with bronchodilators and anti-inflammatory medications.
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD): A group of diseases (primarily emphysema and chronic bronchitis) characterized by persistent airflow limitation. Emphysema destroys alveolar walls, reducing surface area for gas exchange and causing air trapping. Chronic bronchitis involves chronic inflammation and mucus production in the bronchi. Most commonly caused by long-term smoking.
  • Pneumonia: Infection of the alveoli (by bacteria, viruses, or fungi) causing them to fill with fluid and pus, impairing gas exchange.
  • Tuberculosis: A bacterial infection (Mycobacterium tuberculosis) that primarily affects the lungs, causing cough, fever, and progressive lung damage.
  • Pneumothorax: Collapsed lung caused by air entering the pleural space (the space between the lung and chest wall), destroying the negative pressure that keeps the lung expanded.

Mnemonic: Lung Volumes

"TV-IRV-ERV-RV" — Think of a TV in an IRV (car) with an ERV (extra room view) on a road called RV.

Vital Capacity = TV + IRV + ERV (everything you can voluntarily move)

Total Lung Capacity = VC + RV (everything inside, including what you cannot push out)

Key Points — Chapter 26

  • External respiration = gas exchange in lungs. Internal respiration = gas exchange in tissues. Both occur by diffusion.
  • Air path: nasal cavity → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli.
  • About 300 million alveoli provide an enormous surface area (~70 m²) for gas exchange. Surfactant prevents alveolar collapse.
  • Inspiration: diaphragm contracts → thoracic volume increases → intrapulmonary pressure drops below atmospheric → air flows in.
  • Most CO₂ is transported as bicarbonate ions (~70%); the enzyme carbonic anhydrase catalyzes the conversion.
  • Vital capacity = TV + IRV + ERV. Residual volume is the air that always stays in the lungs.

Nervous System — Structure and Function

While the endocrine system sends slow, broadcast messages via blood, the nervous system provides rapid, precise, point-to-point communication using electrical and chemical signals. It enables you to sense your environment, process information, and coordinate responses — all in milliseconds.

Organization of the Nervous System

The nervous system is divided anatomically and functionally:

Anatomical Division

  • Central Nervous System (CNS) — the brain and spinal cord. This is the processing center: it integrates information and generates responses.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) — all the nerves and ganglia outside the CNS. It carries signals to and from the CNS.

Functional Division of the PNS

  • Somatic Nervous System — controls voluntary movements. Motor neurons carry signals from the CNS to skeletal muscles. Sensory neurons carry information from the skin, muscles, and joints back to the CNS.
  • Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) — controls involuntary functions (heart rate, digestion, gland secretion). Further divided into:
    • Sympathetic division — "fight or flight." Prepares the body for action: increases heart rate, dilates pupils, diverts blood to muscles, inhibits digestion.
    • Parasympathetic division — "rest and digest." Promotes calm maintenance: slows heart rate, constricts pupils, stimulates digestion and secretion.

Neuron Structure

The neuron (nerve cell) is the fundamental functional unit of the nervous system. Each neuron has three main parts:

  • Cell body (soma) — contains the nucleus and most organelles. This is the metabolic center of the neuron.
  • Dendrites — short, branching extensions that receive signals from other neurons or from sensory receptors. They conduct impulses toward the cell body.
  • Axon — a single, long extension that carries the electrical signal (action potential) away from the cell body toward other neurons, muscles, or glands. The end of the axon branches into axon terminals (synaptic boutons) that release neurotransmitters.

Myelin Sheath

Many axons are wrapped in a fatty insulating layer called the myelin sheath. Myelin speeds up signal transmission dramatically by forcing the electrical impulse to "jump" between gaps in the myelin called Nodes of Ranvier. This jumping conduction is called saltatory conduction.

  • In the PNS, myelin is produced by Schwann cells (one Schwann cell wraps around one segment of one axon).
  • In the CNS, myelin is produced by oligodendrocytes (one oligodendrocyte can myelinate segments of multiple axons).

Supporting Cells (Glial Cells / Neuroglia)

Glial cells are approximately equal in number to neurons and provide essential support:

  • Schwann cells — myelinate PNS axons; aid nerve regeneration.
  • Oligodendrocytes — myelinate CNS axons.
  • Astrocytes — star-shaped cells in the CNS. Maintain the blood-brain barrier, regulate the chemical environment around neurons, provide nutrients.
  • Microglia — immune cells of the CNS. They phagocytize pathogens and debris (similar to macrophages).
  • Ependymal cells — line the ventricles of the brain; produce cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  • Radial glia — found primarily during embryonic development. They span the thickness of the developing neural tube and serve as scaffolding along which newly formed neurons migrate to their final positions. Some also function as neural stem cells, giving rise to neurons and other glia. In adults, a few persist in specialized forms (Bergmann glia in the cerebellum, Muller glia in the retina).

Cerebrospinal Fluid: Beyond Cushioning

CSF does more than protect the brain from mechanical injury. Produced by the choroid plexus in the brain's ventricles, CSF transports nutrients (glucose, amino acids) from the blood to brain tissue, removes metabolic waste products, and carries signaling molecules including hormones and cytokines. It also contains a small number of immune cells (primarily lymphocytes and monocytes) that provide immunological surveillance. CSF is reabsorbed into the venous blood through arachnoid granulations.

Types of Neurons

  • Sensory (afferent) neurons — carry signals FROM receptors (in skin, organs, etc.) TO the CNS. They tell the brain what is happening in the body and environment.
  • Motor (efferent) neurons — carry signals FROM the CNS TO effectors (muscles and glands). They cause actions.
  • Interneurons (association neurons) — located entirely within the CNS. They connect sensory and motor neurons, integrate information, and are the basis of thought and decision-making.

The Reflex Arc

A reflex is a rapid, automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that does not require conscious thought. The pathway of a reflex is called a reflex arc:

  1. Receptor — detects the stimulus (e.g., heat sensor in skin)
  2. Sensory neuron — transmits signal to the spinal cord
  3. Integration center — interneuron in the spinal cord processes the signal
  4. Motor neuron — carries response signal to the effector
  5. Effector — muscle or gland that carries out the response (e.g., hand pulls away from heat)

Note that the signal goes to the spinal cord and back without needing to reach the brain first. This is why reflexes are so fast. The brain is informed afterward.

A classic clinical example is the patellar (knee-jerk) reflex, a monosynaptic stretch reflex. When the patellar tendon is tapped, it stretches the quadriceps muscle, activating muscle spindle receptors. Sensory neurons carry this signal directly to the spinal cord, where they synapse with motor neurons (without an interneuron intermediary). The motor neurons stimulate the quadriceps to contract, extending the lower leg. This reflex tests the integrity of the L2–L4 spinal segments and is routinely assessed in neurological examinations.

Ganglia vs. Nuclei

A cluster of neuron cell bodies is given different names depending on location:

  • Ganglion (plural: ganglia) — a cluster of cell bodies in the PNS.
  • Nucleus (plural: nuclei) — a cluster of cell bodies in the CNS. (Not to be confused with the nucleus of a cell.)

Membrane Potential and Action Potential

Neurons communicate using electrical signals. Understanding how these signals work requires understanding the electrical properties of the neuron membrane.

Resting Membrane Potential

When a neuron is not sending a signal, it maintains a voltage difference across its membrane of about −70 mV (the inside is negative relative to the outside). This is the resting membrane potential. It is maintained by:

  • The sodium-potassium pump (Na+/K+ ATPase), which pumps 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in, using ATP.
  • Potassium leak channels — more K+ leaks out than Na+ leaks in, keeping the inside negative.

Action Potential

An action potential is a rapid, brief reversal of the membrane potential that travels along the axon like a wave. It follows an all-or-nothing principle: either it fires fully or not at all.

The stages of an action potential:

  1. Resting state (−70 mV) — Voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels are closed. The sodium-potassium pump maintains the resting potential.
  2. Stimulus reaches threshold (−55 mV) — A stimulus (from a synapse or sensory receptor) causes partial depolarization. If it reaches the threshold of about −55 mV, voltage-gated Na+ channels open.
  3. Depolarization (rising phase to +30 mV) — Na+ rushes into the cell (down its concentration gradient), making the inside rapidly positive.
  4. Repolarization (falling phase) — Na+ channels inactivate (close). Voltage-gated K+ channels open, and K+ flows out, restoring the negative interior.
  5. Hyperpolarization (undershoot) — K+ channels are slow to close, so slightly more K+ leaves than needed, briefly making the membrane more negative than resting (about −80 mV).
  6. Return to resting potential — The Na+/K+ pump and leak channels restore −70 mV.
Exam Tip: The action potential is "all-or-nothing." A stronger stimulus does NOT produce a bigger action potential — it produces more action potentials per second (higher frequency). The amplitude of each individual action potential is always the same.

Synaptic Transmission

Electrical Synapses

At electrical synapses, the cells are physically connected by gap junctions — protein channels that allow ions to flow directly from one cell to the next. Transmission is extremely fast and bidirectional. Found in cardiac muscle and some parts of the brain.

Chemical Synapses

Most synapses in the nervous system are chemical. A small gap called the synaptic cleft separates the presynaptic neuron from the postsynaptic cell. The signal crosses this gap using chemical messengers called neurotransmitters:

  1. An action potential arrives at the axon terminal of the presynaptic neuron.
  2. Voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open; calcium ions flow in.
  3. Calcium triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft (exocytosis).
  4. Neurotransmitter crosses the cleft and binds to receptors on the postsynaptic cell.
  5. This binding opens ion channels in the postsynaptic membrane, causing either excitation (depolarization) or inhibition (hyperpolarization).
  6. The neurotransmitter is then removed from the cleft by enzymatic degradation, reuptake into the presynaptic neuron, or diffusion away.

Key Points — Nervous System Structure and Function
  • CNS = brain + spinal cord. PNS = everything else (nerves, ganglia).
  • Somatic NS = voluntary (skeletal muscle). Autonomic NS = involuntary (organs, glands).
  • Sympathetic = fight or flight. Parasympathetic = rest and digest. They are antagonistic.
  • Neurons have dendrites (receive), cell body (integrate), axon (transmit).
  • Myelin speeds conduction via saltatory conduction (jumping between Nodes of Ranvier).
  • Resting potential is −70 mV. Action potentials are all-or-nothing: depolarization (Na+ in) then repolarization (K+ out).
  • At chemical synapses, neurotransmitters cross the synaptic cleft to relay the signal.

8.1 Sexual vs Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring from a single parent. It is fast and efficient but generates no genetic diversity. Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of gametes from two parents, producing genetically unique offspring. This genetic diversity is a major advantage when environments change, as it increases the chance that some individuals will have traits suited to new conditions.

8.2 Haploid and Diploid Phases

Organisms that reproduce sexually alternate between two states of chromosome number:

  • Diploid (2n): Cells contain two copies of each chromosome (one from each parent). In humans, 2n = 46.
  • Haploid (n): Cells contain one copy of each chromosome. In humans, n = 23. Gametes (sperm and eggs) are haploid.

Meiosis is the special type of cell division that reduces the chromosome number from diploid to haploid. Fertilization restores the diploid number when two haploid gametes fuse.

8.3 Meiosis I: The Reductive Division

Meiosis I is where the real magic of genetic shuffling happens. It separates homologous chromosomes (not sister chromatids).

Prophase I

This is the longest and most complex phase of meiosis. Homologous chromosomes (one from each parent) find each other and pair up in a process called synapsis. The paired homologs are held together by a protein structure called the synaptonemal complex, forming a bivalent (or tetrad, since it contains four chromatids total).

During synapsis, crossing over (recombination) occurs: nonsister chromatids from homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA at points called chiasmata (singular: chiasma). This shuffles alleles between maternal and paternal chromosomes, creating new genetic combinations that did not exist in either parent.

The Five Substages of Prophase I

Prophase I is subdivided into five stages with distinct events:

  1. Leptotene: Chromosomes begin to condense and become visible as thin threads.
  2. Zygotene: Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis), connected by the synaptonemal complex, forming bivalents.
  3. Pachytene: Synapsis is complete and each bivalent consists of four chromatids (a tetrad); this is when crossing over occurs between non-sister chromatids.
  4. Diplotene: The synaptonemal complex disassembles and homologs begin to separate but remain connected at chiasmata (the physical sites of crossover).
  5. Diakinesis: Chromosomes reach maximum condensation and chiasmata slide toward chromosome ends (terminalization) in preparation for metaphase I.

The Synaptonemal Complex

The synaptonemal complex is a protein scaffold that forms between paired homologous chromosomes during prophase I. It consists of two lateral elements (one along each homolog) connected by transverse filaments to a central element, creating a zipper-like structure. This complex holds homologs in precise alignment, facilitating crossing over. Defects in synaptonemal complex formation can lead to meiotic errors and infertility.

Metaphase I

Bivalents (paired homologs) line up at the metaphase plate. Crucially, the orientation of each bivalent is random - either the maternal or paternal homolog can face either pole. This is independent assortment, and it means that for n chromosome pairs, there are 2ⁿ possible combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes in the gametes. For humans (n = 23), that is over 8 million combinations - and that is before counting the additional variation from crossing over.

Anaphase I

Homologous chromosomes are pulled to opposite poles. Note: sister chromatids remain joined at this stage. This is the key difference from mitosis, where sister chromatids separate.

Telophase I and Cytokinesis

Two haploid cells form, each with half the original number of chromosomes (but each chromosome still consists of two sister chromatids).

8.4 Meiosis II: The Equational Division

Meiosis II closely resembles mitosis. Sister chromatids separate.

  • Prophase II: Chromosomes condense (if they had relaxed). Spindle forms.
  • Metaphase II: Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate.
  • Anaphase II: Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
  • Telophase II and cytokinesis: Four haploid cells are produced.

8.5 Gametogenesis

Spermatogenesis

In males, a diploid spermatogonium undergoes meiosis to produce four equal-sized haploid spermatids, which then mature into functional sperm cells. This process is continuous from puberty onward and produces millions of sperm daily.

Oogenesis

In females, a diploid oogonium undergoes meiosis, but the cytoplasm is divided unequally. Meiosis I produces one large secondary oocyte and one small first polar body. Meiosis II (completed only upon fertilization) produces one large ovum (egg) and one second polar body. Polar bodies eventually degenerate. The unequal division ensures the egg retains maximum cytoplasm and nutrients for the developing embryo.

Polar Bodies (Polocytes)

The small, nonfunctional cells produced during oogenesis are called polar bodies (also termed polocytes). Because cytoplasm is divided unequally at each meiotic division, polar bodies receive minimal cytoplasm and eventually degenerate. The first polar body is generated after meiosis I, and the second after meiosis II. As a result, each primary oocyte ultimately yields only one functional ovum, in contrast to spermatogenesis, where each primary spermatocyte produces four functional sperm.

Oogenesis Arrest Points

Human oogenesis has two arrest points that are frequently tested. Primary oocytes arrest in the diplotene stage of prophase I (also called dictyotene) during fetal development (around the 3rd month) and remain arrested until puberty - potentially for decades. At ovulation, the secondary oocyte arrests again in metaphase II. Meiosis II is completed only if the oocyte is fertilized by a sperm. This means that the egg released at ovulation is technically a secondary oocyte arrested in metaphase II, not a fully mature ovum.

8.6 Consequences of Meiosis

Meiosis generates genetic diversity through three mechanisms:

  1. Crossing over in prophase I creates recombinant chromosomes.
  2. Independent assortment in metaphase I produces random combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes.
  3. Random fertilization: Any sperm can fuse with any egg, further multiplying the possible genetic combinations.

8.7 Errors in Meiosis

Nondisjunction

Nondisjunction is the failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis. If it occurs in meiosis I (homologs fail to separate) or meiosis II (sister chromatids fail to separate), the resulting gametes will have an abnormal number of chromosomes. Fertilization involving such a gamete produces aneuploid offspring. Examples: trisomy 21 (Down syndrome, three copies of chromosome 21), Turner syndrome (monosomy X, 45,X), and Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY).

Complete Nondisjunction and Triploidy

Beyond single-chromosome nondisjunction, complete failure of meiotic division (all chromosomes failing to separate) produces diploid gametes. If such a gamete is fertilized by a normal haploid gamete, the result is triploidy (3n = 69 chromosomes in humans). Triploidy accounts for approximately 1–3% of all conceptions but is almost always lethal, with most triploid pregnancies ending in early miscarriage. Rare cases that survive to birth are invariably fatal in the neonatal period.

Anaphase Lag

In anaphase lag, a chromosome moves too slowly during anaphase and is excluded from the daughter nucleus, resulting in one daughter cell missing a chromosome (monosomy). The "lost" chromosome may be degraded. Anaphase lag differs from nondisjunction in its mechanism: rather than two chromosomes traveling to the same pole, a single chromosome simply fails to keep pace during migration and is excluded from both daughter nuclei. When anaphase lag occurs during early embryonic mitotic divisions rather than meiosis, it can produce chromosomal mosaicism, where different cell populations within the same individual carry different chromosome numbers.

Genetic Determination of Sex

In humans, sex is genetically determined at fertilization by the sperm's sex chromosome. All eggs carry an X chromosome (23,X). Sperm carry either an X or a Y chromosome (23,X or 23,Y). Fertilization by an X-bearing sperm produces a female (46,XX); fertilization by a Y-bearing sperm produces a male (46,XY). The key gene on the Y chromosome is SRY (Sex-determining Region Y), which triggers male sexual development. Thus, it is the father's gamete that determines the sex of the offspring.

Key Points - Chapter 8

  • Meiosis is two sequential divisions (meiosis I and II) producing four haploid cells from one diploid cell.
  • Meiosis I is the reductive division (homologs separate); meiosis II is equational (sister chromatids separate, like mitosis).
  • Crossing over (prophase I) and independent assortment (metaphase I) generate genetic diversity.
  • Spermatogenesis produces four functional sperm; oogenesis produces one egg and polar bodies.
  • Nondisjunction leads to aneuploidy (abnormal chromosome numbers), causing conditions like Down syndrome.
  • The three engines of genetic diversity in sexual reproduction are crossing over, independent assortment, and random fertilization.

Mnemonic - Meiosis I vs II

Meiosis I = Identical twins separate (homologs part). Meiosis II = II sisters split (sister chromatids part).

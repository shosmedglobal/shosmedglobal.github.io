Lipids

What Are Lipids?

Lipids are a diverse group of biological molecules defined not by a common structure but by a common property: they are hydrophobic (insoluble in water) and soluble in nonpolar organic solvents. Lipids serve as energy storage molecules, structural components of membranes, signaling molecules, and insulation.

Fatty Acids

Fatty acids are long-chain carboxylic acids, typically with 12–24 carbons and an even number of carbon atoms. They are the building blocks of most lipids.

Unsaturated fatty acids have cis double bonds (in nature), which introduce kinks in the chain, preventing tight packing and lowering the melting point (hence they are liquid oils rather than solid fats).

Essential fatty acids: Linoleic acid (ω-6) and α-linolenic acid (ω-3) must come from the diet. Arachidonic acid (precursor of prostaglandins and leukotrienes) can be synthesized from linoleic acid.

Waxes

Waxes are esters of long-chain fatty acids (typically C14–C36) with long-chain monohydric alcohols (C16–C30). They form protective, waterproof coatings on skin, hair, feathers, leaves, and fruit surfaces. Beeswax (secreted by honey bees) and lanolin (from sheep wool) are familiar examples. Waxes are classified alongside fats and sterol esters as simple lipids because they yield only fatty acids and alcohols upon hydrolysis.

Triacylglycerols (Neutral Lipids)

Triacylglycerols (also called triglycerides) are esters of glycerol with three fatty acids. They are the main form of energy storage in adipose tissue. When all three fatty acids are the same, the molecule is a "simple" triacylglycerol; when they differ, it is "mixed."

Saponification: Alkaline hydrolysis (NaOH or KOH) of triacylglycerols produces glycerol and fatty acid salts - these salts are soap.

Phospholipids

Phospholipids are the major structural lipids of biological membranes. They are based on a glycerol backbone with two fatty acids (at positions 1 and 2) and a phosphate group (at position 3) linked to a polar head group (e.g., choline, ethanolamine, serine, inositol).

Phospholipids are amphipathic: they have a hydrophilic (polar) head and two hydrophobic (nonpolar) fatty acid tails. In water, they spontaneously form bilayers - the basis of all cell membranes.

Lysophospholipids

Lysophospholipids are phospholipids that have lost one of their two fatty acid chains, typically at the sn-2 position, through the action of the enzyme phospholipase A₂. The resulting single-tailed molecule has detergent-like properties and can disrupt membranes. Lysophosphatidylcholine is the most abundant lysophospholipid in blood plasma. Excessive lysophospholipid production contributes to inflammation and tissue damage, for example following a snake bite (many snake venoms contain phospholipase A₂).

Sphingolipids

Sphingolipids are based on the amino alcohol sphingosine (not glycerol). A fatty acid linked via an amide bond to sphingosine forms ceramide, which is the backbone of all sphingolipids. Adding a phosphocholine group gives sphingomyelin (found in nerve cell myelin sheaths). Adding sugars gives glycosphingolipids (cerebrosides, gangliosides), important on cell surfaces.

Steroids

Steroids share a characteristic fused four-ring structure (three six-membered rings and one five-membered ring), known as the steroid nucleus or sterane (formally: perhydrocyclopenta[a]phenanthrene). Key examples:

  • Cholesterol: A sterol found in animal cell membranes; precursor of steroid hormones, bile acids, and vitamin D.
  • Steroid hormones: Cortisol (stress response), aldosterone (salt balance), testosterone (male sex hormone), estradiol (female sex hormone), progesterone (pregnancy).
  • Bile acids: Cholic acid, chenodeoxycholic acid; emulsify dietary fats.

The Steroid Ring System in Detail

The sterane skeleton consists of three fused six-membered rings (labelled A, B, and C) and one five-membered ring (ring D), totalling 17 carbon atoms in the ring framework. Carbon atoms are numbered 1–17 in a specific conventional pattern. Two angular methyl groups are typically present at C-10 (designated C-19) and C-13 (designated C-18).

Cholesterol (C₂₈H₄₆O) has an eight-carbon branched alkyl side chain at C-17, a hydroxyl group at C-3, and a double bond between C-5 and C-6. The ring system is nearly flat and rigid, making steroids compact and strongly hydrophobic.

Modifications to ring A and functional group substitutions distinguish the major steroid classes:

  • Glucocorticoids (e.g., cortisol): ketone at C-3, hydroxyl groups at C-11 and C-17.
  • Mineralocorticoids (e.g., aldosterone): aldehyde at C-18.
  • Androgens (e.g., testosterone): ketone at C-3, hydroxyl at C-17.
  • Estrogens (e.g., estradiol): aromatic ring A - the only steroid class with an aromatic ring, which accounts for their distinct receptor-binding properties and the loss of the C-19 angular methyl group.

Humans cannot synthesise the steroid ring system de novo - cholesterol serves as the universal precursor from which all steroid hormones, vitamin D, and bile acids are derived.

Biological Membranes

Cell membranes are primarily composed of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins (the fluid mosaic model). The hydrophobic tails face inward, forming a nonpolar barrier, while the hydrophilic heads face the aqueous environment on both sides. Cholesterol molecules insert between phospholipids, modulating membrane fluidity. Membrane proteins perform transport, signaling, and enzymatic functions.

β-Oxidation of Fatty Acids

β-Oxidation is the metabolic pathway that degrades fatty acids into acetyl-CoA units within the mitochondrial matrix. Before entering the mitochondrion, a fatty acid must first be activated by attachment to coenzyme A in the cytosol:

R–COOH + CoA–SH + ATP → R–CO–S–CoA + AMP + 2 Pᵢ

Long-chain acyl-CoA molecules are transported across the inner mitochondrial membrane by the carnitine shuttle. Each cycle of β-oxidation involves four sequential reactions:

  1. Oxidation by FAD: Acyl-CoA dehydrogenase introduces a trans double bond between the α and β carbons, producing a trans-Δ²-enoyl-CoA and FADH₂.
  2. Hydration: Enoyl-CoA hydratase adds water across the double bond, forming L-β-hydroxyacyl-CoA.
  3. Oxidation by NAD⁺: β-Hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase oxidises the hydroxyl to a ketone, forming β-ketoacyl-CoA and NADH.
  4. Thiolytic cleavage: β-Ketothiolase cleaves the β-ketoacyl-CoA with a free CoA–SH, releasing one acetyl-CoA (C₂) and leaving an acyl-CoA shortened by two carbons.

The shortened acyl-CoA re-enters the cycle, and the process repeats until the entire chain is converted to acetyl-CoA units. Each acetyl-CoA then enters the citric acid cycle for complete oxidation. The FADH₂ and NADH produced in each β-oxidation cycle feed into the respiratory chain (oxidative phosphorylation), generating additional ATP.

Energy yield example: Complete oxidation of palmitic acid (C16:0) requires 7 cycles of β-oxidation, producing 8 acetyl-CoA, 7 FADH₂, and 7 NADH. Using current consensus values (~2.5 ATP per NADH, ~1.5 ATP per FADH₂, ~10 ATP per acetyl-CoA via the citric acid cycle), the total yield is approximately 106 ATP (after subtracting 2 ATP equivalents for activation). This enormous energy density is why fats are the body's preferred long-term energy store.

The Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)

The citric acid cycle (also called the Krebs cycle or tricarboxylic acid cycle) operates in the mitochondrial matrix and completely oxidises the two-carbon acetyl group from acetyl-CoA to two molecules of CO₂. It is the central metabolic hub where carbon skeletons from carbohydrates, fats, and amino acids converge for final oxidation.

The cycle proceeds through eight enzyme-catalysed steps:

  1. Acetyl-CoA (C₂) condenses with oxaloacetate (C₄) to form citrate (C₆).
  2. Citrate is isomerised to isocitrate.
  3. Isocitrate is oxidatively decarboxylated to α-ketoglutarate (C₅), releasing CO₂ and producing NADH.
  4. α-Ketoglutarate undergoes a second oxidative decarboxylation to succinyl-CoA (C₄), releasing CO₂ and producing NADH. This step requires thiamine (vitamin B₁) as a cofactor.
  5. Succinyl-CoA is converted to succinate, with formation of GTP (equivalent to 1 ATP).
  6. Succinate is oxidised to fumarate, producing FADH₂.
  7. Fumarate is hydrated to malate.
  8. Malate is oxidised to regenerate oxaloacetate, producing NADH.

Net yield per acetyl-CoA: 2 CO₂ + 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP. When the reduced coenzymes are reoxidised through the respiratory chain (oxidative phosphorylation), each NADH yields approximately 2.5 ATP and each FADH₂ yields approximately 1.5 ATP. The total ATP yield from one acetyl-CoA through the citric acid cycle plus oxidative phosphorylation is therefore approximately 10 ATP.

The respiratory chain (electron transport chain) consists of four membrane-bound protein complexes (I–IV) in the inner mitochondrial membrane. Electrons from NADH and FADH₂ pass through these complexes to reduce O₂ to H₂O. The energy released drives proton pumping across the membrane, creating an electrochemical gradient that powers ATP synthase (Complex V) to synthesise ATP from ADP + Pᵢ.

Key Points - Chapter 32

  • Lipids are defined by hydrophobicity, not by structure.
  • Fatty acids: saturated (straight, solid) vs. unsaturated (kinked, liquid); essential ones must come from diet.
  • Triacylglycerols: glycerol + 3 fatty acids; main energy storage; saponification gives soap.
  • Phospholipids: amphipathic; form bilayers in cell membranes.
  • Steroids share a 4-ring nucleus; cholesterol is the precursor of steroid hormones and bile acids.
  • Terpenes are built from isoprene (C5) units.
  • Sterane: 3 six-membered rings (A, B, C) + 1 five-membered ring (D); 17 carbons; cholesterol is the universal precursor.
  • β-Oxidation: 4-step cycle in mitochondria cleaves 2 carbons per round as acetyl-CoA; produces FADH₂ and NADH.
  • Citric acid cycle: acetyl-CoA → 2 CO₂ + 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP per turn; ~10 ATP total per acetyl-CoA.
  • Oxidative phosphorylation: electron transport chain + ATP synthase; ~2.5 ATP/NADH, ~1.5 ATP/FADH₂.

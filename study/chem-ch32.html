Chapter 32

Lipids

What Are Lipids?

Lipids are a diverse group of biological molecules defined not by a common structure but by a common property: they are hydrophobic (insoluble in water) and soluble in nonpolar organic solvents. Lipids serve as energy storage molecules, structural components of membranes, signaling molecules, and insulation.

Fatty Acids

Fatty acids are long-chain carboxylic acids, typically with 12–24 carbons and an even number of carbon atoms. They are the building blocks of most lipids.

Unsaturated fatty acids have cis double bonds (in nature), which introduce kinks in the chain, preventing tight packing and lowering the melting point (hence they are liquid oils rather than solid fats).

Essential fatty acids: Linoleic acid (ω-6) and α-linolenic acid (ω-3) must come from the diet. Arachidonic acid (precursor of prostaglandins and leukotrienes) can be synthesized from linoleic acid.

Triacylglycerols (Neutral Lipids)

Triacylglycerols (also called triglycerides) are esters of glycerol with three fatty acids. They are the main form of energy storage in adipose tissue. When all three fatty acids are the same, the molecule is a "simple" triacylglycerol; when they differ, it is "mixed."

Saponification: Alkaline hydrolysis (NaOH or KOH) of triacylglycerols produces glycerol and fatty acid salts — these salts are soap.

Phospholipids

Phospholipids are the major structural lipids of biological membranes. They are based on a glycerol backbone with two fatty acids (at positions 1 and 2) and a phosphate group (at position 3) linked to a polar head group (e.g., choline, ethanolamine, serine, inositol).

Phospholipids are amphipathic: they have a hydrophilic (polar) head and two hydrophobic (nonpolar) fatty acid tails. In water, they spontaneously form bilayers — the basis of all cell membranes.

Sphingolipids

Sphingolipids are based on the amino alcohol sphingosine (not glycerol). A fatty acid linked via an amide bond to sphingosine forms ceramide, which is the backbone of all sphingolipids. Adding a phosphocholine group gives sphingomyelin (found in nerve cell myelin sheaths). Adding sugars gives glycosphingolipids (cerebrosides, gangliosides), important on cell surfaces.

Steroids

Steroids share a characteristic fused four-ring structure (three six-membered rings and one five-membered ring), known as the steroid nucleus (perhydrocyclopentanophenanthrene). Key examples:

  • Cholesterol: A sterol found in animal cell membranes; precursor of steroid hormones, bile acids, and vitamin D.
  • Steroid hormones: Cortisol (stress response), aldosterone (salt balance), testosterone (male sex hormone), estradiol (female sex hormone), progesterone (pregnancy).
  • Bile acids: Cholic acid, chenodeoxycholic acid; emulsify dietary fats.

Biological Membranes

Cell membranes are primarily composed of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins (the fluid mosaic model). The hydrophobic tails face inward, forming a nonpolar barrier, while the hydrophilic heads face the aqueous environment on both sides. Cholesterol molecules insert between phospholipids, modulating membrane fluidity. Membrane proteins perform transport, signaling, and enzymatic functions.

Key Points — Chapter 32

  • Lipids are defined by hydrophobicity, not by structure.
  • Fatty acids: saturated (straight, solid) vs. unsaturated (kinked, liquid); essential ones must come from diet.
  • Triacylglycerols: glycerol + 3 fatty acids; main energy storage; saponification gives soap.
  • Phospholipids: amphipathic; form bilayers in cell membranes.
  • Steroids share a 4-ring nucleus; cholesterol is the precursor of steroid hormones and bile acids.
  • Terpenes are built from isoprene (C5) units.

Mnemonic: Essential Fatty Acids

"Linoleic and Linolenic are Life-essential Lipids"

Both start with "Lin-" and both must come from food.

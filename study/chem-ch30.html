Chapter 30

Nucleic Acids

The Bases

Nucleic acids store and transmit genetic information using a simple alphabet of five bases:

Purine Bases (two-ring)
  • Adenine (A): 6-aminopurine
  • Guanine (G): 2-amino-6-oxopurine
Pyrimidine Bases (one-ring)
  • Cytosine (C): 2-oxo-4-aminopyrimidine
  • Thymine (T): 5-methyluracil (DNA only)
  • Uracil (U): 2,4-dioxopyrimidine (RNA only)

Keto-enol tautomerism of bases: The bases can exist in keto (lactam) and enol (lactim) forms. The keto form predominates at physiological pH, and this is the form that participates in correct Watson-Crick base pairing. If a base shifts to the rare enol form, it can mispair and potentially cause a mutation.

The sugar in DNA is 2-deoxyribose (lacking the OH at C2'); in RNA, it is ribose (with OH at C2').

Naming: Adenine + ribose = adenosine; adenosine + phosphate = adenosine monophosphate (AMP). With deoxyribose: deoxyadenosine, dAMP, etc.

Purine Metabolic Intermediates

Hypoxanthine and xanthine are purine derivatives that arise as intermediates in purine catabolism. Hypoxanthine is the base of inosine (found in the wobble position of some tRNA anticodons), and xanthine is the immediate precursor of uric acid (the end product of purine degradation in humans). Caffeine, theophylline, and theobromine are methylated xanthine derivatives with well-known stimulant properties.

UV Absorption and Nucleotide Properties

All nucleotides absorb ultraviolet light strongly at 260 nm due to the conjugated ring systems of their bases. This property is exploited in molecular biology to quantify DNA and RNA concentrations using spectrophotometry (an absorbance of 1.0 at 260 nm corresponds to approximately 50 μg/mL for double-stranded DNA or 40 μg/mL for single-stranded RNA). Under acidic conditions, the N-glycosidic bond in purine nucleotides hydrolyses more readily than in pyrimidine nucleotides, a phenomenon called depurination that is relevant to DNA damage and repair.

Energy-Rich Phosphoanhydride Bonds

Nucleoside di- and triphosphates contain phosphoanhydride bonds between the phosphate groups that are sometimes called "high-energy" bonds. Hydrolysis of these bonds releases substantial free energy (approximately −30.5 kJ/mol for ATP). This energy drives biosynthetic reactions throughout metabolism. While ATP is the best-known example, GTP powers protein synthesis and signal transduction, UTP is used for polysaccharide synthesis, and CTP is involved in lipid biosynthesis.

Complementary Base Pairing

The specificity of base pairing is the foundation of genetic information storage:

  • Adenine pairs with Thymine (in DNA) or Uracil (in RNA) through 2 hydrogen bonds.
  • Guanine pairs with Cytosine through 3 hydrogen bonds.

Because G-C pairs have three hydrogen bonds, DNA regions rich in G-C are more stable (require higher temperatures to denature) than A-T rich regions.

Bonds in Nucleic Acids

  • N-glycosidic bond: Connects the nitrogenous base to the sugar at C1'. In purines, the bond is to N9; in pyrimidines, to N1.
  • Phosphoester bond: A single phosphate group esterified to one hydroxyl of the sugar.
  • Phosphodiester bond: A phosphate group esterified to two sugars (at C3' of one nucleotide and C5' of the next). This is the backbone linkage of nucleic acid chains.

DNA Structure

DNA is a double helix consisting of two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wound around each other. The sugar-phosphate backbone runs on the outside, and the bases point inward, pairing with their complements on the opposite strand. The two strands run in opposite directions: one 5'→3' and the other 3'→5'.

RNA Structure and Types

RNA is usually single-stranded (but can form local secondary structures through internal base pairing). Key differences from DNA: RNA uses ribose (not deoxyribose) and uracil (not thymine).

Key Points — Chapter 30

  • Purines (A, G): two-ring; Pyrimidines (C, T/U): one-ring.
  • Nucleoside = base + sugar; Nucleotide = base + sugar + phosphate.
  • A pairs with T (or U) via 2 H-bonds; G pairs with C via 3 H-bonds.
  • DNA backbone: sugar-phosphate linked by phosphodiester bonds.
  • DNA is a double helix with antiparallel strands; RNA is typically single-stranded.
  • Three types of RNA: mRNA (message), tRNA (amino acid carrier), rRNA (ribosome component).

Mnemonic: Base Pairing

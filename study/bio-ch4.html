Chapter 4

Cellular Biology — Non-Cell Organisms (Viruses)

4.1 What Are Viruses?

Viruses are not cells. They have no metabolism of their own, no ribosomes, no ability to generate ATP, and they cannot reproduce independently. A virus is essentially a piece of genetic material (DNA or RNA) wrapped in a protein coat. It can only replicate by hijacking the machinery of a living host cell. Because of this, viruses are often described as "obligate intracellular parasites" and exist at the boundary between living and non-living matter.

Size and Scale

Viruses are among the smallest biological entities, ranging from about 20 nm (parvoviruses) to 300 nm (poxviruses) in diameter. For context, most bacteria are 1–10 μm (1,000–10,000 nm), so even the largest viruses are much smaller than most bacterial cells. Viruses can only be visualized with an electron microscope, which has resolution down to about 0.1–1 nm, compared to a light microscope's limit of about 200 nm.

4.2 Classification by Host

  • Bacteriophages (phages): infect bacteria. These are the most abundant biological entities on Earth.
  • Animal viruses: infect animal cells. Examples: influenza, HIV, rabies.
  • Plant viruses: infect plants. Often transmitted by insects. Example: tobacco mosaic virus (TMV).

4.3 Capsid Structure

The protein shell surrounding the viral genome is called the capsid, built from protein subunits called capsomeres. There are three basic shapes:

  • Icosahedral: A 20-sided geometric shape that is roughly spherical. This is very common (e.g., adenovirus, poliovirus).
  • Helical: Capsomeres arranged in a spiral around the nucleic acid, forming a rod shape (e.g., tobacco mosaic virus, Ebola).
  • Complex: Irregular shapes that do not fit neatly into the other categories. Bacteriophage T4 has an icosahedral head, a helical tail, and tail fibers — a classic complex structure. Poxviruses also have complex shapes.

Specific Virus Dimensions

Specific virus dimensions are testable details. Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) measures approximately 18 x 250 nm with a helical capsid. Adenoviruses are roughly 70–90 nm in diameter with an icosahedral capsid bearing glycoprotein spikes at each vertex. Influenza viruses are 80–200 nm in diameter, enveloped, with a genome of eight separate RNA segments each wrapped in a helical capsid. Bacteriophage T4 is approximately 80 x 225 nm with a complex structure consisting of an icosahedral head, contractile tail sheath, baseplate, and tail fibers.

The influenza virus genome is notable for being segmented — it consists of eight separate single-stranded RNA molecules, each encoding one or two proteins. This segmented genome allows genetic reassortment: when two different influenza strains infect the same cell, their segments can mix during assembly, producing novel viral combinations. This is the basis for antigenic shift, which can cause pandemics.

Some viruses have an additional envelope — a lipid bilayer derived from the host cell's membrane, studded with viral glycoproteins. Enveloped viruses (such as influenza and HIV) are generally more susceptible to detergents and desiccation than non-enveloped (naked) viruses.

4.4 Virus Classification by Genome

Viruses can be classified by the nature of their genetic material:

Nucleic acid typeDNA or RNADNA: herpesvirus; RNA: influenza
StrandednessSingle-stranded (ss) or double-stranded (ds)dsDNA: adenovirus; ssRNA: coronavirus
Sense (for ssRNA)Positive (+) or Negative (−)+ssRNA: SARS-CoV-2; −ssRNA: Ebola

Positive-sense (+) RNA can be directly translated by host ribosomes as if it were mRNA. Negative-sense (−) RNA must first be copied into the complementary positive strand by an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase before translation can occur.

Baltimore Classification

The Baltimore classification organizes viruses into seven groups by genome type and replication strategy. Group I (dsDNA): Adenoviruses, Herpesviruses, Poxviruses, Papillomaviruses. Group II (ssDNA): Parvoviruses. Group III (dsRNA): Reoviruses. Group IV (+ssRNA): Coronaviruses, Picornaviruses (poliovirus), Togaviruses. Group V (−ssRNA): Orthomyxoviruses (influenza), Rhabdoviruses (rabies), Paramyxoviruses (measles, mumps). Group VI (ssRNA-RT): Retroviruses (HIV). Group VII (dsDNA-RT): Hepadnaviruses (Hepatitis B). Students should know representative viruses for each group.

SARS-CoV-2

SARS-CoV-2, the virus responsible for COVID-19, belongs to family Coronaviridae, genus Betacoronavirus. It has four structural proteins: the spike glycoprotein (S) that mediates host cell entry by binding the ACE2 receptor, the membrane protein (M) that shapes the viral envelope, the envelope protein (E) involved in viral assembly and release, and the nucleocapsid protein (N) that packages the genomic RNA. Understanding these proteins is important because most vaccines target the spike protein.

DNA Virus Disease Associations

Key DNA virus disease associations for exam preparation: Adenoviruses cause upper respiratory infections, conjunctivitis, and gastroenteritis. Herpes simplex virus (HSV) causes cold sores and genital herpes; varicella-zoster virus (also a herpesvirus) causes chickenpox and shingles. Epstein-Barr virus causes infectious mononucleosis and is associated with certain lymphomas. Variola virus causes smallpox (now eradicated). Human papillomavirus (HPV) causes warts and is the primary cause of cervical cancer.

4.5 Virus Evolution and Origin Theories

Three main hypotheses attempt to explain where viruses came from:

  1. Regressive (degeneracy) hypothesis: Viruses were once small cells that became obligate parasites, losing genes for independent life over time.
  2. Escape hypothesis: Viruses originated from fragments of genetic material that "escaped" from host cells (e.g., mobile genetic elements like transposons or plasmids that acquired protein coats).
  3. Virus-first hypothesis: Viruses or virus-like entities arose in the primordial world before cellular life evolved.

The truth may be that different virus lineages originated through different pathways. Viruses evolve extremely rapidly, especially RNA viruses, because their polymerases lack proofreading ability, leading to high mutation rates.

4.6 Retroviruses and Reverse Transcription

Retroviruses are RNA viruses that carry an enzyme called reverse transcriptase. After entering a host cell, reverse transcriptase copies the viral RNA into double-stranded DNA. This DNA is then integrated into the host chromosome by another viral enzyme called integrase, forming a provirus. The provirus can remain dormant or be transcribed by the host's RNA polymerase to produce new viral RNA and viral proteins. HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is the most well-known retrovirus.

4.7 Lytic vs Lysogenic Cycles

Lytic cycle: The virus takes over the host cell immediately. It hijacks the cell's machinery to replicate its genome and produce viral proteins. New virions (virus particles) are assembled, and the host cell is lysed (burst open), releasing hundreds of new viruses to infect other cells. This kills the host cell.

Lysogenic cycle: Instead of immediately replicating, the viral DNA is integrated into the host chromosome (becoming a prophage in bacteria). The prophage is passively replicated along with the host DNA every time the cell divides. The host cell is not immediately harmed and may carry the viral DNA for many generations. However, under stress (UV light, chemicals), the prophage can be excised from the host chromosome and enter the lytic cycle. Bacteriophage lambda is the classic example of a virus that can switch between lytic and lysogenic pathways.

4.8 Virus Release Mechanisms

Viruses exit host cells through two main mechanisms. In the lytic pathway, the host cell membrane ruptures (lysis), releasing hundreds to thousands of new viral particles at once and killing the cell. Alternatively, enveloped viruses can exit by budding (exocytosis), where new virions push through the plasma membrane, acquiring their lipid envelope in the process. Budding may not immediately kill the host cell but causes a cytopathic effect — visible changes in cell morphology and function that can be observed in cell culture.

4.9 Tumor Viruses and Oncogenes

Some viruses can cause cancer. These tumor viruses (or oncoviruses) carry genes called viral oncogenes that, when expressed in host cells, drive uncontrolled cell division. In retroviruses, the oncogene may have been captured from a host cell's normal proto-oncogene (a gene that normally regulates cell growth). When the viral version is expressed without proper regulation, it pushes the cell toward malignancy. Examples include Human Papillomavirus (HPV, which causes cervical cancer) and Hepatitis B and C viruses (which can cause liver cancer). DNA tumor viruses may also inactivate tumor suppressor genes like p53 and Rb.

Viral oncogenesis can occur through distinct mechanisms depending on the virus type. DNA tumor viruses like papillomavirus can accidentally integrate fragments of their genome into host chromosomes, disrupting normal growth control and driving uncontrolled replication. RNA tumor viruses (retroviruses) like Rous sarcoma virus carry captured cellular oncogenes (such as v-src) that, when expressed without normal regulation, force cells into uncontrolled division. The Rous sarcoma virus src gene was the first viral oncogene discovered and provided a key insight into the molecular basis of cancer.

4.10 Prions

Prions are not viruses at all — they contain no nucleic acid. A prion is a misfolded version of a normal protein (PrPC) found on the surface of nerve cells. The misfolded form (PrPSc) can induce normal PrPC proteins to refold into the abnormal shape, causing a chain reaction. The accumulating misfolded proteins form aggregates that damage brain tissue, creating a sponge-like appearance (hence "spongiform encephalopathy"). Prion diseases include Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) in humans, bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE, or "mad cow disease") in cattle, and scrapie in sheep. Prion diseases are always fatal and currently incurable.

The structural difference between normal and pathological prion protein is well characterized. Normal PrPC is predominantly alpha-helical (about 43% alpha-helix with minimal beta-sheet). The misfolded PrPSc form has dramatically increased beta-sheet content (about 43% beta-sheet, only 30% alpha-helix). This conformational shift makes PrPSc resistant to proteases, heat, and standard sterilization methods, which is why prion diseases are so difficult to control.

Prion diseases have diverse transmission routes. Kuru, found among the Fore people of Papua New Guinea, was transmitted through ritual cannibalism and caused progressive loss of coordination and dementia. CJD causes rapidly progressive dementia by destroying the grey matter of the cerebral cortex. Human-to-human transmission has also occurred iatrogenically through contaminated growth hormone preparations and neurosurgical instruments. Some prion diseases are hereditary, caused by autosomal dominant mutations in the PRNP gene. Carleton Gajdusek received the 1976 Nobel Prize for demonstrating kuru's transmissibility, and Stanley Prusiner received the 1997 Nobel Prize for discovering prions.

4.11 HIV and AIDS

HIV is a retrovirus that specifically targets CD4+ T-helper cells of the immune system. The virus binds to the CD4 receptor and a co-receptor (typically CCR5 or CXCR4), fuses with the cell membrane, and releases its RNA and reverse transcriptase into the cell. Reverse transcriptase produces DNA from the viral RNA, which integrase inserts into the host chromosome. The infected cell then produces new viral particles. Over years, HIV destroys enough CD4+ T cells to severely weaken the immune system, leading to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome), characterized by vulnerability to opportunistic infections and certain cancers.

Key Points — Chapter 4

  • Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites consisting of nucleic acid enclosed in a protein capsid, sometimes surrounded by an envelope.
  • Capsid shapes include icosahedral, helical, and complex.
  • Viral genomes can be DNA or RNA, single or double-stranded, positive or negative sense.
  • The lytic cycle kills the host cell; the lysogenic cycle integrates viral DNA into the host genome for later activation.
  • Retroviruses use reverse transcriptase to convert RNA into DNA, which is then integrated into the host chromosome.
  • Prions are infectious misfolded proteins with no nucleic acid, causing fatal neurodegenerative diseases.
  • HIV targets CD4+ T cells, and progressive destruction of these cells leads to AIDS.

Mnemonic — Retrovirus Enzymes

Reverse transcriptase, Integrase, Protease → "RIP" the host cell's normal function.

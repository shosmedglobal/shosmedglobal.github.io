Chapter 14

pH and Buffers

14.1 The Ionic Product of Water

Pure water undergoes a very slight self-ionization:

H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH−

The equilibrium constant for this process at 25 °C is:

Kw = [H⁺][OH−] = 1.0 × 10−14

In pure water, [H⁺] = [OH−] = 1.0 × 10−7 M.

14.2 The pH Scale

The pH scale expresses hydrogen ion concentration in a compact way:

pH = −log[H⁺]

And for hydroxide ion concentration:

pOH = −log[OH−]

The key relationship at 25 °C:

pH + pOH = 14
pH ValueNature[H⁺]Example
0–2Strongly acidic1–0.01 MStomach acid (~1.5)

Worked Example: pH Calculation

Problem: Calculate the pH of 0.001 M HCl.
Step 1: HCl is a strong acid, so [H⁺] = 0.001 M = 10−3 M.
Step 2: pH = −log(10−3) = 3
Answer: pH = 3.

Worked Example: Finding [OH−] from pH

Problem: A solution has pH = 10. What is [OH−]?
Step 1: pOH = 14 − 10 = 4
Step 2: [OH−] = 10−pOH = 10−4 M = 0.0001 M
Answer: [OH−] = 1.0 × 10−4 M.

14.3 Acid Ionization Constant (Kₐ)

For a weak acid HA ⇌ H⁺ + A−, the equilibrium constant is:

Kₐ = [H⁺][A−] / [HA]

A larger Kₐ means a stronger weak acid (more dissociation). A useful numerical threshold: a weak acid has Kₐ < 10−2 (pKₐ > 2), meaning fewer than about 10% of molecules dissociate in dilute solution. The six common strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄) all have extremely large Kₐ values and dissociate essentially completely. For polyprotic acids (like H₂SO₄ or H₃PO₄), each proton has its own Kₐ, with Ka1 > Ka2 > Ka3.

Relationship Between Kₐ, Kb, and Kw

For any conjugate acid-base pair, the acid ionization constant and the base ionization constant are related through the ionic product of water:

Kw = Kₐ × Kb

At 25 °C, Kw = 1.0 × 10−14, so if you know Kₐ for a weak acid, you can find Kb for its conjugate base (and vice versa): Kb = Kw / Kₐ. In logarithmic form: pKₐ + pKb = 14 (at 25 °C). This relationship is essential for calculating the pH of salt solutions formed from weak acids or weak bases.

Simplified pH Formulas for Pure Acid or Base Solutions

For a strong monoprotic acid at concentration cₐ: pH = −log(cₐ) (assumes complete dissociation).

For a weak acid at concentration cₐ with dissociation constant Kₐ:

pH = ½ pKₐ − ½ log(cₐ)

This simplified formula is valid when cₐ is much larger than Kₐ.

For a strong base at concentration cb: pOH = −log(cb), then pH = 14 − pOH.

For a weak base at concentration cb: pOH = ½ pKb − ½ log(cb).

Worked Example: pH of a Weak Acid

Problem: Calculate the pH of 0.20 M acetic acid (pKₐ = 4.74).
Step 1: pH = ½(4.74) − ½ log(0.20) = 2.37 − (−0.35) = 2.37 + 0.35 = 2.72
Answer: pH = 2.72.

Diprotic Acid pH Calculation

Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) is diprotic: the first dissociation is strong, while the second (HSO₄− → H⁺ + SO₄2−) has Ka2 = 1.2 × 10−2. For dilute solutions, both protons are often assumed to dissociate fully. Thus, 0.05 M H₂SO₄ gives [H⁺] ≈ 0.10 M and pH ≈ 1.0. For diprotic bases like Ba(OH)₂, 0.005 M yields [OH−] = 0.010 M, pOH = 2, and pH = 12.

14.4 Buffer Solutions

A buffer is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. Buffers are mixtures of:

  • A weak acid and its conjugate base (e.g., CH₃COOH / CH₃COO−), or
  • A weak base and its conjugate acid (e.g., NH₃ / NH₄⁺)

How buffers work: If acid (H⁺) is added, the conjugate base neutralizes it: A− + H⁺ → HA. If base (OH−) is added, the weak acid neutralizes it: HA + OH− → A− + H₂O.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

pH = pKₐ + log([A−] / [HA])

where pKₐ = −log(Kₐ). When [A−] = [HA], then pH = pKₐ (the buffer is most effective at resisting pH changes at this point).

Worked Example: Henderson-Hasselbalch

Problem: A buffer contains 0.20 M acetic acid (Kₐ = 1.8 × 10−5) and 0.30 M sodium acetate. What is the pH?
Step 1: pKₐ = −log(1.8 × 10−5) = 4.74
Step 2: pH = 4.74 + log(0.30/0.20) = 4.74 + log(1.5) = 4.74 + 0.18 = 4.92
Answer: pH = 4.92.

14.5 Acid-Base Titration

In a titration, a solution of known concentration (titrant) is gradually added to a solution of unknown concentration (analyte) until the reaction is complete. The equivalence point is when the moles of acid exactly equal the moles of base. An indicator is a substance that changes colour near the equivalence point, signalling when to stop.

For strong acid + strong base titrations, the equivalence point pH is 7. For weak acid + strong base, it is above 7. For strong acid + weak base, it is below 7.

Biological importance: Blood is buffered at pH 7.35–7.45 by the carbonic acid/bicarbonate system (H₂CO₃/HCO₃−). Even small deviations from this range can be life-threatening.

Key Points — Chapter 14

  • Kw = [H⁺][OH−] = 10−14 at 25 °C.
  • pH = −log[H⁺]; pOH = −log[OH−]; pH + pOH = 14.
  • pH < 7 is acidic; pH = 7 is neutral; pH > 7 is basic.
  • Kₐ measures the strength of a weak acid. For a conjugate pair: Kw = Kₐ × Kb (i.e., pKₐ + pKb = 14).
  • Buffers = weak acid + conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid).
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKₐ + log([A−]/[HA]).
  • Titration equivalence point: moles of acid = moles of base.

Test Yourself — Chapter 14

1. What is the pH of 0.01 M NaOH?
Show Answer
[OH−] = 0.01 M = 10−2. pOH = 2. pH = 14 − 2 = 12.
2. A buffer is made from 0.10 M NH₃ (Kb = 1.8 × 10−5) and 0.10 M NH₄Cl. Estimate the pH.
Show Answer
pKb = −log(1.8 × 10−5) = 4.74. pOH = pKb + log([NH₄⁺]/[NH₃]) = 4.74 + log(1) = 4.74. pH = 14 − 4.74 = 9.26.
3. During a titration of a weak acid with a strong base, is the pH at the equivalence point equal to 7?
Show Answer
No, the pH at the equivalence point is above 7 (basic). At the equivalence point, only the conjugate base of the weak acid remains in solution, which hydrolyzes water to produce OH− ions, raising the pH above 7.

Chapter 13

Research Ethics and Integrity

13.1 Why Ethics Is Non-Negotiable

Research ethics is not an abstract philosophical topic - it is the set of rules that protects patients, preserves the integrity of science, and safeguards your career. A single ethical violation can result in paper retractions, institutional sanctions, loss of medical licensure, and permanent professional damage. Understanding and following ethical standards is not optional; it is the foundation upon which all credible research is built.

13.2 The ICMJE Authorship Criteria

The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (ICMJE) defines four criteria that must all be satisfied for someone to be listed as an author:

  1. Substantial contributions to the conception or design of the work, or to the acquisition, analysis, or interpretation of data.
  2. Drafting the work or revising it critically for important intellectual content.
  3. Final approval of the version to be published.
  4. Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work, ensuring that questions about accuracy or integrity are appropriately investigated and resolved.

Every person listed as an author must meet all four criteria. Individuals who contributed but do not meet all four (such as a statistician who only ran analyses, or a technician who collected data) should be acknowledged in the Acknowledgments section instead. This is not a flexible guideline - it is the international standard that journals actively enforce.

13.3 The Three Pillars of Research Misconduct

Fabrication

Inventing data or results that do not exist. This includes creating fictional patient records, fabricating statistical outputs, or generating datasets from imagination rather than observation. Fabrication is the most straightforward form of fraud.

Falsification

Manipulating research materials, processes, or data such that the research is no longer accurately represented. This includes selectively deleting data points to change outcomes, altering images to misrepresent findings, or adjusting statistical methods specifically to achieve significance. Falsification is often more subtle than fabrication but equally serious.

Plagiarism

Presenting another person's ideas, methods, results, or text without appropriate attribution. This encompasses copying passages from published papers (even with minor rewording), using someone else's data without permission, and claiming work done by others as your own. Self-plagiarism - reusing substantial portions of your own previously published text without disclosure - is also problematic.

13.4 Conflicts of Interest

A conflict of interest exists when your professional judgment about a primary interest (patient welfare, research validity) could be influenced by a secondary interest (financial gain, career advancement, personal relationships). All authors must disclose potential conflicts at the time of submission, including:

  • Financial relationships with companies relevant to the research
  • Consulting roles or advisory board memberships
  • Stock ownership in relevant companies
  • Non-financial relationships that could be perceived as influential

Disclosure does not prevent publication - it allows readers and editors to evaluate the work with full transparency.

13.5 IRB Requirements for Human Subjects Research

All research involving human subjects requires ethical review. The Institutional Review Board (or equivalent ethics committee) evaluates your protocol to ensure:

  • The research design is scientifically sound
  • Risks to participants are minimized and proportionate to expected benefits
  • Informed consent is obtained when required
  • Privacy and confidentiality protections are adequate
  • Vulnerable populations receive appropriate additional protections

For retrospective chart reviews, a waiver of informed consent is typically granted because the research poses minimal risk and contacting every patient would be impractical. But you still need formal IRB review and approval before accessing any data.

13.6 Data Management and Reproducibility

Good data management is what separates credible research from work that cannot withstand scrutiny.

Secure Storage

Store research data on password-protected, encrypted drives or institutional servers. Never store identifiable patient information on personal laptops, USB drives, or cloud services that are not HIPAA-compliant (or equivalent for your jurisdiction).

Maintaining an Audit Trail

Document every decision about your data: why certain patients were excluded, how missing values were handled, what transformations were applied to variables. This documentation protects you if your work is ever questioned and demonstrates methodological transparency.

Backup Protocol

Follow the 3-2-1 rule: maintain three copies of your data, on two different types of media, with one copy stored off-site or in an institutionally approved cloud service. Data loss from a single hardware failure should never be possible.

Data Retention

Retain raw data for at least five years after publication. Many institutions and journals mandate longer retention periods. Never delete raw data, even after the paper is published.

Version Control for Analysis Code

If you use R, Python, or any programming language for analysis, use version control (Git) to track changes to your code. This allows you to reproduce your analysis exactly and revert to previous versions if needed. GitHub offers free private repositories for students.

Reproducibility Checklist

  • ☐ Raw data preserved in original form and unmodified
  • ☐ All data cleaning steps documented
  • ☐ Statistical code saved and annotated
  • ☐ Analysis can be rerun from raw data to final results
  • ☐ All manuscript versions tracked with dates

13.7 Plagiarism Detection

Before submitting any manuscript, run it through a plagiarism detection tool to catch unintentional text overlap. Free options include Quetext (limited free checks available). If your institution provides access, iThenticate is the industry standard. Be aware that many journals automatically screen submissions with plagiarism detection software.

13.8 The Paper Mill Threat

Paper mills are organizations that manufacture fraudulent scientific papers and sell authorship positions. They target students and early-career researchers who feel pressure to publish quickly. The consequences of involvement include:

  • Permanent, public retraction of the paper
  • Severe damage to your professional reputation
  • Disciplinary action from your institution
  • Potential complications with medical licensing

How to recognize paper mill offers: Unsolicited emails offering guaranteed authorship, requests for payment in exchange for being listed as an author, papers provided to you that you did not help create, and claims that the process is standard practice. The answer to every such offer is always no. There is no shortcut that does not carry career-threatening risk.

Key Takeaways

  • All four ICMJE authorship criteria must be met for every listed author
  • Fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism are career-ending offenses with no path to recovery
  • Always disclose conflicts of interest - transparency protects you
  • IRB approval is mandatory before accessing any human subjects data
  • Maintain rigorous data management practices with secure storage and audit trails
  • Reject all paper mill solicitations without exception

Common Mistakes

  • Adding or removing authors based on institutional hierarchy rather than actual contribution
  • Failing to disclose potential conflicts of interest
  • Collecting data before receiving IRB approval
  • Not running plagiarism detection before submission
  • Engaging with paper mills out of desperation to build a CV quickly

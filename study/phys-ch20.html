Chapter 20

Torque

Definition

Torque (τ) is the rotational equivalent of force. It measures the tendency of a force to cause rotation about an axis:

τ = F · r · sin(θ)

Or in vector form: τ = r x F (cross product)

Where:

  • τ = torque (in N*m)
  • F = applied force (in N)
  • r = distance from the axis of rotation to the point where the force is applied (lever arm)
  • θ = angle between r and F

Units: Newton-meters (N*m). NOTE: Although N*m has the same dimensions as joules, torque is NOT energy. We write the unit as N*m (not J) for torque.

Maximum and Zero Torque

  • Maximum torque when θ = 90° (force perpendicular to lever arm): τ = F · r
  • Zero torque when θ = 0 or 180° (force parallel to lever arm): τ = 0

Equilibrium Condition

For an object in rotational equilibrium (not rotating, or rotating at constant angular velocity):

Sum of all torques = 0

Convention: Choose a pivot point. Torques causing counterclockwise rotation are positive; clockwise torques are negative (or vice versa, as long as you are consistent).

Lever and Seesaw Problems

For a balanced seesaw (or any lever in equilibrium about a fulcrum):

F_1 · d_1 = F_2 · d_2

Where F_1, F_2 are the forces (often weights) and d_1, d_2 are their distances from the fulcrum.

This is the principle of the lever, known since Archimedes.

Moment of Inertia (Basic Concept)

Just as mass resists linear acceleration (F = ma), the moment of inertia (I) resists angular acceleration:

τ = I · α

Where α is the angular acceleration (rad/s2).

The moment of inertia depends on mass AND how that mass is distributed relative to the rotation axis:

ObjectAxisMoment of Inertia
Point mass at distance rThrough centerI = m · r2
Solid disk/cylinderThrough centerI = (1/2) · m · r2
Solid sphereThrough centerI = (2/5) · m · r2
Thin rodThrough centerI = (1/12) · m · L2
Thin rodThrough one endI = (1/3) · m · L2

Worked Example

Problem: A 3 m long uniform beam of mass 10 kg is supported by a fulcrum at its center. A 20 kg weight is placed 0.5 m from the left end. Where should a 30 kg weight be placed to balance the beam?

Solution:

The fulcrum is at the center (1.5 m from either end). The beam's weight acts at the center, so it creates zero torque about the fulcrum.

The 20 kg weight is at 0.5 m from the left end, which is 1.5 - 0.5 = 1.0 m to the left of the fulcrum.

For balance: torque_left = torque_right

20 · 10 · 1.0 = 30 · 10 · d (using W = mg and g cancels if we use mass directly)

20 · 1.0 = 30 · d

d = 20/30 = 0.667 m to the right of the fulcrum

This is at position 1.5 + 0.667 = 2.167 m from the left end.

Check: 200 N · 1.0 m = 200 N*m counterclockwise. 300 N · 0.667 m = 200 N*m clockwise. Balanced.

Exam Tips:
- Torque depends on BOTH force magnitude AND distance from the pivot.
- Choose the pivot point wisely -- putting it at the location of an unknown force eliminates that force from the equation.
- Weight of a uniform beam acts at its center of mass (midpoint for uniform beams).
- τ = 0 for equilibrium. Sum clockwise torques = sum counterclockwise torques.
- Do not confuse torque (N*m) with energy (J), even though they have the same dimensions.

5.1 Periodic Law and Table Organization

The periodic law states that when elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number, their physical and chemical properties repeat in a regular (periodic) pattern. The periodic table organizes elements into rows (periods) and columns (groups).

  • Periods (horizontal rows, 1–7): The period number equals the highest principal energy level occupied by electrons.
  • Groups (vertical columns, 1–18): Elements in the same group share similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons.

5.3 Periodic Trends

Atomic Radius

Across a period (left → right): Atomic radius decreases. More protons pull the electron cloud inward while the shielding (inner electrons) stays the same.

Down a group (top → bottom): Atomic radius increases. New electron shells are added farther from the nucleus.

Ionization Energy

Ionization energy (IE) is the energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom. The trends are opposite to atomic radius: IE increases across a period and decreases down a group. Small atoms hold their electrons more tightly.

Electronegativity

Electronegativity measures how strongly an atom attracts shared electrons in a bond. It increases across a period and decreases down a group. Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element (3.98 on the Pauling scale).

Electron Affinity

Electron affinity is the energy change when an atom gains an electron. Generally becomes more negative (more energy released) from left to right across a period. Halogens have the highest electron affinities.

5.4 The Octet Rule and Ions

Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a stable configuration of eight valence electrons (like the noble gases). Metals lose electrons to form cations (positive ions). Nonmetals gain electrons to form anions (negative ions).

Ion size: Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (lost an electron shell or reduced repulsion). Anions are larger (extra electron increases repulsion in the cloud).

5.5 Biologically Important Elements

The LF3 curriculum specifically categorizes biologically important elements into three tiers:

  • Macroelements (organic matter): H, O, C, N, S, and P make up nearly 99% of body mass by atom count. These six elements - sometimes remembered as CHONPS - form the structural backbone of all biomolecules.
  • Inorganic ions: Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, and Cl⁻ account for about 0.5% of body mass. They are essential for nerve conduction, muscle contraction, and osmotic balance.
  • Trace elements: Fe, Zn, Cu, Co, Mn, I, and Se are required in very small quantities but serve as essential cofactors in enzymes. Iron is at the centre of hemoglobin; iodine is part of thyroid hormones; selenium is found in glutathione peroxidase.

Water in Living Cells

Water (H₂O) constitutes approximately 60–70% of total cell mass, making it the most abundant molecule in living organisms. This explains why hydrogen and oxygen together account for such a large proportion of body weight. Water serves as the universal biological solvent and participates directly in many biochemical reactions (hydrolysis, condensation).

Neutralization Reaction

The periodic table chapter in the LF3 course introduces the neutralization reaction: an acid reacting with a base produces a salt and water. For example, HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. This reaction demonstrates how the strongly corrosive properties of group IA hydroxides are eliminated when they react with acids. Neutralization is a type of double-replacement reaction and is covered in greater depth in Chapter 13.

Mnemonic: Periodic Trends Direction

"IEEN goes up-right" - Ionization Energy, Electronegativity, and Electron affinity all increase going up and to the right. Atomic radius does the opposite - it increases going down and to the left.

5.6 Descriptive Inorganic Chemistry of Important Elements

Beyond periodic trends, understanding the specific chemistry of individual elements is essential for medicine and biochemistry. This section surveys the most important elements by group, focusing on their compounds, reactions, and biological roles.

Hydrogen (H)

Hydrogen is the lightest element and the most abundant in the universe. It exists as a diatomic gas (H₂) and has three isotopes: protium (¹H, 99.98%), deuterium (²H or D), and radioactive tritium (³H or T). Hydrogen forms two major types of binary compounds called hydrides:

  • Ionic hydrides form with highly electropositive metals (e.g., NaH, CaH₂). In these compounds, hydrogen has the unusual oxidation state of −I as the hydride ion H⁻.
  • Covalent hydrides form with more electronegative elements through polar covalent bonds (e.g., HCl, H₂O, NH₃, CH₄).

Hydrogen gas can be prepared in the laboratory by reacting a reactive metal with dilute acid: Zn + 2 HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑. Only compounds in which hydrogen is bonded to a more electronegative atom can function as acids in water.

Ionic (salt-like) hydrides such as NaH and CaH₂ react vigorously with water, releasing hydrogen gas and forming a hydroxide. For example: NaH + H₂O → NaOH + H₂. This reaction is highly exothermic. Because of their strong tendency to donate the H⁻ ion, ionic hydrides are powerful reducing agents and are commonly employed as drying agents in organic chemistry laboratories.

Oxygen (O)

Oxygen is the most abundant element in Earth's crust. Elemental oxygen exists as diatomic O₂ (dioxygen) and as the reactive allotrope ozone (O₃), which absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation in the stratosphere. Oxides are classified by their acid–base behaviour:

  • Basic oxides are metal oxides that react with water to form hydroxides (e.g., Na₂O + H₂O → 2 NaOH; MgO + H₂O → Mg(OH)₂).
  • Acidic oxides are nonmetal oxides that react with water to form oxoacids (e.g., SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄; CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃).
  • Amphoteric oxides react with both acids and bases (e.g., ZnO, Al₂O₃).

Peroxides contain an O–O bond with oxygen in the −I oxidation state. Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) is a familiar oxidising agent that decomposes to water and oxygen: 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂.

Alkali Metals (Group 1: Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs)

The alkali metals all have the electron configuration ns¹ and consistently exhibit oxidation state +I. They are soft, highly reactive metals and powerful reducing agents that react vigorously with water:

2 Na + 2 H₂O → 2 NaOH + H₂↑

Alkali metals must be stored under oil to prevent reaction with moisture and air. Sodium compounds colour a flame yellow; potassium compounds produce a pale violet flame.

Biological roles: Na⁺ is the principal extracellular cation maintaining osmotic pressure, while K⁺ is the principal intracellular cation. Important compounds include NaCl (table salt), NaOH and KOH (strong bases), NaHCO₃ (baking soda), and Na₂CO₃ (soda). Nearly all sodium and potassium salts are ionic and highly water-soluble.

Alkaline Earth Metals (Group 2: Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba)

These metals have the configuration ns² and consistently show oxidation state +II. They are less reactive than alkali metals but still too reactive to occur free in nature.

Calcium is abundant in limestone (CaCO₃) and in bones and teeth as hydroxyapatite, Ca₅(PO₄)₃OH. About 1 kg of calcium is present in the adult human body. Key reactions:

  • CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (thermal decomposition of limestone yields quicklime)
  • CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (quicklime reacts with water to form slaked lime)

Magnesium is the central ion in chlorophyll and an essential cofactor for hundreds of enzymes (including all ATP-dependent reactions). Free Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions in body fluids are critical for muscle contraction, blood clotting, and nerve signalling. Calcium sulfate dihydrate (CaSO₄·2H₂O) is gypsum, used in plaster of Paris.

Nutritional Mineral Supplements: Salts, Oxides, and Chelates

Magnesium is the second most abundant intracellular cation (after potassium) and is delivered to the body through several types of supplement. Knowing which formulas are chemically valid is a frequent exam topic.

Inorganic salts – oxidation state and stoichiometry must match. Mg always exists as Mg²⁺. Pairing it with O²⁻ gives MgO (magnesium oxide, used as an antacid and laxative). Formulas like "MgO₂" or "Mg₂O" are not real compounds – they would require oxidation states that magnesium does not access.

Amino acid chelates ("organic" or "buffered" magnesium). Modern supplements often deliver Mg²⁺ as a chelate with an amino acid – a coordination complex in which the amino acid wraps around the metal ion through both its carboxylate and amino groups. Glycine, the smallest amino acid (H₂N–CH₂–COOH), is the most common ligand. Because Mg²⁺ has a +2 charge, two deprotonated glycinate anions (H₂N–CH₂–COO⁻) are needed to neutralize the charge:

(H₂N–CH₂–COO)₂Mg   - magnesium bisglycinate (Mg-glycinate)

This chelate is well absorbed in the gut and is gentler on the stomach than MgO. Other valid chelates follow the same logic: magnesium citrate, magnesium malate, magnesium aspartate. The single-ligand formula H₂N–CH₂–COOMg is not charge-balanced (one negative ligand cannot neutralize Mg²⁺) and is therefore wrong.

Exam shortcut: for any divalent metal supplement, count the negative charges on the ligand(s). For Mg²⁺ or Ca²⁺ you need a total of −2: either one doubly-charged anion (CO₃²⁻, SO₄²⁻, O²⁻) or two singly-charged ones (two glycinates, two acetates, two chloride ions).

Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Zinc (Zn), and Aluminium (Al)

Iron is an essential trace element that exhibits oxidation states +II (ferrous, Fe²⁺) and +III (ferric, Fe³⁺), with Fe³⁺ being more stable in the presence of oxygen. Iron ions form many coloured complexes, such as the deep-blue hexacyanoferrate(II) complex [Fe(CN)₆]⁴−. Iron is the functional centre of haemoglobin, myoglobin, and cytochromes.

Copper exhibits oxidation states +I and +II. Cu²⁺ ions in aqueous solution form the blue hexaaquacopper(II) complex [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺. Copper is an essential trace element found in cytochrome c oxidase and superoxide dismutase, but is toxic at high doses.

Zinc exhibits only oxidation state +II. Its oxide (ZnO) and hydroxide (Zn(OH)₂) are amphoteric - Zn(OH)₂ dissolves in excess NaOH to form the zincate ion [Zn(OH)₄]²−. Zinc is a constituent of numerous enzymes including carbonic anhydrase and alcohol dehydrogenase.

Aluminium is the third most abundant element in Earth's crust. Despite having a low reduction potential, it resists corrosion due to a thin protective oxide film. Al(OH)₃ is amphoteric: it dissolves in acid to give Al³⁺ and in base to give the aluminate ion [Al(OH)₄]⁻. Aluminium hydroxide is used in antacid medications.

Halogens (Group 17: F, Cl, Br, I)

The halogens have the configuration ns²np⁵ and are among the most electronegative nonmetals. They exist as diatomic molecules: F₂ and Cl₂ are gases, Br₂ is a liquid, and I₂ is a solid. All are strong oxidising agents, with fluorine being the strongest oxidiser of all elements.

Chlorine gas (Cl₂) is widely used as a disinfectant in water treatment. Hydrogen halides (HF, HCl, HBr, HI) form strong acids in water, except HF, which is a weak acid due to the exceptionally strong H–F bond. Chloride (Cl⁻) is the major extracellular anion in mammals.

Chlorine forms oxoacids of increasing strength with increasing oxidation state: hypochlorous acid (HClO), chlorous acid (HClO₂), chloric acid (HClO₃), and perchloric acid (HClO₄). Iodide intake is essential for thyroid hormone (T₃, T₄) biosynthesis.

The Halogen Oxoacid Naming Pattern

The four-tier prefix/suffix system used for chlorine extends to bromine and iodine. The same halogen atom appears with four different oxidation states (+I, +III, +V, +VII), and the name reflects the oxidation state regardless of which halogen is present:

Oxidation state of XPrefix / suffixCl exampleBr exampleI example
+Ihypo-...-ousHClO - hypochlorous acidHBrO - hypobromous acidHIO - hypoiodous acid
+III(no prefix)...-ousHClO₂ - chlorous acidHBrO₂ - bromous acidHIO₂ - iodous acid
+V(no prefix)...-icHClO₃ - chloric acidHBrO₃ - bromic acidHIO₃ - iodic acid
+VIIper-...-icHClO₄ - perchloric acidHBrO₄ - perbromic acidHIO₄ - periodic acid

Two practical consequences of this pattern are important for medicine and biochemistry:

  • Periodic acid (HIO₄) is a strong oxidising agent used in the periodic acid–Schiff (PAS) histological stain. The acid oxidatively cleaves the C–C bond between adjacent –OH groups in carbohydrates (the vicinal diol motif), generating two aldehyde groups that subsequently react with Schiff’s reagent to give a magenta colour. PAS is therefore the classical stain for glycogen, mucins, glycoproteins, and the basement membrane.
  • Within a halogen, acid strength increases with oxidation state of the central atom (HClO < HClO₂ < HClO₃ < HClO₄) because each additional terminal oxygen further stabilises the conjugate base by delocalising the negative charge. The salts of these acids follow the same naming logic with the suffix -ate (e.g., NaIO₄ is sodium periodate).

Sulfur (S) and Its Compounds

Sulfur occurs in elemental form as S₈ rings and in compounds with oxidation states −II, +IV, and +VI. Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) is an extremely toxic gas with a characteristic rotten-egg odour; in water, it behaves as a weak diprotic acid. Most metal sulfides are insoluble and occur as mineral ores (PbS, ZnS, FeS).

Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) is an air pollutant from burning fossil fuels; it dissolves in water to form the weak sulfurous acid (H₂SO₃). Sulfur trioxide (SO₃) reacts with water to form the strong sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), which is also a powerful dehydrating agent. H₂SO₄ forms two series of salts: sulfates (SO₄²−) and hydrogen sulfates (HSO₄⁻). Most sulfates are soluble, with notable exceptions including BaSO₄ (used as a radiographic contrast agent) and CaSO₄.

Nitrogen (N) and Its Compounds

Nitrogen constitutes about 78% of the atmosphere by volume. The very strong triple bond in N₂ makes molecular nitrogen relatively inert at ordinary temperatures. Nitrogen shows oxidation states from −III to +V.

Ammonia (NH₃) is the most important nitrogen hydride - a weak base that forms the ammonium ion in water: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻. Ammonium salts resemble alkali metal salts in their high solubility.

Several nitrogen oxides are biologically or environmentally important: N₂O (nitrous oxide, used as an anaesthetic), NO (nitric oxide, an important signalling molecule in vasodilation), and NO₂ (nitrogen dioxide, a brown toxic gas). Nitrous acid (HNO₂) is weak and unstable; nitric acid (HNO₃) is a strong acid and powerful oxidiser that dissolves most metals except gold and platinum. Nitrates are very soluble but can be reduced to toxic nitrites by bacteria in the gut.

Phosphorus (P) and Its Compounds

Phosphorus occurs mainly as phosphate minerals and is never found free in nature. Its two principal allotropes are white phosphorus (P₄, highly reactive and toxic) and red phosphorus (polymeric, far less reactive). The most common oxidation state is +V.

Phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) is a weak triprotic acid with stepwise dissociation: H₃PO₄ ⇌ H₂PO₄⁻ ⇌ HPO₄²− ⇌ PO₄³−. The H₂PO₄⁻/HPO₄²− pair (pKₐ₂ ≈ 7.2) is an efficient intracellular buffer near physiological pH.

Calcium phosphate (Ca₃(PO₄)₂) is the main mineral component of bones and teeth, present as hydroxyapatite. Phosphate esters are central to biochemistry: sugar phosphates are glycolysis intermediates, nucleotides (ATP, ADP) carry phosphate groups, and phospholipid membranes contain phosphoric acid ester linkages.

Carbon (C) and Silicon (Si) - Inorganic Aspects

Carbon shows oxidation states from −IV to +IV. Carbon monoxide (CO) is produced by incomplete combustion and is highly toxic because it binds to haemoglobin approximately 200–250 times more strongly than oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) dissolves in water to form the weak carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), though most dissolved CO₂ remains as hydrated molecules.

The hydrogen carbonate (bicarbonate) ion HCO₃⁻ is a crucial component of the blood buffer system. Carbonates of alkali metals are soluble and form alkaline solutions; most other carbonates are insoluble. Calcium carbonate dissolves in CO₂-rich water: CaCO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ → Ca(HCO₃)₂, explaining geological erosion and stalactite formation.

Silicon is the second most abundant element in the crust. Silicon dioxide (SiO₂, quartz) is a network covalent solid with an extremely high melting point. Silicates and aluminosilicates make up the bulk of Earth's rocks and minerals.

Noble Gases (Group 18: He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn)

The noble gases have completely filled outer electron shells (1s² for helium; ns²np⁶ for all others), giving them exceptional chemical stability. They are colourless, odourless monoatomic gases and together constitute about 1% of the atmosphere (almost entirely argon).

Although once called "inert gases," several xenon and krypton compounds were synthesised beginning in the 1960s, demonstrating that noble gas chemistry is possible under extreme conditions. Radon, formed by radioactive decay of radium, can accumulate in poorly ventilated buildings and poses a health risk as an alpha emitter. Helium is used in MRI scanner cooling systems (superconducting magnets); argon is used as an inert atmosphere for welding and metallurgy.

Argon is by far the most abundant noble gas in the atmosphere, comprising approximately 0.93% of air by volume. It is produced primarily by the radioactive decay of potassium-40 (⁴⁰K) in crustal rocks. Despite being classified as a noble gas, argon is not rare at all - it is the third most abundant gas in the atmosphere after nitrogen and oxygen.

5.7 Transition Metals and Coordination Compounds

Transition elements (d-block) are metals whose atoms have partially filled d orbitals. They share several distinguishing properties:

  1. Multiple oxidation states - e.g., Fe shows +II and +III; Mn shows +II through +VII; Cr shows +II through +VI.
  2. Coloured ions and compounds - the partial filling of d orbitals allows d–d electronic transitions that absorb visible light. For example, Cu²⁺ solutions are blue, Cr³⁺ solutions are green, and MnO₄⁻ is deep purple.
  3. Formation of coordination (complex) compounds - a central metal ion bonds to surrounding molecules or ions called ligands through coordinate (dative) covalent bonds. In [Fe(CN)₆]³−, Fe³⁺ is the central atom, CN⁻ ions are the ligands, and the coordination number is 6.
  4. High density, hardness, and melting points compared to s-block metals, due to strong metallic bonding involving d electrons.

Many transition metals serve as essential biological trace elements: iron (haemoglobin, cytochromes), cobalt (vitamin B₁₂), zinc (carbonic anhydrase, alcohol dehydrogenase), copper (cytochrome c oxidase), manganese (superoxide dismutase), and chromium (glucose metabolism). Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) is a widely used strong oxidising agent in both the laboratory and clinical settings.

Key Points - Chapter 5

  • Period number = highest energy level; group number relates to valence electrons.
  • Metals (left), metalloids (staircase), nonmetals (right).
  • Atomic radius: increases down a group, decreases across a period.
  • Ionization energy, electronegativity, electron affinity: increase across a period and up a group.
  • Octet rule: atoms tend toward 8 valence electrons.
  • Cations are smaller than parent atoms; anions are larger.
  • Biologically critical elements include C, H, O, N, P, S, Ca, Na, K, Fe, Zn.
  • Oxides are classified as basic (metal), acidic (nonmetal), or amphoteric (ZnO, Al₂O₃).
  • Alkali metals: ns¹, oxidation state +I, highly reactive; Na⁺ extracellular, K⁺ intracellular.
  • Transition metals: multiple oxidation states, coloured ions, form coordination compounds.
  • Noble gases: filled outer shells, chemically stable; Rn is a radioactive health hazard.

Test Yourself - Chapter 5

1. Arrange the following in order of increasing atomic radius: Na, Mg, K.
Show Answer
Mg < Na < K. Mg is right of Na in the same period (smaller). K is below Na in the same group (larger).
2. Which has a higher ionization energy: nitrogen or oxygen?
Show Answer
Nitrogen has a slightly higher first ionization energy than oxygen. This is because nitrogen has a half-filled 2p subshell (2p³), which is a particularly stable configuration. Removing an electron from this stable arrangement requires extra energy.
3. Why is the Na⁺ ion smaller than a neutral Na atom?
Show Answer
Na has the configuration [Ne]3s¹. When it loses the 3s electron to form Na⁺, it goes to the [Ne] configuration, losing the entire third shell. The remaining electrons are also pulled closer by the unchanged nuclear charge, making the ion significantly smaller.

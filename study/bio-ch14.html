Chapter 14

Cytogenetics in Humans

Cytogenetics is the study of chromosomes and their role in heredity. It bridges cell biology and genetics by examining chromosome number, structure, and behavior.

14.1 Chromosome Structure

Each chromosome consists of a single, very long DNA molecule tightly packaged with proteins called histones. Key structural features include:

  • Centromere: The constricted region where sister chromatids are joined and where the kinetochore forms (attachment point for spindle fibers during cell division).
  • Short arm (p arm): The arm above the centromere. "p" stands for "petit" (French for small).
  • Long arm (q arm): The arm below the centromere. "q" simply follows "p" in the alphabet.
  • Telomeres: Repetitive DNA sequences (TTAGGG in humans) at the ends of chromosomes that protect against degradation and fusion with neighboring chromosomes. Telomeres shorten with each cell division.

14.2 Chromosome Classification by Centromere Position

Type Centromere Position Arm Ratio Shape
Metacentric Middle p and q arms roughly equal V-shaped during anaphase
Submetacentric Slightly off center p arm slightly shorter than q L-shaped or J-shaped
Acrocentric Near one end Very short p arm, long q arm Looks like a drumstick
Telocentric At the very end Only q arm visible I-shaped (not found in normal human chromosomes)

In humans, chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22 are acrocentric. They have small p arms that contain ribosomal RNA genes and satellite DNA. This is clinically important because acrocentric chromosomes can undergo Robertsonian translocations (Chapter 16).

14.3 The Human Karyotype

A karyotype is an organized display of an individual's chromosomes, arranged by size and centromere position. The normal human karyotype consists of:

  • 46 chromosomes total (2n = 46), organized as 23 pairs.
  • 22 pairs of autosomes (numbered 1-22, from largest to smallest approximately).
  • 1 pair of sex chromosomes: XX in females, XY in males.

The 22 autosome pairs are sometimes called homologous chromosomes because both members of each pair are structurally identical in size and gene content. The sex chromosomes (X and Y), by contrast, differ substantially in size and the genes they carry and are therefore termed heteromorphic (or, in some European traditions, "heterologous"). Understanding this terminology distinction can be helpful when reading different textbooks.

A karyotype is typically prepared by arresting cells in metaphase (when chromosomes are most condensed and visible), staining them with Giemsa stain (producing G-banding patterns), and photographing them.

14.4 Cell Cycle and Gamete Maturation

The cell cycle consists of interphase (G₁, S, G₂) and M phase (mitosis + cytokinesis). For reproduction, diploid germ cells undergo meiosis to produce haploid gametes:

  • Spermatogenesis: One diploid spermatogonium produces four functional haploid spermatozoa. This process is continuous from puberty onward.
  • Oogenesis: One diploid oogonium produces one functional haploid ovum and two or three polar bodies. Oogenesis begins before birth (oocytes are arrested in prophase I until puberty), and is completed only upon fertilization.

14.5 Sex Determination Systems

Different organisms use different mechanisms to determine sex:

System How It Works Examples
XX/XY XX = female, XY = male. The Y chromosome carries the SRY gene that triggers male development. The father determines the sex of offspring. Mammals (including humans), many insects
ZW/ZZ ZW = female, ZZ = male. The mother determines sex. Note the reversal compared to XX/XY. Birds, some reptiles, some fish, butterflies
X0 XX = female, X0 = male (only one sex chromosome). There is no Y chromosome. Some insects (grasshoppers)
Haplodiploidy Diploid (2n) = female, haploid (n) = male. Males develop from unfertilized eggs. Bees, wasps, ants

14.6 Barr Bodies and X-Inactivation

Since females (XX) have two X chromosomes and males (XY) have only one, a mechanism exists to equalize X-linked gene dosage between the sexes. This is called X-inactivation (also known as lyonization, after Mary Lyon who proposed it).

In every cell of a female, one of the two X chromosomes is randomly inactivated early in embryonic development. The inactivated X condenses into a dense, dark-staining structure called a Barr body, visible at the edge of the nucleus.

Key points about X-inactivation:

  • The choice of which X to inactivate is random in each cell but permanent for all daughter cells.
  • This means females are genetic mosaics: some cells express one X, other cells express the other.
  • A visible example is the calico cat: patches of orange and black fur result from random X-inactivation of different color alleles in different skin cells.
  • Number of Barr bodies = number of X chromosomes minus 1. A normal female (XX) has 1 Barr body. A normal male (XY) has 0. An individual with XXX has 2.
KEY POINTS - Chapter 14
  • Chromosomes have centromeres, p (short) and q (long) arms, and telomeres.
  • Classification by centromere: metacentric, submetacentric, acrocentric, telocentric.
  • Normal human karyotype: 46 chromosomes (44 autosomes + 2 sex chromosomes).
  • Spermatogenesis yields 4 sperm; oogenesis yields 1 ovum + polar bodies.
  • XX/XY (mammals), ZW/ZZ (birds), X0 (some insects), haplodiploidy (bees/ants).
  • X-inactivation creates Barr bodies; females are genetic mosaics.
  • Number of Barr bodies = number of X chromosomes minus 1.

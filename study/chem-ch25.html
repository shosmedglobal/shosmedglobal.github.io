Chapter 25

Carboxylic Acids

Structure and Naming

Carboxylic acids contain the carboxyl group (–COOH), which is a carbonyl (C=O) and a hydroxyl (–OH) on the same carbon. IUPAC name: longest chain including the COOH carbon + suffix "-oic acid." They are the most oxidized single-carbon functional group (after CO₂).

Key Terminology

Acyl group
R–CO– (the fragment remaining when the –OH is removed from a carboxylic acid). Acyl groups are named by replacing the "-ic acid" ending with "-yl" (or "-oyl" in systematic IUPAC nomenclature). The term "acylation" refers to the transfer of an acyl group to a substrate.
Carboxylate anion
R–COO− (formed when the acid loses a proton). Stabilized by resonance between the two oxygens.
Alpha-carbon (α-C)
The carbon directly adjacent to the carboxyl group.
Omega-carbon (ω-C)
The carbon at the far end of the chain (opposite the carboxyl group).

Acyl Group Names

Acyl groups appear throughout biochemistry - for example, the acetyl group (CH₃–CO–) in acetyl-CoA is central to energy metabolism, and palmitoyl and oleoyl groups are the acyl chains esterified in membrane phospholipids. Adipic acid (hexanedioic acid) is industrially important as a monomer for nylon-6,6 production. It completes the series of commonly tested saturated dicarboxylic acids: oxalic (C2), malonic (C3), succinic (C4), glutaric (C5), adipic (C6).

Aromatic Dicarboxylic Acids

Phthalic acid (benzene-1,2-dicarboxylic acid) is an important aromatic dicarboxylic acid whose anhydride is used in plasticizer and dye production. Its positional isomers include isophthalic acid (1,3-) and terephthalic acid (1,4-), the latter being a monomer for PET (polyethylene terephthalate) plastic used in drink bottles and synthetic fibres.

Fumaric acid and maleic acid are geometric isomers of butenedioic acid (C4 unsaturated dicarboxylic acid). Fumaric acid is the trans isomer (more stable); maleic acid is the cis isomer. Fumaric acid is a key intermediate in the citric acid cycle.

Fatty Acids

Fatty acids are long-chain monocarboxylic acids, typically with an even number of carbons (built from 2-carbon acetyl-CoA units). They are classified as:

  • Saturated: No double bonds (palmitic C16:0, stearic C18:0). Straight chains pack tightly; solid at room temperature (fats).
  • Monounsaturated: One double bond. Oleic acid (C18:1, Δ9) - found in olive oil.
  • Polyunsaturated: Multiple double bonds. Linoleic acid (C18:2, ω-6), linolenic acid (C18:3, ω-3), arachidonic acid (C20:4, ω-6).

Essential fatty acids: Linoleic and linolenic acids cannot be synthesized by the human body and must be obtained from the diet.

Omega (n-) Notation

In fatty acid nomenclature, the α carbon is the carbon adjacent to the carboxyl group (C2), while the omega carbon is the terminal methyl carbon. The omega (ω) or n- notation counts the position of the first double bond from the methyl end: oleic acid is an omega-9 (n-9) fatty acid because its double bond is 9 carbons from the omega end. This system classifies essential fatty acid families: the omega-6 family (starting from linoleic acid) and the omega-3 family (starting from alpha-linolenic acid).

Additional Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids

Beyond the basic essential fatty acids, the LF3 lectures specifically cover: gamma-linolenic acid (GLA, C18:3, n-6, double bonds at positions 6,9,12), eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, C20:5, n-3), and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, C22:6, n-3). EPA and DHA, found abundantly in fish oil, have anti-inflammatory properties. Arachidonic acid (C20:4, n-6) is the precursor to prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes - lipid mediators of inflammation and blood clotting.

Benzoic acid (C₆H₅COOH) is the simplest aromatic carboxylic acid; used as a food preservative.

Key Points - Chapter 25

  • Carboxylic acids (–COOH): acidic because carboxylate anion is resonance-stabilized.
  • Know common names: formic (C1), acetic (C2), propionic (C3), butyric (C4), palmitic (C16), stearic (C18).
  • Dicarboxylic: oxalic (C2), malonic (C3), succinic (C4), glutaric (C5).
  • Fumaric (trans) and maleic (cis) are geometric isomers of butenedioic acid.
  • Essential fatty acids (linoleic, linolenic) must come from diet.

Muscular System

Skeletal Muscle Structure

Skeletal muscles are the organs of movement. Each muscle is wrapped in layers of connective tissue:

  • Epimysium: The outermost layer, surrounding the entire muscle.
  • Perimysium: Surrounds bundles of muscle fibers called fascicles.
  • Endomysium: Surrounds each individual muscle fiber (cell).

A single muscle fiber (cell) is a long, cylindrical, multinucleated cell. Inside each fiber are hundreds to thousands of myofibrils - the actual contractile elements. Each myofibril is made of repeating units called sarcomeres, which are the basic functional units of contraction.

The Sarcomere

The sarcomere is the segment of a myofibril between two Z-lines (also called Z-discs). It contains two types of protein filaments:

  • Thick filaments: Made of the protein myosin. Each myosin molecule has a head that can bind to actin and hydrolyze ATP.
  • Thin filaments: Made primarily of the protein actin, along with two regulatory proteins: tropomyosin (covers the myosin-binding sites on actin when the muscle is relaxed) and troponin (binds calcium, triggering the movement of tropomyosin to expose binding sites).

The visible banding pattern arises from the arrangement of these filaments: the A band (dark) is where thick filaments are present, the I band (light) contains only thin filaments, and the H zone is the central region of the A band with only thick filaments. The M line is at the very center of the sarcomere.

The Sliding Filament Theory of Contraction

Muscles shorten not because the filaments themselves get shorter, but because the thin filaments slide past the thick filaments, pulling the Z-lines closer together. Here is the step-by-step process:

  1. Nerve signal arrives: A motor neuron releases the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh) at the neuromuscular junction.
  2. Action potential spreads: ACh causes an electrical signal to travel along the muscle fiber membrane (sarcolemma) and down into the fiber through T-tubules.
  3. Calcium release: The signal triggers the sarcoplasmic reticulum (a specialized smooth endoplasmic reticulum in muscle cells) to release stored calcium ions (Ca²⁺) into the cytoplasm (sarcoplasm).
  4. Troponin-tropomyosin shift: Ca²⁺ binds to troponin, which changes shape and moves tropomyosin away from the binding sites on actin. The myosin-binding sites are now exposed.
  5. Cross-bridge cycling: Myosin heads bind to actin (forming cross-bridges), pull the thin filaments toward the center of the sarcomere (the power stroke), then release and re-cock. Each cycle uses one molecule of ATP.
  6. Relaxation: When the nerve signal stops, Ca²⁺ is actively pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Tropomyosin re-covers the binding sites, and the muscle relaxes.

Clinical Connection: Rigor Mortis

After death, cells can no longer produce ATP. Without ATP, myosin heads cannot detach from actin. This causes all muscles to become stiff and locked - a condition known as rigor mortis. It begins a few hours after death and eventually resolves as muscle proteins degrade.

Major Muscle Groups

Head and Neck

  • Masseter and temporalis: Muscles of mastication (chewing); close the jaw.
  • Sternocleidomastoid: A prominent neck muscle that tilts and rotates the head.

Trunk (Anterior)

  • Pectoralis major: Large chest muscle; flexes, adducts, and medially rotates the arm.
  • Rectus abdominis: The "six-pack" muscle; flexes the trunk (bending forward).
  • External and internal obliques: Lateral abdominal muscles; rotate and laterally flex the trunk.
  • Diaphragm: The primary muscle of breathing; separates the thoracic and abdominal cavities.
  • Intercostal muscles: Between the ribs; assist in breathing by expanding and compressing the rib cage.

Back

  • Trapezius: Large diamond-shaped muscle of the upper back; moves the scapula (shoulder blade).
  • Latissimus dorsi: Wide muscle of the lower back; extends, adducts, and medially rotates the arm (used in pull-ups).
  • Erector spinae: A group of muscles running along the spine; maintains upright posture and extends the vertebral column.

Upper Limbs

  • Deltoid: Caps the shoulder; abducts the arm.
  • Biceps brachii: Front of upper arm; flexes the elbow and supinates the forearm.
  • Triceps brachii: Back of upper arm; extends the elbow.
  • Flexors and extensors of the forearm: Control wrist and finger movements.

Lower Limbs

  • Gluteus maximus: The largest muscle in the body; extends the hip (standing up from sitting, climbing stairs).
  • Quadriceps femoris: Four muscles on the front of the thigh (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius); extend the knee.
  • Hamstrings: Three muscles on the back of the thigh (biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus); flex the knee and extend the hip.
  • Gastrocnemius and soleus: Calf muscles; plantar flex the foot (pointing toes downward). Together they form the triceps surae and connect to the Achilles tendon.
  • Tibialis anterior: Front of the shin; dorsiflexes the foot (pulling toes upward).

Common Muscular Disorders

  • Muscle strain: Overstretching or tearing of muscle fibers, commonly from overuse or improper use.
  • Muscular dystrophy: A group of genetic diseases characterized by progressive muscle weakness and degeneration. The most common form (Duchenne) is caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene, which is X-linked.
  • Myasthenia gravis: An autoimmune disease where antibodies attack acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, causing progressive muscle weakness and fatigue.
  • Cramps: Sudden, involuntary, painful muscle contractions often caused by dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, or overexertion.
  • Tendinitis: Inflammation of a tendon, often from repetitive motion (tennis elbow, for example).

Mnemonic: Sarcomere Bands - "I-Z A-M-H"

From one end of a sarcomere to its center: Z-line → I band (light, thin filaments only) → A band (dark, thick filaments; overlaps with thin) → H zone (thick only) → M-line (middle).

Tip: During contraction, the I band and H zone shrink, but the A band stays the same width (because thick filaments do not change length).

Key Points - Chapter 24

  • Muscle hierarchy: muscle → fascicle → muscle fiber (cell) → myofibril → sarcomere.
  • The sarcomere is the functional unit. Thick filaments = myosin; thin filaments = actin + troponin + tropomyosin.
  • Sliding filament theory: nerve signal → Ca²⁺ release → troponin shifts tropomyosin → cross-bridge cycling (ATP-driven) → filaments slide → muscle shortens.
  • During contraction, I band and H zone shorten; A band stays constant.
  • Skeletal muscle = voluntary, striated, fast fatigue. Cardiac = involuntary, striated, resistant to fatigue. Smooth = involuntary, non-striated, slow and sustained.
  • Duchenne muscular dystrophy is an X-linked genetic disorder. Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder affecting neuromuscular junctions.

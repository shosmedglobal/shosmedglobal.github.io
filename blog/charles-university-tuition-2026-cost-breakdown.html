Charles University Tuition and Total Cost in 2026: A Real Breakdown

By Eli Zolotov, MD · April 30, 2026 · 7 min read

The single most common question I get from prospective Charles University LF3 applicants and their parents is some version of "what does it actually cost?" The answer is genuinely good news, but it gets buried under marketing copy that quotes only the tuition figure and skips everything else. Here is a real, line-by-line breakdown of what six years of medical school in Prague will cost you in 2026, with the assumptions stated openly so you can adjust the numbers to your own situation.

All figures are in US dollars at current exchange rates (around 23 CZK per USD). I have rounded conservatively in your direction, meaning the actual cost is likely to come in slightly under these estimates rather than over.

Tuition: ~$18,500 Per Year

Charles University Third Faculty of Medicine sets tuition for the English-taught General Medicine program at approximately $18,500 per academic year. This is the figure on the official admissions page, payable in installments by arrangement with the Foreign Department.

For the full 6-year program: about $111,000 in tuition total.

Charles University does not run a sticker-price-versus-discount model the way US private schools do. There is no separate "in-state" rate, no merit aid bidding war, and no need-based financial aid for international students. The number is the number. Once you are admitted, you know exactly what tuition will cost you for the entire program (subject to occasional adjustments by the university, which historically have been modest).

Housing: $400 to $700 Per Month

Prague is one of the most affordable major European capitals for students. The wide range here reflects two real choices:

  • University dormitories ($300 to $500 per month): Charles University operates several student dorms within walking distance or a short tram ride of the LF3 campus in Vinohrady. Rooms are typically shared, and amenities are basic but functional. Many first-year international students start here.
  • Shared private apartment ($500 to $800 per month per person): Most students move into shared apartments with classmates by their second or third year. Vinohrady, Vrsovice, and Zizkov are popular student neighborhoods, all within reasonable distance of campus.
  • Studio apartment ($800 to $1,200 per month): If you want to live alone in a desirable area, this is the price band. Most students do not need this.

For a realistic working assumption: $550 per month average across six years, or about $40,000 total. First and second years often run lower (dorms or roommates); later years often run higher (more privacy, better neighborhoods). It tends to even out.

Food: $300 to $400 Per Month

Groceries in Prague cost noticeably less than in major US cities. The university cafeterias offer subsidized meals to students at very low prices (often $3 to $5 for a full lunch). Eating most meals at home with occasional restaurants and cafe stops puts most students in the $300 to $400 per month range.

6-year food estimate: about $25,000.

Transportation: $150 Per Year (Yes, Per Year)

This is one of the most underrated parts of student life in Prague. The Prague Public Transit System (DPP) sells annual passes to students under 26 for around $150 per year, giving you unlimited access to the metro, trams, and buses across the entire city.

You will not need a car. You will not pay for ride-shares or parking. The trains and buses are clean, frequent, and run late.

6-year transit total: about $900.

Books, Equipment, and Course Materials: $300 to $500 Per Year

Most core medical textbooks are available in the university library, and many students use shared digital resources like Anki decks, USMLE-aligned QBanks, and online lecture series rather than buying every textbook outright. You will need a stethoscope, a basic clinical kit during clinical years, and the occasional required text.

6-year books and equipment estimate: about $2,500.

Visa, Health Insurance, and Administrative Fees

Non-EU students need a long-term student visa, which carries a visa fee plus health insurance covering the duration of your stay. Insurance for international students typically runs $400 to $700 per year. Add document translations, residence permit renewals, and miscellaneous administrative fees. For the full step-by-step on the visa process, see Czech Student Visa for US and Canadian Medical Students.

6-year admin and insurance total: about $4,500.

USMLE and Application Fees (If You Plan to Match in the US)

If your goal is US residency (and for most of our students, it is), you will pay for USMLE Step 1, USMLE Step 2 CK, OET Medicine (or equivalent), ECFMG certification, ERAS application fees, and travel for residency interviews. These are not Charles University costs, but they are real medical-career costs that should be in your budget.

Realistic total for USMLE pathway: $4,000 to $6,000 across the program.

Travel Home and Personal Spending

Most international students fly home for the longer breaks. From Prague, round-trip flights to North America typically run $600 to $1,200 depending on season and how far in advance you book. Add a reasonable allowance for personal spending, weekend trips, social life, clothes, and the occasional unexpected expense.

Realistic personal allowance: about $200 to $400 per month, plus 1 to 2 trips home per year.

The 6-Year Total

Adding everything up for a realistic mid-range scenario:

  • Tuition: ~$111,000
  • Housing: ~$40,000
  • Food: ~$25,000
  • Transit: ~$900
  • Books and equipment: ~$2,500
  • Visa, insurance, admin: ~$4,500
  • USMLE pathway costs: ~$5,000
  • Travel and personal: ~$22,000

Total 6-year cost: roughly $210,000 to $215,000.

If you live frugally and minimize travel home, the lower bound is closer to $185,000. If you live more comfortably with a private apartment and frequent travel, the upper bound is closer to $240,000.

How This Compares to the US Path

For perspective, the average cost of a US medical degree, including the four years of undergraduate education that precede it, runs about $250,000 to $400,000 in tuition and living expenses depending on whether you attend public or private institutions. That is before residency. That is also before student loan interest accrues during your training years.

So the Charles University path is meaningfully less expensive than the typical US path. It is comparable to or somewhat cheaper than most Caribbean medical school options when you include the four pre-med undergraduate years that the Charles path skips entirely.

The financial difference compounds beyond the sticker price. Two years earlier in the workforce as a physician, with less debt to service, changes the trajectory of your career and your life in ways that are hard to quantify but easy to feel.

What This Post Did Not Cover

This is a budget for a typical international student. It does not cover:

  • Foreign exchange risk if you are funding from outside the Eurozone or US dollar zone
  • Currency fluctuation across six years
  • Significant lifestyle upgrades or luxuries
  • The opportunity cost of being in Prague rather than working full-time at home
  • Specific scholarship opportunities that occasionally open up for international students at Charles University (worth checking with the admissions office directly)

Treat the numbers in this post as a thoughtful starting estimate, not as a contract. Your actual costs will depend on your choices, the exchange rate, and the year. But if your family is trying to figure out whether this path is financially feasible, the answer for most middle-class US, Canadian, and Western European families is: yes, considerably more so than the US-based alternatives.

Practical Next Steps

  1. Confirm current tuition directly with the Charles University Foreign Department before you finalize your budget. Numbers can shift.
  2. Decide whether you will live in dorms (year 1) or move directly into shared apartments. This is the single biggest variable in your housing cost.
  3. Build a 6-year budget spreadsheet using the line items above. Adjust each row to your situation.
  4. If currency exposure is a concern, talk to your family about how to hedge or stagger conversions.
  5. If you want help thinking through any of this, the SHOS Med team has been through it. Our consultations are free and we will answer cost questions in detail.

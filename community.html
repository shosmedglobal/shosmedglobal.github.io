Entrance Exams

What subjects are on the Charles University medical school entrance exam, and how hard is it compared to the MCAT?

I'm a pre-med student in the US considering applying to LF3 at Charles University. I've been studying for the MCAT but I'm wondering - what's actually on the Czech entrance exam? Is it harder or easier than the MCAT? How should I prepare?

So the exam is biology and chemistry only - no physics, no math, just those two. Multiple-choice format. The level is roughly what you'd see at the end of a solid high school science track, maybe a bit above.

Honestly, it's hard to compare it directly to the MCAT. They're just different beasts. The MCAT throws passages at you with critical thinking, psych, sociology - it's designed to mess with your head even if you know the science. The Czech exam is way more straightforward - if you know the material, you'll get it right. Less mind games, more "do you actually know this stuff."

That said, don't sleep on it. The bio goes pretty deep - genetics, molecular bio, physiology, ecology. Chemistry covers general, organic, and some biochemistry. It's not something you can wing.

If you've been doing MCAT prep, you're honestly in a great spot already. Just make sure you brush up on topics the MCAT doesn't really cover, like taxonomy or detailed ecology. And get used to the format - it's different from what you're used to.

Residency Match

Can international medical graduates (IMGs) from European schools match into competitive US residencies?

I'm currently a student at a European medical school and want to do my residency in the US. Is it realistic for IMGs from Europe to match into competitive specialties, or should I only aim for primary care? What's the match rate like?

100% yes. But here's the thing - you have to start thinking about it early, not in your last year when everyone's panicking. I went through this myself - matched into Internal Medicine, now doing a Hem/Onc fellowship.

The stuff that actually matters: your Step 2 CK score (Step 1 is pass/fail now so just pass it), US clinical rotations (programs want to see you can work in the American system), research (even one publication helps), and letters from US attendings. That's basically your checklist.

And yeah, competitive specialties are doable. I know European grads who matched derm, radiology, surgery. It's harder than for US grads, no question - but it's not some impossible thing. You just need to be more intentional about it.

Biggest thing I'd tell you: don't wait until after graduation to figure this stuff out. Start USMLE prep during preclinical years, line up your US rotations by 5th year. The people who don't match are usually the ones who started too late.

Med School Life

What is it really like studying medicine in English in Prague? Is the quality comparable to US medical schools?

I got accepted to an English-language medical program in Prague. My family is worried the education won't be as good as a US medical school. Can anyone who went through it share their honest experience?

OK so I'll be straight with you. The preclinical years are legit. Real cadaver dissections, deep physiology and biochemistry - honestly comparable to what my colleagues in US schools were learning. Some topics we actually went deeper on.

Where it gets different is the clinical years. US schools have a really structured clerkship system where you're basically part of the team. In Prague, a lot depends on you being proactive - showing up, asking to do stuff, finding English-speaking attendings. It's not handed to you.

Your family's concern is fair, but it's kind of an outdated worry. Charles University has been around since 1348, ECFMG certifies the degree, and US programs accept it. Grads from there are practicing all over the world.

Here's what I'd say though - the school gives you the medical knowledge, no question. But if you want to end up in the US, you need to take care of the USMLE prep and US rotations yourself. Nobody's going to do that for you. That's just the reality for any IMG, not a knock on the school.

USMLE & Boards

When should I start studying for USMLE Step 1 if I'm at a European medical school?

I just started my first year at a 6-year European medical program. When is the right time to begin USMLE prep? I don't want to start too late but I also don't want to burn out.

You're in year 1, so relax - you've got time. But it's good you're thinking about it now. Here's how I'd break it down for a 6-year program:

Years 1-3: Just focus on your school stuff. Seriously. The overlap between your European curriculum and Step 1 is huge - physiology, biochem, pathology, pharm - you're learning Step 1 material without even realizing it. Maybe grab a copy of First Aid and flip through it to see how things map. But don't stress about dedicated USMLE studying yet.

Years 3-4: This is when you start actually doing USMLE questions. Get a QBank, do like 20-40 questions a day alongside school. You'll quickly figure out what you're solid on and what needs work.

Years 4-5: Take Step 1. It's pass/fail now so the pressure is way less than it used to be. But still - take it seriously, because the study habits you build here carry straight into Step 2 CK, and that one IS scored.

The one thing I'd really drill into you: don't wait until after you graduate. I've seen people do that and it's brutal - you forget so much basic science and end up basically starting from scratch. Slow and steady during school beats a panicked cram after graduation every time.

How much does it cost to study medicine at Charles University compared to US and Caribbean medical schools?

I'm comparing options for medical school and trying to understand the real cost. How does Charles University tuition compare to Caribbean schools and US medical schools? Are there hidden costs?

OK so the numbers are pretty wild when you compare them side by side:

Charles University: tuition is 420,000 CZK/year, which is about $18,500. Over 6 years that's around $111,000 total when you factor in living expenses. Tuition alone is closer to $90,000 - Prague is just really affordable.

Caribbean schools: usually $20,000-$30,000 per semester. Full program runs $200,000-$350,000. And honestly, the attrition rates at some of those schools are scary - 30-50% of students don't make it to graduation.

US medical schools: $40,000-$60,000/year, so $200,000-$350,000 total. And that's after you already paid for 4 years of undergrad.

Living in Prague is also way cheaper than most US cities. You can get by on $800-$1,200/month for rent, food, and getting around. Try doing that in New York or Boston.

Stuff people forget to budget for: USMLE exams ($600-$1,000 each step), flights and housing for US rotations, and ERAS application fees plus interview travel. That's probably another $5,000-$10,000 in your last couple years. Still way less than the alternatives though.

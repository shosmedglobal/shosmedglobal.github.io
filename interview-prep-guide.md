# Charles University – Oral Interview Preparation Guide

## Overview

The entrance exam for the Third Faculty of Medicine at Charles University consists of two parts:

1. **Written exam** – 60 multiple-choice questions (30 Biology + 30 Chemistry), taken electronically
2. **Oral interview** – 10 minutes total, scored 12–62 points

This guide focuses on preparing you for the **oral interview**, which can make or break your admission.

---

## Interview Structure

The oral interview has two components:

### Part 1: Text Analysis
You will be given a popular-scientific text to read and then discuss. Evaluators assess:

| Criteria | Points | What They're Looking For |
|----------|--------|--------------------------|
| Amount of information | 1–5 | Did you identify all key points? Can you recall specific details? |
| Concise, logical presentation | 1–5 | Can you summarize clearly and in order? Do you stay on topic? |
| Synthesis and integration | 1–5 | Can you connect the text to broader scientific knowledge? |
| Discussion ability | 1–5 | Can you answer follow-up questions and think on your feet? |
| Culture of speech | 1–5 | Is your English clear, professional, and well-structured? |

### Part 2: Interview Circuit
A broader evaluation of you as a person and future medical student:

| Criteria | Points | What They're Looking For |
|----------|--------|--------------------------|
| Confidence, directness, openness | 1–5 | Are you calm and honest? Do you make eye contact? |
| Interpersonal sensitivity | 1–5 | Are you empathetic? Do you listen well? |
| Task orientation | 1–5 | Are you focused and goal-driven? |
| Tolerance for ambiguity | 1–5 | Can you handle unclear or complex situations without panicking? |
| Way of thinking | 1–5 | Do you think critically and logically? |
| Interest and motivation to study | 1–5 | Why medicine? Why Prague? Is your motivation genuine? |
| Evaluator recommendation | 1–7 | Overall impression — this is the evaluator's personal assessment |

**Total possible score: 12 (minimum) to 62 (maximum)**

## How to Prepare for the Text Analysis

### What to Expect
- You will receive a **popular-scientific article** (not a textbook passage — think Scientific American, New Scientist, or Nature News level)
- Topics can range across biology, medicine, health, environment, or technology
- You have a few minutes to read, then must **discuss it aloud**

### Preparation Strategy

1. **Practice reading scientific articles daily**
   - Read 1–2 articles per day from sources like: Scientific American, Nature News, BBC Science, New Scientist
   - After reading, practice summarizing the article aloud in 2–3 minutes
   - Focus on: What is the main finding? Why does it matter? How does it connect to medicine?

2. **Build a summary framework**
   Use this structure every time you discuss a text:
   - **Topic sentence**: "This article discusses..."
   - **Key findings**: "The main finding is that..."
   - **Mechanism/Explanation**: "This works because..."
   - **Significance**: "This is important because..."
   - **Connection to medicine**: "This relates to clinical medicine because..."

3. **Practice integration**
   - After summarizing, ask yourself: "What do I already know about this topic from biology or chemistry?"
   - The evaluators want to see you connect new information to your existing knowledge
   - Example: An article about CRISPR → connect to DNA structure, gene mutations, genetic disorders

4. **Improve your speaking skills**
   - Record yourself summarizing articles and listen back
   - Time yourself — aim for clear, concise summaries under 3 minutes
   - Eliminate filler words ("um," "like," "you know")
   - Practice with a friend or mentor who can ask follow-up questions

### Common Follow-Up Questions
- "Can you explain the mechanism described in the text?"
- "What are the potential clinical applications?"
- "What are the limitations of this study?"
- "How does this relate to [biology/chemistry topic]?"
- "What further research would you suggest?"

## How to Prepare for the Interview Circuit

### 1. Confidence, Directness, and Openness (1–5 pts)

**What they assess**: Your composure, honesty, and ability to engage naturally.

**Tips**:
- Practice maintaining eye contact (but don't stare)
- Sit up straight, keep your hands visible and relaxed
- If you don't know something, say so honestly: "I'm not sure about that, but based on what I know..."
- Avoid overly rehearsed or robotic answers
- Smile naturally when appropriate

### 2. Interpersonal Sensitivity (1–5 pts)

**What they assess**: Your empathy and ability to connect with others — essential for a future doctor.

**Tips**:
- Listen carefully to each question before responding
- Acknowledge the interviewer's points: "That's a great question..."
- Show awareness of patient perspectives when discussing medical topics
- Demonstrate that you understand medicine is about people, not just science

### 3. Task Orientation (1–5 pts)

**What they assess**: Your focus and ability to stay on topic.

**Tips**:
- Answer the question that was asked, not a different one
- Keep responses structured and concise (don't ramble)
- If a question has multiple parts, address each one
- Show you can prioritize information

### 4. Tolerance for Ambiguity (1–5 pts)

**What they assess**: Can you handle uncertainty? Medicine is full of it.

**Tips**:
- If given a vague or open-ended question, don't panic
- Think aloud: "There could be several explanations..."
- Show you can consider multiple possibilities before reaching a conclusion
- Avoid black-and-white thinking — medicine rarely has simple answers

### 5. Way of Thinking (1–5 pts)

**What they assess**: Your critical thinking and logical reasoning.

**Tips**:
- When discussing scientific topics, show cause-and-effect reasoning
- Use evidence to support your opinions
- Consider counterarguments or alternative explanations
- Demonstrate scientific thinking: hypothesis → evidence → conclusion

### 6. Interest and Motivation to Study (1–5 pts)

**What they assess**: Is your desire to study medicine genuine?

**Tips**:
- Prepare a clear, personal answer to "Why do you want to study medicine?"
- Be specific — generic answers like "I want to help people" are weak
- Share a personal story or experience that sparked your interest
- Explain specifically why Charles University and why Prague
- Show you've researched the program and know what to expect
- Mention long-term goals and how this program fits into them

## Common Interview Questions to Prepare

### About You and Your Motivation
1. Why do you want to become a doctor?
2. Why did you choose Charles University specifically?
3. Why do you want to study medicine in the Czech Republic?
4. What experience do you have in healthcare or volunteering?
5. What are your strengths and weaknesses?
6. Where do you see yourself in 10 years?
7. How do you handle stress and pressure?
8. What will you do if you don't get accepted?

### About Medicine and Science
9. What area of medicine interests you most and why?
10. What do you think is the biggest challenge facing healthcare today?
11. Can you describe a recent medical breakthrough you read about?
12. What is the difference between a virus and a bacterium?
13. Why is antibiotic resistance a growing concern?
14. What role does genetics play in modern medicine?
15. How has COVID-19 changed the medical field?

### Ethical and Critical Thinking
16. Should organ donation be mandatory? Why or why not?
17. How would you handle a situation where a patient refuses treatment?
18. What is informed consent and why is it important?
19. Is it ethical to use animals in medical research?
20. How do you balance empathy with objectivity as a doctor?

### About Studying Abroad
21. How will you adapt to living in a new country?
22. How do you plan to learn Czech (if applicable)?
23. What challenges do you expect in medical school?
24. How will you manage the workload of a medical program?
25. Do you have a support system in place?

## Sample Answer Framework

**Question**: "Why do you want to study medicine?"

**Weak answer**: "I want to help people and I like science."

**Strong answer structure**:
1. **Personal hook**: A specific experience that inspired you
2. **What you learned**: How that experience shaped your understanding of medicine
3. **Why medicine specifically**: What draws you to this career vs. other helping professions
4. **Why this program**: Specific reasons you chose Charles University
5. **Future vision**: What you hope to achieve

## Week-by-Week Preparation Plan

### 4 Weeks Before the Interview

**Week 1 – Foundation**
- Start reading 1 scientific article per day
- Write your "Why medicine?" answer and refine it
- Research Charles University's program thoroughly
- Practice basic biology and chemistry vocabulary in English

**Week 2 – Practice**
- Begin timed article summaries (read + summarize in 5 minutes total)
- Practice answering interview questions with a friend or in front of a mirror
- Prepare answers for the 25 common questions listed above
- Record yourself and review for filler words and nervous habits

**Week 3 – Simulation**
- Do full mock interviews (10-minute format)
- Practice with unfamiliar scientific articles
- Work on connecting article content to your broader knowledge
- Refine your posture, eye contact, and speaking pace

**Week 4 – Polish**
- Final mock interviews with feedback
- Review your key talking points without memorizing scripts
- Practice staying calm under pressure (try having someone ask unexpected questions)
- Get a good night's sleep before the exam

## Day-of Tips

1. **Dress professionally** — business casual at minimum
2. **Arrive early** — reduce stress by being there with time to spare
3. **Bring water** — your throat may get dry from nerves
4. **Take a breath** before answering each question — a 2-second pause is perfectly fine
5. **Speak slowly** — when nervous, people tend to rush. Slow down.
6. **Be yourself** — authenticity scores higher than perfection
7. **Thank the interviewers** at the end

---

## Key Scoring Insight

The **Evaluator Recommendation** (1–7 points) is based on the evaluator's **overall impression**. This means:
- Your general demeanor matters as much as your answers
- Being likeable, genuine, and passionate can earn you those critical extra points
- A candidate who is slightly less knowledgeable but more personable and motivated may score higher than a brilliant but cold candidate

**Remember**: They're not just selecting students — they're selecting future doctors. Show them you have both the mind and the heart for medicine.
